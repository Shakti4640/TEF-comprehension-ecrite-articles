Chapter 21 taught you to read ACROSS layers (personal → commercial → state) and ACROSS geographies (UK → US → France). This chapter demands you read ACROSS TIME — tracing the journey of artworks from a Renaissance studio in Rome through a French royal court to a modern museum. You will navigate specialized art vocabulary where everyday words carry expert meanings, follow a provenance narrative that reads like a detective story, decode architectural description as aesthetic commentary, and — most critically — distinguish between four distinct voices embedded in a single review: the journalist, the university scholar, the museum institution, and the artist himself. Le corps à corps de Michel-Ange

Exposition. Le Louvre présente son fonds de 43 dessins signés de l'artiste dans de nouvelles salles : peu de grands formats aboutis, mais de nombreux croquis et brouillons qui témoignent de l'évolution du peintre au cours de sa carrière.

Au cours de sa longue carrière, Michel-Ange aurait produit quelque 90 000 dessins. Il en reste peu, de 500 à 600 feuilles. Le temps a fait son œuvre, mais pas seulement : l'artiste, lui-même, a beaucoup détruit, à Rome, au soir de sa vie. Le Louvre en possède moins du dixième : 43, selon l'estimation de Paul Joannides de l'Université de Cambridge, auteur du catalogue raisonné de ce fonds, dont la parution donne au musée l'occasion de l'exposer dans sa totalité, accompagné de dessins d'élèves. Cette présentation, qui n'est pas inédite, inaugure des salles désormais vouées au dessin italien, à proximité des collections permanentes des peintures, au premier étage de l'aile Denon. On ne peut que saluer cette disposition qui facilite l'approche des arts graphiques, aidées par les cimaises de Jean-Michel Wilmotte. Ce fonds est constitué (sans un ajout depuis 1850) de feuilles dont on peut, pour beaucoup, remonter la piste jusqu'à Fontainebleau. Un jeune protégé de Michel-Ange les y avait amenées et probablement troquées contre gîte et couvert, auprès d'artistes de la cour. Leur fortune, qui est ensuite liée à celle des collections royales, explique les lacunes et les richesses de l'ensemble, qui n'a pas l'importance de ceux de Londres, d'Oxford et de Florence. Il est pauvre en dessins postérieurs à 1532, date à laquelle Michel-Ange, avant de se fixer à Rome, remettait deux caisses de dessins à son élève criblé de dettes. Les dessins d'architecture n'y sont pas nombreux, mais il y a cette projection de voûte qui interloque les chercheurs par son calcul bizarre, et le public pour le dessin épuré des arcs qui, présentés de côté, parce que dessinés en largeur au verso d'une crucifixion, font penser à des ondes sonores transcrites par un artiste futuriste. Là ne s'arrête pas d'ailleurs l'envie de creuser le rapport naturel des artistes italiens du début du siècle à Michel-Ange et son « dynamisme des formes dans l'espace ».

Every previous text in this course, no matter how complex, used vocabulary accessible to a general reader. This text does NOT. It is written for an art-literate audience and assumes familiarity with museum terminology, art history concepts, and critical vocabulary.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 32 — What does the exhibition show?

The question: L'exposition consacrée à Michel-Ange permet d'admirer...

This question tests whether you understand the nature of the exhibited works — their TYPE, not their subject matter.

Step 1: Identify what the exhibition contains.

The opening paragraph is explicit:

"Le Louvre présente son fonds de 43 dessins signés de l'artiste dans de nouvelles salles : peu de grands formats aboutis, mais de nombreux croquis et brouillons qui témoignent de l'évolution du peintre au cours de sa carrière."

Key terms:

  • Dessins = drawings (NOT paintings, NOT frescos, NOT engravings)
  • Peu de grands formats aboutis = few large finished works
  • Nombreux croquis et brouillons = many sketches and rough drafts
  • Témoignent de l'évolution = they show/witness the artist's evolution

The exhibition shows: DRAWINGS — mostly preliminary studies and sketches, showing how the artist evolved.

Step 2: Translate this into the vocabulary of the answer options.

What ARE "croquis and brouillons" in standard art terminology? They are études (studies) — preliminary works made in preparation for later, larger works. A "croquis" is a quick study. A "brouillon" is a rough draft. Together, they are studies for œuvres ultérieures (subsequent/later works).

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionWhat it claimsText evidenceVerdict
A) des toiles de jeunesse en trompe-l'œilCanvas paintings with optical illusionsThe text says "dessins" (drawings), NOT "toiles" (canvases/paintings). Also, no mention of trompe-l'œil or youth specifically❌ Wrong medium entirely — drawings ≠ canvas paintings
B) des études pour des œuvres ultérieuresStudies/sketches for later works"Croquis et brouillons" = studies. "Témoignent de l'évolution au cours de sa carrière" = they trace development toward later works✅ "Études" is the art-world synonym for "croquis et brouillons"
C) des fresques et des pièces monumentalesFrescoes and monumental piecesThe text says "peu de grands formats" (few large formats) — the opposite of monumental. And frescoes are wall paintings, not portable drawings❌ Contradicts "peu de grands formats" + wrong medium
D) des gravures sur bois représentant l'artisteWood engravings depicting the artistNo mention of wood engravings or self-portraits. The works are drawings on paper ("feuilles" = sheets)❌ Complete fabrication — wrong medium and wrong subject

Step 4: Understand the critical vocabulary chain.

TEXT:     "croquis et brouillons" = quick sketches and rough drafts
ANSWER:   "études pour des œuvres ultérieures" = studies for later works

TEXT:     "témoignent de l'évolution au cours de sa carrière"
ANSWER:   "ultérieures" = these studies were stepping stones toward finished works

The paraphrase elevates "croquis/brouillons" (informal, workshop vocabulary) to "études" (formal, art-historical vocabulary). This is the same register elevation technique from Chapter 20 (Q21: "minorités" → "groupuscules"), now applied to art terminology.

✅ Answer: B

Art criticism texts use vocabulary at multiple registers simultaneously. The journalist might use informal terms ("croquis," "brouillons") while the answer options use formal art-historical terms ("études"). Your job is to recognize that these refer to the SAME thing:

QUESTION 33 — What happens after this exhibition?

The question: Suite à cette exposition...

This question targets LAYER 3 (the museum arrangement) and tests whether you can distinguish between what the exhibition SHOWS and what the exhibition INAUGURATES.

Step 1: Locate the passage about post-exhibition arrangements.

"Cette présentation, qui n'est pas inédite, inaugure des salles désormais vouées au dessin italien, à proximité des collections permanentes des peintures, au premier étage de l'aile Denon."

This is an extraordinarily dense sentence. Let's unpack it piece by piece:

ClauseMeaningSignificance
"Cette présentation"This exhibition/showingRefers to the Michel-Ange exhibition
"qui n'est pas inédite"which is not unprecedentedThese drawings have been shown before
"inaugure"inaugurates/opens for the first timeThe ROOMS are new, even if the exhibition isn't
"des salles désormais vouées au dessin italien"rooms henceforth dedicated to Italian drawingNEW permanent rooms for Italian drawings (not just Michel-Ange)
"à proximité des collections permanentes des peintures"near the permanent painting collectionsDrawings will be NEAR paintings — in the SAME WING
"au premier étage de l'aile Denon"on the first floor of the Denon wingSpecific location within the Louvre

The KEY information: The exhibition inaugurates new rooms that will be permanently dedicated to Italian drawing, located near the permanent painting collections in the Denon wing.

Step 2: Test each option against this precise information.

OptionWhat it claimsMatches the text?Verdict
A) les œuvres présentées entreront dans les collections permanentesThe exhibited works will join permanent collectionsThe works are ALREADY in the Louvre's collection (since before 1850). The text says "Le Louvre en possède" (present tense — already owns them). What's new is the ROOMS, not the acquisition❌ The works are already in the collection — nothing changes about their status
B) un étage entier du musée sera dédié aux dessins d'artistes italiensAn entire FLOOR for Italian drawingsThe text says "des salles" (some rooms), not an entire floor. "Salles" (plural but limited) ≠ "étage entier" (an entire floor)❌ Exaggeration: rooms ≠ entire floor
C) tableaux et dessins italiens seront réunis dans la même aile du muséeItalian paintings AND drawings will be together in the same wingThe text says drawings will be "à proximité des collections permanentes des peintures" in the Denon wing. D) quelques salles du musée seront consacrées à Michel-Ange et ses élèvesRooms dedicated to Michel-Ange and his studentsThe text says rooms dedicated to "dessin italien" (Italian drawing in general), NOT specifically to Michel-Ange. The exhibition includes Michel-Ange + students, but the ROOMS are for ALL Italian drawing❌ Too narrow: the rooms are for Italian drawing broadly, not just Michel-Ange

Step 3: The critical distinction — exhibition scope vs. room scope.

This question contains a subtle trap based on SCOPE:

THE EXHIBITION:  Michel-Ange's 43 drawings + student drawings (SPECIFIC)
THE NEW ROOMS:   Italian drawing in general (BROAD)

Option D confuses the exhibition's scope (Michel-Ange + students) with the rooms' scope (all Italian drawing). The rooms will continue to exist AFTER this exhibition ends, hosting OTHER Italian drawings too.

Option C correctly captures the rooms' BROADER function: bringing Italian drawings near Italian paintings within the same wing.

Step 4: Verify with "à proximité."

"À proximité des collections permanentes des peintures" means the drawing rooms are NEAR the painting galleries. Since both are in the Denon wing ("aile Denon"), they will be "réunis dans la même aile" (united in the same wing).

TEXT:    "à proximité des collections permanentes des peintures, 
         au premier étage de l'aile Denon"
ANSWER:  "tableaux et dessins italiens seront réunis dans la même aile"

"À proximité" + "même aile" = same idea, different phrasing.

✅ Answer: C

Museum texts often describe both a TEMPORARY exhibition and PERMANENT changes to the museum. Questions will test whether you can distinguish:

Temporary (exhibition)Permanent (rooms/arrangement)

When a question says "suite à cette exposition" (following this exhibition), it asks about LASTING CONSEQUENCES — the permanent arrangement, not the temporary display.

QUESTION 34 — The provenance story

The question: Selon l'article, un disciple de l'artiste a sans doute...

This question targets LAYER 4 (the provenance narrative) — the detective story of how Michel-Ange's drawings ended up at the Louvre.

Step 1: Locate the provenance passage.

"Un jeune protégé de Michel-Ange les y avait amenées et probablement troquées contre gîte et couvert, auprès d'artistes de la cour."

Let's decode every element:

ElementMeaning
"Un jeune protégé"A young protégé/disciple
"les y avait amenées"had brought them there (to Fontainebleau)
"probablement"probably (uncertainty marker — hence "sans doute" in the question)
"troquées"traded/exchanged/bartered
"contre gîte et couvert"in exchange for shelter and food (literally: lodging and board)
"auprès d'artistes de la cour"with/from artists at the [royal] court

The story: A young disciple of Michel-Ange brought drawings to Fontainebleau and probably traded them for room and board with court artists.

Step 2: Understand "gîte et couvert" — the critical idiom.

"Gîte et couvert" is a French expression meaning accommodation and meals — literally "shelter and food/table setting." It's what you'd provide a guest: a roof over their head and food to eat.

This idiom is the KEY to the answer. The disciple exchanged valuable drawings for the basic necessities of a place to sleep and food to eat.

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionWhat the disciple didMatches the text?Verdict
A) vendu des œuvres à des aristocrates fortunésSold works to wealthy aristocrats"Troquées" = traded/bartered, NOT sold. Trading implies no money changed hands — it was an exchange of goods (drawings for shelter). Also, "artistes de la cour" = court artists, NOT aristocrats❌ Wrong verb (sold ≠ traded) + wrong recipient (aristocrats ≠ court artists)
B) dérobé des œuvres pour y puiser son inspirationStole works for inspirationThe text says Michel-Ange's protégé BROUGHT them — implying they were given or entrusted to him. No suggestion of theft❌ "Amenées" (brought) ≠ "dérobé" (stolen). No theft suggested
C) caché des œuvres dans les oubliettes d'un châteauHidden works in a castle dungeonComplete fabrication. "Fontainebleau" is a royal palace, but no "oubliettes" (dungeons) are mentioned. The drawings were TRADED, not hidden❌ Romantic fantasy — nothing in the text supports this
D) donné des œuvres en échange d'un toit pour dormirGave works in exchange for a roof to sleep under"Troquées contre gîte et couvert" = traded for shelter and food. "Donné en échange" = gave in exchange. "Toit pour dormir" = roof to sleep under = "gîte"✅ Perfect paraphrase of "troquées contre gîte et couvert"

Step 4: The "vendu" vs. "troqué" vs. "donné" distinction.

This question tests a precise vocabulary distinction:

VerbMeaningInvolves money?
Vendre (to sell)Exchange for MONEYYes
Troquer (to barter/trade)Exchange for OTHER GOODS or SERVICESNo — exchange of equivalent value
Donner (to give)Transfer without receiving anythingNo — unilateral gift
Donner en échange de (to give in exchange for)Transfer in return for somethingNo money — but bilateral exchange

The text says "troquées" (bartered). Option A says "vendu" (sold) — wrong because no money was involved. Option D says "donné en échange de" (gave in exchange for) — this captures the barter concept without implying money.

"Troquer" and "donner en échange de" are SYNONYMS in this context. Both describe a non-monetary exchange.

Step 5: The "gîte et couvert" → "toit pour dormir" paraphrase.

gîte (shelter/lodging)toit pour dormir (roof to sleep under)A place to stay
couvert (food/board)(not explicitly in option D)Food and meals

Option D captures "gîte" (→ "toit pour dormir") but doesn't explicitly mention "couvert" (food). This is acceptable because the option paraphrases the ESSENTIAL meaning — the disciple exchanged art for basic survival needs — even if it doesn't capture every detail.

✅ Answer: D

QUESTION 35 — The standout piece

The question: L'une des œuvres visibles au Louvre...

This question targets LAYER 5 (the standout piece) and is the most complex question in the chapter. It tests whether you can read an aesthetic description and extract a FACTUAL conclusion from it.

Step 1: Locate the description of the standout piece.

"...mais il y a cette projection de voûte qui interloque les chercheurs par son calcul bizarre, et le public pour le dessin épuré des arcs qui, présentés de côté, parce que dessinés en largeur au verso d'une crucifixion, font penser à des ondes sonores transcrites par un artiste futuriste."

This is the densest sentence in the entire course. Let's break it into components:

ComponentWhat it describes
"cette projection de voûte"This architectural vault drawing
"interloque les chercheurs"Puzzles/astonishes researchers/experts
"par son calcul bizarre"Because of its strange mathematical calculation
"et le public"AND astonishes the general public
"pour le dessin épuré des arcs"Because of the stripped-down/minimalist arc drawing
"présentés de côté"Displayed sideways
"dessinés en largeur au verso d'une crucifixion"Drawn horizontally on the BACK of a crucifixion drawing
"font penser à des ondes sonores"Resemble sound waves
"transcrites par un artiste futuriste"As transcribed by a futurist artist

The key DUAL structure:

RESEARCHERS are astonished → by the BIZARRE CALCULATION
        +
THE PUBLIC is astonished → by the MINIMALIST AESTHETIC (looks like sound waves / futurist art)

= BOTH experts AND non-experts find this work remarkable

Step 2: Test each option.

OptionWhat it claimsText evidenceVerdict
A) présente un détail architectural esquissé au fusainAn architectural detail drawn in charcoalThe text mentions an architectural drawing (projection de voûte) but NEVER mentions the medium (fusain = charcoal). The text describes WHAT it looks like, not WHAT it's drawn with❌ "Fusain" (charcoal) is fabricated — no mention of the medium
B) a fait l'objet de précédentes expositions en EuropeHas been exhibited before in EuropeThe text says "Cette présentation, qui n'est pas inédite" — but this refers to the ENTIRE EXHIBITION of 43 drawings, not to this specific vault drawing. No information about whether this SPECIFIC piece has been shown before❌ "N'est pas inédite" describes the exhibition as a whole, not this individual work

The paraphrase is precise:

  • "Chercheurs" → "initiés" (both = experts)
  • "Le public" → "non-initiés" (both = general audience)
  • "Interloque" → "étonnante" (both = surprising/remarkable)

Step 4: Why option B is the most dangerous trap.

Option B references "n'est pas inédite" from the text. Your brain recognizes the phrase and connects it to "précédentes expositions." But the REFERENT is wrong:

"Cette PRÉSENTATION, qui n'est pas inédite" 
= THIS EXHIBITION (of 43 drawings) is not unprecedented
≠ THIS SPECIFIC VAULT DRAWING has been exhibited before

"Cette présentation" = the act of showing these 43 drawings as a group. It does NOT refer to the vault drawing discussed later. This is a referent confusion trap — the phrase exists in the text, but it refers to something DIFFERENT from what the option claims.

This is the same source-attribution technique from Chapter 19 (Q34), now applied to REFERENTS within a text: you must track which noun a description applies to.

✅ Answer: D

🔑 Phase 5 technique: DUAL-AUDIENCE RECOGNITION

Art criticism texts often describe how a work affects DIFFERENT AUDIENCES differently:

EXPERTS appreciate it for: [technical/intellectual reason]
THE PUBLIC appreciates it for: [visual/emotional reason]

When a question asks about a work's impact, check whether the text describes reactions from MULTIPLE audiences. If it does, the correct answer will acknowledge BOTH audiences.

This technique also applies beyond art criticism — in Phase 5, any text discussing specialists AND the general public will use this dual-audience structure.

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

1. Build an Art Vocabulary Translation Table

For any art criticism text, create a quick translation between everyday and specialized meanings:

EVERYDAY → ART-WORLD:
fonds = collection (not funds)
feuilles = sheets of paper (not leaves)

You don't need to memorize these before the exam. But when you encounter them in context, PAUSE and ask: "Does the everyday meaning make sense here, or is there a specialized meaning?"

2. Create a Provenance Timeline

For any text discussing art collections, draw the journey:

MICHEL-ANGE'S STUDIO (Rome, created ~90,000 drawings)
        ↓ many destroyed by the artist himself
YOUNG PROTÉGÉ (brought drawings to France)
        ↓ traded for room and board
FONTAINEBLEAU (court artists acquired them)
        ↓ linked to royal collections
ROYAL COLLECTIONS → LOUVRE (no additions since 1850)
        ↓ Paul Joannides catalogued them
EXHIBITION (43 drawings in new rooms, Denon wing)

This timeline answers Q34 (protégé story) and helps contextualize Q33 (museum arrangement).

3. Identify the REFERENT of every description

In dense art texts, descriptions can float between referents. Always ask: "What does THIS description apply to?"

"Cette présentation, qui n'est pas inédite" → refers to the EXHIBITION (43 drawings as a group)
"cette projection de voûte qui interloque" → refers to ONE SPECIFIC DRAWING
"des salles désormais vouées au dessin italien" → refers to NEW PERMANENT ROOMS

Confusing these referents leads directly to wrong answers (e.g., Q33D: rooms for Michel-Ange vs. rooms for Italian drawing; Q35B: exhibition shown before vs. specific drawing shown before).

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Specialized Vocabulary Panic"

What happens: You encounter "catalogue raisonné," "cimaises," "projection de voûte," "arts graphiques," and freeze. You feel the text is beyond your level.

Why it's survivable: You do NOT need to know what "cimaises de Jean-Michel Wilmotte" means to answer any question. The questions test your ability to extract RELATIONSHIPS and FACTS, not your art history knowledge.

The survival strategy:

When you hit an unknown term, ask: "Can I answer the questions WITHOUT understanding this word?"

"cimaises de Jean-Michel Wilmotte" → No question asks about this → SKIP
"catalogue raisonné" → No question asks about the catalogue's content → SKIP
"projection de voûte" → Q35 asks about this drawing → MUST UNDERSTAND

For terms you MUST understand, use CONTEXT CLUES:

  • "Projection de voûte" appears alongside "dessins d'architecture" → it's an architectural drawing
  • "Interloque les chercheurs par son calcul bizarre" → it has a strange mathematical aspect
  • "Le dessin épuré des arcs" → it features clean/minimalist arc lines

You now know ENOUGH about this drawing to answer Q35 without knowing what "projection de voûte" technically means in architecture.

🔑 Rule: In specialized texts, separate MUST-UNDERSTAND terms (tested by questions) from NICE-TO-KNOW terms (not tested). Focus your energy on the former.

Pitfall 2: The "Scope Confusion" Trap

What happens: You conflate two different scopes that the text carefully distinguishes.

Examples from this text:

Scope AScope BThe confusion
The exhibition (43 Michel-Ange drawings)The new rooms (Italian drawing generally)Q33D: attributing room scope to exhibition scope
The exhibition ("n'est pas inédite")A specific drawing (vault projection)Q35B: attributing exhibition description to a specific work

The fix: When reading museum/exhibition texts, maintain THREE mental categories:

1. THE SPECIFIC EXHIBITION (temporary, focused, named)
2. THE COLLECTION (what the museum permanently owns)
3. THE INSTITUTIONAL CHANGE (new rooms, new arrangements)

Each question targets ONE of these categories. Don't let information from one leak into another.

🔑 Rule: Exhibition ≠ Collection ≠ Institutional change. Track which category each piece of information belongs to.

Pitfall 3: The "Romantic Fabrication" Trap

What happens: The text tells a fascinating provenance story, and the exam offers an option that ENHANCES the romance with invented details.

Example: Q34C — "caché des œuvres dans les oubliettes d'un château." Fontainebleau IS a château. The story IS dramatic (a young protégé trading drawings for survival). Your imagination fills in the gap: "Maybe he HID them in the dungeons!"

But the text says nothing about hiding or dungeons. The drawings were TRADED openly with court artists, not concealed in secret chambers.

The fix: Separate what the text STATES from what your imagination ADDS:

TEXT STATES:     "troquées contre gîte et couvert, auprès d'artistes de la cour"
                 = traded for room and board with court artists

YOUR IMAGINATION: "hidden in castle dungeons" ← NOT IN THE TEXT

🔑 Rule (reinforced from Chapter 1): Trust the text, not your imagination. The more dramatic an option sounds, the more likely it's fabricated.

Pitfall 4: The "Partial Vocabulary Match" at Expert Level

What happens: You recognize a word from the text in an answer option and select it without checking whether it's applied to the same referent.

Example: Q35C — "inscriptions au sens inconnu." The text mentions "calcul bizarre" (strange calculation). Both "inscriptions" and "calcul" involve markings on paper. Both "sens inconnu" and "bizarre" suggest strangeness. Your brain connects them.

But:

  • "Calcul" = mathematical calculation ≠ "inscriptions" = written text/symbols
  • "Bizarre" = unusual/strange ≠ "sens inconnu" = meaning unknown

A strange calculation is one that uses unexpected mathematical methods. It doesn't mean its MEANING is unknown — researchers understand it's a vault projection, they just find the MATH unusual.

🔑 Rule: Partial vocabulary overlap ≠ semantic match. "Calcul bizarre" and "inscriptions au sens inconnu" share the THEME of strangeness but describe DIFFERENT phenomena.
