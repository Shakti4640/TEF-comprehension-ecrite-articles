CHAPTER 5: FACTS VS. OPINIONS VS. QUOTES — SORTING INFORMATION IN THE SALMON ARTICLE

Foundation Thinking | Phase 1 of 5

📄 THE ARTICLE

Same Texte 102 as Chapter 4 (refer back for full text). In this chapter, we dissect every single statement in the article and classify it as FACT, OPINION, or ATTRIBUTED QUOTE — then show why this classification is the key to answering detail questions correctly.

🧠 THE PROBLEM: NOT ALL INFORMATION HAS THE SAME VALUE

Here is what most students do when answering detail questions:

WHAT MOST STUDENTS DO:

  Read the question → Scan the text for matching words
  → Find a sentence that "sounds right" → Pick the answer
  
  WHAT THEY NEVER ASK:
  
  → WHO says this? The journalist? An expert? A politician?
  → IS this a proven fact or someone's claim?
  → Does the text CONFIRM this or merely REPORT it?
  → Could this statement be BIASED?

This matters enormously because exam questions are designed to test whether you can tell the difference between:

╔═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║         THREE TYPES OF INFORMATION IN ANY TEXT                 ║
╠═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║                                                               ║
║  TYPE 1: FACT                                                 ║
║  = Verifiable, objective, data-based                          ║
║  = The journalist states this as TRUE                         ║
║  = Example: "Les exportations ont atteint 7,5 milliards"     ║
║  = You CAN rely on this for answering questions               ║
║                                                               ║
║  TYPE 2: OPINION                                              ║
║  = Subjective judgment, interpretation, recommendation        ║
║  = Someone THINKS or BELIEVES this                            ║
║  = Example: "Il faut changer le modèle industriel"            ║
║  = You must ATTRIBUTE this to the person who said it          ║
║                                                               ║
║  TYPE 3: ATTRIBUTED CLAIM                                     ║
║  = Reported by the journalist but NOT confirmed as true       ║
║  = Marked by "selon", conditional tense, quotation marks      ║
║  = Example: "Le saumon pourrait contenir des produits         ║
║    toxiques"                                                  ║
║  = Presented as POSSIBLE, not certain                         ║
║                                                               ║
╚═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

Why does this classification matter for the exam? Because wrong answers often present an OPINION as if it were a FACT, or present a CLAIM as if the text CONFIRMED it. If you cannot tell the difference, you will fall into these traps.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE COMPLETE INFORMATION AUTOPSY OF TEXTE 102

PARAGRAPH 1: THE ECONOMIC CONTEXT

Let us isolate each statement:

Statement 1.1:

"La consommation de saumon augmente chaque année"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist (no attribution)
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Present tense, declarative
                      "augmente" = increases (stated as ongoing reality)
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — consumption data can be checked
  MARKERS:          → No "selon", no conditional, no quotes
  
  TYPE: FACT ✅
  
  The journalist presents this as established, 
  verifiable truth. No hedging, no attribution.

Statement 1.2:

"la Norvège, premier producteur mondial"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Appositive phrase, stated as given
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — production rankings are public data
  MARKERS:          → No hedging language
  
  TYPE: FACT ✅
  
  Norway IS the world's top producer. 
  This is verifiable economic data.

Statement 1.3:

"doit s'adapter pour répondre à la demande quitte à industrialiser toujours plus sa production"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "doit s'adapter" = must adapt
                      "quitte à" = even if it means
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Partially — "must adapt" contains 
                      INTERPRETATION
  MARKERS:          → "doit" (must) implies necessity 
                      = slight editorial judgment
                      "quitte à" implies a trade-off
                      = editorial framing
  
  TYPE: FACT WITH EDITORIAL FRAMING ⚠️
  
  The underlying fact (Norway industrializes more) 
  is true. But the framing ("must", "even if it means")
  adds the journalist's interpretation that this is 
  driven by necessity and involves a cost.

📌 This is your first encounter with a HYBRID statement — factual content wrapped in editorial framing. These are the trickiest to classify and the most commonly tested on exams.

Statement 1.4:

"Multiplier par cinq la production d'ici 2050, voilà l'objectif ambitieux de la Norvège"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist, reporting Norway's goal
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "l'objectif ambitieux" = the ambitious goal
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → The goal itself: YES (official target)
                      The word "ambitieux": EDITORIAL CHOICE
  MARKERS:          → "ambitieux" is the journalist's adjective
                      Norway doesn't call its own goal "ambitious"
                      in this text — the journalist does
  
  TYPE: FACT (the x5 goal) + OPINION WORD ("ambitieux") ⚠️
  
  LESSON: Even in factual statements, individual 
  ADJECTIVES can carry editorial opinion.
  "x5 by 2050" = fact
  "ambitieux" = the journalist's judgment

Statement 1.5:

"Les exportations norvégiennes ont atteint un record en 2016 avec un chiffre d'affaire de 7,5 milliards d'euros, rien que pour le saumon"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Past tense, specific numbers, specific year
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Completely — export data is public
  MARKERS:          → Numbers, dates = hard data
                      "rien que" (just for) = emphasis, not opinion
  
  TYPE: PURE FACT ✅✅
  
  This is the MOST factual statement in the entire text.
  Specific number (€7.5B), specific year (2016), 
  specific scope (salmon only). 
  No interpretation, no opinion.

Statement 1.6:

"C'est la France qui se trouve en tête du classement des consommateurs de produits marins norvégiens : nous consommons en moyenne 2,3 kilos de saumon norvégien par an"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Emphatic structure "C'est...qui"
                      Specific number (2.3 kg), specific measure
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — consumption rankings and averages
  MARKERS:          → "nous consommons" = "we consume"
                      → reveals the journalist is FRENCH
                      → addresses a FRENCH audience
                      This is data, not opinion
  
  TYPE: FACT ✅
  
  NOTE: "nous" reveals the text's target audience (French).
  This is a useful contextual clue, not an opinion marker.

PARAGRAPH 2: THE PIVOT

Statement 2.1:

"Cette augmentation constante de la demande pousse le pays scandinave à mettre en place des projets faramineux"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Cause-effect structure
                      "pousse...à" = pushes...to
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → The projects exist: YES
                      "faramineux" (staggering): EDITORIAL
  MARKERS:          → "faramineux" = journalist's adjective
                      expressing that the scale is extreme
  
  TYPE: FACT (projects exist) + OPINION WORD ("faramineux") ⚠️

Statement 2.2:

"comme par exemple une ferme offshore en haute mer capable d'accueillir plus d'un million de saumons"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist, providing an example
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "comme par exemple" = for example
                      Specific detail: offshore farm, 1M+ salmon
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — such projects have been announced
  MARKERS:          → "par exemple" = factual illustration
  
  TYPE: FACT ✅
  
  This is a concrete, verifiable example.

Statement 2.3:

"Mais l'industrialisation massive de l'élevage pose des problèmes pour les saumons sauvages"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "MAIS" = pivot word
                      "pose des problèmes" = creates problems
                      Stated as reality, not hypothesis
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — environmental impact is documented
  MARKERS:          → "massive" = editorial emphasis
                      "pose des problèmes" = factual assessment
                      supported by the rest of the article
  
  TYPE: FACT (with editorial emphasis "massive") ✅
  
  The problems ARE documented. The journalist states 
  this as established reality, then provides evidence 
  in the following paragraphs.

Statement 2.4:

"Le saumon sauvage mis en péril par le saumon industriel"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist (this reads like a 
                      SUB-HEADLINE within the article)
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Declarative, headline-style
                      No verb conjugated = journalistic shorthand
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — ecological studies confirm this
  MARKERS:          → "mis en péril" = endangered
                      Strong language but factually supported
  
  TYPE: FACT (stated as established reality) ✅
  
  NOTE: This sentence functions as an INTERNAL HEADLINE,
  announcing what the next paragraph will detail.
  It's a structural marker, not just content.

PARAGRAPH 3: THE PROBLEMS — WHERE IT GETS COMPLEX

This is where the information types mix heavily. Facts, claims, quotes, and opinions coexist in the same paragraph. This is the paragraph most likely to generate exam traps.

Statement 3.1:

"L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes en ce qui concerne le saumon : la prolifération des poux de mer et l'évasion des saumons d'élevage"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Declarative, specific ("deux problèmes")
                      Lists them precisely
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — both are documented phenomena
  MARKERS:          → "pose deux problèmes" = states as fact
                      No hedging, no "selon", no conditional
  
  TYPE: FACT ✅
  
  The journalist presents these two problems as 
  established, documented realities.

Statement 3.2:

"Ces deux conséquences de l'élevage à très grande échelle mettent en péril l'écosystème et le patrimoine génétique des saumons sauvages"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Declarative, cause-effect
                      "mettent en péril" = endanger (present tense)
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — ecological studies document this
  MARKERS:          → "à très grande échelle" = editorial emphasis
                      "patrimoine génétique" = scientific term
  
  TYPE: FACT (with scientific vocabulary) ✅
  
  This is the journalist's synthesis of scientific 
  knowledge, stated as established reality.

Statement 3.3 — THE FIRST QUOTE:

«Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts d'un point de vue technique pour trouver des solutions pour réduire les traitements chimiques et les remplacer par des techniques naturelles,» indique Catarina Martins, directrice environnement de la compagnie Marine Harvest.

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → CATARINA MARTINS
                      directrice environnement, Marine Harvest
  HOW IS IT STATED? → In GUILLEMETS «...» = direct quotation
                      Verb: "indique" = indicates/states
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Her CLAIM is that they make efforts
                      Whether those efforts are SUFFICIENT 
                      or EFFECTIVE is not verified
  MARKERS:          → «...» = direct speech
                      "indique" = neutral attribution verb
                      
  TYPE: ATTRIBUTED QUOTE (INDUSTRY PERSPECTIVE) 🔵
  
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CRITICAL ANALYSIS:                          │
  │                                              │
  │  WHO is speaking?                            │
  │  → An INDUSTRY executive                     │
  │  → She works FOR the salmon farming company  │
  │  → She has a VESTED INTEREST in presenting   │
  │    the industry positively                   │
  │                                              │
  │  WHAT does she say?                          │
  │  → "We make a lot of effort"                 │
  │  → "to find solutions"                       │
  │  → "to reduce chemical treatments"           │
  │  → "to replace them with natural techniques" │
  │                                              │
  │  WHAT does she NOT say?                      │
  │  → She doesn't say they SUCCEEDED            │
  │  → She doesn't deny the problems EXIST       │
  │  → She doesn't provide DATA on improvements  │
  │                                              │
  │  RELIABILITY: MODERATE                       │
  │  → She admits problems exist (chemicals)     │
  │  → But minimizes by focusing on "efforts"    │
  │  → Potential BIAS: protecting her company     │
  │                                              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

📌 This is the first time in our guide we encounter a BIASED SOURCE. Catarina Martins works for Marine Harvest, one of the world's largest salmon farming companies. Her statement is not WRONG, but it is SELF-INTERESTED. The exam may test whether you recognize this bias.

Statement 3.4:

"Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés à des produits toxiques"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "peuvent être exposés" = CAN be exposed
                      Not "sont exposés" (ARE exposed)
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → The POSSIBILITY is documented
  MARKERS:          → "peuvent" = modal verb of POSSIBILITY
                      Not certainty — HEDGED statement
  
  TYPE: HEDGED FACT / ATTRIBUTED CLAIM ⚠️
  
  The journalist uses "peuvent" (can) instead of 
  "sont" (are), indicating this is POSSIBLE but 
  not presented as universal certainty.

Statement 3.5:

"Le saumon pourrait aussi contenir des produits toxiques comme des dioxydes, des pesticides et même des métaux lourds"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "POURRAIT contenir" = COULD contain
                      CONDITIONAL tense = not confirmed
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Studies exist, but "pourrait" hedges
  MARKERS:          → "pourrait" = conditional
                      "aussi" = also (adding to previous concerns)
                      "même" = even (escalation marker)
  
  TYPE: HEDGED CLAIM ⚠️
  
  The conditional "pourrait" is CRITICAL here.
  The journalist does NOT say salmon CONTAINS toxins.
  The journalist says salmon COULD contain toxins.
  
  This is the SAME hedging technique we saw in 
  Texte 101 (Chapter 1) with Kurzweil's predictions.
  Conditional = reported possibility, not confirmed fact.

Statement 3.6:

"En 2013, la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation de saumon modérée pour les enfants et les femmes enceintes"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist, reporting Norway's action
  HOW IS IT STATED? → Past tense, specific year, specific action
                      "recommandait déjà" = already recommended
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Yes — government health recommendations 
                      are public record
  MARKERS:          → Specific date (2013)
                      "déjà" = already (implies this isn't new)
                      Official government action = verifiable
  
  TYPE: FACT ✅
  
  This is a HISTORICAL FACT — a verifiable government 
  action from a specific year.
  
  NOTE: "déjà" (already) is an editorial word suggesting 
  that concerns existed BEFORE the current debate.
  It adds temporal context, not opinion.

Statement 3.7:

"Mais le risque, dû notamment à la pollution des eaux, ne concerne plus uniquement le saumon d'élevage"

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → The journalist
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "MAIS" = second pivot word
                      "ne concerne PLUS UNIQUEMENT" = 
                      no longer concerns ONLY
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Partially — based on scientific studies
  MARKERS:          → "mais" = pivot/escalation
                      "ne...plus uniquement" = scope expansion
                      "notamment" = notably (specifying cause)
  
  TYPE: FACT WITH EDITORIAL FRAMING ⚠️
  
  The EXPANSION of risk beyond farmed salmon is 
  presented as established reality. The journalist 
  states this directly, not conditionally.
  
  Compare: "pourrait contenir" (3.5) = hedged
           "ne concerne plus uniquement" (3.7) = stated as fact
  
  The journalist is MORE certain about the scope 
  expansion than about specific toxin content.

PARAGRAPH 4: THE ENVIRONMENTALIST'S VOICE

Statement 4.1 — THE SECOND QUOTE:

«Aujourd'hui même le saumon bio est pollué par les métaux lourds et notamment le mercure»

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → JEAN-PHILIPPE MAGNEN
                      ancien élu Europe Ecologie Les Verts

  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → Studies on organic salmon contamination exist

  MARKERS:          → «...» = direct speech
                      "affirme" = stronger than "indique"
                      "même" = even (shock/escalation marker)
                      
  TYPE: ATTRIBUTED QUOTE (ENVIRONMENTALIST PERSPECTIVE) 🟢
  
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CRITICAL ANALYSIS:                          │
  │                                              │
  │  WHO is speaking?                            │
  │  → A former GREEN PARTY politician           │
  │  → He has an ENVIRONMENTALIST agenda          │
  │  → He has a VESTED INTEREST in presenting    │
  │    environmental problems as serious         │
  │                                              │
  │  WHAT does he say?                           │
  │  → Even ORGANIC salmon is polluted           │
  │  → By heavy metals, notably mercury          │
  │                                              │
  │  HOW STRONG is his language?                 │
  │  → "affirme" (affirms) = strong assertion    │
  │  → "même" (even) = shock value               │
  │  → No hedging, no "peut-être"                │
  │  → Very confident, very direct               │
  │                                              │
  │  RELIABILITY: MODERATE                       │
  │  → His claim is likely based on real data    │
  │  → But his political position creates bias   │
  │  → He may EMPHASIZE the worst aspects        │
  │                                              │
  │  COMPARE WITH MARTINS (3.3):                 │
  │  → Martins (industry): minimizes problems    │
  │  → Magnen (green party): maximizes problems  │
  │  → The journalist presents BOTH voices       │
  │  → The READER must evaluate both             │
  │                                              │

Statement 4.2 — THE SECOND QUOTE (continued):

«Il faut changer le modèle industriel d'élevage, aujourd'hui il y a une pollution qui est très inquiétante, qui n'est pas seulement sur le saumon d'élevage puisque les eaux sont polluées avec des métaux lourds.»

CLASSIFICATION:

  WHO SAYS THIS?    → Still MAGNEN (same quotation block)
  HOW IS IT STATED? → "Il faut changer" = we must change
                      = PRESCRIPTIVE (says what SHOULD happen)
  IS IT VERIFIABLE? → "Il faut" = opinion/recommendation
                      "pollution très inquiétante" = judgment
                      "les eaux sont polluées" = factual claim
  MARKERS:          → "il faut" = prescriptive = OPINION
                      "très inquiétante" = emotional judgment
                      
  TYPE: OPINION + FACTUAL CLAIM (mixed) 🟢⚠️
  
  BREAKDOWN:
  → "Il faut changer le modèle" = OPINION (prescription)
  → "il y a une pollution" = CLAIM (stated as fact by him)
  → "très inquiétante" = OPINION (value judgment)
  → "les eaux sont polluées avec des métaux lourds" = 
    CLAIM (potentially verifiable but stated by him)

  1. Establishes CREDIBILITY with hard data (Para 1)
  2. Introduces PROBLEMS with factual evidence (Para 2-3)
  3. Lets EXPERTS speak their perspectives (Para 3-4)
  4. Ends with the STRONGEST voice demanding change (Para 4)

🔧 APPLYING THIS TO THE EXAM QUESTIONS

QUESTION 2 ANALYSIS:

Question 2 : Les éleveurs :
  - Améliorent la couleur des saumons pour le rendre 
    plus appétissant

Testing this answer against our information map:

SEARCH: Does the text say ANYTHING about:
  → "éleveurs" (farmers/breeders)?
  → "améliorer la couleur" (improve color)?
  → "appétissant" (appetizing)?
  
  PARAGRAPH 1: economic data          → NO mention of color
  PARAGRAPH 2: scale + problems       → NO mention of color
  PARAGRAPH 3: lice, toxins, quotes   → NO mention of color
  PARAGRAPH 4: environmentalist quote → NO mention of color
  
  RESULT: The text mentions NOTHING about improving 
  salmon color or making it more appetizing.
  
  THIS ANSWER IS NOT SUPPORTED BY THE TEXT. ❌
  
  This information may be TRUE in real life (salmon 
  farmers do use additives for color), but the TEXT 
  does not mention it.
  
  CRITICAL RULE: In Compréhension Écrite, only what 
  the TEXT says matters. Not your outside knowledge.

📌 This is one of the most important lessons in the entire guide: YOUR KNOWLEDGE OF THE REAL WORLD IS IRRELEVANT. Only the text counts. If the text doesn't say it, the answer is wrong — even if you know it's true in reality.

QUESTION 3 ANALYSIS:

Question 3 : Les méthodes utilisées servent à :
  - Eliminer parasites présentes dans les saumons

Testing this answer against our information map:

SEARCH: Does the text mention methods to eliminate parasites?

  Statement 3.1: "la prolifération des poux de mer"
  → Sea lice ARE mentioned as a PROBLEM
  → But the text doesn't say methods eliminate them
  
  Statement 3.3 (Martins quote): 
  "réduire les traitements chimiques et les remplacer 
   par des techniques naturelles"
  → Chemical treatments ARE mentioned
  → They are being REPLACED by natural techniques
  → But the purpose stated is to "REDUCE chemicals"
  → NOT explicitly to "eliminate parasites"
  
  ANALYSIS:
  → Parasites (sea lice) are mentioned as a PROBLEM ✅
  → Chemical treatments exist ✅  
  → But the text says methods aim to REDUCE CHEMICALS,
    not specifically to ELIMINATE PARASITES
  → The connection between chemicals and parasites 
    is IMPLIED but not EXPLICIT
  
  THIS ANSWER REQUIRES INFERENCE:
  Chemical treatments → probably used against parasites
  → methods to reduce chemicals → probably related to 
    parasite control
  
  BUT: The text never explicitly says 
  "methods to eliminate parasites"

This question demonstrates why the fact/opinion/claim classification matters:

THE ATTRIBUTION PROBLEM:
                                                    
  WHO says methods are being developed?
  → CATARINA MARTINS (industry executive)
  
  IS this confirmed by the journalist as fact?
  → NO — it's in quotation marks, attributed to her
  
  DOES the journalist verify her claim?
  → NO — he simply reports it
  
  SO: If the exam asks "what methods are used,"
  the answer is based on AN INDUSTRY EXECUTIVE'S CLAIM,
  not on verified fact.
  
  This distinction might not change the answer,
  but it changes your CONFIDENCE in the answer.

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: BUILD THE "WHO SAYS IT?" REFLEX

Every time you read a statement in a text, automatically ask: WHO says this?

THE "WHO SAYS IT?" FRAMEWORK:

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                              │
  │  LEVEL 1: THE JOURNALIST says it             │
  │  → No quotes, no attribution                 │
  │  → "L'élevage pose deux problèmes"           │
  │  → Treated as the text's own voice           │
  │  → HIGHEST reliability within the text        │
  │                                              │
  │  LEVEL 2: THE JOURNALIST HEDGES it           │
  │  → Conditional tense, "peut", "pourrait"     │
  │  → "Le saumon pourrait contenir..."          │
  │  → Presented as POSSIBLE, not certain         │
  │  → MODERATE reliability                       │
  │                                              │
  │  LEVEL 3: SOMEONE ELSE says it               │
  │  → Quotation marks, "selon", "affirme"       │
  │  → «Même le saumon bio est pollué»           │
  │  → The text REPORTS but doesn't CONFIRM       │
  │  → Reliability depends on WHO the source is   │
  │                                              │
  │  LEVEL 4: SOMEONE ELSE PRESCRIBES            │
  │  → "Il faut...", "On devrait..."             │
  │  → «Il faut changer le modèle»               │
  │  → This is pure OPINION/RECOMMENDATION        │
  │  → LOWEST factual reliability                 │
  │                                              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

RULE 2: IDENTIFY THE ATTRIBUTION VERBS

French journalists use different verbs to introduce quotes, and each verb signals a different level of commitment:

ATTRIBUTION VERB SPECTRUM:

  NEUTRAL VERBS (journalist doesn't take a position):
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  "dit"        = says
  "indique"    = indicates        ← Martins in Texte 102
  "déclare"    = declares
  "explique"   = explains
  "précise"    = specifies
  "note"       = notes
  "souligne"   = underlines/emphasizes
  
  STRONGER VERBS (implies conviction or controversy):
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  "affirme"    = affirms/asserts  ← Magnen in Texte 102
  "assure"     = assures
  "insiste"    = insists
  "maintient"  = maintains
  "revendique" = claims/demands
  
  DISTANCING VERBS (journalist is skeptical):
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  "prétend"    = claims/pretends
  "avance"     = puts forward
  "estime"     = estimates/considers
  "croit"      = believes
  "suppose"    = supposes
  
  IN TEXTE 102:
  → Martins: "INDIQUE" = neutral attribution
    (journalist simply reports what she says)
  → Magnen: "AFFIRME" = stronger attribution
    (journalist signals this person is making 
     a strong assertion, possibly controversial)

📌 The choice between "indique" for Martins and "affirme" for Magnen is NOT random. "Indique" presents information calmly. "Affirme" signals someone making a forceful claim. The journalist is telling you, through verb choice alone, that Magnen's statement is more assertive/combative than Martins's.

RULE 4: RECOGNIZE THE HEDGING SPECTRUM

Not all claims are equally certain. French uses specific grammatical tools to signal how certain a statement is:

THE CERTAINTY SPECTRUM IN FRENCH:

  MOST CERTAIN ─────────────────────────────── LEAST CERTAIN
  
  ┌─────────┬──────────┬──────────┬──────────┬──────────┐
  │ Present │ "Il est  │ "peut"   │"pourrait" │ "selon   │
  │ tense   │ probable │ (can)    │ (could)   │  certains│
  │ indicat.│  que"    │          │ condition.│  ..."    │
  ├─────────┼──────────┼──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
  │  FACT   │ PROBABLE │ POSSIBLE │HYPOTHETIC.│ ATTRIBUTED│
  │  100%   │  ~75%    │  ~50%    │  ~25%    │  ~10%    │
  └─────────┴──────────┴──────────┴──────────┴──────────┘
  
  TEXTE 102 EXAMPLES:
  
  "augmente chaque année"        → present indicative = FACT (100%)
  "pose des problèmes"           → present indicative = FACT (100%)
  "peuvent être exposés"         → "peuvent" = POSSIBLE (50%)
  "pourrait contenir"            → conditional = HYPOTHETICAL (25%)
  "affirme Jean-Philippe Magnen" → attributed = HIS CLAIM (10%)

📌 The exam may present a CONDITIONAL statement as if it were a FACT. If the text says "pourrait contenir des métaux lourds" (COULD contain), and an answer option says "le saumon contient des métaux lourds" (CONTAINS), the answer is OVERSTATING what the text says. This is a classic trap.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: TREATING ALL QUOTED STATEMENTS AS FACTS

WRONG:
  "Magnen says even organic salmon is polluted, 
   so it's a FACT that organic salmon is polluted."
   
RIGHT:
  "Magnen CLAIMS organic salmon is polluted. 
   This is HIS assertion. The journalist REPORTS 
   it but does not CONFIRM it independently."
   
WHY THIS MATTERS:
  An exam question might ask: "Selon le texte, le saumon bio..."
  → "est pollué" → WRONG (the text says Magnen AFFIRMS this)
  → "serait pollué selon un écologiste" → MORE ACCURATE
  
  The distinction between FACT and ATTRIBUTED CLAIM 
  can be the difference between right and wrong answers.

MISTAKE 2: IGNORING THE CONDITIONAL TENSE

WRONG:
  Text says: "Le saumon POURRAIT contenir des métaux lourds"
  Student reads: "Salmon contains heavy metals"
  
RIGHT:
  Text says: "Le saumon POURRAIT contenir des métaux lourds"
  Student reads: "Salmon COULD contain heavy metals"
  
  "POURRAIT" ≠ "CONTIENT"
  COULD ≠ DOES
  
  The conditional creates UNCERTAINTY.
  The exam tests whether you notice that uncertainty.

MISTAKE 3: ASSUMING THE JOURNALIST AGREES WITH QUOTED SOURCES

WRONG:
  "The article ends with Magnen saying 'we must change 
   the model,' so the article's conclusion is that 
   the model must change."
   
RIGHT:
  "The article ends with Magnen's quote demanding change.
   The journalist CHOSE to end with this quote 
   (which suggests some editorial alignment), 
   but the journalist never EXPLICITLY says 
   'the model must change' in his own voice."
   
SUBTLE BUT CRUCIAL:
  Ending with a quote ≠ endorsing the quote.
  The journalist may agree, but structurally, 
  the demand for change belongs to MAGNEN, 
  not to the article itself.

MISTAKE 4: MISSING THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN "INDIQUE" AND "AFFIRME"

WRONG:
  "Both Martins and Magnen are quoted the same way."
  
RIGHT:
  Martins: "INDIQUE" = calmly states/indicates
  Magnen:  "AFFIRME" = forcefully asserts/affirms
  
  The journalist signals through verb choice:
  → Martins is INFORMING (neutral delivery)
  → Magnen is ASSERTING (forceful delivery)
  
  This affects how you evaluate their credibility 
  and the weight of their statements.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "IT'S IN QUOTES SO IT'S TRUE" FALLACY

DANGER: Quotation marks make statements LOOK authoritative.
        Students automatically trust quoted material.
        
REALITY: Quotes are ATTRIBUTED, not VERIFIED.
  
  «Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts...» — Martins
  → She WOULD say this. She works for the company.
  → This doesn't mean the efforts are SUFFICIENT.
  
  «Même le saumon bio est pollué...» — Magnen
  → He WOULD say this. He's a Green politician.
  → This doesn't mean ALL organic salmon is polluted.
  
  RULE: Quotes show what SOMEONE CLAIMS, 
        not what IS TRUE.

PITFALL 2: THE "NUMBERS = TRUTH" ASSUMPTION

DANGER: Students see "7,5 milliards d'euros" and think 
        "this is objective, so the whole paragraph is objective."
        
REALITY: Numbers CAN be presented selectively.
  
  The journalist chose to highlight:
  → €7.5 billion (impressive, dramatic number)
  → x5 by 2050 (ambitious, attention-grabbing)
  → 2.3 kg per French person (relatable to reader)
  
  These numbers are REAL but CHOSEN for effect.
  Other numbers (like environmental damage costs)
  are NOT mentioned.
  
  LESSON: Numbers are facts, but the SELECTION 
  of which numbers to present is editorial.

PITFALL 3: CONFUSING "THE TEXT SAYS" WITH "THE TEXT MEANS"

THE TEXT SAYS:
  "la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation modérée 
   pour les enfants et les femmes enceintes" (2013)

THE TEXT MEANS (IMPLICATION):
  → If Norway warned about children and pregnant women,
    the health risk is REAL and ACKNOWLEDGED officially
  → "déjà" (already) means concerns are NOT new
  → The problem has existed for YEARS before this article

FOR THE EXAM:
  A question about what the text SAYS → answer with explicit info
  A question about what the text MEANS → answer with implications
  
  THESE ARE DIFFERENT QUESTION TYPES requiring 
  different approaches.

PITFALL 4: OVERLOOKING THE JOURNALIST'S STRUCTURAL CHOICES

THE JOURNALIST'S INVISIBLE OPINIONS:
                                                    
  The journalist never says "I think salmon farming 
  is bad." But his STRUCTURAL CHOICES reveal a lean:
  
  1. SPACE ALLOCATION:
     Pro-industry: 1 paragraph + 1 quote
     Pro-environment: 3 paragraphs + 1 quote
     → More space to PROBLEMS than to PROFITS
     
  2. QUOTE ORDER:
     Industry quote (Martins) comes FIRST
     Environmentalist quote (Magnen) comes LAST
     → The LAST word is the one readers remember most
     → The environmentalist gets the final say
     
  3. ESCALATION DIRECTION:
     Text moves from GOOD → BAD → WORSE
     → Never from BAD → GOOD → BETTER
     → Direction = increasingly alarming
     
  4. WORD CHOICES:
     "faramineux" (staggering) = suggests excess
     "mis en péril" (endangered) = suggests danger
     "même le saumon bio" (even organic) = suggests shock
     → All lean toward concern, not celebration
     
  CONCLUSION: The journalist is STRUCTURALLY biased 
  toward environmental concern, even while LINGUISTICALLY 
  maintaining neutral reporting style.
  
  This is SOPHISTICATED journalism — and the exam may 
  test whether you detect it.
