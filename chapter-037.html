Texte 56 : Le portable c'est tout un roman

Lire des romans sur un portable ? L'idée peut paraître redoutable, elle séduit pourtant de plus en plus de jeunes Nippons. Plusieurs maisons d'édition ont lancé des revues littéraires spécialisées dans les publications pour mobiles, et certains fournisseurs de contenu, devenus libraires, commencent à penser que la littérature pourrait rivaliser avec la musique et les jeux. La lecture sur portable attire surtout les femmes de 15 à 39 ans, une catégorie peu représentée dans le lectorat des magazines littéraires traditionnels.

Les visites les plus nombreuses se situent entre 22 heures et 1 heure. Initialement, on pensait que les gens liraient leurs heures perdues ou dans les transports en commun. En fait, ils lisent très sérieusement.

La sophistication croissante des portables a largement contribué à l'avancée des livres conçus pour ce support. Avec le haut débit et le système du tarif forfaitaire, les frais de téléchargement ne sont plus un problème. Avec la haute définition, les textes apparaissent très lisiblement à l'écran, même en tout petits caractères. Les données peuvent être conservées : on peut stocker l'équivalent d'une dizaine de livres de poche.

À terme, l'objectif des fournisseurs est de créer un nouveau type de librairie en collaboration avec les maisons d'éditions.

D'après Courrier international

Step 4: Detect the Expectation vs. Reality Pattern

This is the chapter's defining new pattern, introduced in Paragraph 2. The text explicitly contrasts what people THOUGHT would happen with what ACTUALLY happens:

EXPECTATION MARKERS:          REALITY MARKERS:
"Initialement"  (Initially)    "En fait" (In fact / Actually)
"on pensait que" (people       "ils lisent très sérieusement"
  thought that)                  (they read very seriously)

  (public transport)

The structure is:

EXPECTATION:  "Initialement, on pensait que [X]"
PIVOT:        "En fait" (In fact)
REALITY:      "[Y — the opposite of X]"

This is structurally similar to the concessive pattern from Chapter 5 (→ Project 5: "Et pourtant"), but instead of acknowledging a flaw and asserting quality, it corrects a prediction with observed reality.

The "En fait" pivot is the decoder: Everything before "En fait" is what people ASSUMED. Everything after is what ACTUALLY happens. The exam will test whether you follow the assumption or the reality.

QUESTION 24: Les romans pour le mobile...

Step 1: Identify the question type

"Les romans pour le mobile" = novels for mobile. This asks about the market position of mobile novels. Go to Layer 1 (Market Positioning) in Paragraph 1.

Step 2: Find the relevant evidence

The text provides several clues about mobile literature's market position:

Clue 1: "elle séduit pourtant de plus en plus de jeunes Nippons"
→ Growing popularity ("de plus en plus" = more and more)

Clue 2: "certains fournisseurs de contenu, devenus libraires, commencent à penser que la littérature pourrait rivaliser avec la musique et les jeux"
→ Literature MIGHT compete with music and games
→ Key words: "commencent à penser" (begin to think) + "pourrait" (could) = tentative, not certain
→ But the DIRECTION is positive: literature is gaining ground

Now, what does "rivaliser avec la musique et les jeux" mean for literature's market position? If literature is beginning to RIVAL music and games, it means literature is GAINING market share that was previously dominated by music and games. In other words, literature is taking market share from other downloadable content.

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "Ne résistent pas à la concurrence de la littérature traditionnelle"
(Can't withstand competition from traditional literature.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text says mobile reading attracts women 15-39 who are "peu représentée dans le lectorat des magazines littéraires traditionnels." Mobile novels reach people who DON'T read traditional literature. There's no competition between mobile and traditional — they serve DIFFERENT audiences. Moreover, mobile novels are GROWING ("de plus en plus"), not declining.No competition with traditional literature — different audiences. Mobile is growing, not declining.❌ WRONG COMPETITOR + WRONG DIRECTION

Option B: "N'ont aucune chance de s'imposer face à la musique et aux jeux"
(Have no chance of establishing themselves against music and games.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text says literature "pourrait rivaliser avec la musique et les jeux." "Pourrait rivaliser" (could compete) is a POSITIVE assessment — providers think literature MIGHT compete successfully. "N'ont aucune chance" (have no chance) is the OPPOSITE. The text expresses cautious optimism; this option expresses absolute pessimism.Cautious optimism (text) ≠ absolute pessimism (option).❌ REVERSAL OF ASSESSMENT + ABSOLUTE WORD ("aucune")

Also note: "aucune chance" is an absolute expression. Recall the absolute word warning from Chapter 6 (→ Project 6, Pitfall 2): options with "sans exception," "aucun," "tous" are often too extreme for nuanced texts.

Option C: "Grignotent des parts de marché aux autres contenus téléchargeables"
(Are nibbling away at market share from other downloadable content.)

TestReasoningVerdict
"Grignoter des parts de marché" = to gradually take market share. The text says: mobile literature is growing ("de plus en plus"), providers think it could compete with music and games. If literature is growing on a platform where music and games dominated, literature is taking share FROM those categories. "Grignoter" (nibble) captures the GRADUAL nature of this shift — not dominant yet, but progressing. "Autres contenus téléchargeables" (other downloadable content) = music and games.Growing literature on a platform dominated by music/games = taking market share. "Grignoter" = gradual = matches "commencent à penser" and "pourrait."✅ MATCH

Option D: "Tendent à éliminer complètement les maisons d'édition traditionnelles"
(Tend to completely eliminate traditional publishing houses.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text says providers work "en collaboration avec les maisons d'éditions" (in collaboration with publishers). Collaboration is the OPPOSITE of elimination. Paragraph 4 envisions a future where providers AND publishers work TOGETHER. This echoes the "complement not replacement" pattern from Chapter 8 (→ Project 8).Collaboration (text) ≠ elimination (option). Same "complement not replacement" pattern.❌ COLLABORATION ≠ ELIMINATION

✅ ANSWER: C — Grignotent des parts de marché aux autres contenus téléchargeables

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Identified the market positioning question → Paragraph 1
  2. Traced the competitive landscape: literature vs. music/games on mobile
  3. Recognized "de plus en plus" + "pourrait rivaliser" = gradual market share gain
  4. Matched this to "grignotent des parts de marché" — the most precise paraphrase
  5. Defeated Trap B by spotting the reversal (optimism → pessimism) and absolute word ("aucune")
  6. Defeated Trap D by recognizing the "complement not replacement" pattern from Chapter 8

New vocabulary concept — "Grignoter des parts de marché":

This is a standard French business expression:

"Grignoter" = to nibble (literally: eat small bites)
"Parts de marché" = market share
"Grignoter des parts de marché" = gradually take market share

This expression implies:
  - GRADUAL progress (not sudden domination)
  - COMPETITIVE context (taking FROM someone else)
  - ONGOING process (not finished)

The expression perfectly captures the text's cautious optimism: literature is growing on mobile but hasn't dominated yet.

THE EXPECTATION/REALITY TRAP:

TEXT STRUCTURE:

The mechanism that got you here:

  2. Identified the "En fait" pivot as the decoder

  4. Defeated Traps C and D by recognizing them as paraphrases of the EXPECTATION, not the REALITY

The Expectation/Reality Pattern — Formalized:

DETECTION:
  Look for: "Initialement" / "Au départ" / "On pensait que"
  Then look for: "En fait" / "En réalité" / "Or"

  Everything AFTER the pivot = what is TRUE

TRAP MECHANISM:
  The exam paraphrases the ASSUMPTION and presents it as truth.
  The correct answer paraphrases the REALITY.

DEFENSE:
  After "En fait," ONLY the reality counts.
  Ignore everything before "En fait" when answering about actual behavior.

Pattern recognition — The "Reversal Cluster" trap:

This question is remarkable because THREE out of four options commit the same error: they reverse the text's message.

TEXT:    Technology ENABLES mobile reading
TRAP A:  Technology PREVENTS good visualization  (enables → prevents)
TRAP B:  Technology OBSTRUCTS growth            (contributes → obstructs)
TRAP D:  Technology SLOWS downloads             (speeds up → slows down)

All three take a POSITIVE technical feature and present it as a NEGATIVE.

When you see multiple options making the same type of error, it confirms the text's direction: the text is POSITIVE about technology. The only option that's also positive (C) must be correct.

New elimination strategy — The Consistency Check:

If 3 out of 4 options contradict the text's clear direction,
and only 1 option matches the direction,
that 1 option is almost certainly correct.

TEXT DIRECTION: Technology helps → 
  A says technology hinders ✗
  B says technology hinders ✗
  C says technology helps   ✓ ← ONLY match
  D says technology hinders ✗

In technology-related texts, determine the text's stance on technology before reading options:

  Markers: "empêche," "constitue un frein," "ralentit"

  → Wrong answers will describe technology POSITIVELY

In this text, ALL technology markers are positive. Therefore, any option presenting technology as a problem is wrong.

4. Use the "Complement Not Replacement" Pattern Across Chapters

Trap D in Q24 suggests mobile novels "eliminate traditional publishers." But the text says providers work "en collaboration avec les maisons d'éditions." This is the same pattern from Chapter 8 (→ Project 8):

Ch 8:  E-books don't replace paper books → they COMPLEMENT them
Ch 11: Mobile novels don't eliminate publishers → they COLLABORATE with them

PATTERN: New technology + traditional industry = COLLABORATION, not destruction

This pattern has now appeared in TWO chapters. When you see a text about new technology entering an established market, expect the "complement not replacement" pattern and watch for trap options that suggest elimination/destruction.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Select the Expectation When the Question Asks About Reality

This is the chapter's cardinal sin. The expectation/reality split is the exam's most elegant trap:

THE TRAP MECHANISM:

The text provides TWO pieces of information:
  1. What people THOUGHT (expectation) — memorable, concrete
  2. What ACTUALLY happens (reality) — abstract, less vivid

The exam creates options from the EXPECTATION
because it's more memorable and concrete.
Candidates recall "transports en commun" and "heures perdues"
more easily than "très sérieusement."

DEFENSE: After "En fait," ONLY the reality counts.

Memory vs. Accuracy: The expectation details are often more vivid and memorable than the reality. "Public transport" and "idle time" are concrete images. "Very seriously" is abstract. The trap exploits the fact that vivid, concrete details are recalled more easily. But recalled ≠ correct.

2. Do NOT Add Characteristics the Text Doesn't Provide

Option C in Q25 describes "femmes inactives" (inactive/unemployed women). The text says "femmes de 15 à 39 ans" — gender and age, but NOT employment status. Adding "inactives" introduces a dimension the text never addresses.

TEXT PROVIDES:     Gender (femmes) + Age (15-39)
OPTION ADDS:       Employment status (inactives)

RULE: If the text doesn't mention a characteristic,
      no option can correctly claim that characteristic.
      
The text's SILENCE on employment ≠ "unemployed."
The text's SILENCE on employment = "we don't know."

This principle builds on Chapter 1's foundational rule (→ Project 1: "Do NOT answer from general knowledge"). Don't fill gaps in the text's information with your own assumptions.

3. Do NOT Confuse "Could Compete" with "Has Dominated"

The text says literature "pourrait rivaliser avec la musique et les jeux." This is:

"Pourrait"  = conditional tense = COULD / MIGHT
"Rivaliser" = compete with = RIVAL, not DOMINATE

"Could compete with" ≠ "has beaten"
"Could compete with" ≠ "has no chance against"

It's a MIDDLE position:
  Not yet dominant (hasn't won)
  But potentially competitive (might win)
  = "grignoter des parts de marché" (gradually taking share)

The conditional tense signals POSSIBILITY, not certainty. Neither absolute optimism ("has won") nor absolute pessimism ("has no chance") matches the text's cautious tone.

4. Do NOT Interpret Night Reading as Insomnia

Option B in Q26 is a classic reader's inference trap (→ Project 9). The text says people read between 10 PM and 1 AM. Some candidates think: "Late-night reading = can't sleep = insomnia."

But the text explicitly says these readers read "très sérieusement" — with dedication and focus. This is CHOSEN behavior, not a medical symptom. People read at night because:

  • It's their free time after work/school
  • They're dedicated readers who set aside evening time
  • The text says they're "serious" about it — this is intentional
READER'S INFERENCE:  Late night = insomnia (medical interpretation)
TEXT'S STATEMENT:     Late night + "très sérieusement" = dedicated reading (behavioral)

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Vivid Expectation vs. Abstract Reality" Memory Bias

This is the chapter's most dangerous pitfall. Human memory favors concrete, visual information over abstract statements:

EXPECTATION (concrete, memorable):
  "Heures perdues" → you can PICTURE waiting in a station
  "Transports en commun" → you can PICTURE reading on a bus

REALITY (abstract, less memorable):
  "Très sérieusement" → what does this LOOK like? Harder to picture.
  "Entre 22h et 1h" → a time range, not a visual scene

When you take the exam, you'll REMEMBER the concrete details better. But the concrete details are the EXPECTATION — the wrong answer. The correct answer is the abstract reality you remember less vividly.

Defense: When a question asks about user behavior, FIND the "En fait" pivot and read ONLY what follows it. Ignore everything before it, no matter how vivid.

Pitfall 2: The "Triple Reversal" Cluster

In Q27, three options (A, B, D) all reverse the text's positive technology stance. This creates a strange situation: candidates might think "If three options say technology is bad, maybe the text IS negative about technology." The sheer number of negative options can create doubt.

Defense: Go back to the text. The text says technology "a largement contribué à l'avancée." That's unambiguous. Three wrong options don't make a right answer. Trust the text, not the option count.

Pitfall 3: The "School-Age" Narrowing

Option A in Q25 says "adolescents en âge scolaire" (school-age adolescents). The age range 15-39 includes some school-age people (15-18), so this option PARTIALLY matches. Candidates might think: "Well, 15-year-olds ARE school-age, so this works."

But the option describes ONLY school-age adolescents, ignoring the 19-39 range — which is the MAJORITY of the demographic. An option that covers 20% of the text's description while ignoring 80% is wrong.

TEXT:     15-39 years old (25-year range)
OPTION A: school age ≈ 15-18 (3-year range)

3 out of 25 years = 12% coverage
This is a PARTIAL match, not a COMPLETE match.

Rule: A demographic option must cover the ENTIRE described population, not just a fraction of it.

Pitfall 4: The "Peu Représentée" Misreading

The text says women 15-39 are "peu représentée dans le lectorat des magazines littéraires traditionnels." Some candidates misread this as "popular among the traditional readership" — confusing "peu représentée" (underrepresented) with "bien représentée" (well represented).

"peu représentée"  = UNDER-represented = NOT many in this group
"bien représentée" = WELL-represented  = MANY in this group

"peu" = little/few → underrepresented

If you misread "peu" as a positive marker, you'd choose Option B ("du lectorat traditionnel") — thinking these women ARE part of the traditional readership. But "peu" means they are NOT.
