CHAPTER 2 — Texte 47 : « Déjeuner sur l'herbe » : Comprendre un texte promotionnel

📋 PHASE 1: VERY EASY — FOUNDATION THINKING

📖 START → COMPLETE ARTICLE

Déjeuner sur l'herbe

Partie de campagne chic, casse-croûte urbain entre amis, pique-nique ludique pour les enfants ou encore barbecue au jardin pour toute la tribu : les thèmes ne manquent pas pour profiter du plaisir de la cuisine au grand air. Les recettes fraîches et festives non plus !

L'ouvrage Cuisine de plein air publié par les éditions Solar vous propose de célébrer l'été sous toutes ses facettes : pour chaque occasion, des idées inventives qui actualisent les traditionnels du pique-nique (cakes salés, terrines, salades, quiches, sandwichs, grillades et brochettes) ou qui s'accordent à la tendance du « snacking » (dips, bouchées, crostinis, tapas et tartines).

En plus des recettes et menus, découvrez des conseils pratiques qui vous aideront à élaborer un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer...), à préparer vos plats à l'avance ou encore à vous équiper judicieusement... Bref, tous les ingrédients pour réussir vos repas et déjeuners sur l'herbe.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What kind of text is this?

This is a promotional text — it looks like an article, reads like a description, but its real purpose is to sell a book. This distinction is the central lesson of Chapter 2.

The New Skill: Identifying TEXT PURPOSE

In Chapter 1, you learned to identify WHAT a text describes (a product, its features, its audience). Now you must identify WHY the text was written — its underlying intent.

There are four main text purposes you'll encounter across all 25 chapters:

PurposeSignal WordsExample
Informingrapporte, selon, d'après, les experts affirmentA news article about an event
Promotingvous propose, découvrez, profitez, tous les ingrédients pourAn ad disguised as an article
Arguingil faut, on doit, il est essentiel, en revancheAn opinion piece or editorial
Advisingoptez pour, n'hésitez pas, évitez, privilégiezA practical guide or tip sheet

This text falls squarely into PROMOTING. Your job is to see through the pleasant cooking descriptions and recognize the sales pitch underneath.

You already know from Chapter 1:

  • Watch for traps ✅

Now let's activate those skills on this new text.

Read the questions first — what are you hunting for?

Notice something critical: all three questions are about interpretation, not location. Unlike Chapter 1 where answers were physical facts (color, price, target), here you must understand the text's FUNCTION.

Map the text structure

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 14 — What is the objective of this article?

The question: cet article a pour objectif...

This is your first PURPOSE question — the most important new question type in this chapter.

Step 1: Identify the core action the text performs.

Ask yourself: After reading this text, what does the author WANT me to do?

  • Learn about outdoor cooking history? → No
  • Compare different cuisines? → No
  • Feel inspired to BUY A BOOK? → Yes

The text explicitly names the product: "L'ouvrage Cuisine de plein air publié par les éditions Solar vous propose..."

The word "vous propose" is a direct sales address. This is not journalism — it's promotion.

Step 2: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) d'opposer la cuisine des villes à celle des campagnes§1 mentions both urban ("casse-croûte urbain") and rural ("partie de campagne") settings, but it does NOT oppose them — it lists them as EQUAL options❌ TRAP — takes two details and invents a contrast
B) de faire la promotion d'un livre de recettes§2 names the book, its publisher, and describes its contents. §3 adds more selling points. The entire text is structured to promote this book✅ CORRECT
C) de proposer une découverte des déjeuners en plein airThe text mentions outdoor eating, but it doesn't teach you about it — it tells you to BUY A BOOK that will teach you❌ TRAP — confuses the topic with the purpose
D) de comparer les modes culinaires du mondeTex-mex, tapas, crostinis appear as EXAMPLES of thematic menus, not as a comparison of world cuisines❌ TRAP — inflates minor details into a major theme

✅ Answer: B

The Critical Distinction: TOPIC vs. PURPOSE

This is the lesson that separates successful candidates from unsuccessful ones:

TOPIC   = What the text talks ABOUT     → outdoor cooking
PURPOSE = What the text tries to DO      → sell a book about outdoor cooking

Option C describes the TOPIC. Option B describes the PURPOSE. The question asks for the objectif (objective/purpose), so B wins.

🔑 Rule: When a question asks "l'objectif" or "le but" or "a pour objectif," it asks WHY the text exists — not WHAT it describes.

QUESTION 15 — What is special about the dishes described?

The question: les plats évoqués...

Step 1: Locate the description of the dishes.

§2 is your goldmine:

"des idées inventives qui actualisent les traditionnels du pique-nique (cakes salés, terrines, salades, quiches, sandwichs, grillades et brochettes) ou qui s'accordent à la tendance du « snacking » (dips, bouchées, crostinis, tapas et tartines)."

Step 2: Extract the two key verbs.

VerbMeaningWhat it tells you
actualisentto modernize, to update, to bring up to dateTraditional picnic dishes are being UPDATED
s'accordent à la tendanceto match the trendThe dishes FOLLOW modern food trends (snacking)

Both verbs point to the same idea: MODERNIZATION of traditional dishes.

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionText evidenceVerdict
A) sont conçus pour éviter le grignotageThe text mentions "snacking" as a POSITIVE trend, not something to avoid. "Bouchées" and "tapas" ARE grignotage❌ WRONG — the text embraces snacking, doesn't fight it
B) ont pour origine des traditions anciennesTraditional dishes are mentioned ("les traditionnels du pique-nique"), but the text emphasizes their MODERNIZATION, not their ancient origins❌ TRAP — half-truth. The origin is traditional, but the emphasis is on updating them
C) présentent l'avantage d'être vite préparés§3 mentions "préparer vos plats à l'avance" — preparing in advance ≠ preparing quickly. The text never says the dishes are fast to make❌ TRAP — confuses "à l'avance" (ahead of time) with "vite" (quickly)
D) marquent la modernisation du pique-nique"actualisent les traditionnels" = modernize the classics. "s'accordent à la tendance du snacking" = match modern trends. This is EXACTLY what the text says✅ CORRECT

✅ Answer: D

The New Vocabulary Concept: MODERNIZATION WORDS

This is your first encounter with modernization vocabulary — words that signal a blend of old and new. Memorize these patterns because they will return in Chapters 13 (Haitian art), 22 (Michel-Ange), and 23 (Mont-Saint-Michel):

French word/phraseMeaningSignal
actualiserto update, modernizeOld thing made new
s'accorder à la tendanceto match the trendFollowing current fashion
revisiterto revisit, reinterpretClassic with a twist
réinventerto reinventComplete transformation
se radicalerto radicalize, become extremeEvolution beyond tradition
virer àto shift towardChange of direction

When you see these words, the text is talking about EVOLUTION — something traditional becoming something modern. The correct answer will almost always reflect this transformation.

QUESTION 16 — What does the article give the reader?

The question: l'article donne...

Step 1: Go to §3 where the "extras" are listed.

"En plus des recettes et menus, découvrez des conseils pratiques qui vous aideront à élaborer un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer...), à préparer vos plats à l'avance ou encore à vous équiper judicieusement..."

Step 2: Identify the key offering.

The text mentions three things the book provides:

  1. Recettes et menus (recipes and menus)
  2. Conseils pratiques (practical advice)
  3. Pique-nique thématique (thematic picnic ideas)

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) des listes d'ingrédients à utiliserThe text lists DISH NAMES (cakes salés, terrines, tapas), not ingredient lists. No quantities, no shopping lists❌ TRAP — confuses dish names with ingredient lists
B) des idées pour organiser un cocktailA cocktail party is NOT mentioned. The text talks about picnics, barbecues, outdoor eating — not indoor cocktail events❌ WRONG — wrong type of event entirely
C) des propositions de repas à thèmes"élaborer un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer)" → THEMATIC meal proposals. This matches perfectly✅ CORRECT
D) des conseils pour une bonne nutritionThe text mentions "conseils pratiques" but these are about ORGANIZATION (preparing in advance, equipping yourself), not about nutrition or health❌ TRAP — "conseils" appears in the text but the TYPE of advice is different

✅ Answer: C

The Trap Anatomy: Word Match ≠ Meaning Match

Option D is a sophisticated trap that deserves special attention:

TEXT says:    "conseils pratiques"
Option D says: "conseils pour une bonne nutrition"

The word "conseils" MATCHES.
But the CONTENT doesn't match.

Text's conseils = how to organize, prepare in advance, equip yourself
Option D's conseils = nutritional/health advice

Same word. Different meaning. WRONG answer.

This is an evolution of the "Partial Match Trap" from Chapter 1. In Chapter 1, you learned that matching a single WORD is dangerous. Now you learn that matching a single CONCEPT (advice) is equally dangerous if the CONTEXT differs.

🔑 Rule: When an answer option contains a word from the text, check whether the SURROUNDING MEANING also matches, not just the word itself.

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

1. Identify the "sales structure" immediately

Whenever you see this pattern, you're reading a promotional text:

§1: HOOK    → Evokes pleasant emotions/situations
§2: PITCH   → Introduces the product by name
§3: SELL    → Lists additional benefits/features

Once you identify this structure, Q14-type questions ("what is the objective?") are instantly answered: the objective is promotion/selling.

2. Circle the "direct address" words

Promotional texts speak TO you. Look for:

WordFunction
vous proposeDirect sales address
découvrezImperative — commanding you to engage
vos repasPossessive — making you imagine ownership
tous les ingrédients pour réussirPromise of completeness — nothing else needed

These words are the fingerprints of a promotional text. They do NOT appear in neutral informative articles.

3. Distinguish between what THE ARTICLE gives and what THE BOOK gives

A subtle but important distinction:

THE ARTICLE gives: a description of the book (promotion)
THE BOOK gives: recipes, menus, practical advice, thematic ideas

Q16 asks what "l'article donne." Since the article IS the promotion of the book, what the article "gives" is essentially a PREVIEW of what the book offers. The article itself doesn't contain full recipes — it gives "propositions" (suggestions/proposals) of thematic meals.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Topic Disguised as Purpose" Trap

What happens: The question asks WHY the text was written. An answer option describes WHAT the text talks about. You select it because it sounds accurate.

Example: Q14C — "de proposer une découverte des déjeuners en plein air."

This is TRUE in a sense — the text does talk about outdoor eating. But the text doesn't exist to TEACH you about outdoor eating. It exists to SELL you a book. The outdoor eating is the BAIT, not the HOOK.

How to avoid it: Ask yourself: "If I removed the book from this text, would the text still have a reason to exist?" If the answer is NO, the purpose is to promote the book.

🔑 Test: Remove "L'ouvrage Cuisine de plein air publié par les éditions Solar" from §2. The text becomes a vague, purposeless list of food ideas. The book IS the reason the text exists.

Pitfall 2: The "Half-Truth" Trap

What happens: An answer option is partially correct but misses the main point.

Example: Q15B — "ont pour origine des traditions anciennes."

The text DOES mention "les traditionnels du pique-nique" — so yes, there IS a traditional origin. But the text's emphasis is on MODERNIZING these traditions ("actualisent"), not on celebrating their ancient origins. The option is true but INCOMPLETE — it captures the starting point but ignores the destination.

How to avoid it: When two options seem partially true, ask: "Which one captures the MAIN EMPHASIS of the text?"

B says: dishes come from tradition     → TRUE but secondary
D says: dishes modernize the picnic    → TRUE and PRIMARY

The text's key verb is "actualisent" (modernize), not "proviennent de" (come from).
D wins because it matches the PRIMARY emphasis.

Pitfall 3: The "Adjacent Concept" Trap

What happens: An answer option uses a concept that is RELATED to the text but not STATED in the text.

Example: Q16D — "des conseils pour une bonne nutrition."

The text mentions food AND advice. "Nutrition advice" is a PLAUSIBLE combination of these two elements. But the text never links them. The "conseils pratiques" are about LOGISTICS (organizing, preparing, equipping), not about HEALTH.

How to avoid it: Always ask: "Does the text EXPLICITLY connect these two ideas, or am I connecting them myself?"

Text connects: "conseils pratiques" + "élaborer un pique-nique thématique"
                → practical advice FOR organizing themed picnics ✅

You might connect: "conseils" + food = "nutrition advice"
                   → logical but NOT what the text says ❌

Pitfall 4: Mistaking EXAMPLES for MAIN THEMES

What happens: The text gives a list of examples to illustrate a point. An answer option takes one or two examples and presents them as the main theme.

Example: Q14D — "de comparer les modes culinaires du monde."

The text mentions tex-mex, tapas, and crostinis. These are international food terms. But they appear in a PARENTHETICAL list — they are examples of thematic picnic ideas, not the subject of a global culinary comparison.

How to avoid it: Check WHERE in the sentence the items appear:

"...élaborer un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer...)"
                                        ↑
                              INSIDE PARENTHESES = examples
                              
Main point = thematic picnic
Examples = tex-mex, seafood grills

Items inside parentheses, after colons, or in lists are ALWAYS examples — never the main point.
