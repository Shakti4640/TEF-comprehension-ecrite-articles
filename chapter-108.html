🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

In Chapters 1-3, you read about things: a beer, a book, clothing rules. The questions asked about features, purposes, and advice. The texts were impersonal — no character, no story, no timeline.

Chapter 4 is fundamentally different. You're reading about a person — Jean Droissard. A portrait text has its own DNA:

PORTRAIT TEXT ANATOMY:

1. HOOK          → Impressive numbers/facts that grab attention
2. IDENTITY      → Who is this person? (name, nationality, age)
3. ACTION        → What are they doing? (the main activity)
4. MOTIVATION    → WHY are they doing it? (initial + evolved)
5. CONTEXT       → External circumstances (Unesco, timing)
6. LOGISTICS     → How do they manage? (funding, housing)
7. FUTURE        → What comes next? (return date, plans)

The questions about portraits test THREE new skills:

SkillWhat It TestsTrap
Character identificationWho is this person REALLY?Confusing them with people/institutions MENTIONED in the text
Motivation timelineINITIAL motivation vs. LATER motivationMixing up what came first with what came after
AttributionWho said/did what?Confusing what HE says with what OTHERS do

This text contains a critical chronological shift in motivation:

Questions about portraits LOVE to test whether you can distinguish the initial reason from the final reason. The word "initialement" in Q18 is your clue: they're asking about the FIRST motivation, not the current one.

🏷️ MOTIVATION SHIFT: his wife suggested adding the peace dimension. "Et puis" = and then (chronological marker: this came AFTER).

Context:

🏷️ COINCIDENCE/CONTEXT: His departure coincided with a Unesco initiative. Note: "correspondait" = coincided with. He didn't work FOR Unesco. The timing was simply aligned.

Logistics:

"Selon ses estimations, le Belge ne sera de retour chez lui qu'en 2015."

🏷️ RETURN DATE: estimated return in 2015. Note: "de retour chez lui" = he WILL return. He IS planning to come back.

🏷️ HOUSING: Over 1100 families have hosted/housed him. "Logé" = provided lodging/accommodation.

"D'aucuns lui donnent de l'argent, d'autres lui offrent un bout de son voyage."

🏷️ FUNDING: Some give money, others offer parts of his journey (travel segments). "D'aucuns" = some people (literary French).

"Ce document fait le portrait..."

This question asks: What category of person is Jean Droissard?

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) d'un scientifique belge en mission"Scientifique" = scientist. Nothing in the text mentions science, research, or any scientific mission. He's walking, not conducting experiments. He IS Belgian, but that's the only match.❌ ELIMINATED
B) d'un haut fonctionnaire de l'Unesco"Haut fonctionnaire" = high-ranking official. The text mentions Unesco, but ONLY as a contextual coincidence: "son départ correspondait au lancement par l'Unesco..." His departure COINCIDED with the Unesco initiative — he doesn't WORK for Unesco.❌ ELIMINATED
C) d'un athlète de haut niveau à la retraite"Athlète de haut niveau" = elite athlete. "À la retraite" = retired. The text says he's like a "recordman" and it was initially a "défi sportif." But nowhere does it say he's a PROFESSIONAL athlete, or that he's RETIRED. He started this at 45 — his regular age, not a retirement age for athletes.❌ ELIMINATED
D) d'un passionné parcourant le monde"Passionné" = passionate person/enthusiast. "Parcourant le monde" = traveling across the world. He IS passionately walking across the world. He's not a scientist, not an Unesco official, not a retired athlete — he's a passionate individual on a personal mission.✅ CORRECT

Answer: D

The critical reading skill here — Distinguishing MENTIONED from CHARACTERIZED:

TRAP TYPE: "MENTIONED ≠ CHARACTERIZED"

The text MENTIONS Unesco → Option B says he's an Unesco official
The text MENTIONS "défi sportif" → Option C says he's a retired athlete

MENTIONED = the text refers to something
CHARACTERIZED = the text says the person IS something

Unesco is MENTIONED as context. He is NOT characterized as an Unesco employee.
Sport is MENTIONED as initial motivation. He is NOT characterized as a pro athlete.

This trap is specific to portrait texts. Whenever a text mentions an institution, a profession, or a label near the subject, an option will try to ASSIGN that label to the person. Your job is to check: Does the text say he IS this, or does it merely MENTION this near him?

Let me formalize this with a test:

THE "IS vs. NEAR" TEST:

Option B: "haut fonctionnaire de l'Unesco"
→ Does the text say Jean WORKS FOR Unesco?
→ No. It says his departure COINCIDED WITH an Unesco initiative.
→ Unesco is NEAR him in the text, but he is NOT part of Unesco.
→ ELIMINATE.

Option C: "athlète de haut niveau à la retraite"
→ Does the text say Jean WAS a professional athlete?
→ No. It says he saw it initially as a "défi sportif."
→ Sport is his MOTIVATION, not his PROFESSION.
→ ELIMINATE.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "initialement" / "au départ" alignment:

QUESTION uses:  "initialement"  (initially)
TEXT uses:      "au départ"     (at the start)

These are SYNONYMS. The question is asking about
what the TEXT marks with "au départ."

"Au départ, je ne voyais que le défi sportif"
= Initially, I only saw the sporting challenge
= He wanted to prove himself capable of a physical feat
= Option B: "voulait se prouver qu'il était capable d'un tel exploit"

Notice how the correct answer REPHRASES the text rather than copying it:

TEXT:     "ne voyais que le défi sportif"
          (only saw the sporting challenge)

OPTION B: "voulait se prouver qu'il était capable d'un tel exploit"
          (wanted to prove himself capable of such a feat)

défi sportif ≈ exploit     → SYNONYM
ne voyais que ≈ se prouver → INTERPRETIVE SYNONYM
                             (seeing only the challenge ≈ wanting to prove oneself)

This is a deeper level of synonym recognition than Chapter 2. In Chapter 2, the synonyms were simple vocabulary swaps (thématique = à thèmes). Here, the synonym is conceptual — "seeing only the sporting challenge" is rephrased as "wanting to prove oneself capable."

Skill 2 — Separating DATE from CAUSE (new and critical):

Option D is a sophisticated trap. Let me dissect it:

TEXT SAYS:
  "son voyage — qui a démarré le jour de ses 45 ans"
  → His journey started ON the day of his 45th birthday

  This tells you WHEN he left, not WHY.

OPTION D SAYS:
  "a eu une révélation le jour de ses quarante-cinq ans"
  → He had a revelation ON his 45th birthday

  This claims the birthday CAUSED the decision.

THE DIFFERENCE:
  TEXT: His 45th birthday = the DATE of departure (temporal fact)
  OPTION D: His 45th birthday = the CAUSE of the project (causal claim)

  DATE ≠ CAUSE
  "When" ≠ "Why"

This is a brand new trap type:

TRAP TYPE: "TEMPORAL FACT → FALSE CAUSATION"

The text states WHEN something happened.
A wrong option claims that timing CAUSED the event.

"He left on his birthday" ≠ "His birthday made him leave"

RULE: A date in the text tells you WHEN, not WHY.
      Never assume a temporal detail is a causal explanation.

This trap will reappear in many future texts. Whenever a text mentions a date, a coincidence, or a timing, expect an option that falsely converts it into a cause.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Precise vocabulary distinction: "logé" vs. "voyager avec":

TEXT:     "l'ont logé"         = housed him, hosted him, gave him shelter
OPTION B: "se fait héberger"   = gets himself accommodated/hosted
OPTION C: "faire le voyage"    = make the journey/travel

"logé" = "héberger"  → SYNONYMS (both mean "provide lodging") ✅
"logé" ≠ "voyager"   → DIFFERENT ACTIONS (housing ≠ traveling) ❌

Option B uses a SYNONYM of the text → CORRECT
Option C uses a DIFFERENT VERB than the text → INCORRECT

This is another instance of the synonym skill from Chapter 2, but now applied to verbs rather than adjectives:

SYNONYM RECOGNITION EVOLUTION:
Chapter 2: adjective synonyms (thématique = à thèmes)
Chapter 3: adjective/verb synonyms (flattent = vont bien)
Chapter 4: verb synonyms (logé = héberger) ← NEW level

Skill 2 — Catching the "ne...que" structure:

Option D claims Jean doesn't want to return. But the text uses "ne sera de retour chez lui qu'en 2015" — the "ne...que" structure.

"NE...QUE" = "ONLY" (restrictive, not negative)

"ne sera de retour qu'en 2015"
= will ONLY be back in 2015
= will return, but NOT BEFORE 2015

This is NOT a negation of return.
This is a RESTRICTION on timing.

He WILL return → just not until 2015.

Many students misread "ne...que" as a simple negation ("ne...pas"). This is one of the most common grammar traps in French comprehension:

GRAMMAR TRAP: "NE...QUE" ≠ "NE...PAS"

"Il ne mange pas"    = He does NOT eat         (negation)
"Il ne mange que"    = He ONLY eats            (restriction)

"Il ne revient pas"  = He does NOT come back   (negation)
"Il ne revient qu'en 2015" = He ONLY comes back in 2015 (restriction)

Option D treats "ne...qu'en 2015" as "ne revient pas"
→ WRONG: he IS coming back, just not until 2015

Skill 3 — Detecting false attribution to institutions:

Option A says the project was "entièrement financé par l'Unesco." The text mentions Unesco ONCE, and only as a temporal coincidence ("son départ correspondait au lancement par l'Unesco"). From this single mention, Option A fabricates an entire FUNDING relationship.

TRAP TYPE: "INSTITUTIONAL INFLATION"

Text mentions an institution in passing → 
Option claims the person WORKS FOR / IS FUNDED BY that institution

TEXT:     "son départ correspondait au lancement par l'Unesco de..."
          (his departure coincided with Unesco's launch of...)
          → COINCIDENCE only

OPTION A: "entièrement financé par l'Unesco"
          (entirely funded by Unesco)
          → FABRICATED financial relationship

One mention of an institution ≠ employment, funding, or affiliation

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Motivation Timeline Swap" (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: A portrait text shows motivation evolving over time. The question asks about ONE point in time (initial or current). You pick the wrong point.

The mechanism:

TEXT TIMELINE:
  T1 (initial): "Au départ → défi sportif"     (sport)
  T2 (later):   "Et puis → marcher pour la paix" (peace)

QUESTION: "initialement" → asks about T1
CORRECT:  Sport (T1)
WRONG:    Peace (T2)

If the question asked "actuellement" or "désormais" → T2 would be correct
But "initialement" = T1

Signal words to watch for:

WORDS POINTING TO T1 (initial):     WORDS POINTING TO T2 (later):
- au départ                          - et puis
- initialement                       - ensuite
- à l'origine                        - par la suite
- au commencement                    - désormais
- d'abord                            - finalement
- la première raison                 - actuellement

Match the question's time marker with the text's time marker.

Pitfall #2: The "Institutional Proximity" Trap

What happens: An institution is mentioned in the same paragraph as the subject. An option claims the subject BELONGS to that institution.

The mechanism:

TEXT: "Jean walks for peace. His departure coincided with Unesco's initiative."
TRAP: "Jean is an Unesco official"

PROXIMITY in text ≠ AFFILIATION in reality

Just because two things appear in the same sentence
doesn't mean one belongs to the other.

How to detect: Ask: "Does the text use a LINKING verb (works for, is employed by, is funded by) or merely a PROXIMITY verb (coincided with, was similar to, happened at the same time as)?"

LINKING verbs (= real connection):
  travaille pour, est employé par, est financé par, dirige, représente

PROXIMITY verbs (= mere coincidence):
  correspondait à, coïncidait avec, se déroulait pendant, avait lieu en même temps que

In this text: "correspondait au lancement par l'Unesco" → PROXIMITY verb → no affiliation.

Pitfall #3: The "Fabricated Detail" Trap

What happens: An option introduces a detail that sounds plausible but is ENTIRELY ABSENT from the text.

Examples in this text:

Option A (Q18): "pari fou avec un ami pendant ses études"
→ No bet, no friend, no studies mentioned ANYWHERE

Option A (Q17): "scientifique belge en mission"
→ No science, no mission mentioned ANYWHERE

Option A (Q19): "entièrement financé par l'Unesco"
→ No funding relationship with Unesco mentioned ANYWHERE

How to detect: After selecting an answer, ask: "Can I point to the EXACT sentence in the text that supports this?" If you can't find a specific sentence, the option is likely fabricated.

THE "POINT TO IT" TEST:

For each option, ask: "Where EXACTLY does the text say this?"

Option B (Q19): "se fait héberger"
→ I can point to: "Plus de 1100 familles l'ont déjà logé"
→ SUPPORTED ✅

Option C (Q18): "pari fou avec un ami"
→ I cannot point to ANY sentence
→ FABRICATED ❌

Pitfall #4: The "Ne...Que" Grammar Trap

What happens: The structure "ne...que" is misread as a simple negation "ne...pas".

This is worth its own detailed explanation because it affects MANY texts:

FRENCH "NE...QUE" MASTERCLASS:

STRUCTURE: Subject + NE + verb + QUE + complement
MEANING:   ONLY (restriction, NOT negation)

EXAMPLES:
  "Il ne boit que de l'eau"
  = He ONLY drinks water (he drinks water, nothing else)
  ≠ He doesn't drink water

  "Elle ne parle que français"
  = She ONLY speaks French
  ≠ She doesn't speak French

  "Il ne sera de retour qu'en 2015"
  = He will ONLY return in 2015 (not before)
  ≠ He won't return

HOW TO TEST: Replace "ne...que" with "seulement" (only)
  "Il sera de retour seulement en 2015" → makes sense ✅
  This confirms it means "only in 2015" not "never"

Bridge to Chapter 5:

Chapters 1-4 have each dealt with a different text TYPE: product description, book promotion, advice article, and portrait. In Chapter 5, you'll encounter a product review — a text that EVALUATES something.

"La TX8 de Solips" (Texte 46) describes a universal remote control. Unlike the beer in Chapter 1 (neutral description), this text actively JUDGES the product — saying it's the best, the most affordable, the most practical. The questions will test:

  1. Can you identify what the product IS (without being confused by the products it CONTROLS)?
  2. Can you find WHERE it's sold (a specific detail buried in enthusiastic prose)?
  3. Can you distinguish EVALUATION language from FACTUAL language — when the text says "the most useful product," is that a fact or an opinion?

This is the shift from reading neutral descriptions to reading evaluative reviews — texts that have an opinion about their subject.
