Chapter 3: "Le travail et les couleurs" — Extracting Practical Advice

(Texte 38 — Phase 1: Very Easy — Foundation Thinking)

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le travail et les couleurs

Lors d'une entrevue professionnelle, la première impression est capitale ! Ne vous habillez pas pour le poste que vous occupez, mais pour celui auquel vous aspirez. Ainsi, vous manifestez déjà que vous avez la carrure pour. Côté couleur, cela dépend du secteur économique. Traditionnel et conservateur comme la banque ? Vous devez inspirer confiance et crédibilité, car vous êtes censé véhiculer l'esprit de l'entreprise. Optez pour du marine, du beige. Un milieu novateur comme la publicité ou la presse ? Osez la couleur ! Laissez transparaître votre créativité, sans exagérer afin de ne pas effrayer les recruteurs. Enfin, pour un poste dans le social, les ressources humaines, où des gens sont amenés à venir vers vous, à se confier, vous devez dégager un abord avenant et une tenue vestimentaire qui va avec.

Votre palette dépendra aussi de la position professionnelle que vous recherchez. Pour vous mettre en valeur, répétez les teintes qui vous flattent.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

In Chapter 1, you located product features.
In Chapter 2, you identified the text's purpose.
In Chapter 3, you must extract conditional advice — recommendations that change depending on the situation.

This text is an advice article. It doesn't describe a product or promote a book. It tells you: "In situation X, do A. In situation Y, do B. In situation Z, do C."

The challenge is that the correct answer is not one single piece of advice — it's the PATTERN behind multiple pieces of advice. The questions ask you to identify the general principle, not just one specific recommendation.

The New Core Concept: Conditional Advice Structure

Advice articles follow a predictable architecture:

ADVICE ARTICLE ANATOMY:

1. GENERAL PRINCIPLE    → "La première impression est capitale !"
2. CONDITION 1 + ADVICE → "Secteur conservateur ? → Optez pour marine, beige"
3. CONDITION 2 + ADVICE → "Milieu novateur ? → Osez la couleur !"
4. CONDITION 3 + ADVICE → "Poste dans le social ? → Abord avenant"
5. ADDITIONAL TIP       → "Répétez les teintes qui vous flattent"

Questions about advice articles typically test THREE things:

Question TypeWhat It TestsExample
Overall purposeCan you see the general principle behind specific examples?Q17
Conditional matchingCan you match the RIGHT advice to the RIGHT condition?Q18
Additional detailCan you find the EXTRA recommendation beyond the main ones?Q19

Applying the Foundation Method (from Chapters 1 & 2)

Same 5 steps. No change. But now, in Step 2, your radar scans for a different pattern: not product features (Ch.1), not purpose signals (Ch.2), but condition-advice pairs.

Let me map the text by identifying every condition-advice pair:

Opening principle:

"Lors d'une entrevue professionnelle, la première impression est capitale !"

🚨 KEY DISTINCTION: Don't dress for your CURRENT job → dress for the job you WANT. This means: dress for the FUTURE position, not your present one.

IF sector = social work, HR

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 17

"Cet article..."

This is a PURPOSE question, similar to Q14 in Chapter 2. But instead of asking "a pour objectif", it simply asks what the article DOES. Same skill applies: identify the overall function.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) Incite les employés à porter des habits colorésThe text only recommends colorful clothing for ONE sector (innovative/advertising). For banking, it recommends navy and beige — NOT colorful. This option takes ONE specific recommendation and presents it as the WHOLE message.❌ ELIMINATED
B) Conseille aux entreprises de se créer une identité vestimentaireThe text addresses individual candidates ("vous"), NOT companies/enterprises. It never tells companies to create dress codes. The advice is personal, not corporate.❌ ELIMINATED
C) Dresse un portrait des salariés selon leur place dans l'entreprise"Dresse un portrait" means "paints a picture/describes." The text doesn't DESCRIBE how employees currently dress. It ADVISES how candidates SHOULD dress. Description ≠ recommendation.❌ ELIMINATED
D) Informe sur les codes vestimentaires à adopter face aux recruteurs"Codes vestimentaires" = dress codes. "À adopter" = to adopt. "Face aux recruteurs" = when facing recruiters. The text gives dress code advice for job interviews. This captures the FULL scope of the article.✅ CORRECT

Answer: D

The critical reading skill here: Option A is the classic "part for whole" trap. The text gives THREE different pieces of advice for THREE sectors. Option A only reflects ONE of them (the colorful one for innovative sectors) and ignores the other two. A correct answer about the WHOLE article must cover ALL its content, not just one section.

TRAP TYPE: "PART FOR WHOLE"

The text says:
  - Banking → navy/beige (NOT colorful)
  - Advertising → colorful
  - Social → approachable

Option A says: "Incite à porter des habits colorés"
→ This is only 1/3 of the text's advice
→ It IGNORES the other 2/3
→ Therefore it cannot describe the WHOLE article

RULE: If an option only covers PART of the text's content,
      it cannot be the answer to a question about the WHOLE text.

Also note the distinction between Option C and Option D:

Option C: "Dresse un portrait" → DESCRIBES (what IS)
Option D: "Informe sur les codes à adopter" → ADVISES (what SHOULD BE)

The text uses imperative verbs: "Optez," "Osez," "Ne vous habillez pas"
→ These are COMMANDS/RECOMMENDATIONS
→ The text ADVISES, it doesn't DESCRIBE
→ D matches the text's function; C mischaracterizes it

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 18

"Selon cet article, le candidat à un emploi doit..."

This question asks for the MAIN recommendation. My text map shows three sector-specific pieces of advice. What is the COMMON THREAD behind all three?

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) Adapter sa tenue au secteur professionnel qu'il cible"Adapter" = adapt. "Au secteur qu'il cible" = to the sector he's targeting. This is EXACTLY the common thread: banking → formal colors, advertising → creative colors, social → approachable look. In ALL cases, the advice is to MATCH your outfit to the TARGET sector.✅ CORRECT
B) S'habiller avec des couleurs vives pour être remarqué"Couleurs vives" = bright colors. This is ONLY true for the innovative sector. For banking, the text says navy and beige — NOT bright. This option, like Q17 Option A, takes one specific case and generalizes it wrongly.❌ ELIMINATED
C) Porter des vêtements classiques pour être pris au sérieux"Vêtements classiques" = classic clothes. This is ONLY true for the banking sector. For advertising, the text says "Osez la couleur!" — the opposite of classic. Same trap as B: one case generalized.❌ ELIMINATED
D) Opter pour un style correspondant à son expérience professionnelle"Son expérience" = his experience. But the text explicitly says the OPPOSITE: "Ne vous habillez pas pour le poste que vous occupez, mais pour celui auquel vous aspirez." Don't dress for your experience/current position → dress for the position you WANT. This option directly contradicts the text.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: A

The critical reading skill here — GENERALIZATION vs. SPECIFIC CASE:

This is the most important skill introduced in this chapter. Let me formalize it:

THE GENERALIZATION PRINCIPLE:

When a text gives MULTIPLE specific examples following ONE pattern,
the correct answer identifies THE PATTERN, not one specific example.

TEXT EXAMPLES:
  Example 1: Banking → navy, beige
  Example 2: Advertising → colorful
  Example 3: Social work → approachable

THE PATTERN: Adapt your clothing to the sector

WRONG answers pick ONE example and present it as the rule:
  Option B picks Example 2 only (colorful)
  Option C picks Example 1 only (classic)

THE CORRECT answer captures ALL examples under one principle:
  Option A: "Adapter sa tenue au secteur" (covers all three)

Why Option D is especially dangerous:

Option D sounds reasonable — "dress according to your experience" seems like sensible advice. But the text says the EXACT OPPOSITE. This is a "common sense" trap: the option sounds like good advice in general, but the TEXT specifically contradicts it.

TRAP TYPE: "COMMON SENSE" CONTRADICTION

Your brain thinks: "Dressing for your experience level makes sense..."
The text says: "DON'T dress for your current position, dress for your TARGET"

RULE: Never pick an answer because it sounds logical.
      Always pick the answer that THE TEXT supports.
      Common sense ≠ text evidence.

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 19

"L'article recommande également au candidat..."

The keyword "également" (also/additionally) tells me: this question is about something BEYOND the main advice (which was about adapting clothing to the sector). I need to find the EXTRA tip.

Looking at my text map, the final paragraph contains the bonus advice:

"Pour vous mettre en valeur, répétez les teintes qui vous flattent."

Translation: To showcase yourself, repeat the shades that flatter you. = Wear colors that look good on you.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) De porter les couleurs qui lui vont bien"Les couleurs qui lui vont bien" = colors that suit him well. This matches "les teintes qui vous flattent" (shades that flatter you). "Vont bien" ≈ "flattent" — synonym pair.✅ CORRECT
B) De viser un secteur qui correspond à son style"Viser un secteur" = aim for a sector. The text says to adapt your STYLE to the sector, NOT to choose a sector based on your style. The direction of adaptation is REVERSED in this option.❌ ELIMINATED
C) De ne pas craindre les excès lors du recrutement"Ne pas craindre les excès" = don't fear excess. But the text says for innovative sectors: "sans exagérer afin de ne pas effrayer les recruteurs" — WITHOUT overdoing it. The text WARNS against excess, it doesn't encourage it.❌ ELIMINATED
D) De prendre conseil auprès de ses futurs collègues"Prendre conseil auprès de futurs collègues" = seek advice from future colleagues. The text never mentions consulting colleagues. This information simply doesn't exist in the text.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: A

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Synonym recognition (reinforcing Chapter 2):

TEXT:     "les teintes qui vous flattent"
OPTION A: "les couleurs qui lui vont bien"

teintes ≈ couleurs      → SYNONYM (shades ≈ colors)
flattent ≈ vont bien    → SYNONYM (flatter ≈ suit well)
vous → lui              → PRONOUN SHIFT (you → him/the candidate)

ALL elements match through synonyms. Answer: A

Skill 2 — Detecting REVERSED logic (new skill):

Option B contains a subtle but devastating logical reversal:

TEXT SAYS:    Adapt your STYLE → to match the SECTOR
             (style adapts to sector)

OPTION B:    Choose a SECTOR → that matches your STYLE
             (sector chosen for style)

The DIRECTION of adaptation is flipped:
  TEXT:     Style → Sector  (you change your clothes)
  OPTION B: Sector → Style  (you change your career)

These are OPPOSITE recommendations.

This "direction reversal" trap will appear in many future texts. Memorize this:

TRAP TYPE: "DIRECTION REVERSAL"

If the text says "Adapt X to Y,"
a wrong option might say "Choose Y based on X."

The elements (X and Y) are the SAME, but the DIRECTION is reversed.
This makes the option LOOK correct at first glance.

ALWAYS CHECK: Who adapts to whom? What changes for what?

Skill 3 — Catching negation contradictions (reinforcing Chapter 1):

Option C says "ne pas craindre les excès" (don't fear excess).
The text says "sans exagérer" (without overdoing it).

OPTION C: Don't fear excess → GO AHEAD, be excessive
TEXT:     Without overdoing it → DON'T be excessive

These are OPPOSITES.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Part for Whole" Trap (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: The text gives three examples. An option takes ONE example and presents it as the general message. You pick it because it IS mentioned in the text.

The mechanism:

TEXT:
  Example 1: Banking → formal
  Example 2: Advertising → colorful
  Example 3: Social → approachable

TRAP OPTION: "The article says to dress colorfully"
→ TRUE for Example 2
→ FALSE for Examples 1 and 3
→ Therefore FALSE as a description of the WHOLE article

CORRECT OPTION: "The article says to adapt clothing to the sector"
→ TRUE for ALL three examples
→ Therefore TRUE as a description of the WHOLE article

How to test: Ask yourself: "Does this answer cover ALL the examples in the text, or just ONE?"

Pitfall #2: The "Direction Reversal" Trap (New and Critical)

What happens: An option uses the SAME two concepts as the text but reverses WHICH ONE adapts to the other.

The mechanism:

TEXT: "Adapt A to B"     → A changes to fit B
TRAP: "Choose B for A"   → B is chosen to match A

Same words (A and B), opposite direction.

How to detect: Identify the TWO elements. Ask: "Which one is FIXED and which one CHANGES?"

In this text:

  • The SECTOR is fixed (you've already chosen where to apply)
  • Your CLOTHING changes to match the sector

Option B in Q19 reverses this: it suggests your sector should match your style. But you don't choose a career based on your wardrobe.

Pitfall #3: The "Audience Confusion" Trap

What happens: The text addresses Person A, but an option says the text addresses Person B.

The mechanism:

TEXT addresses: "vous" = individual job candidates
TRAP says: "conseille aux entreprises" = advises companies

The advice is FOR candidates, not FOR companies.

How to detect: Ask: "WHO is the text talking TO?" Look for pronouns:

  • "Vous/tu" → individual reader
  • "Les entreprises doivent..." → companies
  • "Les employeurs sont invités à..." → employers

In this text, every sentence uses "vous" addressing the candidate: "Ne vous habillez pas," "Vous devez inspirer," "Osez la couleur," etc.

Pitfall #4: Treating Warnings as Encouragements

What happens: The text WARNS against something, but an option presents it as a RECOMMENDATION.

The mechanism:

TEXT: "sans exagérer afin de ne pas effrayer les recruteurs"
     = DON'T overdo it, or you'll SCARE recruiters
     = This is a WARNING against excess

TRAP: "ne pas craindre les excès"
     = DON'T FEAR excess
     = This is an ENCOURAGEMENT toward excess

WARNING against excess ≠ ENCOURAGEMENT toward excess

How to detect: Identify whether the text says "DO this" or "DON'T do this." Then check if the option matches the same polarity.

POLARITY CHECK:
  TEXT says DON'T → Option must also say DON'T (or equivalent)
  TEXT says DO → Option must also say DO (or equivalent)
  
  If polarities MISMATCH → option is WRONG

Bridge to Chapter 4:

Chapters 1-3 all dealt with texts about things — a product (Ch.1), a book (Ch.2), and clothing rules (Ch.3). None of these texts were about a person.

Chapter 4 introduces the portrait — a text that describes a person, their motivations, and their actions. In "Douze ans de marche pour la paix" (Texte 27), you'll read about a Belgian man who walks around the world. The questions will test whether you can:

  1. Distinguish facts from motivations — What did he DO vs. WHY did he do it?
  2. Track chronological markers — What happened FIRST, what came LATER?
  3. Understand reported speech — When the text says "il a dit que...", is this a fact or someone's claim?

This is the shift from reading about objects and rules to reading about people and their stories. The trap landscape changes completely: instead of confusing product features, you'll need to avoid confusing a person's initial motivation with their later justification, or confusing what THEY say with what OTHERS say about them.
