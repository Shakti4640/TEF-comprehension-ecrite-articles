📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

L'alpinisme — Un peu d'histoire !

En 1760, Horace-Bénédict de Saussure savant Genevois désireux de faire
des observations scientifiques sur la cime la plus haute des Alpes, promet
une forte récompense au montagnard qui trouvera une route praticable pour
y parvenir. Sur le moment la chose parut sans doute extravagante, car la
première tentative n'eut lieu qu'au bout de 15 ans. Elle fut arrêtée,
comme les suivantes, plutôt par la longueur de la course, l'étonnement,
le dépaysement des hautes neiges que par de véritables difficultés. Le
glacier des Bossons, la Mer de Glace, l'Arête du Goûter sont
successivement explorés, sans succès. Il faut, pour que le pas décisif
soit franchi, l'entrée en scène de Jacques Balmat. Originaire du village
des Pèlerin, déjà père de famille, Jacques Balmat avait 24 ans en 1786.
C'était un grand coureur de glaciers ; il conduisait des voyageurs, mais
n'était pas guide en titre. Le 30 Juin de cette année-là, descendant
d'une course, il rencontre une caravane qui part conquérir la récompense
promise par Saussure. Il se joint à eux, fait une tentative par l'Arête
des Bosses, est abandonné par ses compagnons et redescend sur le Grand
Plateau. Il raconte ensuite avoir ensuite gravi la pente à droite des
Rochers Rouges, atteint l'épaule du Mont Blanc et vu à ses pieds le
versant italien. Mais pris par le brouillard, il ne peut continuer ce
soir-là. Après un bivouac improvisé, il redescend dans la vallée, sûr
d'avoir trouvé l'accès à la cime. Un mois après, le 7 Août, Balmat
repart avec Michel Gabriel Paccard. C'est le docteur de Chamonix, neveu
du doyen des guides de la vallée, homme instruit, qui a déjà fait trois
tentatives au Mont Blanc. Après une nuit passée au sommet de la montagne
de la Côte, reprenant l'itinéraire des Rochers rouges, ils arrivent tous
deux au sommet à 18h30. Le Mont Blanc était vaincu, la plus grande
«première» des Alpes était accomplie. Saussure lui-même se fit conduire
au Mont Blanc en 1787. Il fit un long séjour en 1788 au col du Géant,
multipliant les observations scientifiques. Si la science lui avait fait
faire la conquête du Mont Blanc, on peut dire qu'en retour les Alpes
l'avaient conquis, comme beaucoup d'entre nous. C'est seulement en 1808,
qu'une servante de Chamonix, Maria Paradis, fit la première ascension
féminine. Citons aussi la tragique expédition du docteur Hamel en 1820,
l'ascension de Mlle d'Angeville (1838) qui, exténuée, disait à ses
guides: «Si je meurs avant d'avoir atteint la cime, promettez-moi d'y
porter mon corps et de l'ensevelir là-haut.» Mais aucun des amateurs qui
suivent les traces de Saussure, ne laisse une trace durable dans
l'histoire alpine. Ils sont venus là par curiosité, peut-être pour la
gloire. La preuve en est que malgré le nombre de ces ascensions,
l'exploration du Mont Blanc n'avance pas. La première voie, avec
toutefois le détour par le mur de la Côte, est la seule connue jusqu'en
1855. Cette année-là seulement est faite l'ascension par l'Arête du
Goûter, bien que la voie ait été reconnue dès les premières tentatives.
L'Arête des Bosses ne sera vaincue qu'en 1859. Quand à la connaissance
du massif, il semble qu'elle se borne à la voie du col du Géant. Dans
le reste des Alpes, c'est le silence.

In Chapter 9, you made your first contact with this text — learning to navigate a long narrative by identifying paragraph functions and mapping proper nouns. In Chapter 13, you learned to validate true/false statements against the text using line-by-line proof.

That is approximately one new piece of data every 8 words. Your brain cannot hold 45 pieces of information simultaneously. So the question becomes:

How do you organize 45 pieces of data so that you can retrieve the RIGHT one when a question demands it?

The answer: you build a timeline, a character map, and a place hierarchy — three tools that turn chaos into searchable structure.

  • Is this a SUMMIT, a ROUTE, a CAMP, or a TOWN?

  • Who explored it?

QUESTION 20: "Quel titre convient le mieux à cet article?"

A- La grande crevasse
B- L'appel des sommets
C- Démodé l'alpinisme
D- Au risque des frontières

Chapter 10 covered title selection logic in detail. Here we apply it with the added power of historical comprehension tools.

APPLYING THE THEMATIC COVERAGE TEST:
(From Chapter 10 — does the title cover the WHOLE text?)

  A- "La grande crevasse" (The great crevasse)
     → A crevasse is a crack in a glacier
     → The text mentions glaciers, but it's NOT about crevasses
     → This title is too SPECIFIC and MISLEADING
     → Coverage: ★☆☆☆☆ — ELIMINATE

  B- "L'appel des sommets" (The call of the summits)
     → "Appel" = call/attraction
     → "Sommets" = summits/peaks
     → The text is about people being DRAWN to climb
     → Saussure: called by SCIENCE
     → Balmat: called by ADVENTURE and REWARD
     → Paradis: called by AMBITION (breaking gender barrier)
     → d'Angeville: called by PASSION (would rather die than stop)
     → EVERY character responds to a "call" from the mountain
     → Coverage: ★★★★★ — STRONG CANDIDATE

  C- "Démodé l'alpinisme" (Alpinism is outdated)
     → The text celebrates the BIRTH of alpinism
     → Nothing suggests it's outdated
     → The title contradicts the text's tone
     → Coverage: ★☆☆☆☆ — ELIMINATE

  D- "Au risque des frontières" (At the risk of borders)
     → "Frontières" = borders
     → The text mentions the Italian side briefly
     → But the text is NOT about borders or border crossing
     → Coverage: ★★☆☆☆ — ELIMINATE

  ANSWER: B ✓

Historical comprehension adds depth here: Without understanding the CHARACTER MAP, you might think the text is just about "climbing a mountain." But when you see that SIX different people across 100 years were all drawn to the same summit — a scientist, an adventurer, a doctor, a servant, another doctor, an aristocratic woman — you understand the text's true theme: the mountain CALLS to people. This is exactly what "L'appel des sommets" captures.

QUESTION 21: "Une seule de ces affirmations est vraie. Laquelle?"

A- L'alpinisme a vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme
B- De tout temps, des hommes illustres ont péri dans les alpes
C- À la fascination pour la montagne a succédé une période de terreur
D- Des mulets furent utilisés pour les premières randonnées en montagne

Chapter 13 taught the validation method for "only one is TRUE" questions. Now we apply it with timeline precision:

STATEMENT-BY-STATEMENT VALIDATION:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  A- "L'alpinisme a vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme"
     (Alpinism was born with the beginnings of tourism)
     
     TIMELINE CHECK:
     → The text says alpinism began with SAUSSURE in 1760
     → Saussure's motivation was SCIENCE, not tourism
     → "désireux de faire des observations scientifiques"
     → Tourism is never mentioned in the text
     → VERDICT: FALSE ✗
     → The text explicitly states science, not tourism,
       was the origin

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  B- "De tout temps, des hommes illustres ont péri 
      dans les alpes"
     (Throughout history, illustrious men have perished 
      in the Alps)
     
     TIMELINE CHECK:
     → "De tout temps" = throughout ALL of history
     → The text only covers 1760-1859
     → Does the text mention DEATHS?
     → "La tragique expédition du docteur Hamel en 1820"
     → "tragique" strongly implies death
     → But ONE tragic expedition ≠ "de tout temps" 
       (throughout all of history)
     → Also: "hommes illustres" (illustrious men)
     → Was Hamel "illustre"? The text doesn't say he was famous
     → The text says later climbers left NO "trace durable"
     → "aucun des amateurs... ne laisse une trace durable"
     → VERDICT: PARTIALLY TRUE but EXAGGERATED ✗
     → "De tout temps" is too sweeping for the evidence

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  C- "À la fascination pour la montagne a succédé une 
      période de terreur"
     (Fascination for the mountain was followed by 
      a period of terror)
     
     TEXT CHECK:
     → The text shows continuous fascination, not terror
     → People kept climbing despite Hamel's tragedy
     → d'Angeville went AFTER Hamel and was willing to DIE
     → No "period of terror" is described
     → The problem after 1786 was STAGNATION, not terror
     → "l'exploration du Mont Blanc n'avance pas" 
       (exploration doesn't advance)
     → VERDICT: FALSE ✗
     → The text describes stagnation, not terror

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  D- "Des mulets furent utilisés pour les premières 
      randonnées en montagne"
     (Mules were used for the first mountain hikes)
     
     TEXT CHECK:
     → The word "mulets" (mules) appears NOWHERE in the text
     → No animals are mentioned at all
     → The text describes human climbing on foot
     → "randonnées" (hikes) is a modern recreational term
       that doesn't match the text's vocabulary of 
       "tentatives" (attempts), "course" (run/climb),
       "ascension" (ascent)
     → VERDICT: FALSE ✗
     → Completely fabricated — no textual support

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Wait — if all four seem false, we must re-examine.

This is the critical moment Chapter 13 warned about: when elimination seems to reject everything, you must find the LEAST false / MOST supported answer.

RE-EXAMINATION:
                                                          
  Let's reconsider B more carefully:
  
  "De tout temps, des hommes illustres ont péri dans les alpes"
  
  → "De tout temps" is hyperbolic, BUT...
  → The text DOES mention a "tragique expédition" (deaths implied)
  → Multiple people risked their lives
  → d'Angeville explicitly mentioned DYING on the mountain
  → The text covers a 100-year span of dangerous climbs
  → People who climb Mont Blanc ARE somewhat "illustres" 
    (notable enough to be recorded in history)
  
  Among all four options, B has the MOST textual support:
  → At least ONE tragic death is confirmed
  → The danger theme runs throughout the text
  → The other three options have ZERO textual support
  
  ANSWER: B ✓ (the least false, most supported)

📌 LESSON: In "only one is TRUE" questions about historical texts, the correct answer may use slightly exaggerated language. Choose the answer with the MOST textual evidence, even if it's not perfectly precise. The other three should have clearly LESS support.

QUESTION 21b: "La particularité du mont Cervin est..."

A- Que tous les hommes qui s'y sont attaqués sont partis en cordée
B- Que ce mont a particulièrement résisté à l'assaut des alpinistes
C- Qu'il a fallu attendre 1932 pour qu'un alpiniste en revienne vivant
D- Que seuls les montagnards de naissance furent capables d'en gravir 
   les flancs

Here we face a unique challenge: The provided text version focuses entirely on Mont Blanc and does not explicitly describe Mont Cervin (the Matterhorn). As noted in the article header, this is "Texte 55 repeated in another version" — the full original likely contained additional paragraphs about other Alpine peaks, including the Cervin.

This is a real exam scenario: sometimes a question references content in a section you must carefully locate, or that requires inference from the text's broader narrative pattern.

STRATEGY: APPLY THE TEXT'S LOGIC TO THE QUESTION
                                                          
  The text's CORE PATTERN about Mont Blanc:
  → Multiple attempts over decades before success
  → "la chose parut extravagante" (seemed impossible)
  → Routes were "explored without success" for years
  → Even after first ascent, routes took decades more
  → Arête du Goûter: known from start → conquered only 1855
  → Arête des Bosses: attempted 1786 → conquered only 1859
  
  THE TEXT'S THEME: Mountains RESIST human conquest.
  
  If Mont Cervin (Matterhorn) has a "particularité," 
  it likely follows this same theme of RESISTANCE.
  
  NOW EVALUATE OPTIONS:
  
  A- "tous... partis en cordée" (all went roped together)
     → This is a climbing TECHNIQUE, not a particularité
     → Roped climbing is NORMAL in alpinism
     → Not distinctive enough → WEAK
  
  B- "ce mont a particulièrement résisté à l'assaut"
     (this mountain particularly resisted climbers' assault)
     → FITS the text's core theme perfectly
     → "Particulièrement résisté" = exceptionally resistant
     → The Matterhorn is historically famous for exactly this
     → It was one of the LAST major Alpine peaks conquered
     → STRONG MATCH with the text's logic
  
  C- "attendre 1932 pour qu'un alpiniste en revienne vivant"
     (wait until 1932 for a climber to return alive)
     → The Matterhorn was first climbed in 1865
     → Many returned alive after that
     → 1932 is historically inaccurate for Cervin
     → ELIMINATE
  
  D- "seuls les montagnards de naissance" 
     (only born mountaineers could climb it)
     → The text shows the OPPOSITE pattern:
       Saussure was a scientist, Paccard was a doctor,
       Paradis was a servant — NOT born mountaineers
     → This contradicts the text's inclusive theme
     → ELIMINATE
  
  ANSWER: B ✓

The Matterhorn's historical reality confirms this: It was indeed one of the most resistant Alpine peaks, not conquered until 1865, and its first ascent ended in tragedy (4 of 7 climbers died on the descent). The mountain's extreme resistance to climbing is its defining "particularité."

DO 4: DECODE ARCHAIC AND LITERARY FRENCH THROUGH GRAMMATICAL STRUCTURE

Unlike Texte 102's scientific vocabulary (Chapter 16), L'Alpinisme uses archaic literary French that feels distant from modern language:

ARCHAIC EXPRESSION DECODER:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "la chose parut sans doute extravagante"
  ┌──────────┐ ┌────────┐ ┌───────────┐ ┌─────────────┐
  │la chose  │ │parut   │ │sans doute │ │extravagante │
  │=the thing│ │=seemed │ │=no doubt/ │ │=extravagant/│
  │(=the idea│ │(passé  │ │ probably  │ │ outlandish  │
  │ of       │ │simple) │ │           │ │             │
  │climbing) │ │        │ │           │ │             │
  └──────────┘ └────────┘ └───────────┘ └─────────────┘
  → "The idea probably seemed outlandish"
  
  KEY: "parut" is the PASSÉ SIMPLE of "paraître"
  Modern French would say "a paru" (passé composé)
  Historical texts use passé simple throughout.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "la première tentative n'eut lieu qu'au bout de 15 ans"
  ┌──────────────┐ ┌────────┐ ┌────────────┐ ┌──────────┐
  │n'eut lieu que│ │=ne...que│ │=only       │ │au bout de│
  │=took place   │ │=only   │ │happened    │ │=at the   │
  │ only         │ │        │ │            │ │ end of   │
  └──────────────┘ └────────┘ └────────────┘ └──────────┘
  → "The first attempt only took place after 15 years"
  
  KEY: "eut" is passé simple of "avoir"
  "avoir lieu" = to take place
  "n'eut lieu que" = only took place

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "Il faut, pour que le pas décisif soit franchi, 
   l'entrée en scène de Jacques Balmat"
  
  SKELETON: Il faut... l'entrée en scène de Balmat
  → "What was needed was Balmat's entrance"
  → "le pas décisif soit franchi" = the decisive step 
     be taken (subjunctive)
  
  MODERN VERSION: "Pour que quelque chose change, 
  il fallait que Balmat arrive"
  → For something to change, Balmat needed to arrive

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "Saussure lui-même se fit conduire au Mont Blanc"
  
  "se fit conduire" = had himself led/guided
  → "se faire + infinitive" = to have something done to oneself
  → Saussure didn't climb alone — he was GUIDED up
  → This is a CAUSATIVE construction
  → Important: Saussure was a SCIENTIST, not a climber
     He needed guides to get him there

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "ensevelir là-haut"
  
  "ensevelir" = to bury / to entomb
  "là-haut" = up there (= at the summit)
  → d'Angeville wanted to be BURIED at the summit
  → This shows extreme devotion/obsession

📌 The passé simple is the biggest grammatical hurdle in historical French texts. It is NOT used in spoken French or modern journalism. Recognizing its forms is essential:

PASSÉ SIMPLE RECOGNITION GUIDE:
(Forms you'll see in historical texts)

  VERB          PASSÉ SIMPLE     MODERN EQUIVALENT
  ────          ────────────     ─────────────────
  paraître   →  parut            a paru (seemed)
  avoir      →  eut              a eu (had)
  être       →  fut              a été (was)
  faire      →  fit              a fait (did/made)
  pouvoir    →  put              a pu (could)
  venir      →  vint             est venu (came)
  voir       →  vit              a vu (saw)
  prendre    →  prit             a pris (took)
  mettre     →  mit              a mis (put)
  dire       →  dit              a dit (said)
  
  PATTERN: Most end in -ut, -it, or -int
  
  IN THIS TEXT:
  "parut" (seemed), "eut lieu" (took place), 
  "fut arrêtée" (was stopped), "fit" (did/made)
  
  RECOGNITION TRICK: If you see a short verb form 
  you don't recognize, and the text is about the past,
  it's probably passé simple. Look for the ROOT:
  "parut" → par- → paraître → seemed ✓

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE CHRONOLOGICAL CONFUSION TRAP

SYMPTOM: You mix up the ORDER of events because the 
         text sometimes references future events within 
         past descriptions.

EXAMPLE:
  The text describes Balmat's June 30 attempt, 
  then says he "reached the shoulder of Mont Blanc 
  and saw the Italian side."
  
  STUDENT THINKS: "Balmat reached the summit on June 30!"
  
  BUT: "Mais pris par le brouillard, il ne peut continuer"
  → He was stopped by FOG
  → He did NOT reach the summit on June 30
  → He only reached the summit on AUGUST 7 with Paccard
  
  THE TRAP: The description of seeing the Italian side 
  sounds like a summit achievement, but it was only 
  the SHOULDER (l'épaule), not the top.
  
  FIX: Track SUCCESS vs. FAILURE markers:
  "ne peut continuer" = FAILURE (could not continue)
  "arrivent au sommet" = SUCCESS (arrived at summit)
  "redescend" = RETREAT (went back down)
  "le Mont Blanc était vaincu" = VICTORY (was conquered)

PITFALL 2: THE "ILLUSTRIOUS" EXAGGERATION TRAP

SYMPTOM: Q21 option B says "des hommes illustres ont péri"
         and you think "the text doesn't say they were 
         illustrious, so this is false."

REALITY: "Illustre" in this context means "notable" or 
         "famous enough to be recorded." The text DOES 
         record these people by name — making them 
         historically notable.
         
         The REAL question is whether "de tout temps" 
         (throughout all of history) is justified.
         
         → The text covers 1760-1859 = about 100 years
         → This is a SIGNIFICANT span but not "all of history"
         → However, it's the MOST supported of all options
         
LESSON: In historical comprehension, don't reject an 
        answer because ONE word seems slightly exaggerated.
        Compare it to the OTHER options. If the others 
        have ZERO support, the slightly exaggerated one wins.

PITFALL 3: THE PLACE NAME OVERLOAD FREEZE

SYMPTOM: You see "glacier des Bossons, la Mer de Glace, 
         l'Arête du Goûter" and your brain freezes because 
         you're trying to visualize or locate each one.

FIX: YOU DON'T NEED TO KNOW WHERE THEY ARE GEOGRAPHICALLY.

  You only need to know their NARRATIVE FUNCTION:
  → These are three routes that were EXPLORED and FAILED
  → "sont successivement explorés, sans succès"
  → FUNCTION: They represent FAILURE before Balmat's success
  
  The specific geographical location of the Mer de Glace 
  is IRRELEVANT for the exam. Its role in the STORY 
  (a failed route) is what matters.

PITFALL 4: MISSING THE "SEULEMENT" / "NE...QUE" EMPHASIS MARKERS

HISTORICAL TEXTS USE RESTRICTION MARKERS TO SIGNAL IMPORTANCE:

  "la première tentative n'eut lieu QU'au bout de 15 ans"
  → ONLY after 15 years (= surprisingly long wait)
  
  "C'est SEULEMENT en 1808 qu'une servante..."
  → ONLY in 1808 (= late, 22 years after first ascent)
  
  "Cette année-là SEULEMENT est faite l'ascension 
   par l'Arête du Goûter"
  → ONLY that year (= 1855, almost 70 years after 
    the route was first identified!)
  
  "L'Arête des Bosses ne sera vaincue QU'en 1859"
  → ONLY in 1859 (= very late)
  
  EVERY "seulement" and "ne...que" carries the author's 
  JUDGMENT: "this took too long" or "this came too late."
  
  These markers reveal the text's ARGUMENT:
  → Progress was painfully slow
  → Exploration stagnated after the first summit
  → The amateurs who followed added nothing new
