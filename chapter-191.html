🧠 THE PROBLEM: A TITLE MUST DO EVERYTHING AT ONCE

Title questions look deceptively simple. "Just pick the best title." But a title must satisfy five criteria simultaneously, and most wrong answers satisfy only one or two:

THE 5 CRITERIA A TITLE MUST SATISFY:

  ╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
  ║                                                          ║
  ║  CRITERION 1: TOPIC COVERAGE                             ║
  ║  → Does the title relate to what the text is ABOUT?      ║
  ║                                                          ║
  ║  CRITERION 2: SCOPE MATCH                                ║
  ║  → Does the title cover the WHOLE text,                  ║
  ║    not just one section?                                 ║
  ║                                                          ║
  ║  CRITERION 3: TONE ALIGNMENT                             ║
  ║  → Does the title match the text's EMOTIONAL register?   ║
  ║                                                          ║
  ║  CRITERION 4: ACCURACY                                   ║
  ║  → Does the title avoid saying anything the text          ║
  ║    contradicts or doesn't support?                       ║
  ║                                                          ║
  ║  CRITERION 5: SPECIFICITY                                ║
  ║  → Is the title specific enough to distinguish           ║
  ║    THIS text from other possible texts?                  ║
  ║                                                          ║
  ╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

Most students evaluate titles using ONLY Criterion 1 (topic). They ask: "Is this title about mountains?" If yes, they pick it. But a title about mountains could fit a geology textbook, a travel guide, a thriller novel, or a history article. Topic alone is never sufficient.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE TITLE EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

  TONE:          Admiring of pioneers, critical of followers

  GENRE:         Historical narrative (educational)

STEP 2: FORMULATE YOUR OWN TITLE FIRST

Before looking at the options, create your OWN title using the Meaning-First approach (Chapter 8, Rule 1):

MY OWN TITLE ATTEMPT:

  Based on the text's content and tone, a good title would be:
  
  → "The Conquest of Mont Blanc: A History"
    (covers topic but misses the stagnation theme)
    
  → "When Mountains Called: The Birth of Alpinism"
    (captures the fascination + historical scope)
    
  → "The Lure of the Summits: From Pioneers to Tourists"
    (captures the arc: initial passion → later stagnation)
    
  → "A Century of Mountain Conquest"
    (covers the timeline but lacks emotional resonance)

  BEST PERSONAL TITLE:
  Something about the CALL/ATTRACTION of mountains 
  that drove people to climb them across a century.
  The text is fundamentally about WHY people went to the Alps.

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: THE 5-CRITERIA TEST ON EACH OPTION

OPTION A: "La grande crevasse"

TRANSLATION: "The Great Crevasse"

CRITERION 1 — TOPIC COVERAGE:
  → A crevasse is a deep crack in a glacier/mountain
  → Relates to MOUNTAINS → topic adjacent ≈
  → But the text is about the HISTORY of climbing,
    not about a specific crevasse
  → The word "crevasse" appears ZERO times in the text
  SCORE: 2/5 (vaguely related to mountains)

CRITERION 2 — SCOPE MATCH:
  → "La grande crevasse" suggests ONE specific feature
  → The text covers 100 YEARS of multiple expeditions
  → A single crevasse ≠ a century of history
  → TOO NARROW
  SCORE: 1/5 (covers one possible element, misses everything else)

CRITERION 3 — TONE ALIGNMENT:
  → "La grande crevasse" sounds dramatic, dangerous, ominous
  → The text's tone is: admiring, educational, slightly nostalgic
  → The text is NOT primarily about danger
  → Tone mismatch
  SCORE: 2/5 (dramatic ≠ educational/admiring)

CRITERION 4 — ACCURACY:
  → The text never mentions a specific great crevasse
  → No crevasse event is described
  → The title implies something the text doesn't contain
  SCORE: 1/5 (references something absent from text)

CRITERION 5 — SPECIFICITY:
  → "La grande crevasse" could be a geology article,
    a novel, a safety guide, a disaster report
  → Not specific to THIS text's content
  SCORE: 2/5 (too generic for this specific text)

TOTAL SCORE: 8/25

ELIMINATION VERDICT: ❌ ELIMINATED
  Primary flaw: The text never discusses a crevasse.
  The title sounds mountain-related but misses the text entirely.
  
  DISTRACTOR TYPE: Type 2 (Scope Error) — too narrow,
  focusing on one geographic feature instead of a century of history.
  Also Type 7 (Outside Knowledge) — you associate mountains with 
  crevasses from general knowledge, not from this text.

OPTION B: "L'appel des sommets"

TRANSLATION: "The Call of the Summits"

CRITERION 1 — TOPIC COVERAGE:
  → "Sommets" (summits/peaks) = directly about mountains ✅
  → "Appel" (call/appeal) = the ATTRACTION, the draw
  → The text IS about what drew people to climb Mont Blanc
  → Saussure: drawn by scientific curiosity
  → Balmat: drawn by the challenge and the reward
  → Followers: drawn by curiosity and glory
  → The ENTIRE text is about why people are CALLED to summits
  SCORE: 5/5 (perfect topic match)

CRITERION 2 — SCOPE MATCH:
  → "L'appel" (the call) is ONGOING — not limited to one event
  → It covers Saussure's initial fascination (1760)
  → It covers Balmat's determination (1786)
  → It covers the followers' curiosity (1808-1838)
  → It even covers the stagnation (people still came, 
    but for glory not exploration)
  → "des sommets" (of the summits) — PLURAL = 
    not just one mountain, but the IDEA of summits
  
  Let me verify against EACH PHASE from Chapter 9:
  
  Phase 1 (Challenge): Saussure offers reward = 
    summit CALLS to scientists ✅
  Phase 2 (Hero): Balmat answers the call = 
    summit CALLS to adventurers ✅
  Phase 3 (Conquest): Summit reached = 
    the call was ANSWERED ✅
  Phase 4 (Followers): Others follow = 
    the call continues to attract ✅
  Phase 5 (Stagnation): People come but don't explore = 
    the call becomes superficial ✅
  
  ALL FIVE PHASES covered ✅
  SCORE: 5/5 (covers entire text)

CRITERION 3 — TONE ALIGNMENT:
  → "L'appel" has a ROMANTIC, POETIC quality
  → It suggests fascination, attraction, irresistible draw
  → The text's tone: admiring of the mountains' power
  → "les Alpes l'avaient conquis" (the Alps conquered him)
    = the mountains CALL and CONQUER those who answer
  → "L'appel des sommets" captures this romantic fascination
  → The text IS romantic about mountains, even while being 
    critical of followers
  SCORE: 5/5 (perfect tone match)

CRITERION 4 — ACCURACY:
  → Does the text support the idea of a "call"?
  → Saussure: "désireux de faire des observations" = drawn by desire ✅
  → Balmat: "grand coureur de glaciers" = passionate about mountains ✅
  → Followers: "venus là par curiosité" = drawn by curiosity ✅
  → Angeville: willing to DIE on the summit = ultimate call ✅
  → "les Alpes l'avaient conquis, comme beaucoup d'entre nous" 
    = the Alps conquer US too = the call is universal ✅
  → The ENTIRE narrative is driven by people being CALLED to the summit
  SCORE: 5/5 (fully supported by text evidence)

CRITERION 5 — SPECIFICITY:
  → "L'appel des sommets" is specifically about 
    mountain fascination and climbing history
  → It wouldn't fit a geology text, a travel guide, 
    or a safety manual
  → It matches THIS text's particular blend of 
    romantic history and mountaineering narrative
  SCORE: 4/5 (highly specific to this type of text)

TOTAL SCORE: 24/25

SURVIVAL VERDICT: ✅ EXTREMELY STRONG CANDIDATE

OPTION C: "Démodé l'alpinisme"

TRANSLATION: "Alpinism: Outdated" / "Out-of-fashion, alpinism"

CRITERION 1 — TOPIC COVERAGE:
  → "L'alpinisme" = directly about the topic ✅
  → "Démodé" = outdated, old-fashioned, passé
  → Is the text about alpinism being OUTDATED?
  SCORE: 3/5 (topic word matches, but characterization doesn't)

CRITERION 2 — SCOPE MATCH:
  → "Démodé" suggests a CURRENT judgment about alpinism
  → The text is a HISTORICAL account (1760-1859)
  → The text never discusses whether alpinism is 
    currently fashionable or outdated
  → The stagnation described is within the 19th century,
    not a modern assessment
  → The text does NOT argue alpinism is passé
  SCORE: 1/5 (imposes a modern perspective on a historical text)

CRITERION 3 — TONE ALIGNMENT:
  → "Démodé" is dismissive, slightly mocking
  → The text's tone is ADMIRING of pioneers
  → Even the criticism of followers is about their 
    MOTIVATIONS, not about alpinism itself being outdated
  → The text's final line "c'est le silence" expresses 
    REGRET, not dismissal
  → The author CARES about alpinism — doesn't dismiss it
  SCORE: 1/5 (dismissive tone ≠ admiring/regretful tone)

CRITERION 4 — ACCURACY:
  → The text never says or implies alpinism is outdated
  → The text says exploration STAGNATED, but that's different:
    Stagnation in 1855 ≠ alpinism being outdated as a concept
  → The text's own subtitle says "Un peu d'histoire !"
    with an exclamation mark = ENTHUSIASM about the history
  → Calling something "démodé" contradicts the author's 
    evident passion for the subject
  SCORE: 1/5 (contradicts the text's attitude)

CRITERION 5 — SPECIFICITY:
  → "Démodé l'alpinisme" could be an opinion piece, 
    a debate article, a sociological essay
  → It doesn't match a historical narrative at all
  SCORE: 2/5 (wrong genre implied)

TOTAL SCORE: 8/25

ELIMINATION VERDICT: ❌ ELIMINATED
  Primary flaw: The text does NOT argue alpinism is outdated.
  It tells the HISTORY with admiration and regret.
  "Démodé" imposes a MODERN, DISMISSIVE perspective 
  that contradicts the text's tone entirely.
  
  DISTRACTOR TYPE: Type 6 (Inference Overreach) — 
  takes the "stagnation" theme and pushes it to 
  "alpinism is outdated," which the text never says.
  Also Type 1 (Wrong characterization) — the text 
  celebrates, doesn't dismiss.

OPTION D: "Au risque des frontières"

TRANSLATION: "At the Risk of Borders/Frontiers"

CRITERION 1 — TOPIC COVERAGE:
  → "Frontières" (borders/frontiers) → mountains ARE borders
  → "Risque" (risk) → climbing IS risky
  → But: Is the text ABOUT borders? About risk?
  → The text mentions seeing "le versant italien" (Italian side)
    = Mont Blanc is on the France-Italy border
  → But this is ONE detail, not the text's focus
  → The text is about the HISTORY OF CLIMBING, not about borders
  SCORE: 2/5 (tangentially related but misses the focus)

CRITERION 2 — SCOPE MATCH:
  → "Frontières" focuses on the GEOGRAPHIC/POLITICAL aspect
  → The text focuses on the HUMAN/HISTORICAL aspect
  → Borders are mentioned once (Italian side) — one sentence
  → Risk is implied (Hamel's tragic expedition, Angeville's 
    willingness to die) — but only in passing
  → The text is 90% about people and exploration,
    10% about geography and risk
  SCORE: 1/5 (captures a tiny fraction of the text)

CRITERION 3 — TONE ALIGNMENT:
  → "Au risque des frontières" sounds POLITICAL or ADVENTUROUS
  → It evokes border-crossing danger, geopolitical tension
  → The text's tone is HISTORICAL and ADMIRING
  → No political or border-related tension in the text
  SCORE: 1/5 (wrong emotional register entirely)

CRITERION 4 — ACCURACY:
  → The text never discusses border-related risks
  → The "risque" in the text is about weather, terrain, 
    and physical endurance — not about borders
  → Balmat was stopped by FOG, not by a border
  → No one is described as crossing a FRONTIER in a 
    meaningful sense
  SCORE: 1/5 (misrepresents the text's content)

CRITERION 5 — SPECIFICITY:
  → "Au risque des frontières" could be about immigration,
    geopolitics, military history, border disputes
  → It doesn't specifically evoke mountaineering history
  SCORE: 1/5 (could fit many unrelated texts)

TOTAL SCORE: 6/25

ELIMINATION VERDICT: ❌ ELIMINATED
  Primary flaw: The text is not about borders or 
  border-related risks. Mountains happen to be on borders,
  but the text's focus is on climbing history, not geography.
  
  DISTRACTOR TYPE: Type 2 (Scope Error) — takes one 
  minor geographic detail (seeing Italy from the shoulder)
  and inflates it into the text's main theme.
  Also Type 7 (Outside Knowledge) — mountains as political 
  borders is general knowledge, not this text's topic.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: CHOOSING BASED ON WORD ASSOCIATION

WRONG:
  "The text is about mountains.
   'La grande crevasse' mentions a mountain feature.
   Therefore A is the right title."
   
  This is WORD ASSOCIATION, not title evaluation.
  
  "Crevasse" associates with "mountains"
  But the text never mentions a crevasse.
  Association ≠ Coverage.
  
RIGHT:
  "The text is about the history of mountain climbing.
   Which title captures the HISTORY and the CLIMBING 
   and the FASCINATION that drove people?"

MISTAKE 2: CHOOSING THE MOST DRAMATIC TITLE

WRONG:
  "La grande crevasse" sounds the most dramatic and 
  mountaineering-related. It must be right."
  
  Students gravitate toward the most VIVID, DRAMATIC option.
  But dramatic ≠ accurate.
  
  The text is HISTORICAL and EDUCATIONAL.
  The best title should be EVOCATIVE but ACCURATE.
  "L'appel des sommets" is evocative (poetic, romantic)
  AND accurate (captures the text's actual theme).

MISTAKE 3: CONFUSING THE TEXT'S TOPIC WITH THE TEXT'S THEME

TOPIC:  What the text talks about
        → Mountain climbing, Mont Blanc, historical expeditions
        
THEME:  What the text is REALLY about (deeper meaning)
        → The irresistible attraction of mountains,
          and how that attraction evolved from scientific 
          curiosity to shallow glory-seeking
          
  "La grande crevasse" → relates to TOPIC (mountains) 
                          but not THEME (attraction)
  "L'appel des sommets" → relates to BOTH topic (summits) 
                           AND theme (the call/attraction)
                           
  A GOOD TITLE captures the THEME, not just the TOPIC.

MISTAKE 4: BEING MISLED BY "DÉMODÉ" AND THE STAGNATION THEME

WRONG REASONING:
  "The text says exploration stagnated, routes weren't 
   explored, 'c'est le silence.' This means alpinism 
   was becoming outdated. So 'Démodé l'alpinisme' fits."
   
RIGHT REASONING:
  The text says exploration stagnated IN THE 19TH CENTURY.
  But the text does NOT say alpinism became OUTDATED.
  
  Stagnation ≠ Obsolescence
  
  "Exploration didn't advance" means people weren't 
  exploring NEW routes. They STILL climbed Mont Blanc.
  They just followed the SAME route repeatedly.
  
  "Démodé" (outdated) implies alpinism itself lost relevance.
  The text never says this. The text says exploration 
  lost its SCIENTIFIC PURPOSE and became mere tourism.
  
  That's a CRITICISM OF MOTIVATION, not of the activity itself.

MISTAKE 5: NOT READING ALL OPTIONS BEFORE CHOOSING

COMMON SHORTCUT:
  Read A: "La grande crevasse" → maybe?
  Read B: "L'appel des sommets" → sounds good! ✅
  → Mark B → Move to next question
  → Never read C or D
  
  THIS TIME you got lucky. B is correct.
  
  But what if C or D had been better?
  What if B had a subtle flaw you missed?
  
  ALWAYS evaluate ALL options.
  The 5-Criteria Test takes 1-2 minutes for all four.
  That investment prevents costly mistakes.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE METAPHORICAL TITLE TRAP

DANGER: Some titles are METAPHORICAL, not literal.
        Students who read literally miss the meaning.
        
  "L'appel des sommets" is PARTLY metaphorical:
  → "L'appel" is not a literal phone call
  → It's a metaphor for ATTRACTION, FASCINATION, DRAW
  → Mountains don't literally "call" — they ATTRACT
  
  Students who read literally might think:
  "The text never says mountains called anyone.
   So this title doesn't match."
  
  But the entire text DEMONSTRATES the call:
  → People are DRAWN to climb (= they hear the call)
  → Saussure is CONQUERED by the Alps (= the call won)
  → Followers come for curiosity (= they hear a weaker call)
  
  RULE: Titles can be metaphorical.
  Check if the metaphor CAPTURES the text's reality,
  not if the literal words appear in the text.

PITFALL 2: THE "LAST SECTION" TITLE ERROR

DANGER: Choosing a title based only on the text's ENDING.
        
  The text ends with stagnation and "silence."
  
  A student might think: "The text ends sadly. 
  So the title should be sad/negative."
  → Picks "Démodé l'alpinisme" (negative title)
  
  BUT: The text BEGINS with excitement and triumph.
  The MIDDLE is heroic and admiring.
  Only the END is critical/regretful.
  
  A title must cover the WHOLE text, not just the ending.
  
  "L'appel des sommets" works for ALL sections:
  → Beginning: the call is issued (Saussure)
  → Middle: the call is answered (Balmat)
  → End: the call continues but weakens (followers)

PITFALL 3: THE GENRE CONFUSION

DANGER: Choosing a title that implies the WRONG genre.
        
  "La grande crevasse" → sounds like a THRILLER or ADVENTURE novel
  "Au risque des frontières" → sounds like a POLITICAL essay
  "Démodé l'alpinisme" → sounds like an OPINION piece
  "L'appel des sommets" → sounds like a HISTORICAL/POETIC narrative
  
  The text IS a historical narrative with poetic elements.
  Only "L'appel des sommets" matches this genre.
  
  RULE: The title should signal the correct GENRE.
  A reader should be able to guess what TYPE of text 
  it is from the title alone.

PITFALL 4: OVER-ANALYZING THE TITLE

DANGER: Spending too much time on one title option,
        looking for hidden meanings or subtle flaws.
        
  "L'appel des sommets — but wait, the text also 
   talks about stagnation. Does 'appel' capture 
   stagnation? Well, the call becomes weaker...
   but is that still an 'appel'? And 'sommets' is 
   plural but the text mainly discusses one summit...
   maybe the plural is wrong? But then..."
   
  STOP. This is over-analysis.
  
  The title doesn't need to be PERFECT.
  It needs to be the BEST among four options.
  
  Compare:
  B scores 24/25 → Best by far
  A scores 8/25
  C scores 8/25
  D scores 6/25
  
  B wins overwhelmingly. No need to find 
  microscopic flaws in the clear winner.

WHAT'S MISSING?

  Everything so far has dealt with EXPLICIT information —
  things the text DIRECTLY SAYS.
  
  But many exam questions ask about IMPLICIT information —
  things the text SUGGESTS, IMPLIES, or LEADS YOU TO CONCLUDE
  without directly stating them.

Phase 3 begins with Chapter 11: "What the Text IMPLIES but Never Says."

This is the transition from reading comprehension to critical reading — the ability to understand not just what words mean, but what the author INTENDED you to understand WITHOUT saying it directly.

We return to Texte 101 and Q26, where the correct answer (B: "l'intelligence artificielle et la dépollution") required you to make a connection the text never explicitly makes. This is INFERENCE — and it is the skill that separates good readers from excellent ones.

PHASE 2 SKILL: "The text SAYS X" → find X
PHASE 3 SKILL: "The text IMPLIES Y" → deduce Y from X

📌 Phases 1-2 gave you the complete toolkit for EXPLICIT comprehension. Phase 3 teaches you to read BETWEEN the lines — where the hardest exam questions live.
