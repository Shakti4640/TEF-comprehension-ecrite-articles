CHAPTER 4 — Texte 38 : « Le travail et les couleurs » : Interpréter des conseils pratiques

📋 PHASE 1: VERY EASY — FOUNDATION THINKING

📖 START → COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le travail et les couleurs

Lors d'une entrevue professionnelle, la première impression est capitale ! Ne vous habillez pas pour le poste que vous occupez, mais pour celui auquel vous aspirez. Ainsi, vous manifestez déjà que vous avez la carrure pour. Côté couleur, cela dépend du secteur économique. Traditionnel et conservateur comme la banque ? Vous devez inspirer confiance et crédibilité, car vous êtes censé véhiculer l'esprit de l'entreprise. Optez pour du marine, du beige. Un milieu novateur comme la publicité ou la presse ? Osez la couleur ! Laissez transparaître votre créativité, sans exagérer afin de ne pas effrayer les recruteurs. Enfin, pour un poste dans le social, les ressources humaines, où des gens sont amenés à venir vers vous, à se confier, vous devez dégager un abord avenant et une tenue vestimentaire qui va avec.

Votre palette dépendra aussi de la position professionnelle que vous recherchez. Pour vous mettre en valeur, répétez les teintes qui vous flattent.

  • A) Incite les employés à porter des habits colorés
  • B) Conseille aux entreprises de se créer une identité vestimentaire
  • C) Dresse un portrait des salariés selon leur place dans l'entreprise
  • D) Informe sur les codes vestimentaires à adopter face aux recruteurs

Question 18 : Selon cet article, le candidat à un emploi doit

  • A) Adapter sa tenue au secteur professionnel qu'il cible
  • B) S'habiller avec des couleurs vives pour être remarqué
  • C) Porter des vêtements classiques pour être pris au sérieux
  • D) Opter pour un style correspondant à son expérience professionnelle.

Question 19 : L'article recommande également au candidat

  • A) De porter les couleurs qui lui vont bien
  • B) De viser un secteur qui correspond à son style
  • C) De ne pas craindre les excès lors du recrutement
  • D) De prendre conseil auprès de ses futurs collègues

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What kind of text is this?

This is a conseil pratique (practical advice text) — it gives specific recommendations for specific situations. Unlike a portrait (Chapter 3) that tells a story, or a promotional text (Chapter 2) that sells a product, this text INSTRUCTS the reader on what to DO.

The New Skill: Reading CONDITIONAL RECOMMENDATIONS

This text doesn't give ONE universal piece of advice. It gives DIFFERENT advice depending on the SITUATION. This is the most important distinction of Chapter 4:

IF sector = banking         → THEN wear navy, beige (conservative)
IF sector = advertising     → THEN wear colors (creative, but don't overdo it)
IF sector = social work     → THEN look approachable (warm, inviting)

The exam will test whether you understand that the advice CHANGES based on context — or whether you incorrectly extract ONE specific piece of advice and apply it UNIVERSALLY.

The structure of advice texts

Advice texts follow a predictable pattern:

GENERAL PRINCIPLE    →  One overarching rule that applies to everyone
SPECIFIC CASES       →  Different recommendations for different situations
CLOSING ADVICE       →  A final tip that applies universally again

Let's map this text to that pattern:

SectionContentType
"la première impression est capitale"First impressions matterGENERAL PRINCIPLE
"Ne vous habillez pas pour le poste que vous occupez, mais pour celui auquel vous aspirez"Dress for the job you WANT, not the one you HAVEGENERAL PRINCIPLE
"Traditionnel et conservateur comme la banque ? Optez pour du marine, du beige"Banking → conservative colorsSPECIFIC CASE 1
"Un milieu novateur comme la publicité ? Osez la couleur !"Advertising → bold colors (with limits)SPECIFIC CASE 2

This three-layer structure (GENERAL → SPECIFIC → CLOSING) is your roadmap.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 17 — What is this article about?

The question: Cet article...

This is a PURPOSE + SUBJECT question — it asks you to identify both WHAT the text discusses and WHO it addresses.

Step 1: Identify the situation described.

The very first sentence sets the scene:

"Lors d'une entrevue professionnelle, la première impression est capitale !"

Key term: "entrevue professionnelle" = job interview. The entire text is about what to wear AT A JOB INTERVIEW.

Step 2: Identify WHO the text addresses.

The text uses "vous" throughout, addressing the READER directly. But who is this reader?

"Ne vous habillez pas pour le poste que vous occupez, mais pour celui auquel vous aspirez."

The reader is someone who:

  • Currently has a job ("le poste que vous occupez")
  • Wants a better/different job ("celui auquel vous aspirez")
  • Will face recruiters ("ne pas effrayer les recruteurs")

This reader is a JOB CANDIDATE — someone preparing for a professional interview.

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) Incite les employés à porter des habits colorésThe text recommends colorful clothes ONLY for creative sectors ("publicité, presse"). For banking, it recommends navy and beige. This option takes ONE specific case and presents it as the UNIVERSAL message❌ TRAP — generalizes one sector's advice to all sectors
B) Conseille aux entreprises de se créer une identité vestimentaireThe text advises INDIVIDUALS (candidates), not ENTERPRISES (companies). It's about what YOU should wear, not about corporate dress codes❌ TRAP — confuses the addressee. The text speaks TO candidates, not TO companies
C) Dresse un portrait des salariés selon leur place dans l'entrepriseThe text doesn't DESCRIBE how employees currently dress — it RECOMMENDS how candidates SHOULD dress. Description ≠ Recommendation❌ TRAP — confuses description (what IS) with advice (what SHOULD BE)
D) Informe sur les codes vestimentaires à adopter face aux recruteurs"Codes vestimentaires" (dress codes) + "à adopter" (to adopt) + "face aux recruteurs" (when facing recruiters) = dress code advice for job interviews. Perfect match✅ CORRECT

✅ Answer: D

The "Generalization" Trap — A New Trap Type

Option A illustrates a trap you haven't seen before: taking sector-specific advice and presenting it as universal advice.

TEXT SAYS:
  Banking      → wear conservative colors
  Advertising  → wear bold colors
  Social work  → look approachable

OPTION A SAYS:
  "Incite les employés à porter des habits colorés"
  = Encourages employees to wear colorful clothes (for ALL sectors)

This is TRUE for advertising.
This is FALSE for banking.
This is IRRELEVANT for social work.

Because it's not true for ALL sectors, it CANNOT be the article's general message.

🔑 Rule for advice texts: If the text gives DIFFERENT advice for DIFFERENT situations, the correct answer about the "overall message" will be the one that captures the GENERAL PRINCIPLE, not any single specific case.

QUESTION 18 — What is the main recommendation?

The question: Selon cet article, le candidat à un emploi doit...

This question asks for the CENTRAL piece of advice — the one recommendation that encompasses ALL the specific cases.

Step 1: Identify the overarching principle.

The text gives three sector-specific recommendations:

Banking      → conservative
Advertising  → creative
Social work  → approachable

What do these three have in COMMON? They all say the same thing at a higher level:

Your clothing should MATCH the sector you're targeting.

The text states this explicitly:

"Côté couleur, cela dépend du secteur économique."

This sentence IS the general principle. Everything after it is illustration.

Step 2: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) Adapter sa tenue au secteur professionnel qu'il cible"Adapter" (adapt) + "au secteur professionnel" (to the professional sector) + "qu'il cible" (that he targets) = adjust your outfit to your target sector. This is EXACTLY "cela dépend du secteur économique" rephrased✅ CORRECT
B) S'habiller avec des couleurs vives pour être remarquéOnly true for advertising/press. For banking, the opposite is recommended. This extracts ONE case and makes it universal❌ TRAP — same generalization trap as Q17A
C) Porter des vêtements classiques pour être pris au sérieuxOnly true for banking. For advertising, you're told to "oser la couleur" (dare to use color). Again, one case made universal❌ TRAP — mirror of option B, but for a different sector
D) Opter pour un style correspondant à son expérience professionnelleThe text says dress for the job you WANT ("celui auquel vous aspirez"), NOT for your current experience. This CONTRADICTS the text❌ WRONG — directly opposes the text's opening advice

✅ Answer: A

The Symmetry of Traps B and C

Notice something elegant about how the exam constructs traps for advice texts:

OPTION B: "S'habiller avec des couleurs vives"    = advice for ADVERTISING only
OPTION C: "Porter des vêtements classiques"        = advice for BANKING only
OPTION A: "Adapter sa tenue au secteur"            = the UMBRELLA that covers BOTH

B and C are SPECIFIC CASES presented as universal rules.
A is the GENERAL PRINCIPLE that encompasses all cases.

The exam deliberately offers you two opposite specific cases (colorful vs. classic) as separate options, hoping you'll pick whichever sector you read last or remember best. The correct answer is always the one that UNIFIES all cases.

🔑 Rule: In advice texts with multiple scenarios, the correct answer for "what is the main recommendation?" is the GENERAL PRINCIPLE that covers all scenarios — never one specific scenario presented as the whole message.

QUESTION 19 — What additional advice does the article give?

The question: L'article recommande également au candidat...

The word "également" (also/additionally) is crucial. It tells you the question is NOT asking about the main advice (Q18 already covered that). It's asking about a SECONDARY, ADDITIONAL recommendation.

Step 1: Identify the "additional" advice.

Since the main body (§1) covers sector-specific dress codes, the ADDITIONAL advice must be in the closing paragraph (§2):

"Votre palette dépendra aussi de la position professionnelle que vous recherchez. Pour vous mettre en valeur, répétez les teintes qui vous flattent."

Key phrase: "les teintes qui vous flattent" = the shades/colors that FLATTER YOU (that suit you personally, that make you look good).

Step 2: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) De porter les couleurs qui lui vont bien"Les couleurs qui lui vont bien" = the colors that suit him/her well. This is a DIRECT paraphrase of "les teintes qui vous flattent" (the shades that flatter you)✅ CORRECT
B) De viser un secteur qui correspond à son styleThe text says to ADAPT your style to the sector, not to CHOOSE a sector based on your existing style. This reverses the cause-effect relationship❌ TRAP — inverts the text's logic (adapt TO sector, not choose sector FOR style)
C) De ne pas craindre les excès lors du recrutementThe text explicitly warns AGAINST excess: "sans exagérer afin de ne pas effrayer les recruteurs" (without overdoing it so as not to scare recruiters). This option says DON'T FEAR excess — the exact OPPOSITE❌ WRONG — contradicts the text's explicit warning
D) De prendre conseil auprès de ses futurs collèguesNo colleagues are mentioned anywhere. The text gives advice from an EXPERT to a READER, not from colleagues to a candidate❌ WRONG — invents a source of advice not in the text

✅ Answer: A

The "Cause-Effect Reversal" Trap

Option B introduces a trap type that will become increasingly dangerous in later chapters:

TEXT'S LOGIC:
  You have a TARGET SECTOR → You ADAPT your style to match it
  CAUSE: sector choice    → EFFECT: style adaptation

OPTION B'S LOGIC:
  You have a PERSONAL STYLE → You CHOOSE a sector that matches it
  CAUSE: personal style   → EFFECT: sector choice

The DIRECTION of causation is reversed.

The text says: "Adapt your clothes TO the sector" (sector drives clothing choice)
Option B says: "Choose a sector that matches your style" (style drives sector choice)

These sound similar but are LOGICALLY OPPOSITE.

🔑 Rule: Watch for answers that reverse the cause-effect relationship. If the text says "adapt X to Y," the wrong answer will say "choose Y based on X." The DIRECTION matters.

2. Identify the THREE LEVELS of advice

Every good advice text operates on three levels:

LevelWhat it isExample from textWhich question tests it
UNIVERSALApplies to everyone, always"La première impression est capitale"Often Q17
CONDITIONALApplies only IF a specific condition is met"Traditionnel comme la banque? Optez pour du marine"Often Q18
PERSONALApplies to your individual characteristics"Répétez les teintes qui vous flattent"Often Q19

The exam tests whether you can distinguish these three levels. Mixing them up is the #1 error on advice texts.

This text is rich with imperative forms — direct commands to the reader:

ImperativeMeaningLevel

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Last Sector Read" Bias

What happens: Your brain retains the LAST specific case most vividly. When the exam asks about the "overall message," you select the option matching the last case you read.

Example: The text's last specific case is social work ("un abord avenant"). If the exam offered "look approachable" as an option for Q18, many candidates would select it — not because it's the general message, but because it's the most RECENT information in their short-term memory.

How to avoid it: After reading an advice text with multiple cases, DELIBERATELY ask yourself:

"What do ALL the cases have in common?"

Banking: match the sector → conservative
Advertising: match the sector → creative  
Social work: match the sector → approachable

COMMON THREAD: Match the sector.

The common thread is always the correct answer for "main recommendation" questions.

Pitfall 2: The "Opposite Advice" Confusion

What happens: The text gives OPPOSITE advice for different sectors. You remember one piece of advice and forget that the opposite was also given.

Example:

For BANKING:      "Optez pour du marine, du beige"     → conservative, muted
For ADVERTISING:  "Osez la couleur !"                   → bold, colorful

These are OPPOSITES. Both are correct — for their respective sectors.

The exam exploits this by offering:

  • Option B: "S'habiller avec des couleurs vives" (the advertising advice)
  • Option C: "Porter des vêtements classiques" (the banking advice)

If you remember only ONE, you'll pick it. But since BOTH are in the text, NEITHER can be the universal message. The correct answer must be the principle that GENERATES both: "adapt to the sector."

🔑 Rule: When a text gives opposite advice for different situations, neither opposite is the main message. The main message is the META-PRINCIPLE that explains WHY the advice differs.

Pitfall 3: The "Aspirez vs. Occupez" Reversal

What happens: You miss a critical distinction between two verbs that define the text's philosophy.

Key sentence:

"Ne vous habillez pas pour le poste que vous occupez, mais pour celui auquel vous aspirez."

VerbMeaningTime reference
occupezcurrently hold, currently occupyPRESENT (your current job)
aspirezaspire to, aim for, dream ofFUTURE (your desired job)

The text says: Dress for the FUTURE, not the PRESENT.

Option D of Q18 says: "Opter pour un style correspondant à son expérience professionnelle."

"Expérience professionnelle" = professional experience = your PAST and PRESENT career.

This directly CONTRADICTS the text's advice to dress for what you ASPIRE to (future), not what you've experienced (past/present).

TEXT:     Dress for ASPIRATION (future)  ✅
OPTION D: Dress for EXPERIENCE (past)   ❌

How to avoid it: When you see paired verbs with "ne...pas...mais" (not X but Y), the text is making a CONTRAST. The correct answer aligns with Y (what you SHOULD do), not X (what you SHOULD NOT do).

Pitfall 4: The "Sans Exagérer" Blindness

What happens: You focus on the positive advice ("Osez la couleur !") and miss the WARNING that follows it.

Full sentence:

"Osez la couleur ! Laissez transparaître votre créativité, sans exagérer afin de ne pas effrayer les recruteurs."

ADVICE:    Dare to use color + Show creativity
WARNING:   WITHOUT overdoing it + so as NOT to scare recruiters

The advice comes with a BUILT-IN LIMIT.

Option C of Q19 says: "De ne pas craindre les excès" = Don't fear excess.

But the text EXPLICITLY says to AVOID excess ("sans exagérer"). Option C tells you to EMBRACE what the text tells you to AVOID.

How to avoid it: Whenever advice is followed by "sans," "mais," "toutefois," "cependant," or "afin de ne pas," there is a LIMIT or WARNING. Read the full sentence, not just the encouraging first half.

🔑 Rule: French advice texts almost always include a "brake" after an "accelerator." "Osez" (accelerator) + "sans exagérer" (brake). The exam will test whether you read BOTH or only the exciting part.
