Chapter 1: "Bière Verte" — Decoding a Product Description

(Texte 31 — Phase 1: Very Easy — Foundation Thinking)

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

À consommer avec modération « bière Verte »

Après la bière blanche, la bière verte ! À base de malt et d'arôme naturel de citron vert, cette boisson à déguster bien fraiche est brassée comme une bière classique sans toutefois en avoir le goût, bien présentée dans une bouteille incolore à l'étiquette discrète et disponible dans un emballage cartonné multicolore, elle est vendue à un prix raisonnable.

À qui va-t-elle plaire avec sa faible teneur en alcool et son petit goût acidulé ? Certainement pas aux vrais amoureux de la bière ; la cible marketing semble privilégier les amateurs de sorties nocturnes dans les endroits branchés, les boites ou clubs privés. D'ailleurs, un jeune créateur de mode très en vue a été choisi comme ambassadeur de cette nouveauté et il va lancer, pour promouvoir le produit, une collection de T-shirt en série limitée.

Cela suffira-t-il à créer l'envie ?

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

This is your very first Compréhension Écrite exercise. Before we solve anything, let's establish the foundational method you will use for EVERY text from now until the final chapter.

THE GOLDEN RULE: Read Questions FIRST, Text SECOND

This sounds counterintuitive. Your instinct says: "Read the text, understand it, then answer." That instinct is wrong for exam conditions. Here's why:

  • When you read the text first without knowing the questions, your brain tries to memorize everything
  • When you then read the questions, you realize you forgot the specific details that matter
  • You re-read the text — wasting precious time
  • Under pressure, you start second-guessing yourself

Instead, do this:

STEP 1: Read ALL questions (not the options yet — just the question stems)
STEP 2: Read the text with those questions as your "radar"
STEP 3: Go back to each question, read ALL four options
STEP 4: Find the answer by matching text evidence to options
STEP 5: Verify by eliminating the three wrong answers

WHY THIS TEXT IS PERFECT FOR STARTING

This article is:

  • Short (one paragraph of description + one of marketing analysis + one closing question)
  • Descriptive (it describes a product — no hidden arguments or irony yet)
  • Concrete (colors, tastes, packaging — tangible things you can visualize)
  • Only 3 questions (less decision fatigue)

The text type is a product review / product description. In Compréhension Écrite, you will encounter several text types:

The green is in the flavor ingredient — citron vert (lime). The product is called "bière verte" because it's flavored with citron vert. The "vert" refers to the flavor/content, not the packaging.

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 14

"Cette nouvelle boisson est..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) un produit sans alcoolText says "faible teneur en alcool" — LOW alcohol, not ZERO. "Sans" means "without." This is factually wrong.❌ ELIMINATED
B) une bière au goût de citronText says "arôme naturel de citron vert" and it's brewed like beer. It IS a beer with lime taste.✅ CORRECT
C) une nouvelle bière classiqueText explicitly says "brassée comme une bière classique sans toutefois en avoir le goût" — it's brewed LIKE a classic beer but is NOT one. The word "sans" negates it.❌ ELIMINATED
D) un produit gastronomique de luxeText says "vendue à un prix raisonnable" — reasonable price contradicts "luxury."❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: B

The critical reading skill here: Option C is the most dangerous trap. The text says it's brewed "comme" (like) a classic beer. Students who skim see "bière classique" and pick C. But the words "sans toutefois en avoir le goût" (without having its taste) completely disqualify this option. "Comme" means "similar to" — NOT "identical to."

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 15

"Dans cette boisson, le vert est la couleur..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) du contenu"Contenu" = the content, what's inside. The beer is made with citron vert (lime). The "vert" in the product name comes from the lime ingredient, which IS the content.✅ CORRECT
B) de l'étiquetteText says "étiquette discrète" — discreet, no color specified. Nothing says the label is green.❌ ELIMINATED
C) de la bouteilleText says "bouteille incolore" — colorless. Explicitly NOT green.❌ ELIMINATED
D) de l'emballageText says "emballage cartonné multicolore" — multicolored, not specifically green.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: A

The critical reading skill here: This question tests whether you understand that "bière verte" doesn't mean the beer is green in color or the bottle is green. The "vert" comes from citron vert (lime). The name describes the flavor ingredient, which is the content. Options B, C, D are eliminated by EXPLICIT textual evidence (incolore, discrète, multicolore).

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 16

"Cette bière est en priorité destinée..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) aux créateurs de modeA fashion designer is the ambassador (spokesperson), not the target customer. Being a spokesperson ≠ being the audience.❌ ELIMINATED
B) au personnel des ambassadesThe word "ambassadeur" in the text means brand ambassador, NOT embassy staff. This is a vocabulary trap exploiting double meaning.❌ ELIMINATED
C) à un public jeune et à la modeText says: nightlife, trendy places, clubs, young fashion designer as ambassador. All signals point to young, fashionable people.✅ CORRECT
D) aux amateurs de bonnes bièresText EXPLICITLY says "Certainement pas aux vrais amoureux de la bière" — certainly NOT beer lovers.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: C

The critical reading skill here: Option B is a devious trap. The word "ambassadeur" in French has two meanings: (1) diplomatic ambassador, (2) brand representative/spokesperson. The text uses meaning #2. Option A confuses the spokesperson WITH the audience. Option D is contradicted by an explicit negation in the text.

✏️ WHAT COULD BE DONE

Beyond the basic method, here are additional strategies that work for product description texts:

Strategy: Build a Mental Product Sheet

As you read, mentally fill out this template:

PRODUCT: Bière verte
INGREDIENTS: Malt + citron vert (lime)
TASTE: Acidulé (tangy), NOT like classic beer
ALCOHOL: Low (faible teneur)
PACKAGING: Colorless bottle, discreet label, multicolored cardboard box
PRICE: Reasonable
TARGET: Young, trendy, nightlife crowd
PROMOTION: Young fashion designer as brand ambassador, limited T-shirt collection

When you have this mental sheet, EVERY question becomes a simple lookup.

Strategy: Watch for "Signal Negations"

This text contains TWO critical negations that determine correct answers:

  1. "sans toutefois en avoir le goût" → eliminates option C in Q14
  2. "Certainement pas aux vrais amoureux" → eliminates option D in Q16

In French Compréhension Écrite, negations are answer-changers. Always underline: ne...pas, sans, jamais, certainement pas, aucun, ni...ni.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Almost Synonym" Trap

What happens: An option uses a word that LOOKS like it matches the text, but with a crucial difference.

Example: Text says "faible teneur en alcool" → Option A says "sans alcool"

"Faible" (low/weak) ≠ "sans" (without). In everyday French, people sometimes say "c'est presque sans alcool" for low-alcohol drinks. But in an exam, "almost" doesn't count. The precision of language matters absolutely.

How to avoid it: When you see a match between text and option, ask: "Is this an EXACT match or an APPROXIMATE match?" Only exact matches count.

Pitfall #2: The "Word Hijack" Trap

What happens: An option steals a word from the text and uses it in a completely different context.

Example: Text uses "ambassadeur" (brand ambassador) → Option B offers "personnel des ambassades" (embassy staff)

The exam designers KNOW you'll recognize the word. They're testing whether you understand meaning in context or just word recognition.

How to avoid it: Never match words — match MEANINGS. Ask: "What does this word mean IN THIS SPECIFIC SENTENCE?"

Pitfall #3: The "Partial Truth" Trap

What happens: An option contains something that IS true but doesn't answer the specific question asked.

Example: It IS true that a fashion designer promotes the beer (Option A for Q16). But the question asks who the beer is "destinée à" (intended for) — the TARGET AUDIENCE, not the spokesperson.

How to avoid it: Re-read the question stem after selecting your answer. Ask: "Does my answer respond to EXACTLY what was asked?"

Pitfall #4: Ignoring the Closing Tone

What happens: Students miss the final sentence: "Cela suffira-t-il à créer l'envie ?"

This rhetorical question reveals the author's skepticism about the product's success. While no question directly asks about this, understanding the author's tone prepares you for more complex texts later where tone IS tested.

Why it matters for future chapters: Starting from Chapter 6, you will encounter questions like "L'auteur pense que..." or "Le ton de l'article est..." Noticing tone NOW builds the habit.

Bridge to Chapter 2:

In Chapter 1, the text told you what the product IS. The questions asked you to locate explicit features. But what if the question asks: "What is the PURPOSE of this text?" — something the text never explicitly states?

That's the leap from locating information to identifying communicative intent. In Chapter 2 ("Déjeuner sur l'herbe"), you'll face a text that could be interpreted as a recipe collection, a lifestyle guide, or a book promotion.
