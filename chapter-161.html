New skill: The Subject vs Sub-Topic Distinction

This is the MOST IMPORTANT concept in Project 5. Many students fail "subject" questions because they confuse the article's MAIN POINT with a SUPPORTING DETAIL.

WHAT'S THE DIFFERENCE?

MAIN SUBJECT: The ONE thing the entire article is about
              → Present in ALL paragraphs
              → The article WOULDN'T EXIST without it
              → The title reflects it

SUB-TOPIC:    A specific example or detail that SUPPORTS the main subject
              → Present in only ONE paragraph
              → The article COULD EXIST without this specific detail
              → It illustrates or proves the main point

Let me map this text:

MAIN SUBJECT: Cigarette companies' strategies to TARGET YOUNG PEOPLE
→ P1: young people are the "cible privilégiée" ✓
→ P2: tobacco brands appear in films (reaching young audiences) ✓
→ P3: flavored cigarettes attract "les plus jeunes" ✓
= Present in ALL three paragraphs

SUB-TOPIC 1: Cigarettes in cinema (P2 only)
→ One EXAMPLE of a marketing method
→ The article would still make sense without this specific example

SUB-TOPIC 2: Flavored cigarettes (P3 only)
→ One EXAMPLE of a marketing method
→ The article would still make sense without this specific example

SUB-TOPIC 3: Harvard study (P3 only)
→ One SOURCE cited for one example
→ The article would still make sense without this specific source

This distinction will save you in Question 14 — and in EVERY future "subject" question.

New skill: Reading institutional language

Paragraph 2 contains a quote from the INPES — a government health institute. Official bodies use careful, hedged language. They don't say "companies are breaking the law." They say:

"semble indiquer au mieux une méconnaissance de la loi, 
et au pire une action voulue de placement du produit"

This means:

"au mieux" (at best) → they DON'T KNOW the law
"au pire" (at worst) → they're DELIBERATELY placing products

The INPES is CAREFUL — they suggest two possibilities 
without directly accusing anyone

Understanding this hedged language is crucial for Q15.

QUESTION 15: L'INPES constate...

Layer targeted: INSTITUTIONAL OBSERVATION (Paragraph 2)

Critical word: "constate" = observes, notes, finds

The question asks: what does the INPES officially observe?

This means I must go to the INPES quote ONLY — not to other paragraphs. This is the Voice Identification skill from Project 3.

The INPES statement:

"le nombre de films français laissant apparaître une marque 
de tabac semble indiquer au mieux une méconnaissance de la 
loi, et au pire une action voulue de placement du produit"

Let me decode this sentence with surgical precision:

ELEMENT 1: "le nombre de films français"
→ The NUMBER of French films (implying: many)

ELEMENT 2: "laissant apparaître une marque de tabac"
→ that SHOW a tobacco brand (make a brand visible)

ELEMENT 3: "semble indiquer"
→ SEEMS to indicate (careful, hedged language)

ELEMENT 4: "au mieux une méconnaissance de la loi"
→ AT BEST: ignorance of the law

ELEMENT 5: "au pire une action voulue de placement du produit"
→ AT WORST: deliberate product placement

FULL MEANING: There are so many French films showing tobacco brands 
that it either means filmmakers don't know the law, or they're 
deliberately placing cigarette brands for money.

The KEY observation is: too many films show tobacco brands. That's the FINDING. The "au mieux / au pire" part is the INTERPRETATION.

ELIMINATE:

Choice A: "qu'il devrait y avoir plus de films sur les méfaits du tabac"

"devrait y avoir plus de films" = there should be MORE films
"sur les méfaits du tabac" = about the HARMFUL EFFECTS of tobacco

The INPES says nothing about wanting MORE films or about films ABOUT tobacco's harm. The INPES talks about tobacco brands APPEARING IN films — which is product placement, not health education.

❌ ELIMINATED. Complete invention. The INPES doesn't recommend anything about film production. It OBSERVES a phenomenon (brands in films). And the issue is brands being VISIBLE, not films being educational.

Choice C: "qu'il y a trop d'acteurs connus qui font de la publicité pour les cigarettes"

"acteurs connus" = famous actors
"font de la publicité" = do advertising/promotion

The text mentions films, not actors. The INPES observes that tobacco BRANDS appear in films — not that ACTORS promote cigarettes. There's a crucial difference:

TEXT: Tobacco BRANDS visible in films → product PLACEMENT
CHOICE C: Famous ACTORS doing cigarette ADVERTISING → celebrity endorsement

Product placement ≠ celebrity endorsement

❌ ELIMINATED. Subject Swap trap (Project 1). The text is about BRANDS in films, not ACTORS promoting cigarettes. The answer swaps the subject from brand visibility to actor involvement.

Choice D: "qu'il devrait y avoir davantage d'information au sujet de la loi anti-tabac"

"devrait y avoir davantage d'information" = there should be more information
"au sujet de la loi anti-tabac" = about the anti-tobacco law

The INPES mentions "méconnaissance de la loi" (ignorance of the law) as ONE possible explanation. But the INPES doesn't RECOMMEND more information about the law. It simply OBSERVES that either filmmakers don't know the law OR they're deliberately breaking it.

❌ ELIMINATED. This is a Inference Overreach trap — you take one element of the text ("méconnaissance de la loi") and extrapolate a RECOMMENDATION that the text never makes. The INPES observes; it doesn't prescribe action here.

🆕 NEW TRAP TYPE: Inference Overreach

The answer draws a conclusion or recommendation that GOES BEYOND what the text actually states. The text provides an observation, but the answer adds an action step or solution that isn't mentioned. Stick to what the text SAYS, not what you think should FOLLOW.

Choice B: "qu'il y a trop de films où l'on peut apercevoir des marques de cigarettes"

TEXT: "le nombre de films français laissant apparaître 
une marque de tabac"

CHOICE: "il y a trop de films où l'on peut apercevoir 
des marques de cigarettes"

PARAPHRASE MAP:
"le nombre de films"     →  "trop de films" (the number → too many)
"laissant apparaître"    →  "où l'on peut apercevoir" (showing → where one can see)

The word "trop" (too many) captures the IMPLICATION of the INPES statement. When the INPES says the number of films "seems to indicate either ignorance or deliberate action," they're implying the number is EXCESSIVE — i.e., too many.

✅ CORRECT. This answer faithfully paraphrases the INPES's observation: there are too many French films showing tobacco brands. It captures the FINDING without overreaching into recommendations.

Choice A: "vend des cigarettes avec des parfums agréables"

TEXT: "attirer les plus jeunes en leur offrant des SAVEURS PLAISANTES"
     "cigarettes à la cerise [...] au chocolat ou à la vanille"

Here's the critical distinction:

CHOICE A: "vend des cigarettes AVEC des parfums agréables"

Strategy 2: The Institutional Language Decoder (NEW — for texts with official statements)

Official bodies (INPES, ministries, WHO, etc.) use careful, hedged language. Learn to decode it:

INSTITUTIONAL LANGUAGE PATTERNS:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"semble indiquer"     = seems to indicate (cautious)
"au mieux... au pire" = at best... at worst (range of possibilities)
"méconnaissance"       = ignorance/unfamiliarity (softer than "violation")
"action voulue"        = deliberate action (accusation, but softened)

WHAT THEY SAY         vs    WHAT THEY MEAN
──────────────────────────────────────────────
"semble indiquer"     →    "we're pretty sure, but can't prove it"
"au mieux"            →    "the most innocent explanation"
"au pire"             →    "what we really suspect"
"méconnaissance"      →    "they might not know the law (but probably do)"

RULE: Read what they OBSERVE (the finding), 
not what they IMPLY (the judgment)

✅ STEP 5: WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

Rules for "Subject" and Investigative Texts

1. For "subject" questions, ALWAYS apply the coverage test

Can you trace the proposed subject through ALL paragraphs? If it only appears in one, it's a sub-topic.

APPLIED TO THIS TEXT:
"Cigarettes in cinema" → P1 and P2 only → SUB-TOPIC ✗
"Youth tobacco consumption" → wrong angle → ✗
"Harvard study" → P3 only, just a source → SUB-TOPIC ✗
"Strategies to target youth" → P1 + P2 + P3 → MAIN SUBJECT ✓

2. Distinguish between OBSERVATION and RECOMMENDATION

When a question asks what an institution "constate" (observes/notes):

  • Look for what they FIND or SEE
  • NOT for what they RECOMMEND or SUGGEST
  • Observations are facts; recommendations are actions
  • The INPES observes too many brands in films — it doesn't recommend making more health films

3. Read the FULL sentence around a keyword

"Parfums" in Choice A means flavors (characteristic). "Parfums" in Choice C means perfume products (separate items). The SAME WORD changes meaning based on its sentence context. Never evaluate a word in isolation.

4. Identify the ACTIVE AGENT in the text

THIS TEXT'S ACTIVE AGENT: Cigarette companies ("cigarettiers")
→ THEY target youth (P1)
→ THEY place products in films (P2)  
→ THEY create flavored cigarettes (P3)

The text is about what COMPANIES DO.
Not about what YOUTH EXPERIENCE.
Not about what INSTITUTIONS RECOMMEND.

5. Decode idioms and cultural references

"font un malheur" = are a huge hit/success (idiom)
NOT "cause a disaster" (literal meaning would be misleading)

"salles obscures" = cinemas/movie theaters (idiomatic)
NOT "dark rooms" (literal translation)

"Jeune fumeur deviendra grand" = ironic twist on a proverb
= "A young smoker will become a big one"

❌ STEP 6: WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

❌ DON'T confuse a SOURCE with the SUBJECT

Harvard is CITED as a source. The article is NOT ABOUT Harvard's study. A newspaper quoting a scientist doesn't make the article about that scientist's research.

CITATION ≠ SUBJECT
"selon le magazine Health Affairs" → source citation
"cet article a pour sujet" → asks about the ARTICLE's topic, 
not about one cited source

❌ DON'T confuse the ANGLE with the TOPIC

Youth and cigarettes are the TOPIC. But the ANGLE matters:

  • "Strategies companies use" = correct angle (companies as agents)
  • "Drama of youth consumption" = wrong angle (youth as victims)

The text describes WHAT COMPANIES DO, not what happens TO youth.

❌ DON'T assume institutions make RECOMMENDATIONS when they only make OBSERVATIONS

INPES OBSERVES: "le nombre de films laissant apparaître une marque..."
INPES DOES NOT SAY: "il faudrait plus de films éducatifs"
                     "il faudrait mieux informer sur la loi"

Observation ≠ recommendation. Don't add action steps the text doesn't include.

❌ DON'T let similar-looking words trick you across choices

"Parfums" appears in BOTH Choice A and Choice C. But it means different things in each:

  • Choice A: flavors AS A FEATURE of cigarettes → correct
  • Choice C: perfume AS A PRODUCT given with cigarettes → wrong

Read the FULL choice, not just the keyword.

❌ DON'T mix information from different paragraphs to build an answer

Choice D combines "présentation des paquets" (P1) with "aujourd'hui" (P3). But the question asks about today's LATEST strategy, which is flavors (P3), not packaging (P1).

The Subject Question Error Pattern — The Most Common Mistake

STUDENTS TYPICALLY CHOOSE SUB-TOPICS OVER MAIN SUBJECTS BECAUSE:

1. Sub-topics are MORE SPECIFIC → they feel "more precise"
2. Sub-topics are MORE VIVID → cinema, Harvard, manga are memorable
3. Sub-topics use EXACT TEXT WORDS → they feel "more textual"

BUT:
4. The MAIN subject is MORE GENERAL → it covers ALL paragraphs
5. The MAIN subject uses PARAPHRASE → it doesn't quote the text
6. The MAIN subject captures the TEXT'S PURPOSE → not just its details

REMEMBER: For "subject" questions, BROADER + ALL-COVERING 
beats NARROWER + ONE-PARAGRAPH.
