Le mal des transports

Le mal des transports est une affection courante. — (Phrase 36) — On considère qu'une personne sur trois en souffrira au moins une fois dans sa vie. Les femmes et les enfants sont plus souvent atteints que les hommes, les nouveau-nés et les personnes âgées.

— (Phrase 38) — Suivent alors le mal de l'air, puis le mal de voiture (chez les enfants) ou de train. On peut en souffrir lorsque l'on est à dos de chameau ou d'éléphant, mais rarement à cheval.

— (Phrase 39) — : nos amies les bêtes, notamment les chevaux, vaches, chiens et chats y sont également sensibles.

— (Phrase 40) — : on peut désormais le traiter en quelques séances de rééducation vestibulaire.

The Core Challenge: MEDICAL PROGRESSION LOGIC

Medical texts follow a specific logical ORDER that you must understand:

GENERAL → SPECIFIC → COMPARATIVE → HOPEFUL

1. How common is it? (prevalence)
2. What are the symptoms? (mild → severe)
3. What types exist? (classification)
4. Who/what else is affected? (comparison)
5. Can it be treated? (prognosis/hope)

Each gap sits at a specific point in this progression. The B.G.A. method (Chapter 9) still applies, but now you also need to ask: "Where am I in the medical progression?"

The New Skill: SYMPTOM-SEVERITY ESCALATION

Section 2 (Symptoms) follows a SEVERITY SCALE — from mild to severe:

MILD:        Blêmir (turning pale) → somnoler (becoming drowsy)
MODERATE:    Nausées (nausea) → vomissements (vomiting)
SEVERE:      Froid (cold) → salivation → pupil dilation
EXTREME:     Semi-syncope → perte de connaissance (losing consciousness)

The text uses ESCALATION MARKERS to signal increasing severity:

MarkerMeaningPosition
"On commence souvent par..."One often starts by...BEGINNING (mild)
"Ensuite viennent..."Then come...MIDDLE (moderate)
"On peut même..."One can even...ESCALATION (severe)
"certains vont jusqu'à..."some go as far as...EXTREME

These markers are like progression markers in Chapter 5 ("d'abord," "puis," "maintenant"), but applied to medical severity instead of project development.

The New Skill: CLASSIFICATION LOGIC

Section 3 introduces different TYPES of motion sickness, ordered by FREQUENCY:

MOST COMMON → LESS COMMON → RARE

"L'affection la plus déclarée" → most reported = most common
"Suivent alors..." → then follow = less common
"On peut en souffrir..." → one can suffer from it = possible but rarer
"mais rarement..." → but rarely = rare

The text RANKS the types from most to least common. The gap must introduce this ranking.

The New Skill: SPECIES BRIDGE

Section 4 shifts from HUMANS to ANIMALS. This requires a BRIDGE sentence that connects the two:

BEFORE gap 39: Discussion of motion sickness in HUMANS (on horseback, camel, elephant)
GAP 39:        ??? (must bridge to ANIMALS)
AFTER gap 39:  "nos amies les bêtes... chevaux, vaches, chiens et chats 
                y sont ÉGALEMENT sensibles"

The word "également" (also/too) in the AFTER is critical — it means animals are ALSO affected, implying humans are NOT the only ones. The gap must set up this "also."

Step 2: Identify the gap's function.

The BEFORE says it's "courante" (common). The AFTER gives SPECIFIC DATA: 1 in 3 people, with demographic breakdowns. The gap must BRIDGE from "common" to specific prevalence data.

What type of sentence fits between "it's common" and "1 in 3 people suffer"?

The gap should REINFORCE or EXPAND on the "common" characterization. It should be CONSISTENT with "common" — not contradict it.

BEFORE: "Affection courante" = COMMON
GAP:    Must be CONSISTENT with "common"
AFTER:  1 in 3 = CONFIRMS it's common (33% of population)

Step 3: Check each option.

"It affects a tiny/minuscule portion of the population."

BEFORE: "affection COURANTE" (common)

"Infime" contradicts BOTH "courante" (common) AND "1 in 3" (33%).
33% of the population is NOT "infime."

GAP B:   "chronique" = repeated, ongoing condition
AFTER:   "au moins une fois" = at least once (could be just once)

"Chronique" implies REPEATED suffering.
"Au moins une fois" allows for SINGLE occurrence.
These aren't directly contradictory (chronic is compatible with "at least once"),
but "chronique" overstates the persistence implied by "at least once."

This is CLOSE but slightly misaligned. "Chronique" suggests the condition PERSISTS, while "at least once" suggests it can be a one-time experience. Let me check other options.

Potentially problematic — hold for comparison.

GAP D:   "On ignore l'importance des populations atteintes" (we don't know)

Back to Option B:

"Affection courante" (common) → GAP → "1 in 3 people" (33%)

The GAP must be CONSISTENT with BOTH:
  BEFORE: "common" → gap can't say "rare" or "tiny portion"
  AFTER: "1 in 3" → gap can't say "we don't know how many"

CONSISTENCY CHECK:
  A: "infime" (tiny) → contradicts "courante" AND "1 in 3" ❌

  C: "rare" → contradicts "courante" ❌

RULE: In medical texts, prevalence descriptors must form a

GAP 37: Before "Ensuite viennent les nausées et les vomissements..."

Step 1: Apply B.G.A.

BEFORE (end of Section 1):

"Les femmes et les enfants sont plus souvent atteints que les hommes, les nouveau-nés et les personnes âgées."

Demographic breakdown of who's affected.

AFTER:

"Ensuite viennent les nausées et les vomissements. On a froid, il arrive que l'on salive beaucoup et que la pupille se dilate. On peut même se trouver en état de semi-syncope : certains vont jusqu'à perdre connaissance brutalement."

Step 2: Analyze the CRITICAL connector.

"Ensuite" (then/next) — this is a SEQUENCE marker. It signals that what follows comes AFTER something. "Ensuite viennent les nausées" = "THEN come nausea and vomiting."

If nausea comes "ensuite" (next), then the GAP must describe what comes FIRST — the INITIAL symptoms that PRECEDE nausea.

GAP:    [FIRST symptoms]
"Ensuite": [NEXT symptoms = nausea, vomiting]
"Même":   [EVEN = semi-syncope]
"Jusqu'à": [AS FAR AS = losing consciousness]

SEVERITY PROGRESSION:
  GAP (mild) → "ensuite" (moderate) → "même" (severe) → "jusqu'à" (extreme)

The gap must describe the MILDEST symptoms — the first signs that appear before nausea.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "Chaque année arrivent de nouvelles victimes."

"Every year new victims arrive."

This is about INCIDENCE (how many new cases per year), not about SYMPTOMS. The section is about what HAPPENS TO YOU (symptoms), not how many people are affected (prevalence — that was Section 1).

Also, "ensuite viennent les nausées" would make no sense after "new victims arrive every year" — "ensuite" needs a preceding SYMPTOM to create a symptom sequence.

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong section (prevalence, not symptoms); "ensuite" needs a preceding symptom

This is a SECTION MISPLACEMENT TRAP — a sentence that belongs to one section (prevalence) is offered for a gap in another section (symptoms). In Chapter 8's WHAT-HOW-WHY framework, this would be like putting a WHY sentence in the HOW section.

Option B — "Les symptômes sont multiples et assez mal définis."

"The symptoms are multiple and rather poorly defined."

"Assez mal définis" (rather poorly defined) — but the AFTER describes symptoms with GREAT PRECISION: nausea, vomiting, cold, salivation, pupil dilation, semi-syncope, loss of consciousness. These are NOT "poorly defined" — they're VERY specifically described.

GAP B:   "symptômes assez mal définis" (poorly defined)
AFTER:   Precise list: nausea, vomiting, cold, salivation, pupil dilation...
→ "Poorly defined" → immediately followed by very precise definitions
→ CONTRADICTION

❌ ELIMINATED — "Mal définis" contradicted by the highly specific symptom list that follows

Option C — "Les personnes fragiles sont souvent les plus atteintes."

"Fragile people are often the most affected."

This is about WHO is affected (demographics) — but Section 1 already covered this: "Les femmes et les enfants sont plus souvent atteints." Adding another demographic statement would be REDUNDANT and would delay the symptom section.

Also, "ensuite viennent les nausées" needs a SYMPTOM before it, not a demographic statement. "Ensuite" creates a SEQUENCE within symptoms, not between demographics and symptoms.

❌ ELIMINATED — Redundant with Section 1's demographics; "ensuite" needs a symptom predecessor, not a demographic statement

Option D — "On commence souvent par blêmir puis par somnoler."

"One often starts by turning pale then by becoming drowsy."

Let's test the flow:

BEFORE: [Demographics: who's affected]
GAP D:  "On commence souvent par blêmir puis par somnoler."
AFTER:  "Ensuite viennent les nausées et les vomissements..."

  • "On commence souvent par..." (one often starts by...) — introduces the FIRST symptoms. "Commence" (starts) marks the BEGINNING of the severity scale. ✓

  • "Blêmir" (turning pale) — a MILD symptom. ✓

  • "Puis somnoler" (then becoming drowsy) — a second mild symptom, slightly more advanced. ✓

  • "Ensuite" in AFTER — "THEN come nausea and vomiting." The sequence is:

    1. First: turning pale, drowsiness (GAP) = MILD
    2. Then: nausea, vomiting (AFTER) = MODERATE
    3. Even: semi-syncope (AFTER) = SEVERE
    4. As far as: losing consciousness (AFTER) = EXTREME

The severity escalation is PERFECT:

MILD          MODERATE           SEVERE              EXTREME
pale, drowsy → nausea, vomiting → cold, salivation → loss of consciousness
    ↑               ↑                  ↑                    ↑
  "commence"     "ensuite"           "même"             "jusqu'à"
    (start)       (then)             (even)           (as far as)

✅ CORRECT — "Commence par" introduces the mildest symptoms; "ensuite" naturally follows with more severe ones; perfect severity escalation

✅ ANSWER: D

🔬 THE SEVERITY ESCALATION LESSON:

MEDICAL SYMPTOM TEXTS follow a SEVERITY PROGRESSION:

MARKER WORDS and their POSITION on the scale:
  "On COMMENCE par..."     = BEGINNING (mildest symptoms)
  "ENSUITE viennent..."    = NEXT (moderate symptoms)
  "On peut MÊME..."        = ESCALATION (severe symptoms)
  "Certains vont JUSQU'À..." = EXTREME (worst case)

The GAP before "ensuite" MUST contain the FIRST/MILDEST symptoms.
The GAP before "même" MUST contain something less severe than what follows.

RULE: In severity escalation texts, identify WHERE on the scale 
      the gap sits, then ensure the inserted sentence matches 
      that severity level.

      If the AFTER starts with "ensuite" → gap = MILD (beginning)
      If the AFTER starts with "même" → gap = MODERATE (middle)
      If the AFTER starts with "pourtant" → gap = OPPOSITE direction

GAP 38: Before "Suivent alors le mal de l'air, puis le mal de voiture..."

Step 1: Apply B.G.A.

BEFORE (end of Section 2):

"...certains vont jusqu'à perdre connaissance brutalement."

End of the symptom description — the most extreme symptom (loss of consciousness).

AFTER:

"Suivent alors le mal de l'air, puis le mal de voiture (chez les enfants) ou de train. On peut en souffrir lorsque l'on est à dos de chameau ou d'éléphant, mais rarement à cheval."

"Then follow airsickness, then car sickness (in children) or train sickness."

Step 2: Analyze the AFTER carefully.

"Suivent alors" (then follow) — this is a RANKING marker. It means: after the FIRST/MOST COMMON type, here come the OTHERS.

The AFTER lists types in DECREASING frequency:

  1. Air sickness
  2. Car sickness (children) / train sickness
  3. Camel/elephant (possible)
  4. Horse (rarely)

If these "follow" something, the gap must introduce the MOST COMMON type — the one that comes FIRST in the ranking.

What's the most common type of motion sickness? Seasickness (mal de mer) — the oldest and most famous form.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "La fréquence des malaises s'accroît."

"The frequency of episodes is increasing."

This is about TRENDS (increasing frequency), not about CLASSIFICATION (types of motion sickness). It doesn't introduce a specific type that the ranking in AFTER would follow.

Also, "suivent alors le mal de l'air" needs a FIRST type before it. "Frequency is increasing" doesn't provide one.

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong function (trend, not classification); doesn't introduce a first type for "suivent alors"

Option B — "Cet état ne dure que quelques minutes."

"This state only lasts a few minutes."

This is about DURATION of symptoms — it belongs in Section 2 (symptoms), not Section 3 (classification). The text has moved PAST symptoms to types.

Also, "suivent alors le mal de l'air" makes no sense after "it only lasts a few minutes."

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong section (symptoms, not classification); doesn't bridge to type ranking

Option C — "L'affection la plus déclarée est le mal de mer."

"The most reported condition is seasickness."

Let's test the flow:

BEFORE: [End of symptoms: loss of consciousness]
GAP C:  "The most reported condition is seasickness."
AFTER:  "Then follow airsickness, car sickness, train sickness..."

  • "L'affection la plus déclarée" (the most reported) — introduces the RANKING by stating the #1 type. ✓

  • "Le mal de mer" (seasickness) — the most common form of motion sickness. ✓

  • "Suivent alors" (then follow) — naturally continues the ranking: after #1 (seasickness), here come #2, #3, etc. ✓

The classification flow is perfect:

#1 (most common): Seasickness (GAP) → "la plus déclarée"
#2: Airsickness → "suivent alors"
#3: Car sickness (children) / Train sickness
#4 (possible): Camel / elephant
#5 (rare): Horse → "rarement"

✅ CORRECT — Introduces the most common type (seasickness), which "suivent alors" naturally follows with less common types

CLASSIFICATION TEXTS rank items from most to least common.

RANKING MARKERS:
  "La plus déclarée" = THE MOST reported → #1 position
  "Suivent alors..."  = THEN FOLLOW... → #2, #3, etc.
  "On peut..."        = ONE CAN... → possible but less common
  "Rarement"          = RARELY → uncommon

"Suivent" (follow) implies something CAME FIRST.

GAP 39: Before "nos amies les bêtes... y sont ÉGALEMENT sensibles"

Step 1: Apply B.G.A.

BEFORE (end of Section 3):

"On peut en souffrir lorsque l'on est à dos de chameau ou d'éléphant, mais rarement à cheval."

Discussion of motion sickness on various animals (human perspective — humans riding animals).

AFTER:

": nos amies les bêtes, notamment les chevaux, vaches, chiens et chats y sont également sensibles."

"Our animal friends, notably horses, cows, dogs and cats are ALSO sensitive to it."

Step 2: Analyze the CRITICAL word.

"Également" (also/too) — this means animals are ALSO affected, IN ADDITION TO someone else. Who is the "someone else"? HUMANS.

For "also" to work, the gap must establish that HUMANS are affected, and then the AFTER adds that ANIMALS are too:

GAP:    [Humans are affected] / [It's not just humans]
AFTER:  "Animals are ALSO affected" (également)

The gap must BRIDGE from humans to animals by stating that humans aren't the ONLY ones affected.

Also note the COLON (:) after the gap — the colon signals that what follows SPECIFIES or EXPLAINS what the gap states. If the gap says "animals are also affected," the colon introduces WHICH animals.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "Sauf en cas d'allure non maîtrisée."

"Except in the case of uncontrolled gait."

This is a CONDITION about horseback riding (connecting to "rarement à cheval" before). It doesn't bridge to animals being affected. It stays in the HUMAN perspective (riding horses), not shifting to the ANIMAL perspective (animals themselves getting sick).

Also, "nos amies les bêtes... sont également sensibles" needs a statement about humans first. "Except in case of uncontrolled gait" doesn't establish humans as the reference point.

❌ ELIMINATED — Stays in human perspective; doesn't bridge to animal susceptibility; "également" has no reference point

Option B — "L'homme n'est pas le seul à en être victime."

"Humans are not the only ones to be victims of it."

Let's test the flow:

BEFORE: "...possible on camel/elephant, rarely on horseback."
GAP B:  "Humans are not the only ones to be victims of it."
AFTER:  ": our animal friends... are ALSO sensitive to it."

  • "L'homme n'est pas le seul" (humans are not the only ones) — establishes that OTHERS are affected too. ✓

  • "Également" in AFTER — "animals are ALSO sensitive" — "also" confirms the gap's statement that humans aren't alone. ✓

  • The COLON after the gap → what follows SPECIFIES who else is affected: animals. ✓

The bridge is perfect:

[Humans suffer from motion sickness] (established throughout text)
→ "Humans are NOT the only victims" (gap = BRIDGE statement)
→ : [Animals are ALSO sensitive] (colon introduces specification)

✅ CORRECT — "Not the only ones" sets up "also" (également); colon introduces specification; perfect human→animal bridge

Option C — "Ce triste privilège appartient seulement à l'homme."

"This sad privilege belongs ONLY to humans."

"Seulement à l'homme" (only to humans) — but the AFTER says animals are "également sensibles" (also sensitive). If the gap says "ONLY humans," the next sentence can't say "animals ALSO."

GAP C:   "ONLY humans" (exclusive)
AFTER:   "Animals ALSO" (inclusive)
→ "Only" ← contradicts → "also"
→ DIRECT LOGICAL CONTRADICTION

❌ ELIMINATED — "Seulement" (only) directly contradicts "également" (also) in the AFTER

This is a EXCLUSIVITY-INCLUSION CONTRADICTION — the gap claims exclusivity ("only humans") while the AFTER establishes inclusion ("animals also"). These are logically incompatible. This is the clearest possible contradiction — "only X" followed by "Y also."

Option D — "Les espèces animales en sont curieusement protégées."

"Animal species are curiously protected from it."

"Protégées" (protected) — but the AFTER says animals are "sensibles" (sensitive/susceptible). "Protected" and "sensitive" are OPPOSITES:

GAP D:   Animals are PROTECTED (not affected)
AFTER:   Animals are SENSITIVE (affected)
→ Protected ≠ sensitive → direct contradiction

❌ ELIMINATED — "Protégées" (protected) contradicts "sensibles" (sensitive) in the AFTER

✅ ANSWER: B

🔬 THE SPECIES BRIDGE LESSON:

BRIDGING FROM HUMANS TO ANIMALS:

STRUCTURE:
  [Human discussion] → GAP (bridge) → [Animal discussion]

The bridge sentence must:
  1. REFERENCE humans (the current subject)
  2. OPEN the door to other species
  3. Be CONSISTENT with "également" (also) in the AFTER

CORRECT BRIDGE: "Humans are NOT the ONLY ones"
  → Sets up "animals are ALSO affected" (également)
  → "Not the only" + "also" = logically consistent pair

WRONG BRIDGES:
  "Only humans" → contradicts "also" ❌
  "Animals are protected" → contradicts "sensitive" ❌
  "Except uncontrolled gait" → doesn't bridge to animals at all ❌

RULE: When "également" (also/too) appears in the AFTER,
      the gap must establish the FIRST subject that "also" adds to.
      "X is affected" → "Y is ALSO affected"
      NOT: "Only X is affected" → "Y is ALSO affected" (contradiction)

GAP 40: Before "on peut désormais le traiter en quelques séances..."

Step 1: Apply B.G.A.

BEFORE (end of Section 4):

"...nos amies les bêtes, notamment les chevaux, vaches, chiens et chats y sont également sensibles."

Animals are also affected by motion sickness.

AFTER:

": on peut désormais le traiter en quelques séances de rééducation vestibulaire."

"One can now treat it in a few vestibular rehabilitation sessions."

Step 2: Analyze the CRITICAL word.

"Désormais" (from now on / now) — signals a CHANGE. Something that wasn't possible before IS possible now. This implies the gap must reference the PAST state (when it couldn't be treated) or the TRANSITION from untreatable to treatable.

Also, the COLON (:) after the gap — the AFTER specifies HOW the condition can now be treated. The gap must make a GENERAL statement that the colon then details.

GAP:    [General statement: this is no longer inevitable/untreatable]
AFTER:  : [Specific: can be treated with vestibular rehabilitation]

"Désormais" = NOW (implies a change from the past)
→ Gap should reference the change: from fatalité to treatability

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "Mais ce mal n'est plus une fatalité."

"But this illness is no longer a fatality/inevitability."

Let's test the flow:

BEFORE: "Animals are also sensitive to it."
GAP A:  "But this illness is no longer a fatality."
AFTER:  ": one can now treat it in a few vestibular rehabilitation sessions."

  • "Mais" (but) — introduces a SHIFT from the problem (sections 1-4: how bad motion sickness is) to the SOLUTION (section 5: treatment). ✓

  • "N'est plus une fatalité" (is no longer inevitable) — establishes the CHANGE: it USED TO BE inevitable, NOW it's not. ✓

  • "Désormais" in AFTER — "now" confirms the change: NOW it can be treated. "N'est plus" (no longer) + "désormais" (from now on) = perfect temporal pairing. ✓

  • COLON — "is no longer inevitable : here's how it's treated" — the colon specifies HOW it's no longer inevitable. ✓

The flow is perfect:

[The condition is widespread and affects many species]
→ "BUT it's no longer inevitable" (shift from problem to solution)
→ : "One can NOW treat it with rehabilitation" (specification)

"Mais" creates the PIVOTAL TURN from the problem description (Sections 1-4) to the hopeful conclusion (Section 5). This is a classic medical article structure: describe the problem, then end with hope.

✅ CORRECT — "Mais" pivots from problem to solution; "n'est plus une fatalité" pairs perfectly with "désormais"; colon introduces specification

Option B — "Une fatalité à laquelle nul n'échappe."

"A fatality from which no one escapes."

"Fatalité" + "nul n'échappe" = it's inevitable, no one escapes. But the AFTER says "on peut désormais le traiter" — it CAN now be treated. If the gap says it's inescapable, the next sentence can't say it's treatable.

GAP B:   "No one escapes" (inevitable, hopeless)
AFTER:   "Can now be treated" (treatable, hopeful)
→ Inevitable ≠ treatable → logical contradiction
→ Also: no "mais" or "pourtant" to create a contrast

❌ ELIMINATED — "Nul n'échappe" (no one escapes) contradicts "on peut traiter" (can be treated); tone clash (hopeless → hopeful without transition)

Option C — "Ils n'ont pas accès aux nouveaux remèdes."

"They don't have access to new remedies."

"Ils" — who is "they"? The most recent subject is "les bêtes" (animals). So "they" = animals don't have access to remedies. But the AFTER says "on peut le traiter" — "on" = people/we, "le" = it (the condition). The AFTER is about HUMANS being treated, not animals.

Also, "don't have access to remedies" is NEGATIVE, while the AFTER is POSITIVE (can be treated). Without a contrast connector, this creates a tone clash.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Ils" likely refers to animals (wrong subject for human treatment); negative tone clashes with positive AFTER

Option D — "Ce malaise n'a pas fini de faire des dégâts."

"This malaise hasn't finished causing damage."

"N'a pas fini de faire des dégâts" = is still causing damage = ONGOING PROBLEM. But the AFTER says it CAN now be treated. The gap should introduce HOPE, not ongoing damage.

GAP D:   "Still causing damage" (ongoing problem, pessimistic)
AFTER:   "Can now be treated" (solution available, optimistic)
→ No transition word to bridge pessimism to optimism
→ The colon (:) can't bridge opposites — it specifies, not contrasts

❌ ELIMINATED — Pessimistic tone ("still causing damage") clashes with optimistic AFTER ("can be treated"); colon can't bridge opposites

✅ ANSWER: A

🔬 THE PROBLEM-TO-SOLUTION PIVOT LESSON:

MEDICAL ARTICLES typically end with a PIVOT from problem to solution.

STRUCTURE:
  Sections 1-4: THE PROBLEM (prevalence, symptoms, types, scope)
  Section 5:    THE SOLUTION (treatment, hope)

The GAP at this pivot must:
  1. Signal the SHIFT with a connector ("mais" = but)
  2. State the GENERAL change ("n'est plus une fatalité" = no longer inevitable)
  3. Allow the AFTER to SPECIFY the solution (colon → treatment details)

KEY PAIR: "N'est PLUS" (no longer) + "DÉSORMAIS" (from now on)
  → Both signal CHANGE from past to present
  → "N'est plus" in the gap = what's no longer true
  → "Désormais" in the AFTER = what's now true

WRONG: Statements that maintain the PROBLEM tone
  → "No one escapes" (still inevitable) ❌
  → "Still causing damage" (still a problem) ❌
  These can't bridge to "can now be treated" without contradiction.

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

New Strategy: The Medical Progression Map

For medical/scientific texte à trous, map the SECTION STRUCTURE before solving:

MEDICAL ARTICLE PROGRESSION:
  Section 1: PREVALENCE (how common?)
  Section 2: SYMPTOMS (what happens? mild → severe)
  Section 3: CLASSIFICATION (what types? most → least common)
  Section 4: SCOPE (who/what else is affected?)
  Section 5: PROGNOSIS/TREATMENT (can it be cured?)

Each gap belongs to ONE section.
The gap sentence must match that section's FUNCTION.
A prevalence sentence can't go in the symptoms section.
A treatment sentence can't go in the classification section.

New Strategy: The Escalation Marker Dictionary

For texts with severity or ranking progressions:

ESCALATION MARKERS (from mild to extreme):
  "On commence par..."      → FIRST (mildest)
  "Ensuite viennent..."     → NEXT (moderate)
  "On peut même..."         → EVEN (severe)
  "Certains vont jusqu'à..." → AS FAR AS (extreme)

RANKING MARKERS (from most to least common):
  "La plus déclarée..."     → #1 (most common)
  "Suivent alors..."        → #2, #3... (less common)
  "On peut..."              → POSSIBLE (uncommon)
  "Rarement..."             → RARE

TRANSITION MARKERS (section shifts):
  "Mais"                    → PROBLEM → SOLUTION pivot
  "Également"               → ADDING another category
  "Désormais"               → PAST → PRESENT change

New Strategy: The Colon-Bridge Check

When a gap is followed by a COLON (:):

COLON RULE: What follows the colon SPECIFIES or EXPLAINS the gap.

GAP: [General statement]
  :  [Specific details/evidence/examples]

The gap makes a CLAIM → the colon introduces PROOF or DETAIL.

EXAMPLES:
  "This is no longer inevitable" : [here's the treatment] ✓
  "Humans aren't the only ones"  : [here are the animals affected] ✓
  "Many advantages"              : [here's the list] (from Chapter 9) ✓

If the gap's claim doesn't MATCH the colon's specification, it's wrong.

New Strategy: The "Également/Aussi" Compatibility Check

When "également" or "aussi" (also) appears in the AFTER:

"ALSO" requires:
  1. A FIRST subject (established before or in the gap)
  2. A SECOND subject (in the AFTER)
  3. The gap OPENS the door from first to second

COMPATIBLE gap:    "X is not the only one" → "Y is ALSO affected" ✓
INCOMPATIBLE gap:  "Only X is affected"    → "Y is ALSO affected" ✗
INCOMPATIBLE gap:  "Y is protected"        → "Y is ALSO affected" ✗

RULE: "Également/aussi" in AFTER = the gap must be INCLUSIVE, 
      not EXCLUSIVE or contradictory.

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

DO #1: Map the medical progression before solving ANY gap

Identify which SECTION each gap belongs to. This immediately narrows your options:

Gap in PREVALENCE section → sentence about how common it is
Gap in SYMPTOMS section → sentence about what happens (with correct severity)
Gap in CLASSIFICATION section → sentence introducing a type ranking
Gap in SCOPE section → sentence bridging to other affected groups
Gap in TREATMENT section → sentence about hope/cure

DO #2: Use escalation markers as your PRIMARY navigation tool

"Ensuite" (then) → the gap before it must contain what comes FIRST.
"Même" (even) → the gap before it must contain something LESS severe.
"Suivent alors" (then follow) → the gap before it must contain the #1 item.

These markers tell you EXACTLY what the gap needs.

DO #3: Check colon compatibility

When a colon follows the gap, verify that the AFTER sentence SPECIFIES or DETAILS the gap's content. If the gap says "X is possible" and the colon introduces "here's how X works," that's consistent. If the gap says "X is impossible" and the colon introduces "here's how X works," that's contradictory.

DO #4: Test "également/aussi" compatibility

When "also" appears in the AFTER, the gap must be INCLUSIVE (opening to additional subjects), not EXCLUSIVE (claiming only one subject). "Not the only one" + "also" = ✓. "The only one" + "also" = ✗.

DO #5: Verify the PIVOT direction at the end of medical texts

Medical texts typically end with HOPE. The last gap usually marks a SHIFT from problem to solution. Look for "mais" (but) as the pivot connector and "désormais" (now) or "n'est plus" (no longer) as change markers.

❌ WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

DON'T #1: Put a PREVALENCE sentence in a SYMPTOM gap

GAP 37 is in the SYMPTOMS section.
Option A ("new victims every year") is about PREVALENCE.
→ Wrong section. Even if factually plausible, it doesn't fit the structural position.

DON'T #2: Ignore escalation markers

"ENSUITE viennent les nausées" → "ensuite" means AFTER something
→ The gap MUST contain what comes BEFORE nausea
→ That must be MILDER symptoms (not more severe, not demographics)

DON'T #3: Create logical contradictions with "également"

Gap: "Only humans are affected" + After: "Animals are ALSO affected"
→ "Only" + "also" = logical impossibility
→ ALWAYS check whether gap and AFTER are logically compatible

DON'T #4: Put pessimistic sentences before optimistic conclusions

Gap: "This illness is still causing damage" (pessimistic)
After: ": it can now be treated" (optimistic)
→ A colon (:) can't bridge opposites — it SPECIFIES, not CONTRASTS
→ Only "mais" (but) or "pourtant" (yet) can bridge opposites

DON'T #5: Ignore the COLON as a logical constraint

A colon (:) after the gap means:
  GAP = GENERAL statement
  AFTER = SPECIFIC details

If the AFTER gives specific treatment details,
the GAP must make a general statement about treatability.
NOT about ongoing damage, NOT about being inescapable.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: The Section Misplacement Trap (NEW — Core concept of Chapter 14)

MECHANISM:  A sentence from one medical section is offered for a gap 
            in a different section
EXAMPLE:    Gap is in SYMPTOMS section (Section 2)
            Option is about PREVALENCE (Section 1): "new victims each year"
DEFENSE:    Map the medical progression. Each gap belongs to ONE section.
            The gap sentence must match that section's FUNCTION.

PITFALL 2: The Severity Misplacement Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  A symptom of the wrong severity level is placed at the 
            wrong position in the escalation
EXAMPLE:    Gap is before "ensuite" (next = moderate symptoms)
            → Gap must contain MILD symptoms (the first ones)
            → Not moderate or severe ones
DEFENSE:    Use escalation markers to determine the gap's severity level.
            "Commence" = mild. "Ensuite" = moderate. "Même" = severe.

PITFALL 3: The Exclusivity-Inclusion Contradiction (NEW — builds on Ch.10)

MECHANISM:  The gap claims exclusivity ("only X") while the AFTER 
            establishes inclusion ("Y also")
EXAMPLE:    Gap: "Only humans are affected"
            After: "Animals are ALSO sensitive"
            → "Only" + "also" = logical impossibility
DEFENSE:    When "également/aussi" appears in AFTER, the gap must be 
            INCLUSIVE ("not the only ones"), not EXCLUSIVE ("only").

PITFALL 4: The Colon Direction Violation (NEW)

MECHANISM:  The gap states something that the colon's specification CONTRADICTS
EXAMPLE:    Gap: "Still causing damage" (negative)
            : "Can now be treated" (positive specification)
            → A colon SPECIFIES the gap — it can't reverse its direction
DEFENSE:    Colons introduce DETAILS of the gap's claim.
            The gap and colon-introduced content must be in the SAME direction.
            Opposite directions need "mais"/"pourtant," not a colon.

PITFALL 5: The Double Contradiction Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  The gap contradicts BOTH the BEFORE and the AFTER simultaneously
EXAMPLE:    Before: "affection courante" (common)
            Gap: "partie infime" (tiny portion)
            After: "1 in 3" (33%)
            → Contradicts "common" AND contradicts "33%"
DEFENSE:    Always check consistency with BOTH neighboring sentences.
            If the gap contradicts either one, eliminate.
            If it contradicts BOTH, eliminate instantly.
