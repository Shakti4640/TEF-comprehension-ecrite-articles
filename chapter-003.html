CHAPTER 3: THE DOCTOR WHO CYCLES

📰 Texte 81 — Character Profile | Phase 1: Foundation Thinking

📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le vélo du Docteur Pineda

Ce jour-là le téléphone a sonné vers midi : une infection urinaire à 2 km de son cabinet, dans le 5ème arrondissement de Paris. Le temps de prendre son matériel et le docteur Roland Pineda a sauté sur son vélo. « J'y étais en moins de dix minutes », assure-t-il : « quelle que soit l'urgence, je pars à bicyclette ». Comme cette autre fois où trois habitants du 13ème arrondissement avaient été intoxiqués à l'oxyde de carbone. SAMU, pompiers, police et le docteur Pineda avaient été prévenus : « je suis arrivé cinq minutes avant tout le monde ». Pour Roland Pineda, médecin généraliste et urgentiste à l'hôpital des Gardiens de la paix, le vélo, « c'est un choix idéologique ». Il « hait » la voiture : elle « pollue », « rend irritable », parce qu'elle coupe le conducteur des autres usagers de la rue. Bref, « c'est tout sauf convivial et fraternel ». À la différence du vélo, synonyme de gain de temps et de « sensation de liberté ».

  • D — il est urgent de réduire le nombre de voitures dans les villes.

🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's NEW in this chapter

Chapters 1 and 2 dealt with factual articles — a crime report and a historical event. The answers came from what happened.

This chapter introduces a character profile — an article built around one person's actions and opinions. This creates two new challenges:

Challenge 1: Fact vs. Opinion
Some information in the text is factual (he rode his bike, he arrived in 10 minutes) and some is opinion (the car "pollutes," is "not convivial"). You must know which type each question is asking about.

Challenge 2: The Character's Voice
Notice the quotation marks throughout the text: « J'y étais en moins de dix minutes », « quelle que soit l'urgence », « c'est un choix idéologique ». These are the doctor's OWN words. When a question says "selon le docteur Pineda" (according to Dr. Pineda), you must answer from HIS perspective, not yours, not the journalist's.

Applying the method (Rule #1 from Chapter 1)

STEP 1: Read all questions first

Q17: "se sert de son vélo..." → HOW OFTEN / WHEN does he use his bike?
Q18: "l'un des avantages du vélo..." → WHAT ADVANTAGE does the bike have?
Q19: "Selon le docteur Pineda..." → WHAT DOES HE THINK about the car?

Notice something crucial about these three questions:

Q17 → Tests BEHAVIOR (what he DOES)
Q18 → Tests STATED ADVANTAGE (what the text SAYS)
Q19 → Tests PERSONAL OPINION (what he BELIEVES)

Three different question types, each requiring a different mental filter.

STEP 2: Read the title

Le vélo du Docteur Pineda

The article is about a doctor and his bicycle. The title already suggests the bike is central to his identity — it's not just transport, it's personal.

✅ HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 17: Le docteur Pineda se sert de son vélo...

The key phrase in the text:

« quelle que soit l'urgence, je pars à bicyclette »

This is the most important expression in the entire article for this question. Let's decode it precisely:

FrenchMeaning
quelle que soitwhatever / no matter what
l'urgencethe emergency / the urgency
je pars à bicycletteI go by bicycle

Full meaning: "No matter what the emergency is, I go by bicycle."

This means: he ALWAYS uses his bike. There is NO exception. Not "sometimes." Not "only when it's urgent." Not "only when it's not too urgent." ALWAYS.

Now let's understand the four answer choices — this is where absolute words become critical:

ChoiceKey wordsAnalysisVerdict
A — à l'occasion"à l'occasion" = occasionally, sometimesThis means he uses it SOMETIMES. But the text says "quelle que soit l'urgence" = ALWAYS. Contradiction.❌ Too infrequent
B — en toute occasion"en toute occasion" = on every occasion, alwaysThis matches "quelle que soit l'urgence" = no matter what. EVERY situation.✅ CORRECT
C — seulement en cas d'urgence"seulement" = onlyThis means ONLY during emergencies. But the text gives an example of a non-emergency call (infection urinaire at noon — not life-threatening) AND says "quelle que soit" = no matter what. He uses it for EVERYTHING, not only emergencies.❌ Too restrictive
D — si la situation n'est pas trop urgente"si... n'est pas trop urgente" = if it's not too urgentThis means he switches to something else when it's very urgent. But the text says the OPPOSITE — even during a carbon monoxide poisoning emergency, he took his bike.❌ Opposite of text

✅ Answer: B

🔬 THE ABSOLUTE WORDS PRINCIPLE

Look at how the four answers differ ONLY in their frequency/condition words:

A: "à l'occasion"           → SOMETIMES
B: "en toute occasion"      → ALWAYS ✅
C: "seulement en cas de..." → ONLY WHEN
D: "si... n'est pas trop..."→ CONDITIONALLY

The text uses "quelle que soit" which is an absolute expression meaning "no matter what" = ALWAYS, WITHOUT EXCEPTION.

The correct answer must match this absolute level.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #6: Pay extreme attention to ABSOLUTE WORDS and FREQUENCY MARKERS in both the text and the answer choices. They change meaning dramatically.

Here's a reference table you'll use throughout the exam:

French expressionMeaningAbsoluteness level
toujours / en toute occasionalways🔴 Maximum
quelle que soitno matter what🔴 Maximum
systématiquementsystematically🔴 Maximum
souvent / fréquemmentoften🟡 High
parfois / à l'occasionsometimes🟢 Medium
rarementrarely🔵 Low
jamais / en aucun casnever🔴 Maximum (negative)
seulement / uniquementonly🔴 Maximum (restrictive)

When the text says "quelle que soit" (🔴 Maximum), the answer must also be at 🔴 Maximum level → "en toute occasion."

QUESTION 18: Selon le texte, l'un des avantages du vélo sur la voiture est que...

Important detail in the question: It says "selon le texte" (according to the text) — not "selon le docteur." This means the answer can come from ANYTHING in the text, whether it's the journalist's words or the doctor's quotes.

Also notice: "l'un des avantages" = ONE OF the advantages. Not "the main advantage" or "the only advantage." Several advantages are mentioned — we need to find which ONE appears in the answer choices.

Advantages of the bike mentioned in the text:

Text evidenceAdvantage category
"j'y étais en moins de dix minutes" / "cinq minutes avant tout le monde"SPEED / time-saving
"elle pollue" (about the car — so the bike doesn't pollute)ECOLOGY
"gain de temps"SPEED / time-saving
"sensation de liberté"FREEDOM
"convivial et fraternel" (bike is, car isn't)SOCIAL CONNECTION

Now let's match against the answer choices:

ChoiceAdvantage claimedIs it in the text?Verdict
A — c'est plus écologiqueEcologyYES — "elle pollue" (car pollutes) implies the bike is more ecological✅ CORRECT
B — c'est plus économiqueEconomy / moneyNOWHERE in the text. No mention of cost, price, fuel, savings.❌ Not mentioned
C — c'est plus facile à garerParkingNOWHERE in the text. No mention of parking.❌ Not mentioned
D — c'est meilleur pour la formePhysical fitness / healthNOWHERE in the text. No mention of health benefits or exercise.❌ Not mentioned

✅ Answer: A

🔬 THE "NOT MENTIONED" ELIMINATION

This question demonstrates a powerful technique: three answers are about things the text NEVER discusses.

Text discusses:  Speed ✓  Ecology ✓  Freedom ✓  Social connection ✓
Text NEVER discusses: Economy ✗  Parking ✗  Fitness ✗

Answers B, C, and D all sound like perfectly reasonable advantages of cycling. In real life, yes, bikes are cheaper, easier to park, and better for fitness. But the text doesn't say any of this.

This connects back to Rule #3 from Chapter 1: If you can't point to a specific line in the text, the answer is wrong — no matter how true it is in real life.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #3 (EXPANDED): "True in real life" ≠ "True in the text." The exam tests READING, not general knowledge. An answer can be 100% true in reality and still be 100% wrong if the text doesn't state it.

This is one of the hardest habits to build. Your brain wants to say "But bikes ARE cheaper!" Yes, they are. But the text doesn't say so. Move on.

QUESTION 19: Selon le docteur Pineda...

Critical phrase in the question: "Selon le docteur Pineda" = According to Dr. Pineda. This means we ONLY care about what HE says. His words are in quotation marks « ».

The doctor's statements about the car:

His wordsWhat he's saying
"Il « hait » la voiture"He HATES the car
"elle « pollue »"It pollutes
"« rend irritable »"It makes you irritable
"elle coupe le conducteur des autres usagers de la rue"It CUTS OFF the driver from other road users
"« c'est tout sauf convivial et fraternel »"It's anything BUT friendly and brotherly

Now let's decode the critical sentence for this question:

"elle coupe le conducteur des autres usagers de la rue"

FrenchMeaning
couperto cut / to cut off / to separate
le conducteurthe driver
des autres usagersfrom the other users
de la rueof the street

Full meaning: The car SEPARATES the driver from other people on the street. It creates a barrier. It ISOLATES.

And the follow-up:

"c'est tout sauf convivial et fraternel"

FrenchMeaning
tout saufanything but / everything except
convivialfriendly / sociable
fraternelbrotherly / warm

Full meaning: The car is ANYTHING BUT sociable and warm = it is NOT sociable, NOT warm = it ISOLATES people.

Now let's test each answer:

ChoiceAnalysisVerdict
A — le seul avantage de la voiture, c'est sa rapiditéThe doctor NEVER mentions any advantage of the car. He says the BIKE is fast ("cinq minutes avant tout le monde"), not the car. He hates the car completely. "Le seul avantage" implies the car HAS an advantage — but according to him, it has NONE.❌ He attributes no advantage to the car
B — la voiture isole les êtres humains les uns des autres"Isole" = isolates. "Les êtres humains les uns des autres" = human beings from one another. The text says the car "coupe le conducteur des autres usagers" = cuts off the driver from others. PARAPHRASE!✅ CORRECT
C — la voiture apporte une grande liberté à ses utilisateursThe doctor says the BIKE gives a "sensation de liberté." He says the car is "tout sauf convivial." He NEVER attributes freedom to the car. This answer STEALS a bike quality and gives it to the car.❌ Attributes bike's quality to car
D — il est urgent de réduire le nombre de voitures dans les villesThe doctor expresses personal hatred for cars and praises bikes. But he NEVER makes a political/policy statement about reducing car numbers. This goes beyond what he says.❌ Not stated — political extrapolation

✅ Answer: B

🎯 WHAT COULD BE DONE

The "Opinion Article" Reading Strategy

When an article is built around a person's opinions, organize your reading into two mental columns:

┌─────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────┐
│      FACTS              │      OPINIONS                │
│   (journalist's words)  │   (character's words « »)    │
├─────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────┤
│ He's a doctor in Paris  │ "The bike is an ideological  │
│ He rode to a call       │  choice"                     │
│ He arrived in 10 min    │ "The car pollutes"           │
│ He beat SAMU by 5 min   │ "It makes you irritable"     │
│                         │ "It cuts you off from others"│
│                         │ "It's anything but friendly"  │
│                         │ "The bike = freedom"          │
└─────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────┘

When the question says "selon le texte" → you can use BOTH columns.
When the question says "selon le docteur Pineda" → you can ONLY use the right column.

This distinction will become even more critical in later chapters when multiple people express different opinions in the same text (Chapter 11, Chapter 15).

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

Master the "Selon..." filter

Every time you see "selon" (according to) in a question, immediately ask:

"Selon le texte" → Answer can come from ANYWHERE in the text
"Selon [person]" → Answer can ONLY come from that person's words
"Selon l'auteur" → Answer comes from the writer's perspective

Build your "Frequency Word" vocabulary

From this chapter, these expressions are permanent knowledge:

ExpressionMeaningWatch out for
quelle que soitno matter whatMatches "toujours" / "en toute occasion"
tout saufanything butMeans the OPPOSITE of what follows
à la différence deunlike / as opposed toSignals a CONTRAST between two things

Use the "Attribute Theft" detector

In Q19-C, the answer STOLE a quality from the bike ("liberté") and gave it to the car. This is a sneaky trap that works like this:

Text says: BIKE = freedom, CAR = isolation
Trap answer: CAR = freedom

The trap SWAPS the subject while keeping the quality.

How to detect it: When an answer attributes a positive quality to something the text criticizes (or vice versa), it's almost certainly wrong.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #8: The "Reasonable Extrapolation" Trap 🧩

Doctor hates cars → Doctor probably wants fewer cars in cities → Answer D seems logical

Reality: There's a difference between:

  • What someone FEELS (he hates cars — STATED)
  • What someone WANTS to happen (reduce car numbers — NOT STATED)

Just because someone hates X doesn't mean they've called for reducing X. The text reports his feelings, not his political positions.

How to avoid it: Ask: "Does the text SAY this, or am I CONCLUDING this?" If you're concluding, stop. Choose only what is SAID.

PITFALL #9: The "Attribute Swap" Trap 🔄

TEXT: Bike = freedom, Car = isolation
TRAP ANSWER: Car = freedom

The exam takes a quality that belongs to Object A and puts it on Object B. If you're reading fast, you see the word "liberté" and think "Yes! That's in the text!" — but it's attached to the WRONG subject.

How to avoid it: Always check: "In the text, WHICH subject has this quality?" Match both the quality AND the subject.

PITFALL #10: The "Sounds Strong But Is Accurate" Hesitation 💪

Students sometimes hesitate over answer B ("en toute occasion") because it sounds too absolute. "Does he REALLY use his bike in EVERY situation? That seems extreme."

But the text literally says: "quelle que soit l'urgence, je pars à bicyclette" — which IS absolute. The doctor IS extreme about this. Don't soften his words.

How to avoid it: Trust the text's own intensity level. If the text uses absolute language, the answer should match that absoluteness.
