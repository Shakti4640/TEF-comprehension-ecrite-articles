Tourisme : « on visitera avant de payer »

Dans l'industrie touristique, le bouleversement ne viendra pas de nouvelles destinations mais du consumérisme des voyageurs. Car le temps disponible augmentera bien plus vite que les revenus du touriste moyen. 🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What kind of text is this?

This is a prediction article — a text that describes what the FUTURE will look like, based on current trends. Unlike previous chapters where the text described the PRESENT (Chapter 6) or reported a PAST event (Chapter 7) or discussed a PROPOSED change (Chapter 8), this text projects forward into a HYPOTHETICAL FUTURE.

Why prediction texts are uniquely challenging

Prediction texts create a THREE-TIMEFRAME problem:

PRESENT (what IS true now):
  → Tourists have limited income
  → Tourism is mainly domestic
  → Accommodation matters to tourists

FUTURE (what the author PREDICTS):
  → More free time, same income
  → Even stronger preference for domestic/rural tourism
  → Flexibility will matter more than accommodation

COMPARISON (how PRESENT differs from FUTURE):
  → Time: more | Money: same | Priorities: shifted
  → The exam tests whether you can track EACH variable separately

The danger: you might confuse a PRESENT characteristic with a FUTURE prediction, or vice versa. You might also misread the DIRECTION of change (more vs. less, same vs. different).

QUESTION 20 — How will time and money change?

The question: selon l'auteur, les futurs touristes auront...

This question tests your ability to read an ASYMMETRIC COMPARISON — two variables that change at DIFFERENT rates.

Step 1: Locate the relevant sentence.

"Car le temps disponible augmentera bien plus vite que les revenus du touriste moyen."

Step 2: Parse the comparison.

This sentence contains a "bien plus vite que" (much faster than) structure:

VARIABLE 1: "le temps disponible"  = available free time
DIRECTION 1: "augmentera"          = will INCREASE
RATE 1:      "bien plus vite"      = much FASTER

VARIABLE 2: "les revenus"          = income/money
DIRECTION 2: (implied: will also increase, but SLOWER)
RATE 2:      (implied: slower than time)

ASYMMETRIC COMPARISON:
  Time:  ↑↑↑ (increases a LOT)
  Money: ↑ (increases a LITTLE, or stays roughly the same)

The key insight: "augmentera bien plus vite que les revenus" doesn't say income will DECREASE — it says time will grow MUCH FASTER than income. Income may grow slightly, but the relative difference is dramatic.

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionTime changeMoney changeMatches text?Verdict
A) beaucoup plus d'argent et beaucoup plus de temps libre↑↑↑ time↑↑↑ moneyNO — text says time grows MUCH FASTER than income. Both growing equally is wrong❌ WRONG — ignores the asymmetry
B) beaucoup plus d'argent mais peu de temps libre supplémentaire↑ time↑↑↑ moneyNO — this REVERSES the asymmetry. Text says time grows faster, not money❌ WRONG — exact reversal of the text

D) beaucoup moins d'argent et beaucoup moins de temps libre↓↓↓ time↓↓↓ moneyNO — both DECREASE, but the text says time INCREASES and income at worst stays stable❌ WRONG — wrong direction for both variables

✅ Answer: C

The "Asymmetric Comparison" Technique

This is a critical technique for prediction texts. When a text compares two variables growing at DIFFERENT rates, you must track EACH variable SEPARATELY:

TEXT: "X augmentera bien plus vite que Y"

STEP 1: Identify X and Y
  X = temps disponible (free time)
  Y = revenus (income)

STEP 2: Identify the direction of X
  X → INCREASES ("augmentera")

STEP 3: Identify the relationship between X and Y
  X grows "bien plus vite que" Y
  = X grows MUCH FASTER than Y
  = Y grows MUCH SLOWER than X (or barely grows)

STEP 4: Translate to answer language
  X: "beaucoup plus de temps libre" ✅
  Y: "pas plus d'argent" ✅ (income barely changes relative to time)

🔑 Rule: "X augmente plus vite que Y" does NOT mean Y decreases. It means Y grows SLOWER than X. The exam will offer options that claim Y decreases (wrong) or that both grow equally (wrong). Track each variable independently.

The "Reversal" Trap (Option B)

Option B is a PERFECT MIRROR of the correct answer:

CORRECT (C): Much more TIME but not more MONEY
REVERSED (B): Much more MONEY but not more TIME

B takes the two variables and SWAPS their rates of change.

This is the cleanest example of a reversal trap you've seen. The exam bets that under time pressure, you'll confuse which variable grows faster.

The defense: Underline or mentally highlight the FIRST variable in the comparison:

"le TEMPS DISPONIBLE augmentera bien plus vite que les REVENUS"
       ↑ THIS grows fast                        ↑ THIS grows slow

FIRST variable mentioned = the one that grows FASTER

🔑 Rule: In "X augmentera plus vite que Y," X is always the FAST-GROWING variable. The exam will offer an option that reverses X and Y.

The "Reaction Against" Logic

The text explains WHY rural tourism will grow:

CAUSE:    urbanisation + mondialisation → people live increasingly in cities
REACTION: tourisme rural → people ESCAPE to the countryside for vacation

The Synonym Bridge: "Souplesse" = "Flexibilité"

This question requires you to recognize that two different words mean the same thing:

TEXT uses:    "souplesse"    (suppleness, flexibility, adaptability)
ANSWER uses:  "flexibilité"  (flexibility)

These are SYNONYMS — the answer PARAPHRASES the text.

This is the essence of Phase 2 reading: the correct answer almost NEVER uses the exact same words as the text. It REPHRASES the text's ideas using synonyms.

Common synonym pairs in TCF:

Option D introduces a crucial distinction that will recur throughout the exam:

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Asymmetric Reversal" Trap

What happens: The text compares two variables growing at different rates. The exam offers an option that swaps which variable grows faster.

Full mechanism:

TEXT:     "le TEMPS augmentera bien plus vite que les REVENUS"
          TIME ↑↑↑ >> INCOME ↑

CORRECT (C): beaucoup plus de TEMPS mais pas plus d'ARGENT
             TIME ↑↑↑, MONEY →

REVERSED (B): beaucoup plus d'ARGENT mais peu de TEMPS supplémentaire
              MONEY ↑↑↑, TIME →
              
Option B is a PERFECT MIRROR of C — every element is swapped.

The defense: When you encounter "X augmente plus vite que Y," immediately note:

FAST GROWER = X (first mentioned) = temps
SLOW GROWER = Y (second mentioned) = revenus

Then match: FAST GROWER should be "beaucoup plus" in the answer
            SLOW GROWER should be "pas plus" or "peu" in the answer

Pitfall 2: The "Reaction Misreading" Trap

What happens: The text says a preference exists "en réaction à" something. You confuse the preference with the thing it reacts AGAINST.

Example:

TEXT: "tourisme RURAL en réaction à l'URBANISATION"
      PREFERENCE = rural (countryside)
      REACTING AGAINST = urbanisation (cities)

If you misread this:
  → You might think urbanization drives tourism TO cities (wrong)
  → Instead of: urbanization drives tourism AWAY from cities (correct)

The "en réaction à" structure means the preference is the OPPOSITE of what it reacts against:

  • Reaction to urbanization → preference for RURAL
  • Reaction to globalization → preference for LOCAL/domestic

🔑 Rule: "En réaction à X" means AWAY from X, toward the opposite of X.

Pitfall 3: The "Future Tense Certainty" Confusion

What happens: You treat predictions (future tense) as if they were confirmed facts, or conversely, you doubt them and look for hedging language.

In prediction texts, future tense IS the "truth" of the text:

In a NEWS ARTICLE (Ch. 7):
  Future/conditional = uncertain, hypothetical
  → "seraient pompés" = MIGHT be pumped

In a PREDICTION ARTICLE (Ch. 9):
  Future = the author's projected reality
  → "visitera son propre pays" = WILL visit their own country
  
The exam treats predictions AS TRUE within the text's framework.
Questions ask "selon l'auteur" or "selon cet article" — within this framework, predictions are facts.

🔑 Rule: In prediction texts, the future tense functions as the "truth" of the article. Don't second-guess predictions — extract them as stated. The exam asks what the AUTHOR predicts, not whether the prediction is correct.
