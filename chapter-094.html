The Three New Skills of Chapter 16

Skill 1: SELECTING CORRECT LOGICAL CONNECTORS

French connectors create RELATIONSHIPS between ideas. Different connectors create COMPLETELY DIFFERENT meanings from the same facts:

FACT A: "Citizens feel poorly informed about GMOs"
FACT B: "GMO destructions make headlines regularly"

CONNECTED WITH "même si" (even though/concession):
  "Citizens feel poorly informed EVEN THOUGH destructions make headlines"
  → PARADOX: despite media coverage, people feel uninformed

CONNECTED WITH "puisque" (since/cause):
  "Citizens feel poorly informed SINCE destructions make headlines"
  → CAUSATION: the destructions cause the poor information (?)
  → This doesn't make logical sense

CONNECTED WITH "grâce à" (thanks to/positive cause):
  "Citizens feel poorly informed THANKS TO ecologists' battles"
  → POSITIVE CAUSATION: ecologists' battles cause poor information (?)
  → Contradictory — "thanks to" implies a positive outcome

CONNECTED WITH "c'est pourquoi" (that's why/consequence):
  "Citizens feel poorly informed, THAT'S WHY ecologists..."
  → CONSEQUENCE: poor information leads to ecologist action
  → Grammatically incomplete (option A)

The SAME two facts produce FOUR different meanings depending on the connector. Choosing the wrong connector doesn't just create a grammatical error — it creates a LOGICAL ABSURDITY.

Skill 2: READING CONCESSION vs. CAUSE vs. CONSEQUENCE

The three most commonly confused logical relationships:

RelationshipFrench connectorsLogicExample
CONCESSIONmême si, bien que, malgré, quoiqueDespite X, Y (surprising coexistence)"Even though it rains, he goes out"
CAUSEpuisque, parce que, car, grâce à, à cause deBecause of X, Y (X produces Y)"Because it rains, he stays home"
CONSEQUENCEdonc, c'est pourquoi, par conséquent, aussiX, therefore Y (Y follows from X)"It rains, therefore he stays home"

The exam tests whether you can identify which RELATIONSHIP the text requires — then select the connector that creates it.

The Concession Logic — "Même si"

"Même si" creates a CONCESSION — it acknowledges that two facts COEXIST despite seeming contradictory:

CONCESSION STRUCTURE:
  X, même si Y
  = X is true, EVEN THOUGH Y (which would normally prevent X) is also true

APPLICATION:
  "Citizens feel poorly informed" (X)
  EVEN THOUGH
  "GMO destructions regularly make headlines" (Y)
  
  Y (headlines) should normally prevent X (poor information).
  But X is still true DESPITE Y.
  → CONCESSION: acknowledges the paradox without resolving it.

When to use "même si" (concession):

  • Two facts that seem to CONTRADICT each other
  • The first fact persists DESPITE the second
  • The second fact would normally PREVENT the first

When NOT to use "puisque" (cause) or "grâce à" (positive cause):

  • When the alleged cause would produce the OPPOSITE of the stated effect
  • "Headlines → poorly informed" is ILLOGICAL as causation
  • Headlines should INCREASE information, not decrease it

🔑 Rule for gap-fill: When two facts seem to CONTRADICT each other, the connector is CONCESSIVE (même si, bien que, malgré). When one fact PRODUCES the other, the connector is CAUSAL (puisque, parce que, car). When one fact FOLLOWS from the other, the connector is CONSECUTIVE (donc, c'est pourquoi). Test the LOGIC, not just the grammar.

The Specification Connector — "Quant à"

"Quant à" introduces the TOPIC or DOMAIN that a previous statement applies to:

STRUCTURE:
  [General statement] quant à [specific topic]
  = [General statement] REGARDING [specific topic]

  "Nothing has been demonstrated" (general statement)
  REGARDING
  "Possible GMO toxicity in food consumption" (specific topic)
  
  "Quant à" narrows the general statement to a specific domain.

The connector test for specification gaps:

The gap needs to SPECIFY what hasn't been demonstrated.

  → "Grâce à" = thanks to (introduces a cause) ❌
  → "Comme" = like (introduces a comparison) ❌
  → "C'est-à-dire" = that is (introduces an equivalence) ❌

🔑 Rule: When a gap follows a general statement and needs to specify WHAT that statement applies to, the connector is "quant à" (regarding/as for). Test by asking: "Does the gap answer 'regarding what?' or 'about what topic?'"

The "Mais" Pivot — Contrast that introduces change

"Mais" (but) is the most important PIVOT connector in French. It signals a CHANGE OF DIRECTION:

STRUCTURE:
  [Initial state], mais [change agent] [forced new state]
  = First X happened, BUT THEN Y caused Z

APPLICATION:
  "The Commission chose tolerance" (initial state)
  BUT
  "The publication of the blacklist" (change agent)
  "forced it to harden its position" (new state)

Why "malgré" (despite) fails here:

"MALGRÉ" LOGIC:
  "The Commission chose tolerance DESPITE the blacklist"
  → This means: the Commission MAINTAINED tolerance even though the blacklist existed
  → But the text says: the Commission CHANGED (hardened) because of the blacklist
  → "Malgré" preserves the initial state; the text describes a DEPARTURE from it

"MAIS" LOGIC:
  "The Commission chose tolerance, BUT the blacklist forced a change"
  → This means: the initial state (tolerance) was DISRUPTED by the blacklist
  → The Commission CHANGED its position
  → "Mais" signals the departure from the initial state

🔑 Rule: "Malgré" (despite) means the initial situation PERSISTS despite the obstacle. "Mais" (but) means the initial situation CHANGES because of what follows. If the text describes a CHANGE, use "mais." If it describes PERSISTENCE despite opposition, use "malgré."

The Threshold Condition — "Dès lors que"

"Dès lors que" is a CONDITIONAL TRIGGER — it specifies the MOMENT when a rule activates:

APPLICATION:
  "Manufacturers must label GMOs" (obligation)
  FROM THE MOMENT THAT
  "GMO presence exceeds 1% in a product" (threshold condition)

  At/above 1%: labeling mandatory

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

1. Master the CONNECTOR TAXONOMY

The single most important tool for texte à trous:

CONNECTOR TAXONOMY:

CONCESSION (despite/even though — surprising coexistence):
  même si, bien que, quoique, malgré, en dépit de
  → FACT A exists DESPITE FACT B (which should prevent A)

CAUSE (because/since — one produces the other):
  parce que, puisque, car, comme, étant donné que
  → FACT A CAUSES FACT B

POSITIVE CAUSE (thanks to):
  grâce à
  → FACT A POSITIVELY causes FACT B (good outcome)

NEGATIVE CAUSE (because of — blame):
  à cause de
  → FACT A NEGATIVELY causes FACT B (bad outcome)

CONSEQUENCE (therefore — one follows from the other):
  donc, c'est pourquoi, par conséquent, aussi, ainsi
  → Because of FACT A, FACT B follows

CONTRAST (but — change of direction):
  mais, cependant, toutefois, néanmoins, pourtant, or
  → FACT A, BUT THEN FACT B (direction changes)

CONDITION (if/when — trigger):
  si, dès lors que, à condition que, pourvu que
  → FACT B applies WHEN/IF FACT A is true

SPECIFICATION (regarding — narrows the topic):
  quant à, en ce qui concerne, pour ce qui est de
  → The statement applies TO [specific topic]

ADDITION (moreover/also):
  de plus, en outre, par ailleurs, également
  → FACT B is ADDED to FACT A

EQUIVALENCE (that is to say):
  c'est-à-dire, autrement dit, en d'autres termes
  → FACT A = FACT B (same thing said differently)

2. Apply the THREE-STEP GAP-FILL METHOD

For every gap:

STEP 1: READ BOTH SIDES
  → What does the sentence BEFORE the gap say?
  → What does the sentence AFTER the gap say?

STEP 2: DETERMINE THE RELATIONSHIP
  → Do the two sides CONTRADICT each other? → Concession or Contrast
  → Does one CAUSE the other? → Cause
  → Does one FOLLOW FROM the other? → Consequence
  → Does one SPECIFY the other? → Specification
  → Does one TRIGGER the other? → Condition

STEP 3: SELECT THE MATCHING CONNECTOR
  → Find the option with the connector that creates the identified relationship
  → Verify by reading the COMPLETE sentence with the option inserted

3. Use the "INSERTION TEST" for final verification

After selecting an option, INSERT it into the gap and read the complete sentence aloud (mentally):

INSERTION TEST for Gap 36:

"Les citoyens s'estiment toujours très mal informés sur les OGM,
MÊME SI les destructions des OGM par des écologistes
font régulièrement la une des journaux."

Does this sentence make LOGICAL sense? ✅
  → Citizens feel uninformed EVEN THOUGH destructions make headlines
  → Paradox acknowledged — makes sense

Does this sentence make GRAMMATICAL sense? ✅
  → "même si" + indicative tense → correct
  → Subject ("les destructions") + verb ("font") → correct agreement

BOTH logic AND grammar must pass the insertion test.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Concession-Cause Confusion"

What happens: You can't decide between "even though" (concession) and "because" (cause) because both seem to connect the two facts.

The decision test:

ASK: "Does FACT B logically PRODUCE FACT A?"

Gap 36:
  FACT A: Citizens feel poorly informed
  FACT B: GMO destructions make headlines

  Does "making headlines" PRODUCE "poor information"?
  → NO. Headlines should INCREASE information, not decrease it.
  → The relationship is PARADOXICAL, not causal.
  → CONCESSION ("même si") is correct.

GENERAL RULE:
  If B should PREVENT A but A exists anyway → CONCESSION (même si, bien que)
  If B actually PRODUCES A → CAUSE (puisque, parce que)
  If B would normally produce A but doesn't → ALSO CONCESSION but reversed

Pitfall 2: The "Malgré vs. Mais" Confusion

What happens: Both "malgré" (despite) and "mais" (but) seem to introduce contrast. You can't decide which one fits.

The decision test:

ASK: "Does the initial situation PERSIST or CHANGE?"

Gap 38:
  Initial situation: Commission chose tolerance
  After the gap: Commission hardened its position

  Did tolerance PERSIST? → NO, it CHANGED to hardness
  → "MAIS" (but) signals the CHANGE ✅
  → "MALGRÉ" (despite) would signal PERSISTENCE ❌

GENERAL RULE:
  Initial state CONTINUES despite obstacle → MALGRÉ (despite)
  Initial state CHANGES because of what follows → MAIS (but)
  
  "Malgré X, Y persiste"  = despite X, Y continues
  "Y, mais X a changé Z"  = Y was true, but X changed things to Z

Pitfall 3: The "Wrong Connector, Right Content" Trap

What happens: An option contains the right CONTENT (Greenpeace's blacklist, 1% threshold) but the wrong CONNECTOR. You select it because the content matches, ignoring the logical error.

Example: Gap 38 — Option D: "à cause de la publication de la liste noire"

CONTENT: "la publication de la liste noire de Greenpeace" ✅
  → Yes, the blacklist IS mentioned and IS relevant

CONNECTOR: "à cause de" ❌
  → "À cause de" connects the blacklist to the PRECEDING idea (tolerance)
  → "The Commission chose tolerance BECAUSE OF the blacklist"
  → This is backwards — the blacklist caused CHANGE, not tolerance

RIGHT content + WRONG connector = WRONG answer

🔑 Rule: In texte à trous, the CONNECTOR matters more than the CONTENT. All options typically contain relevant content — the difference is in HOW they connect it to the surrounding text. Focus on the LOGICAL RELATIONSHIP, not just the factual content.

Pitfall 4: The "Grammar Check" Failure

What happens: You select an option that creates good logic but is grammatically broken.

Example: Gap 36 — Option A: "c'est pourquoi les écologistes."

LOGIC CHECK:   "Citizens feel poorly informed, that's why ecologists..."
               → The logic COULD work if completed, but...

GRAMMAR CHECK: "c'est pourquoi les écologistes."
               → "Les écologistes" is a SUBJECT with no VERB
               → The sentence is INCOMPLETE
               → "Font régulièrement la une" needs a subject
               → But "c'est pourquoi les écologistes" already ends the clause

RESULT: Grammatically broken = WRONG, regardless of potential logic

The defense: After choosing an option for its logic, perform a quick GRAMMAR verification:

GRAMMAR CHECKLIST:
  □ Does the subject have a verb?
  □ Does the verb agree with the subject?
  □ Is the sentence complete (not trailing off)?
  □ Are prepositions correct (dans, de, à, pour)?
  □ Is the mood correct (indicative vs. subjunctive)?

🔑 Rule: In texte à trous, BOTH logic AND grammar must be correct. A logically sound option with broken grammar is wrong. A grammatically perfect option with broken logic is also wrong. Verify BOTH.
