Chapter 16 is a completely different beast.

Here is what makes technical vocabulary harder than literary vocabulary:

LITERARY UNKNOWN WORD:
  "sans ciller" → You don't know it → Context says he's confident
  → You skip it → You still understand the paragraph ✓

TECHNICAL UNKNOWN WORD:
  "prolifération des poux de mer" → You don't know it
  → It IS the main problem being described
  → If you skip it, you miss the CORE argument
  → You CANNOT answer Q1 or Q3 correctly ✗

📌 Technical vocabulary is not decoration. It IS the content. You must develop strategies to decode it, not skip it.

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: THE 5 DECODING STRATEGIES FOR TECHNICAL VOCABULARY

In Chapter 3, you learned general context clue techniques. Now we build five specialized strategies for technical and scientific vocabulary.

STRATEGY 1: THE COGNATE BRIDGE (French ↔ English ↔ International Scientific Terms)

Many technical terms in French are cognates — they look and sound similar to English or other languages because they share Latin, Greek, or international scientific roots.

THE COGNATE BRIDGE IN TEXTE 102:
                                                          
  FRENCH              ROOT              ENGLISH
  ─────────           ────              ───────
  prolifération   ←── Latin proliferare  ──→ proliferation
  écosystème      ←── Greek oikos+systema──→ ecosystem
  pesticides      ←── Latin pestis+caedere─→ pesticides
  dioxydes        ←── Greek di+oxys     ──→ dioxides
  mercure         ←── Latin Mercurius   ──→ mercury
  toxiques        ←── Greek toxikon     ──→ toxic
  industrialisation←─ Latin industria   ──→ industrialization
  patrimoine      ←── Latin patrimonium ──→ patrimony/heritage
  génétique       ←── Greek genetikos   ──→ genetic
  parasites       ←── Greek parasitos   ──→ parasites
  pollution       ←── Latin polluere    ──→ pollution

📌 RULE: If a technical word LOOKS like an English or international word, it almost certainly MEANS the same thing. Trust the cognate.

But beware of FALSE COGNATES (Chapter 3 warned about these):

FALSE COGNATE ALERT IN TEXTE 102:
                                                          
  "élevage" does NOT mean "elevation"
  → It means "farming" or "breeding" (from élever = to raise)
  
  "évasion" does NOT mean "evasion" (as in avoiding something)
  → Here it means "escape" (fish literally escaping from farms)
  
  "ferme" does NOT mean "firm/solid"
  → Here it means "farm" (une ferme = a farm)
  
  These are CONTEXTUAL false friends — same word,
  different meaning depending on domain.

STRATEGY 2: THE COMPOUND TERM DECODER

Technical French frequently builds complex terms by combining simple words. The strategy: break the compound into its parts.

COMPOUND TERM DECODING:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "poux de mer"
  ┌──────┐   ┌──────┐
  │ poux │ + │ mer  │
  │=lice │   │=sea  │
  └──────┘   └──────┘
  → sea lice (a type of parasite)
  
  Even if you don't know "poux":
  The text says "prolifération des poux de mer"
  + it's listed as a PROBLEM of fish farming
  → It must be something BAD that MULTIPLIES in the sea
  → PARASITE is the logical conclusion

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "métaux lourds"
  ┌───────┐   ┌───────┐
  │métaux │ + │lourds │
  │=metals│   │=heavy │
  └───────┘   └───────┘
  → heavy metals (toxic chemical elements)
  
  Context confirms: listed alongside "dioxydes" 
  and "pesticides" as TOXIC substances

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "patrimoine génétique"
  ┌───────────┐   ┌──────────┐
  │patrimoine │ + │génétique │
  │=heritage/ │   │=genetic  │
  │ patrimony │   │          │
  └───────────┘   └──────────┘
  → genetic heritage / genetic patrimony (DNA pool)
  
  Context: "mettent en péril le patrimoine génétique 
  des saumons sauvages"
  → Something about wild salmon DNA being ENDANGERED

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "traitements chimiques"
  ┌────────────┐   ┌──────────┐
  │traitements │ + │chimiques │
  │=treatments │   │=chemical │
  └────────────┘   └──────────┘
  → chemical treatments
  
  Context: they want to "réduire" (reduce) these
  and "remplacer par des techniques naturelles"
  → Chemical treatments are the CURRENT method
  → Natural techniques are the DESIRED alternative

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "produits marins"
  ┌─────────┐   ┌───────┐
  │produits │ + │marins │
  │=products│   │=marine│
  └─────────┘   └───────┘
  → marine products (seafood)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "saumon d'élevage" vs "saumon sauvage"
  
  saumon + d'élevage = salmon + of farming = FARMED salmon
  saumon + sauvage   = salmon + wild       = WILD salmon
  
  THE ENTIRE ARTICLE IS ABOUT THIS OPPOSITION.

📌 RULE: Never panic at a long French technical expression. Break it into 2 or 3 simple words. Decode each part. Reassemble.

STRATEGY 3: THE LISTING PATTERN DECODER

Scientific and technical texts love lists. When a text lists items together, they belong to the same category. If you know ONE item in the list, you can infer the category of the others.

LIST PATTERN IN TEXTE 102, PARAGRAPH 3:

  "des produits toxiques comme des dioxydes,
   des pesticides et même des métaux lourds"
                                                          
  LIST STRUCTURE:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "produits toxiques" = CATEGORY LABEL  │
  │                                        │
  │  "comme" = such as (introduces examples│
  │                                        │
  │   ① dioxydes     ─┐                   │
  │   ② pesticides   ─┼── EXAMPLES        │
  │   ③ métaux lourds─┘                   │
  │                                        │
  │  "et même" = and even (escalation)     │
  │  → métaux lourds is the WORST one      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────┘

How this helps you decode:

  • You might not know dioxydes
  • But the category label says "produits toxiques" = toxic products
  • And pesticides is an international word you recognize
  • Therefore dioxydes must also be a type of toxic substance
  • "et même" tells you métaux lourds is the most dangerous of the three
ANOTHER LIST IN TEXTE 102, PARAGRAPH 3:

  "deux problèmes : la prolifération des poux de mer 
   et l'évasion des saumons d'élevage"
   
  LIST STRUCTURE:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  "deux problèmes" = CATEGORY LABEL    │
  │                    = TWO PROBLEMS      │
  │                                        │
  │   ① prolifération des poux de mer     │
  │   ② évasion des saumons d'élevage     │
  │                                        │
  │  Both are PROBLEMS caused by farming   │
  └────────────────────────────────────────┘
  
  Even if you don't know "poux de mer":
  → It's a PROBLEM
  → It PROLIFERATES (= multiplies rapidly)
  → It's in the SEA (de mer)
  → It threatens WILD salmon
  → CONCLUSION: It's a parasite or pest that spreads

📌 RULE: In a list, the CATEGORY LABEL tells you what every item means. Find the label ("produits toxiques", "deux problèmes"), and the unknown items become decodable.

STRATEGY 4: THE OPPOSITION DECODER

Technical texts often explain concepts through contrasts. When you see opposition markers, the unknown term is the OPPOSITE of the known term.

OPPOSITIONS IN TEXTE 102:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

  OPPOSITION 1:
  "saumon d'élevage" ←──VS──→ "saumon sauvage"
   farmed salmon                wild salmon
   
  If you know "sauvage" = wild,
  then "d'élevage" = NOT wild = farmed/raised

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  OPPOSITION 2:
  "traitements chimiques" ←──VS──→ "techniques naturelles"
   chemical treatments              natural techniques
   
  "réduire les traitements chimiques 
   et les REMPLACER PAR des techniques naturelles"
   
  REMPLACER PAR = replace with
  → Chemical is CURRENT (bad)
  → Natural is DESIRED (good)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  OPPOSITION 3:
  "saumon d'élevage" ←──VS──→ "saumon bio"
   industrial salmon             organic salmon
   
  Paragraph 4: "même le saumon bio est pollué"
  → "même" = EVEN → if EVEN bio salmon is polluted,
     then industrial salmon is OBVIOUSLY polluted
  → "bio" must mean something BETTER/PURER than industrial
  → "bio" = organic/biological farming

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  OPPOSITION 4 (Implied):
  "la demande" ←──VS──→ "les problèmes"
   market demand            environmental problems
   
  Paragraph 1 = demand grows → positive (economics)
  Paragraph 2 = problems arise → negative (ecology)
  
  THE WHOLE TEXT IS BUILT ON THIS MACRO-OPPOSITION:
  Economic success VS Environmental damage

📌 RULE: When a technical term appears OPPOSITE another term you DO understand, the unknown word means the reverse. Oppositions are free vocabulary lessons hidden in the text.

DO 2: USE THE "WHAT FAMILY DOES THIS WORD BELONG TO?" TEST

When you encounter an unknown technical word, ask: "What family of words does this belong to?"

EXAMPLE: You don't know "prolifération"

  WORD FAMILY TEST:
  → Does it look like "profit"? No.
  → Does it look like "prolific"? YES!
  → "Prolific" = producing abundantly
  → "Prolifération" = rapid multiplication/spread
  → Context: "prolifération des poux de mer"
  → Sea lice are MULTIPLYING RAPIDLY ✓
EXAMPLE: You don't know "patrimoine"

  WORD FAMILY TEST:
  → Does it contain "patri-"? YES
  → "Patri-" = father (Latin pater)
  → Related words: patrimony, patriarchal, paternal
  → "Patrimoine" = heritage, inherited wealth
  → "Patrimoine génétique" = genetic heritage = DNA pool
  → Context: wild salmon's DNA pool is threatened ✓
EXAMPLE: You don't know "enceintes" in "femmes enceintes"

  WORD FAMILY TEST:
  → "Femmes" = women
  → Context: "consommation modérée pour les enfants 
     et les femmes enceintes"
  → Children AND [type of] women should eat less salmon
  → What type of women are VULNERABLE like children?
  → PREGNANT women ✓
  → "Enceintes" = pregnant

DO 3: TRACK THE ESCALATION PATTERN

Technical texts about problems almost always escalate — each paragraph reveals a BIGGER problem than the last. Recognizing this pattern helps you understand terms through their position in the escalation:

  LEVEL 1 (Paragraph 1):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Norway produces a LOT of salmon │ → Neutral (economics)
  │ Demand is growing               │
  └────────────────┬────────────────┘
                   ▼
  LEVEL 2 (Paragraph 2):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │ This scale creates PROBLEMS     │ → Concerning (ecology)
  │ Wild salmon is at risk          │
  └────────────────┬────────────────┘
                   ▼
  LEVEL 3 (Paragraph 3):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Specific problems: parasites    │ → Serious (biology)
  │ + escaping fish + chemicals     │
  │ + toxic substances in salmon    │
  └────────────────┬────────────────┘
                   ▼
  LEVEL 4 (Paragraph 4):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐

  │ The model must be CHANGED       │
  └─────────────────────────────────┘
                                                          
  ESCALATION: Neutral → Concerning → Serious → Alarming

Why this helps with vocabulary: When you encounter an unknown term in Level 4, you know it must represent something MORE SERIOUS than what came before. This constrains the possible meanings.

EXAMPLE: "même le saumon bio est pollué par... le mercure"

  You might not know "mercure"
  But you know:
  → This is Level 4 (most alarming)
  → It's worse than "dioxydes" and "pesticides" (Level 3)
  → It's a specific example of "métaux lourds" 
  → "Notamment" = especially/in particular
  → So "mercure" is the MOST NOTABLE heavy metal
  → Mercury is the most famous toxic heavy metal ✓

  traitements chimiques..chemical treatments (Q3 reference)
  produits toxiques...toxic products (core problem)
  métaux lourds......heavy metals (core problem)
  pollution..........pollution (core problem)
  
NICE TO KNOW (background comprehension):
═══════════════════════════════════════
  ferme offshore.....offshore farm (illustrative detail)
  dioxydes...........dioxides (one example among many)
  mercure............mercury (specific detail)
  patrimoine génétique..genetic heritage (supporting detail)
  chiffre d'affaire..revenue (economic context only)
  
DON'T NEED TO KNOW (zero impact on answers):
═══════════════════════════════════════════
  "Marine Harvest"...company name (just a label)
  "7,5 milliards"....specific number (just a detail)
  "Europe Ecologie Les Verts"...party name (just context)

📌 Focus your decoding energy on NEED TO KNOW terms. Don't spend time decoding a company name or a political party label when you could be understanding the core argument.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: TREATING TECHNICAL TERMS AS IMPOSSIBLE WALLS

WRONG MINDSET:
  "I see 'patrimoine génétique des saumons sauvages' 
   and I have NO IDEA what that means."
   → Gives up on the paragraph
   → Skips to next question
   → Gets it wrong

RIGHT MINDSET:
  "patrimoine" → heritage/patrimony (cognate)
  "génétique"  → genetic (cognate)
  "saumons"    → salmon (cognate)
  "sauvages"   → wild/savage (cognate)
  → "The genetic heritage of wild salmon"
  → In context: wild salmon's DNA is at risk
  
  FOUR OUT OF FOUR words were cognates.
  The "impossible" expression was actually easy.

MISTAKE 2: MEMORIZING ISOLATED TECHNICAL WORDS WITHOUT CONTEXT

WRONG APPROACH:
  Flashcard: "poux de mer" = "sea lice" 
  → You memorize the translation
  → On exam day, you recognize the word
  → But you don't understand WHY it matters in the text
  → You can't connect it to the question

RIGHT APPROACH:
  "Poux de mer" = sea lice
  → They PROLIFERATE because of industrial farming
  → They threaten WILD salmon's ecosystem
  → They are one of TWO MAIN PROBLEMS (with fish escape)
  → The industry uses CHEMICAL TREATMENTS to fight them
  → But chemicals create ANOTHER problem (toxic products)
  
  NOW you understand the CHAIN, not just the word.

MISTAKE 3: ASSUMING TECHNICAL = COMPLICATED MEANING

WRONG ASSUMPTION:
  "évasion des saumons d'élevage" must be some complex 
  scientific concept I don't understand...
  
REALITY:
  "évasion" = escape (think: prison evasion)
  "des saumons" = of salmon
  "d'élevage" = from farms
  
  → Farmed salmon ESCAPE from their pens into the ocean
  → They mix with wild salmon
  → This contaminates the wild gene pool
  
  IT'S LITERALLY FISH ESCAPING FROM CAGES.
  The concept is simple. Only the vocabulary sounds technical.

📌 RULE: Technical language often describes SIMPLE concepts with COMPLEX words. Strip away the jargon and ask: "What is actually HAPPENING here?"

MISTAKE 4: IGNORING THE EXPERT'S INSTITUTIONAL IDENTITY

WRONG: Reading the quote without noting WHO is speaking

  «Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts...» 
  → Who is "nous"? Unclear if you skip the attribution.

RIGHT: Always read the ATTRIBUTION TAG

  "indique Catarina Martins, directrice environnement 
   de la compagnie Marine Harvest"
   
  → She is the ENVIRONMENTAL DIRECTOR
  → Of a FISH FARMING COMPANY
  → "Nous" = her company = the industry
  → Her quote is the INDUSTRY'S DEFENSE
  → She is not a neutral scientist — she has a business interest
  
  This changes HOW you interpret every word she says.

Chapter 12 explored cause-and-effect chains in this text. Chapter 21 will go deeper into quote attribution. Here, the key point is: the expert's job title tells you their vocabulary BIAS.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "COGNATE CONFIDENCE" TRAP

SYMPTOM: You see a cognate and assume it means exactly 
         what it means in English, without checking context.

DANGER CASE: "évasion"
  
  In English: "evasion" = avoiding, dodging (tax evasion)
  In this text: "évasion" = PHYSICAL ESCAPE of fish from pens
  
  If you read "évasion des saumons" as "salmon avoidance"
  → You completely misunderstand the second problem
  → The text means fish PHYSICALLY ESCAPING into the ocean
  
  ANOTHER DANGER CASE: "ferme"
  
  In English: "firm" = solid, or a company
  In this text: "ferme" = FARM
  "ferme offshore" = offshore FARM, not offshore FIRM
  
FIX: Always verify cognates against the SENTENCE context.
     Does "farm" make more sense than "firm" when talking 
     about housing one million salmon? Obviously yes.

PITFALL 2: THE DENSITY OVERWHELM

SYMPTOM: Paragraph 3 has SO many technical terms that 
         your brain shuts down entirely.

  "L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes en ce qui 
   concerne le saumon : la prolifération des poux de mer 
   et l'évasion des saumons d'élevage. Ces deux 
   conséquences de l'élevage à très grande échelle mettent 
   en péril l'écosystème et le patrimoine génétique des 
   saumons sauvages."

  TECHNICAL TERM COUNT IN THIS PASSAGE: 11 terms
  SENTENCE COUNT: 2 sentences
  
  → ~5.5 technical terms per sentence
  → Brain overload threshold: ~3 per sentence
  → RESULT: Comprehension crashes

FIX: THE SKELETON TECHNIQUE
  Strip the sentence to its GRAMMATICAL BONES:
  
  "L'élevage pose deux problèmes : [A] et [B]."
  → Farming creates two problems: A and B.
  
  "Ces deux conséquences mettent en péril [X] et [Y]."
  → These two consequences endanger X and Y.
  
  SKELETON = farming creates 2 problems → they endanger 2 things
  
  NOW fill in the details:
  Problem A = poux de mer proliferating (parasites spreading)
  Problem B = saumons d'élevage escaping (farmed fish escaping)
  Endangered X = écosystème (ecosystem)
  Endangered Y = patrimoine génétique des saumons sauvages 
                 (wild salmon DNA)

PITFALL 3: CONFUSING TECHNICAL CATEGORIES

SYMPTOM: You mix up which technical terms belong 
         to which PROBLEM.

  THE TEXT DESCRIBES MULTIPLE LAYERS OF PROBLEMS:
  
  Layer 1: Production problems
    → parasites (poux de mer)
    → fish escape (évasion)
    
  Layer 2: Treatment problems  
    → chemical treatments needed (traitements chimiques)
    → chemicals are toxic to humans
    
  Layer 3: Environmental problems
    → water pollution (pollution des eaux)
    → affects ALL fish, not just farmed
    → even organic salmon is contaminated
    
  COMMON CONFUSION:
  Students think "poux de mer" are toxic to HUMANS.
  NO — poux de mer are parasites on FISH.
  The toxic substances for HUMANS are dioxydes, 
  pesticides, and heavy metals.
  
  Parasites ≠ Toxins
  Fish problems ≠ Human health problems
  
  KEEPING THESE CATEGORIES SEPARATE IS ESSENTIAL
  FOR ANSWERING Q1, Q2, AND Q3 CORRECTLY.

PITFALL 4: NOT RECOGNIZING THE "MÊME" ESCALATION MARKER

DANGER WORD: "même" (= even)

  "Aujourd'hui MÊME le saumon bio est pollué"
  = Today EVEN organic salmon is polluted
  
  "MÊME" is an ESCALATION MARKER that means:
  → What follows is SURPRISING
  → It goes BEYOND what you'd expect
  → If even the BEST case (organic) is affected...
  → Then the NORMAL case (industrial) is obviously worse
  
  Students who miss "même" think this sentence is just 
  repeating the previous information about farmed salmon.
  
  IT'S NOT. It's ESCALATING:
  "Not just farmed salmon, but EVEN organic salmon"
  = The problem is BIGGER than the industry admits
  
  SIMILARLY: "et même des métaux lourds"
  = and EVEN heavy metals
  → Heavy metals are presented as the WORST in the list
  → Worse than dioxides, worse than pesticides
