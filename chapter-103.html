📖 START → COMPLETE ARTICLE

La télévision sous contrôle médical

Ils se trouvent trop gras, veulent perdre du poids, et ont décidé de le faire savoir à tout le monde. Six candidats à l'amaigrissement n'ont pas hésité à se montrer en caleçon dans une émission télévisée pour convaincre le public de la justesse de leur combat. Grâce à la mobilisation de six équipes de journalistes pendant quatre mois, les curieux pourront observer l'évolution de leurs formes et de leurs courbes de poids. Un nouvel avatar de ces programmes de télé réalité qui envahissent nos écrans ? « Absolument pas », assurent en chœur les dirigeants de la chaîne de télévision qui préfèrent parler de « feuilleton documentaire » ou de « docu-vérité ». Et d'avancer un argument… de poids : il s'agit de « faire une œuvre utile » en sensibilisant le téléspectateur à un problème de santé publique, dans un pays qui compte 15 % d'adultes obèses. Pas question ici de titiller les penchants voyeuristes du public. La preuve ? Six médecins ont accepté de prêter leur concours à l'entreprise, en prenant en charge chacun un patient qui testera sa méthode, sous le regard des caméras. Dès le lendemain de sa mise à l'antenne, l'émission a soulevé un tollé chez les médecins, certains jugeant « cynique ou inconsciente » la démarche de leurs confrères. Une quarantaine de médecins, universitaires et chercheurs ont fait part de leur désaccord dans une lettre publiée dans un quotidien national. Ils se désolidarisent de ces médecins qui apportent leur caution médicale à des messages aberrants et ils mettent en garde, en particulier les jeunes, contre les risques d'une quête effrénée de minceur. Les médecins qui participent à l'émission refusent ces leçons de morale. « Les gens attendent de nous des solutions à leurs problèmes de poids. Ils sont assez malins pour faire le tri entre ce qui relève de l'information et du divertissement ». La polémique a le mérite de mettre le doigt sur une composante essentielle, mais jusqu'alors peu évoquée, de la téléréalité : le médecin-alibi. Plus la téléréalité progresse, plus elle fait prendre des risques physiques ou psychiques aux candidats et plus les médecins sont mis en avant. Producteurs et diffuseurs se donnent ainsi bonne conscience et les éventuels annonceurs, soucieux de ne pas associer leur marque à un programme avilissant, sont rassurés.

The "Médecin-alibi" concept — the text's intellectual core

The author's thesis is concentrated in a single coined term: "le médecin-alibi" (the alibi-doctor).

What does this mean?

In criminal law, an alibi is proof that you were somewhere else when the crime was committed — it establishes your innocence. By extension, an "alibi" in everyday French means something that provides a cover of legitimacy for questionable behavior.

A "médecin-alibi" is therefore: a doctor whose presence provides a cover of medical legitimacy for a show that is actually entertainment, not healthcare.

The author's argument:

REALITY TV becomes more dangerous → producers need LEGITIMACY
Doctors are brought in → they provide MEDICAL COVER
Producers feel justified → "we have doctors, so it's responsible"
Advertisers feel safe → "there are doctors, so it's not degrading"
THE TRUTH (author's thesis): The doctors are ALIBIS, not caregivers
Their role is to LEGITIMIZE, not to TREAT

This concept is what Q35 tests. And it is ONLY articulated by the author — none of the three debating voices uses this term or this analysis.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 32 — What do the TV producers claim?

The question: Les réalisateurs de l'émission affirment...

"Réalisateurs" = the people who made/directed the show. "Affirment" = claim/assert. This targets VOICE 1 only.

Step 1: Isolate Voice 1's claims.

The producers make several claims:

"« Absolument pas », assurent en chœur les dirigeants de la chaîne qui préfèrent parler de « feuilleton documentaire » ou de « docu-vérité »."

Claim 1: This is NOT reality TV — it's a "documentary series" or "docu-truth."

"Et d'avancer un argument… de poids : il s'agit de « faire une œuvre utile » en sensibilisant le téléspectateur à un problème de santé publique, dans un pays qui compte 15 % d'adultes obèses."

Claim 2: The show does "useful work" by raising public awareness about a public health issue (obesity).

"Pas question ici de titiller les penchants voyeuristes du public."

Claim 3: This is NOT about exploiting voyeurism.

"La preuve ? Six médecins ont accepté de prêter leur concours"

Claim 4: The proof is that doctors agreed to participate — medical endorsement validates the show.

Step 3: The "sensibiliser" vs. "régler" distinction — the critical trap.

Option B is the most dangerous trap because it SOUNDS like what the producers say:

PRODUCERS SAY:  "sensibiliser le téléspectateur" (raise awareness)
OPTION B SAYS:  "régler un des plus graves problèmes" (solve a major problem)

"Sensibiliser" and "régler" are in the same DOMAIN (health) but describe vastly different ACTIONS:

VerbMeaningScope
SensibiliserTo raise awareness / make people conscious ofINFORMATION — modest claim
RéglerTo solve / resolve / fixACTION — enormous claim

The producers make a MODEST claim (awareness). Option B inflates it into an ENORMOUS claim (solving the problem). This is the claim inflation trap — the exam exaggerates a moderate statement into an extreme one.

✅ Answer: D

🔑 Final course technique #1: THE CLAIM INFLATION TRAP

Throughout this course, you've encountered paraphrases that MATCH the text at different vocabulary levels. The claim inflation trap is the OPPOSITE: it takes a modest claim and INFLATES it:

Text (modest)Trap option (inflated)The inflation
"Sensibiliser" (raise awareness)"Régler" (solve)Awareness → Solution
"Un des problèmes" (one of the problems)"Le plus grave problème" (the gravest problem)One problem → The worst problem
"Prêter leur concours" (lend support)"Garantir la guérison" (guarantee cure)Support → Guarantee

Always check whether an option INFLATES the original claim beyond what the text actually states.

Step 4: The "participants" vs. "jeunes" distinction — option B's subtle misdirection.

Option B says the show risks harming its "participants" (the six people on the show). But the protesting doctors' warning is about "les jeunes" (young people in general) — viewers who might be inspired to pursue dangerous thinness.

OPTION B's target:  The six participants ON the show
TEXT's target:      Young people WATCHING the show

These are DIFFERENT populations:
Participants = adults who chose to be on TV
Young viewers = impressionable people influenced by what they see

This is a target substitution trap — the option correctly identifies the CONCERN (risks of extreme thinness pursuit) but attributes it to the wrong POPULATION.

Step 5: Why option D captures the CORE of the protest.

The protesting doctors' letter has ONE central message: their colleagues have violated medical ethics by lending their credibility to entertainment.

Every element of their protest points to this:

Text elementEthical dimension
"Cynique ou inconsciente"Their colleagues are either deliberately unethical (cynical) or recklessly unethical (unconscious)
"Se désolidarisent"They formally DISTANCE themselves — a professional ethics gesture
"Caution médicale"Medical ENDORSEMENT — the ethics issue is about lending professional credibility
"Messages aberrants"The messages are ABSURD — unworthy of medical endorsement
"Confrères"COLLEAGUES — the conflict is within the medical profession, about professional standards

This is an intra-professional ethics dispute. Option D captures this perfectly: "indignation devant la violation des règles d'éthique par leurs confrères."

An option can correctly describe an ACTION but attribute it to the wrong TARGET:

CORRECT ACTION:  Warning about risks of extreme thinness pursuit ✓
WRONG TARGET:    Risks to participants (option B)
CORRECT TARGET:  Risks to young viewers (text says "les jeunes")

QUESTION 35 — The author's hidden thesis

The question: Selon l'auteur, le recours des émissions de téléréalité à une caution médicale...

This is the FINAL QUESTION of the entire course. It targets VOICE 4 — the author's OWN analysis, found ONLY in the last paragraph. This is the "Selon l'auteur" question type from Chapter 20, now at its absolute peak of difficulty.

Step 1: Isolate Voice 4 — the author's analysis.

The author's voice emerges in the FINAL paragraph, clearly separated from the three debating voices:

"La polémique a le mérite de mettre le doigt sur une composante essentielle, mais jusqu'alors peu évoquée, de la téléréalité : le médecin-alibi."

The author's KEY CONCEPT: the "médecin-alibi" — doctors used as alibis.

"Plus la téléréalité progresse, plus elle fait prendre des risques physiques ou psychiques aux candidats et plus les médecins sont mis en avant."

The author's OBSERVATION: as reality TV becomes MORE dangerous, doctors are pushed MORE prominently to the front.

"Producteurs et diffuseurs se donnent ainsi bonne conscience et les éventuels annonceurs, soucieux de ne pas associer leur marque à un programme avilissant, sont rassurés."

The author's CONCLUSION: doctors provide MORAL COVER for producers and REASSURANCE for advertisers.

Step 2: Reconstruct the author's complete argument.

OBSERVATION:  Reality TV is getting more dangerous
MECHANISM:    Doctors are used more prominently as shows get riskier
FUNCTION:     Doctors serve as ALIBIS — providing moral legitimacy
BENEFICIARIES: Producers (clear conscience) + Advertisers (reassured)
THESIS:       Medical endorsement is a COVER for increasingly dangerous entertainment

The thesis is NOT that:

  • The show is good or bad (that's the debate between Voices 1-3)
  • Doctors should or shouldn't participate (that's Voice 2's argument)
  • Viewers are smart or stupid (that's Voice 3's argument)

The thesis IS:

  • Medical presence in reality TV serves as MORAL COVER for practices that are becoming more dangerous

🔑 FINAL COURSE TECHNIQUE: IDENTIFYING THE THESIS THAT TRANSCENDS THE DEBATE

This is the culminating skill of the entire 25-chapter course. In complex texts presenting multiple competing viewpoints, the AUTHOR's thesis is often:

NOT: Taking one side of the debate
NOT: Synthesizing all sides into a compromise
BUT: Revealing the HIDDEN MECHANISM that makes the debate possible

The debate exists BECAUSE of this mechanism.
The author sees THROUGH the debate to the mechanism beneath.

In this text:

  • The debate (useful show vs. unethical show) exists because doctors participate
  • The author asks: WHY do doctors participate? Not for health. For LEGITIMACY.
  • The "médecin-alibi" concept EXPLAINS the debate rather than RESOLVING it

When "Selon l'auteur" appears in a question about a debate text:

  1. Identify ALL debating voices and their positions
  2. Locate the paragraph where the AUTHOR speaks in their OWN voice (usually the last)
  3. Find the concept or term the AUTHOR introduces that NO debating voice uses
  4. This concept IS the thesis
  5. Select the answer that captures this concept

In this text: "médecin-alibi" → "couverture morale" → Option B.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES — THE FINAL CATALOGUE

Pitfall 1: The "Sympathetic Voice" Trap

What happens: You find one voice more sympathetic or convincing than the others and unconsciously attribute the author's authority to that voice.

Example: The protesting doctors (Voice 2) seem morally righteous — they defend ethics and protect youth. You might assume the AUTHOR agrees with them and select Voice 2's argument for Q35 ("selon l'auteur").

Why it fails: The author's thesis ("médecin-alibi") goes BEYOND Voice 2's critique. Voice 2 says "our colleagues violated ethics." The author says "doctors are being USED as moral alibis by an industry." These are different claims at different levels of analysis.

🔑 Final rule: "Selon l'auteur" NEVER asks you to identify which debating voice the author agrees with. It asks for the author's OWN, INDEPENDENT analysis.

Pitfall 2: The "Everything is in the Text" Overconfidence

What happens: You see a word from the text in an answer option and assume it must be correct because "it's in the text."

Example: The text says "15 % d'adultes obèses." Option A says "15 % des téléspectateurs." The number "15%" IS in the text — but it refers to French adults, not to viewers. The REFERENT has been changed.

🔑 Final rule: A number or phrase FROM the text can appear in a WRONG answer if its REFERENT has been changed. Always check: "15% of WHAT?"

Pitfall 3: The "Close but Wrong Register" Trap

What happens: An option uses vocabulary from the correct DOMAIN but the wrong REGISTER within that domain.

Example: Q33C says the doctors warn about "anorexie" (anorexia). The text says "quête effrénée de minceur" (frenzied quest for thinness). These are in the same DOMAIN (eating disorders) but at different REGISTERS:

TextOption
"Quête effrénée de minceur" (behavioral description)"Anorexie" (clinical diagnosis)

The text describes a BEHAVIOR (obsessive pursuit of thinness). The option names a DIAGNOSIS (anorexia). The protesting doctors describe a societal risk, not a clinical condition.

🔑 Final rule: Behavioral descriptions ≠ clinical diagnoses. Societal warnings ≠ medical recommendations. Check the REGISTER within the domain.
