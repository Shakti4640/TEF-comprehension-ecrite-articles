Why medical gap-fills are the hardest type

In a business text, the order of events can sometimes be flexible. But in a medical text:

❌ You CANNOT vomit before feeling nauseous
❌ You CANNOT faint before turning pale
❌ You CANNOT rank seasickness after car sickness if the 
   text says "the most reported is..."

Medical sequences have a biological logic that constrains which gap-fill can go where. If your chosen sentence breaks the symptom sequence, it's WRONG — even if it sounds plausible in isolation.

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: BIOLOGICAL SEQUENCE CONSTRAINT
In medical gap-fills, the human body dictates the order. Mild symptoms precede severe symptoms. General categories precede specific types. Human experience precedes animal comparison. This biological/logical order is NON-NEGOTIABLE.

The anatomy of THIS specific text

TYPE:        Article médical vulgarisé (popularized medical article)
LENGTH:      ~180 words (short, but dense with gaps)
STRUCTURE:   
  Section 1 = PREVALENCE (how common is it?)
  Section 2 = SYMPTOMS (what happens to you?)
  Section 3 = TYPES BY TRANSPORT (ranking by frequency)
  Section 4 = ANIMAL COMPARISON (humans aren't alone)
  Section 5 = TREATMENT (hope for the future)
DIFFICULTY:  ★★★★☆ (4/5)
TRAP LEVEL:  ★★★★☆ (4/5) — contradiction traps + sequence traps

💡 KEY TECHNIQUE FROM PROJECTS 13-15: Always read BEFORE and AFTER the gap. The gap must create a BRIDGE between what precedes and what follows. If either connection breaks, the choice is wrong.

The COLON Decoder — New for This Project

This text uses colons (:) after two gaps (39 and 40). The colon is your most powerful clue in gap-fills:

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: THE COLON AS GAP-FILL COMPASS
In French, a colon (:) means "here is the proof/explanation/example of what I just said." What comes AFTER the colon EXPLAINS or ILLUSTRATES what comes BEFORE.

[GAP 39] : "nos amies les bêtes...y sont également sensibles"
           ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
           This PROVES/EXPLAINS what Gap 39 claims

[GAP 40] : "on peut désormais traiter en quelques séances"
           ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
           This PROVES/EXPLAINS what Gap 40 claims

Therefore:

  • Gap 39 must make a CLAIM that "animals are also sensitive" PROVES
  • Gap 40 must make a CLAIM that "can now be treated" PROVES

This technique alone eliminates most wrong answers instantly.

🧩 STEP 3: HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 36: First gap — Prevalence confirmation

Context:

BEFORE: "Le mal des transports est une AFFECTION COURANTE."
                                       ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
[GAP 36]
AFTER:  "On considère QU'UNE PERSONNE SUR TROIS en souffrira 
         au moins une fois dans sa vie."
         ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^

What the gap MUST do:

REQUIREMENT 1: Must be COMPATIBLE with "affection courante" (common)
               → Cannot say it's rare or affects few people

REQUIREMENT 2: Must LEAD logically to the "1 in 3" statistic
               → Must set up a quantitative statement

REQUIREMENT 3: Must be a BRIDGE — confirming commonality AND 
               introducing the scale of the problem

Eliminate systematically:

Choice A: "Elle atteint une partie infime de la population."

"partie infime" = tiny/minuscule portion

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct Contradiction trap. The sentence before says it's "courante" (common). "Partie infime" (tiny portion) CONTRADICTS common. And the sentence after says 1 in 3 — which is NOT a tiny portion. This choice contradicts BOTH the sentence before AND the sentence after.

🆕 NEW GAP-FILL CONCEPT: THE DOUBLE CONTRADICTION TEST
A gap-fill choice must be compatible with BOTH the sentence before AND the sentence after. If it contradicts EITHER one, it's wrong. If it contradicts BOTH, it's catastrophically wrong. Choice A fails both directions.

Choice C: "Ce trouble rare existe pourtant depuis la nuit des temps."

"trouble rare" = rare disorder

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct Contradiction trap. Same problem as Choice A: "rare" contradicts "courante." The text just told us it's COMMON. Calling it "rare" in the very next sentence creates a logical absurdity.

Note: "depuis la nuit des temps" (since the dawn of time) is irrelevant information — the text never discusses the HISTORY of motion sickness, only its PREVALENCE and SYMPTOMS.

Choice D: "On ignore encore l'importance des populations atteintes."

"On ignore encore" = We still don't know
"l'importance des populations atteintes" = the scale of affected populations

❌ ELIMINATED — Forward Contradiction trap (new variant). If we "still don't know" the scale, how can the NEXT sentence give a PRECISE statistic ("une personne sur trois")? The sentence after the gap provides EXACT numbers — which proves we DO know. Choice D says we DON'T know. The sentence after PROVES we DO.

🆕 NEW GAP-FILL TRAP: FORWARD CONTRADICTION
The gap-fill choice contradicts not the sentence BEFORE, but the sentence AFTER. The information following the gap DISPROVES the claim made in the gap. Always check: does the next sentence CONFIRM or DENY what the gap says?

Choice B: "Elle touche de façon chronique nombre de personnes."

"Elle touche" = It affects
"de façon chronique" = chronically / on an ongoing basis
"nombre de personnes" = many people

Verification — Double Bridge Test:

BRIDGE BACKWARD: "affection courante" → "touche nombre de personnes"
                  common condition    →  affects many people
                  ✅ COMPATIBLE (common = many affected)

BRIDGE FORWARD:  "nombre de personnes" → "une personne sur trois"
                  many people          →  1 in 3
                  ✅ COMPATIBLE (many people = 1 in 3 is precise proof)

✅ CORRECT. Choice B confirms the commonality stated before ("courante" → "nombre de personnes") and leads naturally to the quantification that follows ("nombre de personnes" → "une personne sur trois"). The word "chronique" adds that this is not occasional but ongoing — which the rest of the text supports with its detailed symptom descriptions.

✅ Answer 36: B

QUESTION 37: Second gap — First symptoms before "ensuite"

Context:

BEFORE: [End of prevalence section — demographics]
[GAP 37]
AFTER:  "ENSUITE viennent les nausées et les vomissements."
         ^^^^^^^

The CRITICAL word is "ENSUITE" (then/next).

🆕 NEW GAP-FILL CONCEPT: SEQUENCE TRIGGER WORDS
When the sentence AFTER a gap begins with a SEQUENCE word, the gap MUST contain the PREVIOUS step in that sequence:

"Ensuite" (then)     → Gap must contain what happens FIRST
"De plus" (moreover)  → Gap must contain the FIRST point
"En revanche" (however) → Gap must contain the opposite claim
"C'est pourquoi" (that's why) → Gap must contain the CAUSE

"Ensuite viennent les nausées" means nausea is the SECOND stage. Therefore, Gap 37 must describe the FIRST stage of symptoms.

What the gap MUST do:

REQUIREMENT 1: Must describe INITIAL symptoms (what happens FIRST)
REQUIREMENT 2: These symptoms must be MILDER than nausea/vomiting
               (because they come BEFORE in the progression)
REQUIREMENT 3: Must be a SYMPTOM description, not a general 
               statement about victims

Eliminate systematically:

Choice A: "Chaque année arrivent de nouvelles victimes."

❌ ELIMINATED — Category Mismatch trap. This sentence is about PREVALENCE (how many people are affected each year), not about SYMPTOMS. The text has already finished the prevalence section. Gap 37 opens the SYMPTOMS section. A prevalence statement here would break the text's structural flow.

🆕 NEW GAP-FILL CONCEPT: SECTION BOUNDARY AWARENESS
Texts are organized into SECTIONS (prevalence → symptoms → types → comparison → treatment). A gap-fill choice from the WRONG section will break the text's structure even if it's factually reasonable. Choice A belongs in Section 1 (prevalence), not Section 2 (symptoms).

Choice B: "Les symptômes sont multiples et assez mal définis."

❌ ELIMINATED — Forward Contradiction trap (same as Q36). If symptoms are "assez mal définis" (rather poorly defined), why does the text then proceed to define them with EXTREME precision (pale → drowsy → nauseous → vomiting → cold → salivation → dilated pupils → syncope)? The text gives one of the most DETAILED symptom descriptions possible. Calling them "poorly defined" contradicts everything that follows.

Choice C: "Les personnes fragiles sont souvent les plus atteintes."

❌ ELIMINATED — Category Mismatch + Sequence Break. This is a statement about WHO is affected (fragile people), not about WHAT HAPPENS (symptoms). It belongs in the demographics/prevalence section, not the symptoms section. And critically: it does NOT set up "ensuite" — there's no logical reason why "fragile people are most affected" would be followed by "THEN come nausea and vomiting."

THE "ENSUITE" TEST: Ask yourself: does this sentence create a FIRST STEP that "ensuite" continues? "Fragile people are most affected" → "Then come nausea" — does this make sense? NO. There's no sequence here. The "then" has nothing to follow from.

Choice D: "On commence souvent par blêmir puis par somnoler."

"On commence souvent par" = One often begins by
"blêmir" = turning pale / going white
"puis par" = then by
"somnoler" = becoming drowsy / dozing off

Verification — Sequence Logic Test:

MEDICAL SYMPTOM SEQUENCE:
Step 1: Blêmir (turn pale)        ← MILD, visible
Step 2: Somnoler (become drowsy)  ← MILD, internal
        ↓ [GAP 37 = Choice D]
Step 3: "ENSUITE" nausées         ← MODERATE
Step 4: Vomissements              ← MODERATE-SEVERE
Step 5: Froid, salivation         ← SEVERE
Step 6: Pupille dilatée           ← SEVERE
Step 7: Semi-syncope              ← VERY SEVERE
Step 8: Perte de connaissance     ← MOST SEVERE

BIOLOGICAL LOGIC: ✅ 
Pallor → drowsiness → nausea → vomiting → autonomic responses → syncope
This is the CORRECT medical escalation from mild to severe.

Verification — "Ensuite" Bridge Test:

"On commence souvent par blêmir puis par somnoler"
                                    ↓
"ENSUITE viennent les nausées et les vomissements"

"On commence" (one begins) → "ensuite" (then) 
= PERFECT sequential link
First you turn pale and get drowsy, THEN come nausea and vomiting.
✅ BRIDGE WORKS

✅ CORRECT. Choice D provides the FIRST stage of a medical symptom progression. The words "on commence par" (one begins by) explicitly signal a STARTING POINT, and "ensuite" in the next sentence explicitly signals the CONTINUATION. The symptoms escalate from mild (pallor, drowsiness) to moderate (nausea, vomiting) to severe (syncope) — a biologically correct sequence.

✅ Answer 37: D

QUESTION 38: Third gap — Introducing the ranking of types

Context:

BEFORE: "certains vont jusqu'à perdre connaissance brutalement."
         (some even lose consciousness suddenly)
[GAP 38]
AFTER:  "SUIVENT ALORS le mal de l'air, puis le mal de voiture 
         (chez les enfants) ou de train."
         ^^^^^^^^^^^^^

The CRITICAL phrase is "SUIVENT ALORS" (then follow).

This is ANOTHER sequence trigger word — but this time it introduces a RANKING:

"Suivent alors" = Then follow (in order)
→ What FOLLOWS is a LIST ranked from most to least common
→ "le mal de l'air" comes SECOND
→ "le mal de voiture" comes THIRD
→ Gap 38 must contain what comes FIRST in the ranking

What the gap MUST do:

REQUIREMENT 1: Must introduce the MOST COMMON type of motion 
               sickness (the #1 in the ranking)
REQUIREMENT 2: Must create a logical bridge FROM symptoms 
               TO types of motion sickness
REQUIREMENT 3: "Suivent alors" must make sense as "then follow 
               [these others]" — meaning Gap 38 stated the FIRST

Eliminate systematically:

Choice A: "La fréquence des malaises s'accroît."

"La fréquence des malaises s'accroît" = The frequency of episodes increases

❌ ELIMINATED — Non-Sequitur trap. This sentence is about frequency INCREASING — but the text never discusses whether motion sickness is becoming MORE frequent over time. This is a claim about TRENDS, but the text section is about TYPES. Furthermore, "suivent alors" (then follow) doesn't logically follow from "frequency increases" — there's no ranking being set up.

Choice B: "Cet état ne dure que quelques minutes."

"Cet état ne dure que quelques minutes" = This state lasts only a few minutes

❌ ELIMINATED — Section Mismatch trap. This sentence refers to the DURATION of the syncope/fainting state described BEFORE the gap. While it might seem like a logical comment on syncope, it does NOT set up "suivent alors le mal de l'air..." The transition from "fainting lasts a few minutes" to "then follow airsickness, car sickness" makes no sense. There's no RANKING being introduced.

THE "SUIVENT ALORS" TEST: Ask: does this sentence create a #1 item that "suivent alors" continues with #2, #3? "This state lasts a few minutes" → "Then follow airsickness, car sickness" — NO. Duration of fainting has nothing to do with a ranking of transport types.

Choice D: "On restera prudent devant l'évanouissement."

"On restera prudent devant l'évanouissement" = One should be careful about fainting

❌ ELIMINATED — Category Mismatch trap. This is MEDICAL ADVICE about dealing with fainting. The text section is transitioning from SYMPTOMS to TYPES BY TRANSPORT. Medical advice about fainting doesn't introduce a ranking. And again, "suivent alors" makes no sense after a caution statement.

Choice C: "L'affection la plus déclarée est le mal de mer."

"L'affection la plus déclarée" = The most commonly reported condition
"est le mal de mer" = is seasickness

Verification — Ranking Logic Test:

RANKING ESTABLISHED:
#1: "L'affection la plus déclarée est le MAL DE MER"  ← Gap 38
#2: "Suivent alors le mal de L'AIR"                    ← After gap
#3: "puis le mal de VOITURE ou de TRAIN"               ← After gap

LOGICAL STRUCTURE:
"The most reported is seasickness. THEN FOLLOW airsickness, 
then car/train sickness."

This creates a perfect DESCENDING RANKING:
Sea (most common) → Air → Car/Train (less common)
✅ RANKING LOGIC WORKS

Verification — Bridge Test:

BACKWARD: "perdre connaissance" → "L'affection la plus déclarée..."
          End of symptoms section → Beginning of types section
          ✅ NATURAL TRANSITION (from what it feels like → 
             to where it happens most)

FORWARD:  "le mal de mer" → "SUIVENT ALORS le mal de l'air..."
          #1 (seasickness) → then follow #2, #3
          ✅ PERFECT RANKING CONTINUATION

✅ CORRECT. Choice C introduces the #1 most reported type (seasickness), and "suivent alors" naturally continues with #2 (airsickness), then #3 (car/train sickness). This creates a clean descending ranking. The transition from the symptom section to the transport-type section is smooth and logical.

✅ Answer 38: C

QUESTION 39: Fourth gap — Species comparison transition

Context:

BEFORE: "On peut en souffrir lorsque l'on est à dos de chameau 
         ou d'éléphant, mais rarement à cheval."
         (on camel or elephant yes, horse rarely)
[GAP 39]
AFTER:  ": nos amies les bêtes, notamment les chevaux, vaches, 
          chiens et chats y sont ÉGALEMENT sensibles."
          ^^^^^^^^^                     ^^^^^^^^^

TWO critical clues here:

Clue 1: The COLON (:)

As established in Step 2, a colon means "here is the proof/explanation." What comes AFTER the colon ("animals are also sensitive") must PROVE what the gap CLAIMS.

[GAP 39] : animals are also sensitive
           ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
           This PROVES Gap 39's claim

So Gap 39 must make a claim that IS PROVEN by "animals are also sensitive."

Clue 2: The word "ÉGALEMENT" (also)

"Également" means "also/too" — implying that the gap mentioned SOMEONE ELSE being a victim first, and now animals are being ADDED to that list.

[Someone is a victim] → [GAP 39] → : animals are ALSO sensitive
                                      ^^^^^
"Also" requires a PREVIOUS subject to which animals are being COMPARED

What the gap MUST do:

REQUIREMENT 1: Must state that HUMANS are victims (so animals 
               can be "ALSO" victims)
REQUIREMENT 2: Must be a TRANSITION from humans → animals
REQUIREMENT 3: Must be CONFIRMED by the colon-introduced proof 
               (animals are sensitive too)

Eliminate systematically:

Choice A: "Sauf en cas d'allure non maîtrisée."

"Sauf en cas d'allure non maîtrisée" = Except when the pace is uncontrolled

❌ ELIMINATED — Scope Mismatch trap. This sentence adds a footnote about horses (connecting to "mais rarement à cheval" before the gap). But it does NOT transition to animals as fellow sufferers. And critically: the colon test FAILS. "Except when the pace is uncontrolled : animals are also sensitive" — this makes no logical sense. The proof after the colon doesn't support a claim about pace.

Choice C: "Ce triste privilège appartient seulement à l'homme."

"Ce triste privilège" = This sad privilege
"appartient seulement à l'homme" = belongs only to humans

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct Contradiction with what follows (Forward Contradiction). If motion sickness belongs "SEULEMENT" (only) to humans, then the NEXT sentence — which says animals are "ÉGALEMENT sensibles" (also sensitive) — would be a CONTRADICTION. The gap cannot say "only humans" when the very next sentence says "animals too."

This is the most elegant Forward Contradiction trap in all 25 projects. The word "seulement" (only) creates an ABSOLUTE claim that the colon-introduced sentence IMMEDIATELY disproves. If you test the gap + the sentence after together, the contradiction is obvious.

Choice D: "Les espèces animales en sont curieusement protégées."

"Les espèces animales en sont curieusement protégées" 
= Animal species are curiously protected from it

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct Contradiction with what follows. Same mechanism as Choice C but even more obvious. If animals are "protected" from motion sickness, the next sentence CANNOT say they are "sensibles" (sensitive/susceptible). Protected ≠ sensitive. These are antonyms.

Choice B: "L'homme n'est pas le seul à en être victime."

"L'homme n'est pas le seul" = Humans are not the only ones
"à en être victime" = to be victims of it

Verification — Colon Proof Test:

"L'homme n'est pas le seul à en être victime"
        ↓ COLON
": nos amies les bêtes...y sont ÉGALEMENT sensibles"

CLAIM: Humans are NOT the only victims
PROOF: Animals are ALSO sensitive

Does the proof CONFIRM the claim? 
✅ YES — if animals are also sensitive, then humans are 
indeed NOT the only ones affected.

Verification — "Également" Logic Test:

"L'homme n'est pas le seul" → humans are mentioned first
"également sensibles" → animals are ADDED to the list

"Not only humans" + "animals ALSO" = PERFECT pair
✅ TRANSITION FROM HUMANS → ANIMALS WORKS

Verification — Flow Test:

"...mais rarement à cheval" (humans ON animals)
        ↓
"L'homme n'est pas le seul à en être victime" (PIVOT)
        ↓
": nos amies les bêtes..." (animals THEMSELVES suffer)

The text moves from:
Humans riding animals → Humans aren't alone → Animals suffer too
✅ ELEGANT THREE-STEP TRANSITION

✅ CORRECT. Choice B is the perfect pivot sentence. It takes the subject from HUMANS (who have been the focus of the entire text) and opens the door to ANIMALS (who are introduced after the colon as fellow sufferers). The "not the only ones" + "also" pairing is logically flawless.

✅ Answer 39: B

QUESTION 40: Fifth gap — Transition to treatment/hope

Context:

BEFORE: "nos amies les bêtes, notamment les chevaux, vaches, 
         chiens et chats y sont également sensibles."
         (animals are also sensitive to it)
[GAP 40]
AFTER:  ": on peut DÉSORMAIS traiter en quelques séances de 
          rééducation vestibulaire."
          ^^^^^^^^^

TWO critical clues again:

Clue 1: The COLON (:) — same technique as Gap 39

[GAP 40] : "on peut désormais traiter en quelques séances"
            ^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^^
            This PROVES Gap 40's claim

Gap 40 must make a claim that "one can now treat it in a few sessions" PROVES.

Clue 2: The word "DÉSORMAIS" (from now on / henceforth)

"Désormais" signals a CHANGE — something that was NOT possible before but IS possible now. This implies the gap introduces a SHIFT from hopelessness to hope.

What the gap MUST do:

REQUIREMENT 1: Must signal a POSITIVE TURN (since treatment follows)
REQUIREMENT 2: Must be PROVEN by the colon sentence (treatment exists)
REQUIREMENT 3: Must mark a CHANGE from the negative tone of the 
               entire text (all symptoms, suffering) to HOPE

Eliminate systematically:

Choice B: "Une fatalité à laquelle nul n'échappe."

"Une fatalité à laquelle nul n'échappe" = A fatality nobody escapes

❌ ELIMINATED — Forward Contradiction (Colon Test failure). If it's an inescapable fatality, how can the NEXT sentence say "on peut désormais TRAITER" (one can NOW treat it)? "Nobody escapes" is contradicted by "can now be treated." The colon test fails catastrophically: the proof DISPROVES the claim.

Furthermore, "fatalité" (fatality/inevitability) is an ABSOLUTE negative. The treatment sentence is a POSITIVE breakthrough. An absolute negative cannot be followed by a positive breakthrough — unless there's a contrast word like "mais" (but). There IS no "mais" in Choice B. The sentence presents fatality as a FACT, then the colon "proves" it with... a treatment? Illogical.

Choice C: "Ils n'ont pas accès aux nouveaux remèdes."

"Ils n'ont pas accès aux nouveaux remèdes" = They don't have access to new remedies

❌ ELIMINATED — Forward Contradiction + Subject Confusion trap. "Ils" (they) presumably refers to animals (the last subject mentioned). If animals don't have access to remedies, the NEXT sentence should continue about animal treatment — but it shifts to HUMAN treatment ("on peut traiter" = one can treat). The subject doesn't connect.

Moreover, "n'ont pas accès" (don't have access) is NEGATIVE, but the sentence after the colon is POSITIVE (treatment exists). Without a contrast word, this creates an incoherent flow.

Choice D: "Ce malaise n'a pas fini de faire des dégâts."

"Ce malaise n'a pas fini de faire des dégâts" = This ailment hasn't finished causing damage

🎯 STEP 4: WHAT COULD BE DONE

Strategy 1: The "Three-Test Protocol" for Every Gap

For EVERY gap in EVERY texte à trous, apply these three tests:

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  TEST 1: BACKWARD BRIDGE                        │
│  Does the gap connect logically to what PRECEDES?│
│                                                 │
│  TEST 2: FORWARD BRIDGE                         │
│  Does the gap connect logically to what FOLLOWS? │
│                                                 │
│  TEST 3: SECTION COHERENCE                      │
│  Does the gap belong to the same THEMATIC        │
│  section as its surroundings?                    │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

A choice must PASS all three tests.
Failing even ONE test = elimination.

Strategy 2: The "Colon Equation"

Whenever a gap is followed by a colon, set up this equation:

[GAP] : [sentence after colon]
  ↑           ↑
CLAIM    =   PROOF

The proof must CONFIRM the claim.
If proof CONTRADICTS claim → wrong answer.
If proof is IRRELEVANT to claim → wrong answer.

This technique instantly eliminates 2-3 wrong answers in colon gaps.

Strategy 3: The "Sequence Trigger Word" Decoder

Build a mental dictionary of sequence words and what they demand from the gap:

WORD AFTER GAP    → GAP MUST CONTAIN
──────────────────────────────────────────
"Ensuite"         → The FIRST step (before "next")
"Suivent alors"   → The #1 item in a ranking
"De plus"         → The FIRST point (before "moreover")
"Cependant"       → The CLAIM being contradicted
"C'est pourquoi"  → The CAUSE of the result
"En effet"        → The CLAIM being proven
"Mais"            → The OPPOSITE of what follows
"Également"       → The FIRST subject (before "also")

Strategy 4: The "Medical Escalation" Principle

For health/symptom texts specifically:

MILD → MODERATE → SEVERE → MOST SEVERE

Pale → Drowsy → Nauseous → Vomiting → Cold → 
Salivation → Dilated pupils → Semi-syncope → 
Loss of consciousness

The gap-fill CANNOT break this escalation.
If a choice places a SEVERE symptom before a MILD one,
it's wrong — regardless of how good it sounds.

✅ STEP 5: WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

The 7 Rules for Medical/Scientific Gap-Fills

1. Map the ENTIRE text flow BEFORE solving individual gaps

As shown in Step 2's Flow Map. Understand the text's structure (prevalence → symptoms → types → comparison → treatment) before choosing any answer. This was introduced in Project 13 and is now essential.

2. Apply the Three-Test Protocol to EVERY choice

Backward Bridge + Forward Bridge + Section Coherence. A choice must pass ALL three. One failure = elimination.

3. Use colons as your compass

When a gap is followed by a colon, the sentence after the colon is your KEY to the answer. Set up the Claim → Proof equation and test each choice against it.

4. Respect biological/medical sequence logic

Symptoms must escalate from mild to severe. Rankings must descend from most common to least common. Transitions must flow from one category to the next. The body's logic constrains the text's logic.

5. Watch for sequence trigger words in the sentence AFTER the gap

"Ensuite," "suivent alors," "également," "désormais" — each one tells you EXACTLY what the gap must contain. Decode these words FIRST, then choose.

6. Check for temporal marker alignment

"N'est plus" (no longer) must pair with a word signaling change ("désormais," "maintenant"). "On commence par" (one begins by) must pair with a continuation word ("ensuite," "puis"). If the temporal markers don't align, the choice is wrong.

7. Verify that the gap belongs to the correct SECTION of the text

A prevalence statement doesn't belong in the symptoms section. A symptom description doesn't belong in the treatment section. Match the gap's content to its position in the text's architecture.

❌ STEP 6: WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

❌ SIN 1: Choosing a gap-fill that sounds true IN ISOLATION without checking its surroundings

"Symptoms are multiple and poorly defined" (Choice B, Q37) sounds like a reasonable medical statement. But it CONTRADICTS the highly detailed symptom list that follows. Always check the CONTEXT, not just the sentence alone. (Connects to Project 13's fundamental gap-fill rule.)

❌ SIN 2: Ignoring the colon

The colon is NOT decorative punctuation. It's a LOGICAL OPERATOR meaning "here's the proof." If you ignore it, you lose your most powerful elimination tool. Both Gaps 39 and 40 are solved primarily through colon logic.

❌ SIN 3: Placing a CONTRADICTION before a colon

"Nobody escapes this fatality : one can now treat it" — this is ABSURD. The proof DISPROVES the claim. Never let a choice through that fails the Colon Equation test.

❌ SIN 4: Breaking the medical escalation sequence

If Gap 37 contained a SEVERE symptom (like fainting), and the text then said "ENSUITE come nausea" — you'd be placing a severe symptom BEFORE a moderate one. The body doesn't work that way. Respect the biological order. (New concept in this project.)

❌ SIN 5: Ignoring "seulement" and "également" as paired signals

"Only humans" CANNOT be followed by "animals ALSO." These words create a LOGICAL IMPOSSIBILITY. When you see "seulement" (only) in a gap choice, immediately check if the next sentence says "aussi" or "également" (also). If it does, the choice is wrong. (Connects to Project 1's precision reading of small words.)
