🧠 THE PROBLEM: TEXTS RARELY STATE CAUSE AND EFFECT DIRECTLY

Here is what students expect cause-and-effect to look like:

WHAT STUDENTS EXPECT:

  "Because X happened, Y resulted."
  "X caused Y."
  "Due to X, Y occurred."
  
  CLEAR. DIRECT. LABELED.

Here is what cause-and-effect ACTUALLY looks like in French journalistic texts:

WHAT TEXTS ACTUALLY DO:

  Paragraph 1: "La consommation de saumon augmente..."
  Paragraph 1: "la Norvège doit s'adapter..."
  Paragraph 1: "quitte à industrialiser toujours plus..."
  Paragraph 2: "Cette augmentation constante pousse..."
  Paragraph 2: "Mais l'industrialisation massive pose..."
  Paragraph 3: "L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes..."
  Paragraph 3: "Ces deux conséquences mettent en péril..."
  Paragraph 3: "Le saumon pourrait contenir des produits toxiques..."
  Paragraph 3: "le risque ne concerne plus uniquement..."
  Paragraph 4: "même le saumon bio est pollué..."
  
  The cause-effect chain is SPREAD ACROSS 4 PARAGRAPHS.
  Each sentence is ONE LINK in a LONG CHAIN.
  No single sentence states the FULL chain.
  You must RECONSTRUCT the chain yourself.

📌 In real texts, cause-and-effect is not a single statement. It is a CHAIN of linked events distributed across multiple paragraphs. The exam tests whether you can see the WHOLE chain, not just individual links.

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: ANSWERING CAUSE-EFFECT QUESTIONS

Cause-effect questions can ask about ANY link in the chain. Here are the main types:

TYPE 1: "WHAT CAUSES X?"

EXAMPLE: "What causes problems for wild salmon?"

  STEP 1: Find "wild salmon problems" in the chain
  → Level 4-6: industrialization → sea lice + escapes 
    → ecosystem endangered
  
  STEP 2: Trace BACKWARDS to find the cause
  → IMMEDIATE cause: sea lice proliferation + salmon escapes
  → ROOT cause: massive industrialization of farming
  → ULTIMATE cause: rising global demand
  
  STEP 3: Determine which level the question targets
  → "What causes problems" = IMMEDIATE cause
  → Answer: sea lice and escaped farmed salmon
  
  IMPORTANT: Different questions target different levels:
  → "What directly causes..." = immediate cause
  → "What is the root cause of..." = root cause
  → "What ultimately leads to..." = the full chain

TYPE 2: "WHAT RESULTS FROM X?"

EXAMPLE: "What results from massive industrialization?"

  STEP 1: Find "massive industrialization" in the chain
  → Level 2-3: industrialization → huge projects
  
  STEP 2: Trace FORWARD to find effects
  → IMMEDIATE effect: enormous offshore farms
  → SECONDARY effect: problems for wild salmon
  → FURTHER effects: ecosystem damage, toxic contamination
  
  STEP 3: Determine which level the question targets
  → "What results" = immediate consequences
  → Answer: problems for wild salmon (sea lice, escapes)

TYPE 3: "WHY DOES X HAPPEN?"

EXAMPLE: "Why does Norway recommend moderate salmon consumption?"

  STEP 1: Find "moderate consumption recommendation" in chain
  → Level 9: health warnings (2013)
  
  STEP 2: Trace BACKWARDS
  → IMMEDIATE reason: salmon may contain toxic products
  → DEEPER reason: industrial farming uses chemicals
  → ROOT reason: massive scale of production
  
  STEP 3: Choose the level the text EXPLICITLY connects
  → The text places the recommendation AFTER mentioning 
    toxic products → IMMEDIATE reason is toxins
  → Answer: because salmon may contain toxic substances
    (dioxides, pesticides, heavy metals)

QUESTION 1 REVISITED THROUGH CAUSE-EFFECT LENS

Question 1: L'article explique :
  - Les pratiques utilisées en vigueur dans l'élevage du saumon

CAUSE-EFFECT ANALYSIS:
  
  The article doesn't just LIST practices.
  It shows HOW practices LEAD TO consequences:
  
  Practice: Industrial-scale farming
  → Consequence: Environmental damage
  
  Practice: Chemical treatments
  → Consequence: Toxic contamination risk
  
  Practice: Massive offshore farms
  → Consequence: Wild salmon threatened
  
  The article explains practices IN THE CONTEXT OF 
  their consequences. Not just "what is done" but 
  "what happens BECAUSE of what is done."
  
  A complete answer about what the article explains 
  would capture BOTH the practices AND their effects.

QUESTION 3 REVISITED THROUGH CAUSE-EFFECT LENS

Question 3: Les méthodes utilisées servent à :
  - Eliminer parasites présentes dans les saumons

CAUSE-EFFECT ANALYSIS:

  The text's cause-effect chain for methods:
  
  CAUSE: Industrial farming at scale
  → EFFECT: Sea lice proliferate (PROBLEM)
  → RESPONSE: Chemical treatments used
  → GOAL: Reduce chemicals, replace with natural methods
  
  The answer says: methods serve to ELIMINATE PARASITES
  
  But the text's chain says:
  → Parasites are a PROBLEM (Level 5A)
  → Chemical treatments exist (Level 7)
  → The stated goal is to REDUCE CHEMICALS (Level 7, Martins)
  → NOT to eliminate parasites
  
  The answer SKIPS the text's actual cause-effect chain 
  and creates a FABRICATED LINK (Trap 3):
  methods → eliminate parasites
  
  The text's ACTUAL chain:
  problems (including parasites) → chemicals used → 
  goal: reduce chemicals → replace with natural methods
  
  The PURPOSE of the methods (as stated by Martins) is

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: MAP THE CHAIN BEFORE ANSWERING

THE CHAIN-MAPPING PROTOCOL:

  Before answering any cause-effect question:
  
  1. IDENTIFY the chain's starting point (root cause)
  2. TRACE each link forward to the final effect
  3. MARK each link as explicit, inferred, or attributed
  4. LOCATE the specific link the question asks about
  5. VERIFY your answer against the text's EXACT words
  
  DO NOT skip step 3 — the reliability classification
  determines whether your answer is supported or speculative.

RULE 2: DISTINGUISH IMMEDIATE FROM ROOT CAUSES

THE CAUSE HIERARCHY:

  For any effect, there are MULTIPLE causes at different levels:
  
  EFFECT: "Wild salmon ecosystems are endangered"
  
  IMMEDIATE CAUSE (Level 5→6):
  → Sea lice proliferation + farmed salmon escapes
  → This is what DIRECTLY causes the danger
  
  INTERMEDIATE CAUSE (Level 3→4):
  → Massive industrialization of farming
  → This is what CREATES the conditions for lice/escapes
  
  ROOT CAUSE (Level 0→1):
  → Rising global demand for salmon
  → This is what DRIVES the entire chain
  
  WHICH CAUSE DOES THE QUESTION WANT?
  
  → "Quels problèmes pose l'élevage?" 
    → IMMEDIATE: lice + escapes
    
  → "Pourquoi l'élevage est-il problématique?"
    → INTERMEDIATE: massive industrialization
    
  → "Quelle est l'origine de ces problèmes?"
    → ROOT: rising demand
    
  THE QUESTION'S WORDING tells you which level to target.

RULE 3: FOLLOW THE TEXT'S OWN SIGNAL WORDS

THE SIGNAL-FOLLOWING TECHNIQUE:

  Don't invent cause-effect relationships.
  Follow the ones the TEXT marks with signal words.
  
  TEXT: "Cette augmentation constante de la demande 
        POUSSE le pays scandinave à mettre en place 
        des projets faramineux"
        
  SIGNAL: "pousse... à" (pushes toward)
  CAUSE: augmentation de la demande (demand increase)
  EFFECT: projets faramineux (enormous projects)
  → This link is MARKED by the text → RELIABLE ✅
  
  TEXT: "Ces deux CONSÉQUENCES de l'élevage à très grande 
        échelle METTENT EN PÉRIL l'écosystème"
        
  SIGNAL: "conséquences" + "mettent en péril"
  CAUSE: sea lice + escapes (the "deux conséquences")
  EFFECT: ecosystem endangered
  → This link is MARKED by the text → RELIABLE ✅
  
  UNMARKED LINK:
  "Chemical treatments → toxic contamination"
  → No signal word connects them explicitly
  → The connection is logical but INFERRED
  → Less reliable for exam answers ⚠️

RULE 4: CHECK THE DIRECTION OF CAUSATION

THE DIRECTION CHECK:

  Cause-effect has a DIRECTION: Cause → Effect
  
  CORRECT DIRECTION:
  "Industrialization CAUSES pollution"
  (action → consequence)
  
  REVERSED DIRECTION (TRAP 1):
  "Pollution CAUSES industrialization"
  (consequence → action — BACKWARDS)
  
  HOW TO CHECK:
  Ask: "Does X happen BECAUSE of Y, or does Y happen BECAUSE of X?"
  
  "Does industrialization happen because of pollution?"
  → NO. Industrialization happens because of DEMAND.
  
  "Does pollution happen because of industrialization?"
  → YES. Pollution is a CONSEQUENCE of industrial farming.
  
  DIRECTION: Industrialization → Pollution ✅
  NOT: Pollution → Industrialization ❌
  
  ALWAYS verify direction by asking "which came first?"
  and "which one causes the other?"

RULE 5: RESPECT THE TEXT'S UNCERTAINTY GRADIENT

From our link classification, we know the chain gets LESS CERTAIN over time:

THE UNCERTAINTY GRADIENT:

  EARLY CHAIN (Para 1-2): HIGH CERTAINTY
  → Demand rises → Norway industrializes → problems emerge
  → All stated as facts by the journalist
  → SAFE to cite as established cause-effect ✅
  
  MIDDLE CHAIN (Para 3): MODERATE CERTAINTY
  → Problems → chemicals used → toxins possible
  → Mix of facts and hedged claims ("pourrait", "peuvent")
  → CAUTION: Preserve the hedging language ⚠️
  → "Salmon MAY contain toxins" not "Salmon CONTAINS toxins"
  
  LATE CHAIN (Para 4): LOW CERTAINTY
  → Organic polluted → water polluted → model must change
  → Attributed to Magnen (environmentalist politician)
  → His CLAIMS, not verified facts
  → CAUTION: Attribute to source, don't state as fact ⚠️
  
  EXAM IMPLICATIONS:
  → Questions about early chain: answer confidently
  → Questions about middle chain: preserve hedging
  → Questions about late chain: attribute to source

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: TREATING THE WHOLE CHAIN AS EQUALLY CERTAIN

WRONG:
  "The text says demand rises AND water is polluted 
   with heavy metals. Both are facts in the text."
   
RIGHT:
  "The text says demand rises" → JOURNALIST'S FACT ✅
  "The text says water is polluted" → MAGNEN'S CLAIM 🔵
  
  The first is stated by the journalist as verified data.
  The second is stated by an environmentalist politician
  in a direct quote.
  
  DIFFERENT LEVELS OF CERTAINTY.
  DIFFERENT levels of reliability for exam answers.
  
  (Chapter 5 taught us this — it applies directly to 
   cause-effect chains)

MISTAKE 2: INVENTING LINKS NOT IN THE TEXT

WRONG:
  "Industrial farming uses antibiotics, which contaminate 
   the salmon and make it dangerous to eat."
   
  The text NEVER mentions antibiotics.
  
  The text mentions:
  → "traitements chimiques" (chemical treatments)
  → "produits toxiques" (toxic products)
  → "dioxydes, pesticides, métaux lourds"
  
  "Antibiotics" is OUTSIDE KNOWLEDGE (Chapter 5 rule).
  Even if true in real life, it's not in THIS text.
  
  RULE: Only cite cause-effect links that the TEXT provides.
  Your knowledge of salmon farming doesn't count.

MISTAKE 3: CONFUSING CORRELATION WITH CAUSATION

WRONG:
  "The text mentions sea lice AND chemical treatments 
   in the same paragraph. Therefore chemical treatments 
   CAUSE sea lice."
   
RIGHT:
  "The text mentions sea lice as a PROBLEM caused by 
   industrial farming. Chemical treatments are mentioned 
   as a RESPONSE to farming problems. The text doesn't 
   say chemicals cause lice."
   
  SAME PARAGRAPH ≠ CAUSAL RELATIONSHIP
  (Chapter 7's proximity rule applies here too)
  
  Sea lice and chemicals are BOTH mentioned in Para 3,
  but their relationship is:
  → Problem (lice) → Response (chemicals to treat)
  NOT:
  → Cause (chemicals) → Effect (lice)

MISTAKE 4: JUMPING FROM BEGINNING TO END OF THE CHAIN

WRONG:
  "Rising demand for salmon causes water pollution."
   
  While the FULL chain connects these (Level 0 to Level 12),
  they are separated by 12 intermediate steps.
  
  The text NEVER directly connects demand to water pollution.
  It connects demand → industrialization → problems → 
  lice/escapes → ecosystem damage → toxins → health risks 
  → scope expansion → organic pollution → water pollution.
  
  EACH intermediate step matters.
  
  If the question asks "What causes water pollution?",
  the IMMEDIATE cause is "pollution des eaux" from 
  industrial contamination and heavy metals (Levels 10-12),
  NOT "rising demand" (Level 0).
  
  RULE: Answer at the level the question targets.
  Don't jump across the chain unless the question 
  specifically asks about root causes.

MISTAKE 5: TREATING OPINIONS AS CAUSES

WRONG:
  "The industrial model must change because of pollution."
  Stated as if this is a cause-effect FACT.
  
RIGHT:
  "MAGNEN says the industrial model must change 
   because of pollution."
  
  "Il faut changer le modèle" is MAGNEN'S OPINION (Level 13).
  The pollution-model connection is HIS reasoning.
  The journalist reports it but doesn't endorse it.
  
  RULE: When a cause-effect link comes from a QUOTED SOURCE,
  attribute it. Don't present it as the text's own conclusion.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "EVERYTHING CAUSES EVERYTHING" CONFUSION

DANGER: After mapping the chain, you feel like everything 
        in the text is connected to everything else.
        
  "Demand causes industrialization which causes problems 
   which cause lice which cause escapes which cause 
   ecosystem damage which relates to chemicals which 
   relate to toxins which relate to health which..."
   
  You lose track of WHICH specific link the question asks about.
  
  CURE: The LEVEL TARGETING technique.
  
  1. Identify what the question asks about (the EFFECT)
  2. Go to that LEVEL in the chain
  3. Look ONE LEVEL BACK for the immediate cause
  4. Answer with THAT level, not the whole chain
  
  EXAMPLE:
  Q: "What endangers wild salmon?"
  → Find "wild salmon endangered" = Level 6
  → One level back = Level 5: sea lice + escapes
  → Answer: sea lice proliferation and escape of farmed salmon
  → NOT: "rising demand" (too far back)
  → NOT: "water pollution" (too far forward)

PITFALL 2: THE PARALLEL vs. SEQUENTIAL CONFUSION

DANGER: Confusing causes that happen IN PARALLEL 
        with causes that happen IN SEQUENCE.
        
  PARALLEL CAUSES (happen simultaneously):
  → Sea lice proliferation AND salmon escapes
  → Both are caused by industrial farming
  → Both TOGETHER endanger wild salmon
  → They are CO-CAUSES, not a sequence
  
  ┌──────────┐
  │Industrial│──→ Sea lice ──────┐
  │ farming  │                    ├──→ Ecosystem endangered
  │          │──→ Salmon escape──┘
  └──────────┘
  
  SEQUENTIAL CAUSES (one leads to the next):
  → Demand rises → Norway industrializes → problems emerge
  → Each step CAUSES the next
  → They form a SEQUENCE
  
  Demand → Industrialization → Problems
  
  WHY THIS MATTERS:
  If a question asks "What are the TWO problems?",
  the answer is the PARALLEL pair (lice + escapes),
  not two sequential steps (demand + industrialization).

PITFALL 3: THE "DÛ À" REVERSAL TRAP

DANGER: Misreading "dû à" (due to) and reversing 
        the cause-effect direction.
        
  TEXT: "le risque, DÛ NOTAMMENT À la pollution des eaux, 
        ne concerne plus uniquement le saumon d'élevage"
        
  CORRECT READING:
  → The risk (EFFECT)
  → is due to (CAUSE SIGNAL)
  → water pollution (CAUSE)
  → extends beyond farmed salmon (SCOPE)
  
  → CAUSE: water pollution
  → EFFECT: risk extends beyond farmed salmon
  
  WRONG READING:
  "The risk of farmed salmon causes water pollution"
  → This REVERSES the direction
  → Water pollution CAUSES the risk, not the other way
  
  "DÛ À" always points FROM effect TO cause:
  EFFECT + "dû à" + CAUSE
  
  Like English "due to":
  "The delay, DUE TO traffic, was significant"
  → Traffic = CAUSE; delay = EFFECT

PITFALL 4: MISSING THE TWO-TRACK CHAIN

THE TWO TRACKS IN TEXTE 102:

  The text actually contains TWO parallel cause-effect tracks 
  that MERGE at the end:
  
  TRACK A: ECOLOGICAL
  Industrialization → lice + escapes → ecosystem damage
  → wild salmon genetics threatened
  
  TRACK B: HEALTH
  Industrial farming → chemicals used → toxic contamination
  → health risk for humans → Norway warns consumers
  
  MERGER POINT (Level 10):
  "le risque ne concerne PLUS UNIQUEMENT le saumon d'élevage"
  → Both tracks converge: ecological AND health problems
  → Now affect ALL salmon, not just farmed
  
  FINAL MERGER (Level 12):
  "les eaux sont polluées"
  → The water itself is contaminated
  → Both ecological and health tracks are unified
  
  WHY THIS MATTERS:
  A question might ask about ecological effects ONLY
  or health effects ONLY. You need to know which 
  TRACK to follow.
  
  "What threatens wild salmon?" → TRACK A (ecological)
  "What risks do consumers face?" → TRACK B (health)
  "What is the overall problem?" → BOTH tracks merged
