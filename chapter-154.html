Internet et notre cerveau

"Mon vieux cerveau me manque." Nicholas Carr, 52 ans, essayiste américain, n'a rien d'un réac allergique au Web. Les nouvelles technologies, au contraire, c'est sa spécialité. Entre son blog, son Twitter, son Facebook, Marshall était ce qu'on peut appeler un féru du Web. Pourtant, un beau jour, le « serpent du doute » s'est inséré dans son éden numérique. Il avait l'impression désagréable que « quelqu'un ou quelque chose bricolait son cerveau ». Se concentrer plus de deux minutes devenait une véritable gageure, la lecture de longs textes un obstacle insurmontable. Partant de ce constat, il a écrit Abruti par Internet ? Le livre du moment.

Où l'on aperçoit que la donne a changé, que l'antagonisme entre anciens et modernes a fait long feu, les plus alarmistes venant d'ailleurs des milieux les plus activistes du Net. Au Ve siècle av. J.-C, c'était la pratique de l'écriture qui était controversée. Socrate s'inquiétait qu'elle nous fasse négliger notre mémoire, à tort : la lecture nous a aidés à mieux mémoriser, cependant qu'elle a modifié en profondeur le fonctionnement de notre cerveau, comme en témoigne une étude récente, qui a mesuré par IRM les modifications des zones du cerveau « liseur ».

Le cerveau est un organe éminemment plastique qui s'est adapté, comme il le fait maintenant à la pratique du web. Quand nous surfons sur Internet, le cortex préfrontal en charge de tout notre processus de décision est particulièrement sollicité. Tout comme les mots croisés, le surf, de par sa capacité à exercer l'agilité de l'esprit, aurait des effets particulièrement heureux sur les séniors. Les zones dédiées au langage, et à la mémoire seraient, elles, sollicitées dans une moindre mesure.

Alors, sommes-nous en train de devenir « des crétins numériques » accros à un outil bêtifiant ? Faut-il saluer l'avènement d'un mutant capable de jongler avec les informations, un esprit multitâches plus créatif et apte à la prise de décision ?

This text is harder because the author deliberately refuses to conclude. The article ends not with a verdict but with TWO OPEN QUESTIONS. This forces you to identify an author's stance that is defined by its very REFUSAL to take a definitive side.

The Conditional Verb Alert: "Aurait" / "Seraient"

Paragraph 3 uses CONDITIONAL verbs — a critical detail:

"le surf... aurait des effets particulièrement heureux sur les séniors"
"Les zones dédiées au langage et à la mémoire seraient, elles, sollicitées dans une moindre mesure"

"Aurait" = would have (conditional). "Seraient" = would be (conditional).

These conditionals mean the effects are HYPOTHESIZED, not proven. The text is careful to present these as POSSIBLE effects, not established facts. This nuance will be tested.

SOCRATES' FEAR:    Writing will damage our memory

BUT THE TEXT DOESN'T SAY: "Internet fears are definitely wrong"
IT SAYS:                  "The situation is more complex than simple fear"

The analogy doesn't PROVE Internet is harmless. It SUGGESTS that technological fears have been wrong before. This is a PROBABILISTIC argument, not a proof.

QUESTION 32: Selon cet article, le cerveau humain...

This question targets Paragraph 3 — the scientific description of how the brain responds to technology. It asks for a GENERAL statement about the brain based on the article's evidence.

Step 1: What does the text say about the brain?

Core statement:

"Le cerveau est un organe éminemment plastique qui s'est adapté, comme il le fait maintenant à la pratique du web."

The brain is HIGHLY PLASTIC — it ADAPTS. It adapted to reading in the past, and it's now adapting to the web.

Specific effects of web surfing:

"le cortex préfrontal en charge de tout notre processus de décision est particulièrement sollicité"

Decision-making area: HIGHLY ACTIVATED (stimulated more).

"Les zones dédiées au langage et à la mémoire seraient, elles, sollicitées dans une moindre mesure"

Language and memory areas: LESS ACTIVATED (stimulated less).

Step 2: Synthesize the brain's behavior.

THE BRAIN'S RESPONSE TO THE WEB:
├── PLASTICITY: It adapts (changes in response to new practices)
├── SOME AREAS GAIN: Decision-making cortex = more activated
├── SOME AREAS LOSE: Language and memory zones = less activated
└── OVERALL: The brain EVOLVES by developing some areas
             while others are less stimulated

The key concept: DIFFERENTIAL EVOLUTION — some zones develop MORE, others develop LESS. It's not pure growth or pure degradation; it's a REDISTRIBUTION.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "se transforme essentiellement de manière accidentelle"

"De manière accidentelle" = accidentally / by chance.

The text describes the brain's changes as ADAPTIVE responses to specific practices: reading caused certain changes, web surfing causes others. These are SYSTEMATIC ADAPTATIONS, not accidents. The brain responds to what it's EXPOSED TO — that's the meaning of "plastique" (plastic = adaptable, responsive).

❌ ELIMINATED — Adaptive responses to stimuli ≠ accidental changes

Option B — "évolue en développant une zone au détriment des autres"

  • "évolue" = evolves ✓ (the brain changes/adapts)
  • "en développant une zone" ↔ "cortex préfrontal... particulièrement sollicité" ✓ (one zone develops more)
  • "au détriment des autres" ↔ "les zones dédiées au langage et à la mémoire seraient sollicitées dans une moindre mesure" ✓ (at the expense of other zones = less stimulated)

Let's map:

TEXT                                              OPTION B
"cortex préfrontal particulièrement sollicité" →  "développant une zone"
(decision area highly activated)                   (developing one zone)

"langage et mémoire sollicitées dans une       →  "au détriment des autres"
 moindre mesure"                                   (at the expense of others)
(language and memory less activated)

"Au détriment de" (at the expense of) captures the TRADE-OFF described: when one zone is more activated, others are less activated. The brain's evolution involves REDISTRIBUTION — some areas gain at others' expense.

✅ CORRECT

Option C — "s'atrophie au contact prolongé des nouvelles technologies"

"S'atrophie" = atrophies / wastes away / shrinks.

The text describes the brain as "éminemment plastique" (highly plastic) that "s'est adapté" (has adapted). Adaptation ≠ atrophy. The brain CHANGES, it doesn't WASTE AWAY. Some zones are more active, others less — but the text doesn't say the brain as a whole SHRINKS.

Also, the text describes POSITIVE effects of web surfing: "effets particulièrement heureux sur les séniors" (particularly happy effects on seniors). A brain that atrophies wouldn't have "happy effects."

❌ ELIMINATED — "Plastique" and "adapté" = adaptation, not atrophy; positive effects mentioned

Option D — "se dégrade quand certaines capacités ne sont plus sollicitées"

"Se dégrade" = degrades / deteriorates.

The text says language and memory zones are "sollicitées dans une moindre mesure" — stimulated to a LESSER extent. "Moindre mesure" ≠ "ne sont plus sollicitées" (no longer stimulated). "Less" ≠ "not at all."

Furthermore, "se dégrade" implies overall DETERIORATION. The text presents a TRADE-OFF (some gain, some lose), not degradation. The brain is ADAPTING, not degrading.

TEXT:     "sollicitées dans une MOINDRE MESURE" = LESS stimulated (reduced)
OPTION D: "NE SONT PLUS sollicitées" = NO LONGER stimulated (zero)
          LESS ≠ ZERO

Additionally, "se dégrade" frames the change as purely NEGATIVE. The text presents it as MIXED (decision-making improves, language/memory decrease).

❌ ELIMINATED — "Moindre mesure" (less) ≠ "ne sont plus" (zero) + "dégrade" implies pure negativity

✅ ANSWER: B

The Lesson Here: "Moindre mesure" vs "Ne... plus"

This question introduces a crucial DEGREE distinction:

DEGREE SCALE:
Fully active ←→ Less active ←→ Not active at all ←→ Atrophied

TEXT: "moindre mesure" = LESS active (middle of scale)
OPTION D: "ne sont plus" = NOT AT ALL active (extreme of scale)
OPTION C: "s'atrophie" = DETERIORATED (beyond the scale)
OPTION B: "au détriment" = TRADE-OFF (captures the redistribution) ✓

The text describes a MODERATE shift — some zones less stimulated. Wrong answers AMPLIFY this to total cessation (D) or overall deterioration (C). The correct answer captures the TRADE-OFF nature of the change.

RULE: Degree words matter enormously. "Dans une moindre mesure" (to a lesser extent) ≠ "ne... plus" (no longer). "Moins sollicité" (less stimulated) ≠ "atrophié" (wasted away). Wrong answers amplify moderate changes to extreme states. Always match the DEGREE of change precisely.

Option A — "aiderait à la mémorisation chez les personnes âgées"

The Lesson Here: Domain Substitution and Vocabulary Precision

This question combines TWO trap types:

TRAP TYPE 1: Domain Substitution (Option A)

TEXT:     Web surfing helps seniors with MENTAL AGILITY
TRAP:     Web surfing helps seniors with MEMORIZATION
ERROR:    Substitutes one cognitive domain (agility) with another (memory)
DANGER:   Memory is explicitly the domain that DECREASES

TRAP TYPE 2: Degree Amplification (Option D)

TEXT:     Language zones "less stimulated" (moderate decrease)
TRAP:     "Progressive deterioration" of language (severe degradation)
ERROR:    Amplifies a moderate effect to an extreme consequence

The correct answer avoids both traps by PRECISELY paraphrasing the text's core finding:

TEXT:     "cortex préfrontal en charge du processus de décision
          particulièrement sollicité"
ANSWER:   "renforcerait les capacités d'arbitrage et de délibération"

PRECISION: Decision process → arbitrage + délibération (synonyms)
           Particularly stimulated → reinforced (logical consequence)
           Conditional mood preserved ("renforcerait")

RULE: In scientific texts, wrong answers perform DOMAIN SUBSTITUTION — replacing one cognitive domain (agility) with another (memory) or one brain area's effect with another's. Always verify: does the answer attribute the CORRECT effect to the CORRECT domain? Cross-check: is the substituted domain perhaps the one that actually DECREASES?

QUESTION 35: L'auteur...

This is the AUTHOR'S STANCE question — the most important question in this chapter and one of the most challenging in all 25 chapters.

Step 1: Map the author's full trajectory through the text.

PARAGRAPH 1: PRESENTS Carr's alarm (negative experience)
             → Author is REPORTING, not endorsing

PARAGRAPH 2: PRESENTS historical analogy (Socrates was wrong)
             → SUGGESTS current fears might also be overblown
             → But doesn't PROVE this

PARAGRAPH 3: PRESENTS scientific evidence
             → SOME effects positive (decision-making, seniors)
             → SOME effects negative (language, memory less stimulated)
             → Uses CONDITIONAL verbs (hypothesized, not proven)

PARAGRAPH 4: ASKS two questions, gives NO answer
             → "Crétins numériques?" (negative extreme)
             → "Mutants multitâches plus créatifs?" (positive extreme)
             → DELIBERATELY OPEN-ENDED

Step 2: Characterize the author's stance.

The author:

  1. Does NOT dismiss concerns (reports Carr's experience seriously)
  2. Does NOT amplify fears (historical analogy suggests fears may be wrong)
  3. Presents BOTH positive and negative effects (balanced science)
  4. Ends with OPEN QUESTIONS (refuses to conclude definitively)

The author is neither purely positive nor purely negative. They are BALANCED with a SLIGHT LEAN toward acknowledging positive possibilities — evidenced by:

  • The Socrates analogy (past tech fears were wrong)
  • "Effets particulièrement heureux sur les séniors" (positive effects)
  • The positive extreme question ("mutant multitâches plus créatif") is given LAST — the final word leans positive

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

Strategy 1: The Evidence-Type Classifier

For texts mixing multiple evidence types, classify each as you read:

TYPE 1: PERSONAL TESTIMONY → Carr's experience
        → SUBJECTIVE, individual, anecdotal
        → Function: introduces the problem emotionally

TYPE 2: HISTORICAL ANALOGY → Socrates on writing
        → COMPARATIVE, suggestive, not proof
        → Function: reframes the debate

TYPE 3: SCIENTIFIC DATA → IRM studies, brain plasticity
        → OBJECTIVE, measurable, but tentative (conditionals)
        → Function: provides nuanced evidence

TYPE 4: PHILOSOPHICAL QUESTIONING → "crétins" or "mutants"?
        → OPEN-ENDED, deliberately unresolved
        → Function: leaves the reader to decide

Questions will target specific evidence types. Knowing which type each passage represents prevents you from treating testimony as proof or questions as conclusions.

Strategy 2: The Conditional Verb Tracker

In scientific texts, track the VERB MOOD carefully:

INDICATIVE (certain):
"Le cortex préfrontal EST particulièrement sollicité"
= The prefrontal cortex IS highly activated (established fact)

CONDITIONAL (hypothesized):
"Le surf AURAIT des effets heureux sur les séniors"
= Surfing WOULD HAVE positive effects on seniors (hypothesis)

"Les zones de langage SERAIENT sollicitées dans une moindre mesure"
= Language zones WOULD BE less stimulated (hypothesis)

When the text uses conditionals, the correct answer will ALSO use tentative language ("renforcerait," "éventuelles"). Answers that state effects as CERTAIN when the text uses conditionals are wrong.

Strategy 3: The Paradox Preservation Method

When the text builds a paradox (tech critics are tech experts), the correct answer PRESERVES the paradox. Wrong answers RESOLVE it:

PARADOX:   Tech's biggest critics = tech's biggest fans
PRESERVED: "appartiennent à la frange avertie" (expert users) ✓
RESOLVED:  "réfractaires à la technologie" (tech-resistant) ✗
           "néophytes du numérique" (beginners) ✗

Strategy 4: The Intensity Calibration Grid

For author's stance questions, calibrate against a five-point scale:

DO #2: Track conditional verbs as "uncertainty markers"

Every "aurait" and "seraient" means: this is HYPOTHESIZED, not proven. The correct answer must reflect this tentativeness ("renforcerait," "éventuelles").

DO #3: Recognize that open-ended questions = author's refusal to conclude

❌ WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

DON'T #1: Substitute one cognitive domain for another

"Mental agility for seniors" ≠ "memorization for seniors." The text specifies WHICH domain benefits. Don't swap agility for memory — especially when memory is the domain that DECREASES.

DON'T #2: Amplify moderate effects to extreme consequences

"Less stimulated" ≠ "progressively deteriorating." "Moindre mesure" ≠ "altération progressive." Respect the text's DEGREE of change.

DON'T #3: Treat rhetorical questions as the author's answers

"Sommes-nous des crétins numériques?" is a QUESTION, not a statement. The author is NOT saying we ARE digital idiots. They're ASKING whether we are. Questions explore possibilities; they don't assert conclusions.

DON'T #4: Resolve the text's deliberate ambiguity

The text is INTENTIONALLY ambivalent. Don't force it into a clear positive ("celebrates") or clear negative ("warns") position. The ambivalence IS the stance.

DON'T #5: Ignore the Socrates analogy's argumentative function

The Socrates passage isn't decorative — it ARGUES that past tech fears were wrong, SUGGESTING current fears might also be overblown. This UNDERMINES any answer claiming the author simply warns against technology.
