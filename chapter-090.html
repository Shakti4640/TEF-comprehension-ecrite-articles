Le français a colonisé l'anglais

Henriette Walter, linguiste, vient de publier un essai sur les relations tumultueuses entre l'anglais et le français.

Journaliste. À vous lire, sans Jeanne d'Arc, le français occuperait aujourd'hui la place de l'anglais dans le monde.

H. Walter. Presque ! En boutant les Anglais hors de France, Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française ses chances d'expansion mondiale. Si son intervention n'avait pas eu lieu, le roi d'Angleterre, Henri V, aurait été couronné à Reims et serait devenu roi de France à la place du dauphin. Le français aurait pu devenir la langue des deux pays réunis en un seul royaume.

J. Car, pendant très longtemps, les Anglais ont parlé le français ?

H.W. Henri IV, en 1395, est le premier roi en trois siècles et demi à parler l'anglais. Jusqu'au XIVe siècle, le français domine, c'est la langue élégante ; la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long. Les tribunaux anglais n'ont cessé de siéger en français qu'en 1731.

J. Donc, le français a colonisé l'anglais ?

H.W. Exactement. Les Anglais, eux, auraient vraiment des raisons de se plaindre ! Ouvrez un dictionnaire anglais : plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française, alors que les 55 000 mots du Robert, 2 500 à peine sont anglais. Il faut rétablir la vérité !

J. Quand les Français commencent-ils à être attirés par l'anglais ?

H.W. Au XVIIIe siècle, parce que l'élite était en admiration devant le régime parlementaire britannique. La première apparition du franglais, nous la devons aux sans-culottes. Ensuite, il y a eu le sport, au début du XIXe siècle, et puis la mode. Avec la mondialisation, le langage qui s'élabore est constitué en grande partie de français par le biais du latin. Dans l'informatique, par exemple, 80 % du vocabulaire est d'origine latine. Mais qui le sait ?

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What kind of text is this?

This is a historical interview — a Q&A format conversation between a journalist (J.) and a linguistic expert (H.W. — Henriette Walter) about the history of French-English language relations. This is your first encounter with the INTERVIEW FORMAT, which has unique reading challenges.

Why interview texts are uniquely challenging

Interview texts create problems that narrative texts don't:

PROBLEM 1: TWO SPEAKERS with different roles
  → The journalist ASKS questions and sometimes SUMMARIZES
  → The expert ANSWERS with facts, opinions, and arguments
  → You must know WHO says WHAT

PROBLEM 2: Q&A FLOW creates implicit connections
  → The journalist's question often FRAMES the expert's answer
  → The expert's answer may CONFIRM, CORRECT, or EXPAND the journalist's framing
  → Understanding requires following the DIALOGUE LOGIC

PROBLEM 3: COUNTER-INTUITIVE THESIS
  → The expert's main argument is SURPRISING: French colonized English (not vice versa)
  → Your cultural assumptions may fight against the text's claims
  → You must follow the TEXT's logic, not your ASSUMPTIONS

The Three New Skills of Chapter 12

Skill 1: READING INTERVIEW FORMAT (J. vs. H.W.)

In an interview, each speaker has a distinct FUNCTION:

SpeakerFunctionSignalReliability for exam
Journalist (J.)Asks questions, summarizes, provokesQuestions, simplified statementsProvides CONTEXT but may oversimplify
Expert (H.W.)Answers, explains, provides evidenceDetailed responses, historical dataProvides AUTHORITATIVE CONTENT — exam answers come from here

When the exam says "Selon H. Walter" or "D'après Henriette Walter," you must draw EXCLUSIVELY from H.W.'s responses — not from the journalist's questions or summaries.

When the exam asks a general question ("Avant le XIVe siècle..."), you draw from WHOEVER provides the relevant information — but the expert's statements carry more weight.

Skill 2: FOLLOWING HISTORICAL CAUSALITY CHAINS

This text presents a chain of historical cause-and-effect:

CAUSALITY CHAIN:

CAUSE 1: Jeanne d'Arc drives the English out of France
    ↓
EFFECT 1: Henri V is NOT crowned king of France
    ↓
EFFECT 2: France and England remain SEPARATE kingdoms
    ↓
EFFECT 3: French does NOT become the language of a united kingdom
    ↓
EFFECT 4: English eventually EXPANDS globally instead of French
    ↓
CONCLUSION: Jeanne d'Arc inadvertently prevented French from becoming 
            the world language → English took that position instead

The exam tests whether you can follow this chain — especially the PARADOXICAL conclusion: a French heroine inadvertently helped ENGLISH become dominant.

Skill 3: INTERPRETING COUNTER-INTUITIVE ARGUMENTS

The expert's thesis REVERSES common assumptions:

COMMON ASSUMPTION:          English has influenced/colonized French
                           (Franglais, English words in French, etc.)

EXPERT'S COUNTER-THESIS:   French has colonized English
                           (More than half of English words come from French)

THE SURPRISE:              The "victim" (French) is actually the "colonizer"
                           The "colonizer" (English) is actually the "colonized"

Your job is to follow the EXPERT'S argument as presented in the text, regardless of what you might believe or know from elsewhere.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 24 — What is Jeanne d'Arc's unintended consequence?

The question: Selon H. Walter, Jeanne d'Arc pourrait être tenue responsable de...

Key phrase: "pourrait être tenue responsable de" = could be held responsible for. This signals an UNINTENDED, PARADOXICAL consequence — Jeanne d'Arc didn't MEAN to cause this, but she DID.

Step 1: Follow the causality chain from the expert's answer.

"En boutant les Anglais hors de France, Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française ses chances d'expansion mondiale."

The core statement: Jeanne d'Arc made the French language LOSE its chances of WORLDWIDE EXPANSION.

Now continue the chain:

"Si son intervention n'avait pas eu lieu, le roi d'Angleterre, Henri V, aurait été couronné à Reims et serait devenu roi de France..."
"Le français aurait pu devenir la langue des deux pays réunis en un seul royaume."

The counterfactual: WITHOUT Jeanne d'Arc → French could have become the language of a united Franco-English kingdom → French would have expanded worldwide through England's later empire.

WITH Jeanne d'Arc:     France stays French, England stays English
                       → English eventually expands globally
                       → French does NOT become the world language

WITHOUT Jeanne d'Arc:  Henri V rules both countries
                       → French becomes the language of the combined kingdom
                       → French would have expanded globally instead of English

PARADOXICAL CONCLUSION:
By saving France, Jeanne d'Arc inadvertently PREVENTED French from becoming the world language — which means she inadvertently ENABLED English to take that position.

Step 2: Translate into answer language.

Jeanne d'Arc is responsible for:
  French NOT becoming the world language
  → Therefore: English BECOMING the world language instead
  → "la large expansion de l'anglais dans le monde"

Step 3: Test each option.

A) l'abandon de la royauté en FranceThe text discusses WHICH king would rule, not the abandonment of monarchy itself. France kept its royalty — just with a different king (the dauphin instead of Henri V)❌ WRONG — monarchy wasn't abandoned; only the identity of the king changed
B) la faible place de l'anglais en FranceThe text argues the OPPOSITE dynamic: Jeanne d'Arc's action eventually STRENGTHENED English globally (by keeping France and England separate). The "place of English in France" isn't the topic — the topic is the place of French IN THE WORLD❌ TRAP — reverses the geographic scope. The issue is French's place in the WORLD, not English's place in FRANCE

✅ Answer: D

The "Counterfactual Reasoning" Technique

This question requires you to follow a counterfactual argument — reasoning about what WOULD have happened if something HAD been different:

COUNTERFACTUAL STRUCTURE:

REAL HISTORY:     Jeanne d'Arc → English expelled → Kingdoms separate → English expands
ALTERNATIVE:      No Jeanne d'Arc → Henri V rules both → French becomes world language

THE LOGIC:
  Jeanne d'Arc's action PREVENTED the alternative
  → The alternative was: French becomes the world language
  → What happened INSTEAD: English became the world language
  → THEREFORE: Jeanne d'Arc is indirectly responsible for English's expansion

The conditional verbs are your roadmap:

VerbTenseWhat it signals
"fait perdre"Present (historical)What DID happen (real consequence)
"aurait été couronné"Conditional pastWhat WOULD HAVE happened (counterfactual)
"serait devenu"Conditional pastWhat WOULD HAVE happened (counterfactual)
"aurait pu devenir"Conditional pastWhat COULD HAVE happened (counterfactual possibility)

From Chapter 7, you know the conditional signals uncertainty. Here, it signals COUNTERFACTUAL — events that did NOT happen but WOULD HAVE happened under different circumstances.

🔑 Rule: In historical texts, conditional past ("aurait été," "serait devenu," "aurait pu") describes COUNTERFACTUAL scenarios — things that did NOT happen. The REAL consequence is stated in indicative tense. The exam tests whether you can identify the ACTUAL consequence, not the counterfactual scenario.

The "Geographic Scope Swap" Trap (Option B)

Option B commits a subtle scope error:

TEXT'S ARGUMENT:
  Jeanne d'Arc prevented French from expanding IN THE WORLD
  → Consequence: English expanded IN THE WORLD instead
  → SCOPE: worldwide/global

OPTION B:
  "la faible place de l'anglais EN FRANCE"
  → English's weak position IN FRANCE
  → SCOPE: within France only

The text talks about GLOBAL linguistic dominance.
Option B talks about NATIONAL linguistic presence.
These are completely different scales.

🔑 Rule: Check the GEOGRAPHIC SCOPE of both the text's argument and the answer option. "Dans le monde" (in the world) ≠ "en France" (in France). A worldwide argument cannot be answered with a national-level option.

QUESTION 25 — What did speaking French signify in medieval England?

The question: Avant le XIVe siècle, quand un Anglais parlait français, cela signifiait qu'il...

Step 1: Locate the relevant passage.

"Jusqu'au XIVe siècle, le français domine, c'est la langue élégante ; la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long."

Step 2: Decode the idiomatic expression.

The key expression: "avoir le bras long"

LITERAL:    "to have a long arm"
FIGURATIVE: to have INFLUENCE, POWER, CONNECTIONS
            = to be well-connected, to have authority
            = to be able to reach far (metaphorically)

In context: Speaking French in England proved you HAD POWER/INFLUENCE
           = French was the language of the ELITE, the POWERFUL

Additional context:

"Henri IV, en 1395, est le premier roi en trois siècles et demi à parler l'anglais."

If KINGS spoke French for 350 years, then French = the language of ROYALTY and POWER.

"Les tribunaux anglais n'ont cessé de siéger en français qu'en 1731."

If COURTS operated in French until 1731, then French = the language of LEGAL AUTHORITY.

SYNTHESIS:
  Kings spoke French       → royal power
  Courts used French       → legal power
  "Avoir le bras long"     → general power/influence
  "Langue élégante"        → prestige

Speaking French = HAVING POWER

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) était étrangerThe text describes ENGLISH people who spoke French — they weren't foreign. They were English aristocrats/officials who used French as their language of power. French was the PRESTIGE language, not a marker of foreign origin❌ WRONG — speaking French in England marked POWER, not foreign origin
B) était royalisteKings DID speak French, but the text's point isn't about political allegiance (royalism). It's about social STATUS. Judges, nobles, and powerful people all spoke French — not because they were "royalist" but because French = power❌ TRAP — confuses the ASSOCIATION with royalty (kings spoke French) with political IDEOLOGY (being royalist)
C) avait du pouvoir"Avoir le bras long" = having power/influence. French = "langue élégante" = prestige language. Kings, courts, and powerful people spoke French. "Avait du pouvoir" = had power✅ CORRECT
D) venait d'un milieu populaire"Milieu populaire" = working class/common background. This is the OPPOSITE — French was the language of the ELITE, not the common people. Common English people spoke English; the powerful spoke French❌ WRONG — exact opposite of the text's meaning

✅ Answer: C

Decoding "Avoir le bras long" — Idiomatic Expression Analysis

This question tests your ability to decode a French idiom through context:

EXPRESSION: "avoir le bras long"
LITERAL:    to have a long arm
FIGURATIVE: to have power, influence, connections

CONTEXT CLUES:
  1. "le français domine" → French is the DOMINANT language
  2. "la langue élégante" → it's the PRESTIGIOUS language
  3. "la parler PROUVE qu'on a le bras long" → speaking it PROVES something
  4. Kings spoke French for 350 years → ROYAL association
  5. Courts used French until 1731 → AUTHORITY association

ALL CLUES POINT TO: power, influence, authority, prestige
→ "avoir le bras long" = having power/influence ✅

Even if you've never encountered this expression, the CONTEXT provides overwhelming evidence for its meaning.

🔑 Rule (reinforcing Chapter 6): When you encounter an unknown idiomatic expression, DON'T panic. Read the SURROUNDING CONTEXT — the sentences before and after the expression almost always provide enough clues to deduce its meaning. The exam tests your DEDUCTION ability, not your knowledge of every idiom.

The "Association vs. Ideology" Trap (Option B)

Option B confuses an ASSOCIATION with an IDEOLOGY:

ASSOCIATION:  Kings spoke French → French is ASSOCIATED with royalty
IDEOLOGY:     Being royalist → supporting the monarchy as a political system

The text says French was the language of kings (association with power).
It does NOT say speaking French meant you supported the monarchy (political ideology).

Powerful nobles, judges, and officials ALSO spoke French.
They weren't necessarily "royalists" — they were simply POWERFUL PEOPLE
who used the prestige language of their era.

🔑 Rule: Don't confuse an ASSOCIATION (X is connected to Y) with an IDEOLOGY (X believes in Y). The fact that kings spoke French doesn't mean French speakers were royalists — it means French was the language of POWER in general.

QUESTION 26 — What is the linguistic balance?

The question: D'après Henriette Walter...

This question asks about SPECIFIC DATA the expert provides about the linguistic relationship between French and English.

Step 1: Locate the numerical evidence.

"Ouvrez un dictionnaire anglais : plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française, alors que les 55 000 mots du Robert, 2 500 à peine sont anglais."

Step 2: Extract the two data points.

DATA POINT 1: English dictionary
  "plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française"
  = MORE THAN ONE WORD OUT OF TWO is of French origin
  = MORE THAN 50% of English words come from French
  = The MAJORITY of English vocabulary is French-origin

DATA POINT 2: French dictionary (Le Robert)
  "55 000 mots...2 500 à peine sont anglais"
  = Out of 55,000 words, BARELY 2,500 are English
  = 2,500 / 55,000 ≈ 4.5% of French words are English-origin
  = A TINY MINORITY of French vocabulary is English-origin

ASYMMETRY:
  French → English: MORE THAN 50% (massive influence)
  English → French: LESS THAN 5% (minimal influence)
  
  French has COLONIZED English vocabulary (not the other way around)

Step 3: Test each option.

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) le français est menacé par l'anglaisThe expert's ENTIRE thesis is the OPPOSITE — French has colonized English, not the other way around. The expert says "les Anglais auraient vraiment des raisons de se plaindre!" (the English would have reason to complain!). If anyone is "threatened," it's English by French influence❌ WRONG — contradicts the expert's central thesis
B) peu de mots anglais viennent de la langue française"Peu" = few. But the text says "plus d'un terme sur deux" = MORE THAN HALF. More than half ≠ few. This is the OPPOSITE of what the data shows❌ WRONG — direct numerical contradiction. More than 50% ≠ "peu"
C) une majorité de mots français sont d'origine anglaise"Majorité de mots français d'origine anglaise" = most French words come from English. But the text says only 2,500 out of 55,000 (about 4.5%) are English-origin. 4.5% ≠ majority❌ WRONG — REVERSES the direction. It's English that has a majority of French-origin words, not French having a majority of English-origin words
D) la moitié du vocabulaire anglais est d'origine française"La moitié" = half. "Plus d'un terme sur deux" = more than one out of two = more than half. "La moitié" is a slightly conservative paraphrase of "plus de la moitié," but it captures the essential fact: roughly HALF of English vocabulary is French-origin✅ CORRECT

✅ Answer: D

The "Direction Reversal" Trap (Option C)

Option C commits a devastating DIRECTION REVERSAL:

TEXT SAYS:
  More than half of ENGLISH words are of FRENCH origin
  French → English (French influenced English)

OPTION C SAYS:
  A majority of FRENCH words are of ENGLISH origin
  English → French (English influenced French)

THE REVERSAL:
  DONOR language:     French → English (text) vs. English → French (option)
  RECIPIENT language: English ← French (text) vs. French ← English (option)
  
  The direction of influence is COMPLETELY REVERSED.

This is the same asymmetric reversal trap from Chapter 9, but applied to LINGUISTIC data instead of economic data:

Ch. 9:  TIME grows faster than MONEY → trap reverses to MONEY grows faster
Ch. 12: FRENCH influenced ENGLISH more → trap reverses to ENGLISH influenced FRENCH more

🔑 Rule: When a text presents an asymmetric relationship (X influenced Y much more than Y influenced X), expect a trap that REVERSES the direction. Always verify: WHO is the donor? WHO is the recipient? Which direction does the influence flow?

The "Approximately Equal" Paraphrase

Option D says "la moitié" (half) while the text says "plus d'un terme sur deux" (more than one out of two = more than half).

TEXT:     "PLUS D'un terme sur deux" = MORE THAN half (>50%)
OPTION D: "LA MOITIÉ"                = half (≈50%)

Is this close enough?
→ YES — in multiple choice, "la moitié" is the closest available option
→ The text says "more than half" and the option says "half"
→ This slight understatement is acceptable because all other options are WRONG
→ In TCF, the BEST available option wins, even if not perfectly precise

🔑 Rule: In multiple choice, you select the BEST available answer — not a perfect one. If the text says "more than 50%" and the closest option says "half," that's the correct answer when all alternatives are clearly wrong.

The "Timeline Position" Technique — Reinforcing Chapter 3

This question applies the same Motivation Timeline technique from Chapter 3, but in a HISTORICAL context:

CHAPTER 3 (personal timeline):
  Question: "initialement" → targets FIRST motivation
  Text: "Au départ, je ne voyais que le défi sportif" → sports challenge = first

CHAPTER 12 (historical timeline):
  Question: "ont commencé" → targets FIRST historical reason
  Text: "Au XVIIIe siècle...le régime parlementaire" → political admiration = first

The technique is IDENTICAL — find the TIME MARKER that corresponds to "first/began/started" and extract the reason from THAT point only.

TIME MARKERS FOR "FIRST":
  "Au départ"         = at the start
  "Initialement"      = initially
  "Ont commencé"      = began
  "Au XVIIIe siècle"  = first chronological entry
  "D'abord"           = first
  "La première..."    = the first...

When you see these, ONLY extract from the EARLIEST point on the timeline.
Ignore all subsequent developments (sport, fashion, IT, globalization).

🔑 Rule (reinforcing Chapter 3): "Commencé" and "au départ" and "initialement" ALL target the FIRST point on a timeline. Build the complete timeline, then select information ONLY from the first entry. Later entries are DISTRACTORS.

The "Added Detail" Trap (Option C)

Option C adds a detail NOT in the text:

TEXT:     "la mode" (fashion)
OPTION C: "la mode AMÉRICAINE" (American fashion)

The text says "la mode" without specifying nationality.
The option adds "américaine" — a detail the text doesn't provide.

This is an ADDITION trap — the option takes a real element from the text and ADDS information that isn't there.

🔑 Rule: When an answer option contains a detail that the text doesn't mention, that detail is likely ADDED to make the option sound more specific (and therefore more tempting). If the text says "la mode" and the option says "la mode américaine," the added nationality is unsupported.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Counterfactual-as-Real" Confusion

What happens: The text describes what WOULD HAVE happened (counterfactual), and you treat it as what DID happen.

Example:

COUNTERFACTUAL (didn't happen):
  "Henri V aurait été couronné à Reims"
  = Henri V WOULD HAVE been crowned (but wasn't)
  
  "Le français aurait pu devenir la langue des deux pays"
  = French COULD HAVE become the language of both (but didn't)

REAL (did happen):
  "Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française ses chances"
  = Joan of Arc DID cause French to lose its chances

The exam asks about the REAL consequence (what DID happen),
not the counterfactual (what WOULD HAVE happened).

The defense: Separate conditional verbs (counterfactual) from indicative verbs (real):

CONDITIONAL = what WOULD HAVE happened → NOT real
INDICATIVE  = what DID happen          → REAL

Q24 asks: what is Joan of Arc RESPONSIBLE for?
→ The REAL consequence (indicative): French lost its expansion chances
→ Therefore: English expanded instead
→ NOT the counterfactual scenario (which didn't happen)

Pitfall 2: The "Common Assumption Override"

What happens: Your pre-existing beliefs about French-English relations override what the TEXT says.

Example:

YOUR ASSUMPTION: English is invading French (Franglais, tech words, etc.)
                 → You might select Q26A: "le français est menacé par l'anglais"

THE TEXT'S THESIS: French has COLONIZED English (50%+ of English is French-origin)
                   → The correct answer reflects the TEXT's argument, not your assumption

The expert says: "Les Anglais auraient vraiment des raisons de se plaindre!"
                 = The ENGLISH would have reason to complain (not the French!)

The defense: When a text presents a COUNTER-INTUITIVE thesis, trust the text over your assumptions. The exam asks "D'après Henriette Walter" or "Selon cet article" — these phrases mean: "What does the TEXT say?" — not "What do you believe?"

🔑 Rule: "Selon l'article" and "d'après l'expert" mean: answer based on the TEXT's claims, regardless of your personal knowledge or beliefs. In an exam, the text is always right within its own framework.

Pitfall 3: The "Intermediate Step" Selection

What happens: You correctly follow the causality chain but select an INTERMEDIATE step instead of the FINAL consequence.

Example:

CAUSALITY CHAIN:
  Step 1: Jeanne d'Arc expels the English
  Step 2: Kingdoms remain separate
  Step 3: French stays limited to France
  Step 4: English expands globally → FINAL CONSEQUENCE

The question asks: "What is Jeanne d'Arc responsible for?"

IF YOU SELECT Step 2: "Kingdoms remaining separate" → too intermediate
IF YOU SELECT Step 3: "French not expanding" → closer but not the answer option
IF YOU SELECT Step 4: "English expanding globally" → CORRECT (final consequence)

The defense: When following a causality chain, look for the FURTHEST consequence that appears in the answer options. The exam typically asks about the END of the chain, not the middle.

Pitfall 4: The "Interview Attribution" Error

What happens: You attribute a statement to the wrong speaker.

Example:

JOURNALIST says: "Sans Jeanne d'Arc, le français occuperait la place de l'anglais"
                 → This is the journalist's SUMMARY of the expert's thesis

EXPERT says:     "Presque! En boutant les Anglais hors de France..."
                 → This is the expert's DETAILED explanation

For exam purposes:
  "Selon H. Walter" → Use the EXPERT's detailed answer
  The journalist's summary is a SIMPLIFIED version that may be imprecise
  ("Presque!" = "Almost!" → the expert corrects the journalist's slight oversimplification)

The defense: When the question says "Selon [expert name]," go to that expert's RESPONSES (not the journalist's questions). The expert may correct, nuance, or expand what the journalist says.

🔑 Rule: In interview texts, the journalist's questions provide CONTEXT but may oversimplify. The expert's answers provide AUTHORITATIVE CONTENT. When the exam attributes a question to the expert ("Selon H. Walter"), always draw from the expert's words, not the journalist's framing.
