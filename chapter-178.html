But it adds a final, crucial layer: the author takes a STANCE on the concerns themselves. Not just "concerns exist" — but "here's how seriously we should take them." This is the most sophisticated form of reading comprehension: understanding not just WHAT is said, but HOW the author EVALUATES what is said.

The text's unique rhetorical architecture

This text follows a three-act structure that mirrors a courtroom drama:

ACT 1 — THE CASE FOR THE DEFENSE (Enthusiasm):
  "Très réaliste et facile d'accès, totalement gratuit"
  "se répandre comme une traînée de poudre"
  "cartographes en herbe" / "trésors cachés"
  → TONE: Wonder, excitement, celebration

ACT 2 — THE CASE FOR THE PROSECUTION (Concerns):
  "polémiques" / "détracteurs"
  "atteinte à la vie privée"
  "usage malveillant" / "sites nucléaires"
  → TONE: Worry, alarm, suspicion

ACT 3 — THE JUDGE'S VERDICT (Relativization):
  "Ces inquiétudes sont-elles légitimes ?"
  "Elles semblent très relatives"
  "il est pour l'instant exclu de..."
  → TONE: Measured, objective, reassuring

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: THE THREE-ACT RHETORICAL STRUCTURE
Some texts don't just present two sides — they present two sides AND THEN ADJUDICATE between them. The author acts as a judge:

  1. First, presents the positive case (enthusiasm)
  2. Then, presents the negative case (concerns)
  3. Finally, delivers a VERDICT (relativization — concerns are overstated)

Q35 specifically tests whether you can identify Act 3 — the verdict.

QUESTION 32: Quel titre convient le mieux à cet article ?

This is a "BEST TITLE" question — second encounter (first was Project 17, Q28).

From Project 17, you learned: a title must match the text's DOMINANT CONTENT + TONE.

But this text has a MORE COMPLEX tone than Project 17's alarm article. Here, the tone is:

ENTHUSIASTIC (Act 1) + CONCERNED (Act 2) + RELATIVIZING (Act 3)
= OVERALL: Fascinated but aware of tensions

The title must capture BOTH the virtual exploration AND the controversy.

Evaluate each title:

Choice A: "Du bon usage de l'image"

"Du bon usage de l'image" = On the proper use of images

❌ ELIMINATED — Scope Reduction trap (from Project 18). The text is about a SPECIFIC SOFTWARE that allows virtual globe exploration, not about the general "proper use of images." This title is too abstract and philosophical for an article about a concrete technology. It sounds like a treatise on media ethics, not a tech review with controversy.

Furthermore, the text doesn't prescribe "bon usage" (proper use) — it describes a phenomenon and its debates. The title implies a prescriptive/advisory tone that the text doesn't have.

Choice C: "Voyages pas chers : la polémique enfle"

"Voyages pas chers" = Cheap travel
"la polémique enfle" = the controversy grows

❌ ELIMINATED — Double Mismatch trap. Two problems:

Problem 1: "Voyages pas chers" (cheap travel) implies PHYSICAL travel at low cost. The text describes VIRTUAL exploration — not actual travel. The software is free, but calling it "cheap travel" mischaracterizes it. It's not a travel service — it's a satellite imagery tool.

Problem 2: "La polémique enfle" (the controversy grows/swells) implies the controversy is INCREASING. But the text's Act 3 actually DEFLATES the controversy — "très relatives" (very relative). The text ends by DIMINISHING concerns, not amplifying them. This title's tone (growing alarm) contradicts the text's conclusion (relativized concerns).

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: TITLE TONE MUST MATCH THE TEXT'S CONCLUSION, NOT ITS MIDDLE
A text may present alarming information in its middle (Act 2), but if it RELATIVIZES those concerns at the end (Act 3), the title should NOT sound alarming. The title must reflect the text's FINAL position, not an intermediate one.

Choice D: "Arcanes secrètes d'un logiciel baladeur"

"Arcanes secrètes" = Secret mysteries / hidden workings
"logiciel baladeur" = wandering/roaming software

❌ ELIMINATED — Thematic Mismatch trap. "Arcanes secrètes" (secret mysteries) implies the article reveals HIDDEN technical aspects of the software. But the text doesn't discuss the software's internal workings — it discusses its USE, its POPULARITY, and its CONTROVERSIES. Nothing is "secret" in this article. The software is described as "facile d'accès" (easy to access) and "totalement gratuit" (completely free) — the opposite of mysterious.

"Baladeur" (wandering) is a creative description of the software's function (it lets you wander the globe), but the "arcanes secrètes" part fundamentally mischaracterizes the article's content.

Choice B: "Voyage virtuel contre dangers réels"

"Voyage virtuel" = Virtual travel/journey
"contre" = versus / against
"dangers réels" = real dangers

Verification — Title-to-Text Mapping:

"Voyage virtuel":
→ "survoler littéralement le monde" (fly over the world)
→ "se déplacer autour du globe" (move around the globe)
→ "descendre sur n'importe quel point" (zoom into any point)
✅ The software provides VIRTUAL TRAVEL

"contre" (versus):
→ The text presents ENTHUSIASM vs CONCERNS
→ Act 1 (virtual exploration) vs Act 2 (real worries)
✅ "Contre" captures the TENSION between the two

"dangers réels":
→ "atteinte à la vie privée" (privacy invasion)
→ "usage malveillant" (malicious use)
→ "sites nucléaires" (nuclear sites visible)
✅ These are REAL dangers evoked by detractors

But wait — doesn't the text RELATIVIZE the dangers? If the author says concerns are "très relatives," why does the title say "dangers réels" (real dangers)?

The answer is NUANCED: the title captures the DEBATE, not the verdict. "Voyage virtuel contre dangers réels" describes the TENSION the article explores. The article's conclusion (relativization) is the author's position WITHIN that debate — but the debate itself is between virtual exploration and real-world concerns. A title frames the TOPIC, not necessarily the conclusion.

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: THE "RELATIVIZATION SPECTRUM"
Authors can treat concerns in different ways:

DISMISS:      "These concerns are ridiculous" (emotional rejection)
RELATIVIZE:   "These concerns are very limited because..." (factual limitation)
VALIDATE:     "These concerns are fully justified" (full endorsement)
AMPLIFY:      "These concerns are even worse than stated" (escalation)

This text RELATIVIZES — it doesn't dismiss (too harsh), validate (contradicts "très relatives"), or amplify (contradicts the factual limitations provided). Choice D's "remises en perspective" captures "relativize" perfectly.

Strategy 2: The "Concern Treatment" Identification Technique

When a question asks HOW concerns are treated, look for these textual signals:

SIGNAL                    → TREATMENT TYPE
──────────────────────────────────────────────
"sont-elles légitimes?"   → Author QUESTIONS legitimacy
                            (= will relativize)
"très relatives"          → Author MINIMIZES scope
"dans la mesure où"       → Author provides EVIDENCE
                            for limitation
"il est exclu de"         → Author states FACTUAL 
                            impossibility
"pour l'instant"          → Author adds TEMPORAL 
                            qualifier (= not forever,
                            but currently limited)

When you see "sont-elles légitimes?" followed by "très relatives" and factual evidence, the treatment is: objective relativization.

Strategy 3: The "Concern ≠ Action" Checkpoint

Whenever a text discusses concerns/worries/controversies, explicitly check:

CHECKPOINT: Has anyone ACTED on these concerns?
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Filed a complaint?     → text says "aucune" = NO
Taken to court?        → text says "ni traîné en justice" = NO
Passed a law?          → not mentioned = NO
Banned the software?   → not mentioned = NO

CONCLUSION: Concerns are VOCAL but not INSTITUTIONAL
= people worry, but nobody has done anything about it

Any answer that implies ACTION (lawsuits, bans, prohibitions) contradicts the text's explicit "aucune...n'a porté plainte."

✅ STEP 5: WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

The 7 Rules for Technology/Ethics Texts

1. Identify the THREE ACTS: enthusiasm, concerns, verdict

Map where each act begins and ends. This tells you where to look for each question's answer. (New concept: Three-Act Rhetorical Structure.)

2. For "best title" questions, capture the TENSION, not just one side

A good title for a text with two sides frames the DEBATE, not just the positive or negative aspect. "Voyage virtuel contre dangers réels" captures both sides. (Evolves from Project 17's title-tone matching.)

3. Distinguish CONCERN from ACTION

Vocal criticism ≠ legal action. Worry ≠ prohibition. The text can describe intense concern alongside zero institutional response. Don't escalate concern into action unless the text explicitly does. (New concept in this project.)

4. For "how are concerns treated" questions, find the author's VERDICT signals

"Sont-elles légitimes?" + "très relatives" + factual evidence = objective relativization. Don't confuse the LISTING of concerns (Act 2) with the TREATMENT of concerns (Act 3). (New question type in this project.)

5. Check for CONDITIONAL language vs FACTUAL language

"Pourrait être fait" (could be done) = POTENTIAL. "A été fait" (was done) = ACTUAL. The text describes potential risks, not confirmed incidents. Answers that convert potential into actual are wrong. (Connects to Project 1's Hope vs Reality.)

6. Watch for "en quelques mois" and "traînée de poudre" as UNPRECEDENTED markers

When a text describes wildfire-speed adoption, the correct answer about impact will use words like "inédit" (unprecedented) or "engouement" (craze). (Connects to Project 17's vocabulary precision.)

7. Match the title's TONE to the text's CONCLUSION, not its middle

If the text concludes with relativization (concerns are limited), the title shouldn't sound purely alarmist. But it CAN acknowledge the tension. "Voyage virtuel contre dangers réels" acknowledges tension without concluding in alarm. (New refinement of Project 17's title technique.)
