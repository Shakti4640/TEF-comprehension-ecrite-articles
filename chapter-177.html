🔍 STEP 2: HOW TO APPROACH IT

Why this text is a PHASE 4 challenge

Every text you've encountered so far has had ONE dominant perspective. Even when multiple viewpoints existed (Project 16's antique dealers, Project 20's employers vs employees), one perspective was clearly the author's.

This text does something MORE SOPHISTICATED: it presents three perspectives simultaneously and the author SYMPATHIZES with aspects of ALL three:

PERSPECTIVE 1: "Le" bio — commercial organic (just sells organic products)
PERSPECTIVE 2: "La" bio — political organic (cares about production conditions, 
               equality, ecology — a whole societal project)
PERSPECTIVE 3: The old generation — defends conventional agriculture because 
               it genuinely IMPROVED their lives

The author CRITIQUES intensive agriculture BUT ALSO EXPLAINS why rejecting it is "sometimes poorly understood." This is not a black-and-white text. It requires you to hold nuance — to recognize that the author can BOTH criticize something AND understand why others value it.

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: NUANCED ARGUMENTATION
In nuanced texts, the author does NOT simply attack or defend one position. They:

  1. DEFINE the issue precisely (sometimes with multiple definitions)
  2. CRITIQUE one side with specific evidence
  3. PRESENT an alternative with its advantages
  4. BUT THEN acknowledge WHY the criticized side has defenders

The questions test whether you can navigate this nuance — picking up the CRITIQUE without missing the UNDERSTANDING, and vice versa.

The anatomy of THIS specific text

TYPE:        Article d'opinion nuancé (nuanced opinion piece)
LENGTH:      ~200 words (medium)
STRUCTURE:   
  Paragraph 1 = DUAL DEFINITION ("le" bio vs "la" bio)
  Paragraph 2 = CRITIQUE of intensive agriculture
  Paragraph 3 = ALTERNATIVE (agroecology) + its METHOD
  Paragraph 4 = HISTORICAL JUSTIFICATION of intensive 
                agriculture (why the old generation defends it)
DIFFICULTY:  ★★★★★ (5/5)
TRAP LEVEL:  ★★★★★ (5/5) — nuance traps, "reproche" precision

QUESTION 20: Cet article...

This is a "WHAT IS THE ARTICLE ABOUT" question — similar to Project 16's Q28.

But here it's more precise: the question asks what the article DOES (expose, analyse, explique, compare), not just what it's about.

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: DISTINGUISHING THE ARTICLE'S FUNCTION
Different verbs describe different things the article DOES:

"expose"   = lays out / presents (describes problems/issues)
"analyse"  = analyzes (examines strengths AND weaknesses)
"explique" = explains (gives reasons for a phenomenon)
"compare"  = compares (puts two things side by side)

You must match the verb to what the text ACTUALLY does.

What does this text ACTUALLY do?

Paragraph 1: DEFINES bio (two versions)           → exposes
Paragraph 2: CRITIQUES intensive agriculture       → exposes problems
Paragraph 3: PRESENTS agroecology as alternative   → exposes solution
Paragraph 4: EXPLAINS why old generation disagrees → exposes complexity

DOMINANT FUNCTION: The text EXPOSES the various ISSUES 
surrounding bio agriculture — definitions, critiques, 
alternatives, and historical resistance.

Eliminate systematically:

Choice B: "analyse les atouts et faiblesses de l'agriculture bio"

❌ ELIMINATED — Subject Swap + Partial Truth trap. The text does NOT analyze the strengths AND weaknesses of bio agriculture. It presents bio as the POSITIVE alternative and critiques INTENSIVE agriculture as the problematic one. The "weaknesses" discussed belong to CONVENTIONAL agriculture, not to bio. This choice swaps which system's weaknesses are discussed.

Furthermore, the text doesn't conduct a balanced analysis (pros AND cons of bio). It's more of an ADVOCACY piece for bio, tempered by an understanding of why conventional agriculture has defenders.

Choice C: "explique l'engouement actuel pour les produits bio"

"l'engouement actuel" = the current enthusiasm/craze

❌ ELIMINATED — Thematic Drift trap (Semantic Drift from Project 16). The text never discusses public ENTHUSIASM for bio products. It doesn't mention consumer behavior, market trends, or growing popularity. Instead, it discusses the PHILOSOPHICAL and AGRICULTURAL dimensions of bio. "Engouement" implies a consumer trend — the text discusses a societal project.

Choice D: "compare les produits issus du bio et du conventionnel"

❌ ELIMINATED — Function Mismatch trap. "Compare" means putting two things side by side to examine their similarities and differences. The text does NOT compare the PRODUCTS themselves (taste, nutrition, price, quality). It discusses the SYSTEMS of production — their philosophies, consequences, and historical context. Comparing products ≠ discussing production systems.

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: PRODUCT VS SYSTEM DISTINCTION
Bio texts can discuss either PRODUCTS (the food items themselves) or SYSTEMS (how agriculture works). This text is about SYSTEMS — agricultural methods, environmental consequences, societal visions. Any answer that focuses on PRODUCTS misidentifies the level of discussion.

Choice A: "expose les problématiques liées à l'agriculture bio"

"expose" = lays out / presents
"les problématiques liées à" = the issues related to
"l'agriculture bio" = organic agriculture

Verification — Function Match:

Does the text EXPOSE (lay out/present) issues?
Paragraph 1: Presents the DEFINITION problem (le vs la bio) ✅
Paragraph 2: Presents the CRITIQUE of intensive agriculture ✅
Paragraph 3: Presents the AGROECOLOGY alternative ✅
Paragraph 4: Presents the RESISTANCE to change ✅

Does it discuss "problématiques" (issues/problems)?
- Definition ambiguity → issue ✅
- Environmental damage → issue ✅
- Food inequality → issue ✅
- Climate vulnerability → issue ✅
- Resistance to change → issue ✅

CONTENT: "problématiques liées à l'agriculture bio" → ✅ MATCHES

✅ CORRECT. The text's primary function is to EXPOSE (lay out) the various ISSUES surrounding bio agriculture: what it means, what it critiques, what it proposes, and why the transition is complicated. "Expose les problématiques" is the most accurate description of what the article does.

QUESTION 21: L'auteur reproche à l'agriculture intensive...

🆕 NEW QUESTION TYPE: "L'AUTEUR REPROCHE" (the author blames/criticizes)
This question asks specifically what the author HOLDS AGAINST intensive agriculture. You must find the CRITICISM, not just any description. The verb "reproche" (blames/reproaches) signals that you're looking for NEGATIVE consequences attributed to intensive agriculture.

Locate the critique section (Paragraph 2 + beginning of Paragraph 3):

CRITIQUE 1: "ses CONSÉQUENCES ENVIRONNEMENTALES"
            → environmental consequences

CRITIQUE 2: "les INÉGALITÉS ALIMENTAIRES qu'engendre 
             le libre-échange agricole"
            → food inequalities caused by free trade

CRITIQUE 3: "la production s'avère non seulement ÉCLATÉE, 
             mais extrêmement SENSIBLE AUX ALÉAS CLIMATIQUES"
            → production is fragmented AND vulnerable to 
              climate hazards

Now synthesize: what does the author BLAME intensive agriculture for?

BLAME 1: Environmental damage
BLAME 2: Food inequality
BLAME 3: Fragile, climate-vulnerable production

Check each choice:

Choice A: "un surplus de production dû à l'exploitation intensive"

"surplus de production" = overproduction

❌ ELIMINATED — Invention trap. The text never mentions overproduction. In fact, it describes the OPPOSITE problem: production is "éclatée" (fragmented/scattered) and vulnerable. Fragmented production suggests INSUFFICIENCY, not surplus. This choice invents a critique the text doesn't make.

Choice B: "une confusion entre les différents produits du marché"

"confusion entre les produits" = confusion between products

❌ ELIMINATED — Product vs System confusion (new concept from Q20). The text discusses SYSTEMS of production, not confusion between PRODUCTS on the market. There is no mention of consumers being confused about which products are organic and which aren't. The text operates at the level of agricultural philosophy, not consumer experience.

Choice C: "une perte croissante des bénéfices de l'agriculture intensive"

"perte croissante des bénéfices" = growing loss of benefits/profits

❌ ELIMINATED — Invention + Contradiction trap. The text never says the BENEFITS of intensive agriculture are decreasing. In fact, Paragraph 4 DEFENDS those benefits historically ("a permis à de nombreux paysans de gagner moins péniblement leur vie"). The text acknowledges that intensive agriculture DID have benefits. It doesn't say those benefits are being lost — it says the METHOD has harmful consequences.

This is a subtle but critical distinction: the author criticizes the CONSEQUENCES of intensive agriculture, not a loss of its BENEFITS. The benefits existed (historically). The consequences exist too (environmentally). Both are true simultaneously — this is the nuance of the text.

Choice D: "une production agricole fragile et des disparités alimentaires"

"production agricole fragile" = fragile agricultural production
"des disparités alimentaires" = food disparities

Verification — Matching to textual critiques:

CRITIQUE FROM TEXT              → CHOICE D PARAPHRASE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"sensible aux aléas             → "production agricole 
 climatiques"                      fragile"
(vulnerable to climate hazards)    (fragile production)
✅ MATCH: vulnerable = fragile

"inégalités alimentaires"       → "disparités alimentaires"
(food inequalities)                (food disparities)
✅ MATCH: inégalités = disparités

✅ CORRECT. Choice D captures BOTH critiques in one formulation:

  • "Fragile" paraphrases "sensible aux aléas climatiques" (climate-vulnerable)
  • "Disparités alimentaires" paraphrases "inégalités alimentaires" (food inequalities)

This is the "combine" answer pattern — the correct answer merges two separate textual critiques into a single formulation using synonyms.

🆕 NEW CONCEPT: THE "COMBINE" ANSWER PATTERN
Sometimes the correct answer MERGES two pieces of evidence from different parts of the text into one sentence. Neither piece alone would be sufficient — you need BOTH. If you only found "fragile production" but missed "food inequalities," you might hesitate. The exam rewards COMPREHENSIVE reading.

QUESTION 22: L'un des défis de l'agroécologie consiste à...

This question asks about a CHALLENGE facing agroecology.

Locate the agroecology section (Paragraph 3):

"l'agroécologie envisage toute production agricole dans son 
contexte écologique et politique."

"Ses tenants affirment que c'est une pratique où LA SCIENCE 
ET LES SAVOIRS ANCESTRAUX PAYSANS SE RÉPONDENT, invitant à 
RÉORIENTER LA RECHERCHE AGRONOMIQUE."

Decode the key phrase:

"la science"                    = modern science / research
"les savoirs ancestraux paysans" = ancestral peasant knowledge / tradition
"se répondent"                  = complement each other / 
                                  dialogue with each other / 
                                  work in harmony

SYNTHESIS: Agroecology brings together MODERN SCIENCE 
and TRADITIONAL KNOWLEDGE so they complement each other.

And: "invitant à réorienter la recherche agronomique" = inviting a REDIRECTION of agricultural research — meaning the challenge is to CHANGE how research is done, integrating traditional knowledge.

Eliminate systematically:

Choice A: "revenir aux savoir-faire agricoles oubliés"

"revenir aux" = return to
"savoir-faire oubliés" = forgotten know-how

❌ ELIMINATED — Partial Truth + Reduction trap (from Project 18). The text says traditional knowledge should be COMBINED with science ("se répondent"). Choice A says we should RETURN to traditional knowledge alone. "Revenir" (return to) implies GOING BACK — abandoning modern science. The text doesn't propose going backward — it proposes COMBINING old and new.

This is a critical distinction: COMBINING tradition + science ≠ RETURNING to tradition alone.

Choice B: "intensifier la production sans la dénaturer"

"intensifier la production" = intensify production
"sans la dénaturer" = without distorting it

❌ ELIMINATED — Thematic Contradiction trap. The text CRITIQUES intensive agriculture. Agroecology is presented as the ALTERNATIVE to intensification. Proposing to "intensify production" as agroecology's challenge contradicts the entire argument. Agroecology doesn't want to intensify — it wants to REORIENT.

Choice C: "soustraire l'agriculture aux variations climatiques"

"soustraire aux variations climatiques" = protect/shield from climate variations

❌ ELIMINATED — Scope Overreach trap. The text mentions that intensive production is "sensible aux aléas climatiques" (vulnerable to climate hazards) — this is a CRITIQUE of intensive agriculture. But the text never says agroecology's challenge is to ELIMINATE climate vulnerability. Agroecology's described challenge is about integrating knowledge systems, not about defeating climate change.

Furthermore, "soustraire" (shield/remove from) implies complete protection from climate — which no agricultural system claims to achieve. This is Amplification (from Project 17) applied to a challenge description.

Choice D: "combiner tradition et expérimentation scientifique"

"combiner" = combine
"tradition" = tradition (ancestral knowledge)
"expérimentation scientifique" = scientific experimentation

✅ STEP 5: WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

The 6 Rules for Nuanced Argumentation Texts

1. Map PERSPECTIVES before answering — who says what?

The author's critique ≠ the old generation's defense. Modern concerns ≠ historical experience. Each paragraph may represent a DIFFERENT voice. Assign each question to the correct voice. (Evolves from Project 18's worldview comprehension.)

2. For "reproche" questions, find NEGATIVE evaluations only

Don't include neutral descriptions or positive aspects. "Reproche" means BLAME — find what the author considers WRONG with the target. (New question type in this project.)

3. For "défi" (challenge) questions, find what the text says NEEDS TO BE DONE

Not what HAS been done, not what WAS done, but what SHOULD be done. "Réorienter la recherche" and "la science et les savoirs se répondent" describe the CHALLENGE. (Connects to Project 17's status awareness.)

4. Watch for the "combine" pattern in correct answers

When the text presents multiple critiques or multiple features, the correct answer often MERGES them. Look for choices that capture MORE of the text than single-element alternatives. (New pattern in this project.)

5. Never attribute MODERN critiques to the OLD generation

Environmental concerns, sustainability, climate vulnerability — these are the author's perspective. The old generation talks about economic improvement and livelihood. Don't cross-pollinate perspectives. (Connects to Project 18's perspective isolation.)

6. Decode "se répondent" and similar relational verbs precisely

"Se répondent" = complement each other (not "respond to" or "answer back"). These relational verbs describe HOW two things INTERACT. The correct answer must capture the nature of the interaction. (Connects to Project 16's abstract vocabulary decoding.)

❌ STEP 6: WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

❌ SIN 1: Confusing "expose" with "analyse" or "compare"

"Expose" (presents/lays out) is DIFFERENT from "analyse" (examines pros and cons) and "compare" (puts side by side). The text PRESENTS issues — it doesn't conduct a balanced analysis of bio's strengths AND weaknesses, nor does it compare individual products. Match the verb to the function. (New concept: function matching.)

❌ SIN 2: Interpreting "combiner" as "revenir à"

"Combine tradition and science" ≠ "return to tradition." Combining means BOTH work together. Returning means abandoning one for the other. This is the Reduction trap from Project 18 — shrinking a dual concept into a single one.

❌ SIN 3: Assuming the author's critique IS the old generation's view

The author criticizes environmental damage. The old generation defends economic progress. These are DIFFERENT perspectives in DIFFERENT paragraphs. If a question asks about the old generation, answer from Paragraph 4, not Paragraph 2. (Perspective Swap trap — variant of Worldview Inversion from Project 18.)

❌ SIN 4: Choosing "surplus de production" when the text describes "production éclatée"

"Éclatée" (fragmented/scattered) is the OPPOSITE of "surplus" (overproduction). The text describes a production system that is INSUFFICIENT and vulnerable, not one that produces too much. Read vocabulary precisely. (Connects to Project 1's every-word-matters rule.)

❌ SIN 5: Choosing "intensifier" when the text CRITIQUES intensification

The text criticizes "l'agriculture intensive." Proposing "intensifier la production" as agroecology's challenge would mean agroecology wants to do MORE of what the text criticizes. This contradicts the entire argument. (Thematic Contradiction — seen throughout the projects.)
