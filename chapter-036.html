Texte 68 : Un piège pour les baleines

Sans doute attirées par une espèce particulière de poissons, des baleines blanches sont remontées vers le nord du Canada en suivant d'immenses fissures dans la banquise. Mais un refroidissement soudain a transformé ces prédateurs en proies, la glace se refermant sur elles. Des ours polaires les ont rapidement repérées et les ont attaquées jusqu'à ce qu'elles soient assez faibles pour être traînées hors de l'eau. « C'est brutal et ce n'est pas joli à voir », déclare un écologiste canadien. Il se félicite de cette occasion unique offerte cette année à quelques privilégiés de voir « le côté noir de la nature », celui qu'on ne voit pas souvent à la télévision ou dans les films de Walt Disney.

Selon les spécialistes, ce phénomène se produit sans doute tous les ans (le plus important de notre histoire récente a rassemblé plusieurs centaines de baleines près du Groenland dans les années 60). Mais ce qui est rare, c'est de pouvoir y assister. Les baleines ont survécu en maintenant une ouverture de quelques dizaines de mètres carrés, qui leur permet de remonter à la surface pour respirer.

Une communauté Inuit s'est aussi mobilisée pour sauver les baleines. Ils ont utilisé des sortes de tire-bouchons géants et des scies pour forer une demi-douzaine de trous afin que les baleines puissent respirer. Ils espèrent ainsi pouvoir les guider vers la mer.

Les scientifiques aimeraient pouvoir effectuer des recherches sur ce phénomène biologique mais ils ont du mal à trouver des financements pour des expéditions coûteuses, surtout en urgence. Ce phénomène n'a cependant rien d'alarmant, soulignent les chercheurs. Ils savent que dans quelques mois, le détroit de Lancaster sera de nouveau envahi de baleines blanches qui attendront que la glace se fissure.

  3. Attempted ≠ Accomplished — The Inuit "espèrent" (hope) to guide whales; they haven't succeeded yet

Step 4: Master the "Attempted vs. Accomplished" Distinction

This is the chapter's most critical analytical skill. The text contains actions at different stages of completion:

COMPLETED ACTIONS (past tense, definite):
  ✓ Whales went north → "sont remontées" (went up)
  ✓ Ice closed on them → "la glace se refermant" (ice closing)
  ✓ Bears attacked → "les ont attaquées" (attacked them)
  ✓ Inuit drilled holes → "ont utilisé... pour forer" (used... to drill)
  
ATTEMPTED/HOPED-FOR ACTIONS (conditional, hopeful):
  ○ Inuit hope to guide whales → "espèrent pouvoir les guider" (HOPE to guide)
  ○ Scientists want to research → "aimeraient pouvoir" (WOULD LIKE to)
  
NOT HAPPENED:
  ✗ Scientists haven't organized expedition → "ont du mal à trouver des financements"
  ✗ Whales haven't been saved yet → still trapped, Inuit are trying

The exam LOVES testing whether you confuse "hoped for" with "accomplished" and whether you assign the right action to the right actor.

Step 5: Detect the "Emotion vs. Assessment" Split

The text contains two conflicting emotional registers:

EMOTIONAL REGISTER (Paragraphs 1, 3):
  - "Brutal," "pas joli à voir" → dramatic, alarming
  - "Sauver les baleines" → rescue narrative, urgent
  - "Piège" (trap) → danger framing

SCIENTIFIC REGISTER (Paragraphs 2, 4):
  - "Se produit sans doute tous les ans" → normal, routine
  - "N'a rien d'alarmant" → explicitly NOT alarming
  - "Dans quelques mois... de nouveau envahi" → cyclical, will resolve naturally

The exam will test whether you follow the emotional register (alarming, urgent, rare) or the scientific register (normal, cyclical, not alarming). The correct answers almost always align with the scientific register because it represents the text's final, authoritative assessment.

EMOTIONAL IMPRESSION:    This is dramatic, rare, and alarming!
SCIENTIFIC ASSESSMENT:   This is normal, annual, and not alarming.
TEXT'S FINAL WORD:       Scientific assessment overrides emotional impression.

QUESTION 30: Le plus grand danger pour les baleines...

Step 1: Identify the question type

"Le plus grand danger" = the greatest danger. This is a PRIORITY/HIERARCHY question — you must determine which threat is the MOST dangerous, not just which threats exist.

Step 2: Identify all threats mentioned

From the text, the whales face multiple threats:

ThreatEvidenceSeverity
Cold/ice"un refroidissement soudain... la glace se refermant"Created the trap — but whales can survive in cold water
Bears"Des ours polaires les ont... attaquées"Killed some whales — but only after they were already trapped
Lack of air"maintenant une ouverture... pour remonter à la surface pour respirer" + "forer des trous afin que les baleines puissent respirer"The PRIMARY concern — both whales and Inuit focus on BREATHING

Now determine the hierarchy. Which threat is the GREATEST?

The text provides two critical clues:

Clue 1 — What the whales prioritize:

"Les baleines ont survécu en maintenant une ouverture... qui leur permet de remonter à la surface pour respirer."

The whales' survival strategy is maintaining a breathing hole. They don't fight bears or flee the cold — they maintain ACCESS TO AIR. This tells us that air is their primary need.

Clue 2 — What the Inuit prioritize:

"forer une demi-douzaine de trous afin que les baleines puissent respirer"

The Inuit's rescue strategy also focuses on BREATHING. They don't fight bears or break ice to free the whales entirely — they drill breathing holes. Two independent actors (whales + Inuit) both prioritize the same thing: air access.

Clue 3 — The causal hierarchy:

ROOT CAUSE:     Ice closes (cold)
    ↓
PRIMARY DANGER: Whales can't reach surface to BREATHE
    ↓
SECONDARY:      Weakened whales become vulnerable to BEARS
    ↓
BEARS attack:   Only possible BECAUSE whales are already trapped and weakened

The bears are a secondary threat — they attack BECAUSE the whales are already weakened by the primary threat (can't breathe). The cold is the trigger (it closed the ice), but the cold itself doesn't kill the whales — they're adapted to cold water. What kills them is being unable to BREATHE.

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "le froid"
(The cold.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The cold caused the ice to close, which trapped the whales. But the cold is the TRIGGER, not the ongoing danger. Whales are Arctic animals — they can survive cold water. The danger isn't the temperature; it's the ice blocking their access to air. The cold created the situation, but the situation's danger is suffocation, not freezing.Cold is the trigger/cause, not the ongoing greatest danger.❌ TRIGGER ≠ GREATEST DANGER

Option B: "les ours"
(The bears.)

TestReasoningVerdict
Bears do attack and kill whales. But they can only do so because the whales are ALREADY trapped and weakened. If the whales could breathe freely, they would swim away. The bears are an OPPORTUNISTIC secondary threat, not the primary one. Also, the text's focus on breathing (both whales and Inuit prioritize it) suggests air is more critical than bears.Bears are secondary — they exploit the primary danger (trapped whales), they don't create it.❌ SECONDARY THREAT, NOT PRIMARY

Option C: "le manque d'air"
(The lack of air.)

TestReasoningVerdict
Both whales and Inuit focus on BREATHING. Whales maintain a breathing hole. Inuit drill breathing holes. The word "respirer" (breathe) appears TWICE in the text — once for each actor's strategy. The entire survival effort centers on air access. Without air, whales die regardless of bears or cold.Both survival strategies focus on breathing → air is the primary need → lack of air is the greatest danger.✅ MATCH

Option D: "l'homme"
(Humans.)

TestReasoningVerdict
Humans in this text are either HELPING (Inuit drill holes) or OBSERVING (ecologist, scientists). No human action threatens the whales. The Inuit are explicitly trying to SAVE them. Scientists want to STUDY them. There is zero human threat.Humans are helpers/observers, not threats.❌ OPPOSITE OF TEXT'S PORTRAYAL

✅ ANSWER: C — le manque d'air

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Listed all threats from the text
  2. Applied causal hierarchy analysis: trigger (cold) → primary danger (no air) → secondary danger (bears)
  3. Confirmed with the dual-strategy test: both whales and Inuit prioritize breathing
  4. Used the word frequency signal: "respirer" appears twice, highlighting its importance
  5. Defeated Trap A by distinguishing the trigger from the ongoing danger
  6. Defeated Trap B by identifying bears as secondary/opportunistic

New concept — Causal Hierarchy Analysis:

When multiple threats exist, determine which is the ROOT danger by asking:

HIERARCHY TEST:
  1. Which threat would STILL exist if the others disappeared?
     → If bears disappeared, whales still can't breathe → AIR is primary
     → If air access were restored, bears couldn't catch whales → BEARS are secondary
     
  2. Which threat do the ACTORS in the text prioritize?
     → Whales maintain breathing hole → prioritize AIR
     → Inuit drill breathing holes → prioritize AIR
     → No one fights bears → bears are secondary concern
     
  3. Which threat is CAUSED by another?
     → Cold causes ice closure → ice blocks air → bears exploit weakness
     → CHAIN: cold → no air → bears
     → ROOT of chain = cold (trigger), but ONGOING danger = no air

"aimeraient pouvoir" (would like to)○ WISHEDScientists want to research
"ont du mal à" (struggle to)✗ FAILED/BLOCKEDScientists can't find funding

Verb form signals:

SignalStatus
Passé composé (ont fait)Completed
Conditionnel (aimeraient)Wished/hypothetical
Espérer + infinitiveHoped for, not accomplished
Avoir du mal àStruggling, not succeeding

If an option uses passé composé (completed) but the text uses conditionnel (wished) or espérer (hoped), the option is wrong.

3. Apply the Emotion vs. Assessment Split

Formalized from Q28 (Trap C) and Q31 (Trap C):

EMOTIONAL REGISTER:                    SCIENTIFIC REGISTER:
  What the event LOOKS like              What the event IS
  "Brutal, pas joli à voir"             "Se produit tous les ans"
  "Occasion unique"                      "N'a rien d'alarmant"
  "Sauver les baleines"                 "Dans quelques mois, ils reviendront"

  FEELS: rare, alarming, urgent         IS: annual, not alarming, cyclical

  EXAM RULE: The scientific register overrides the emotional register.
  When the text provides BOTH, follow the SCIENCE.

This principle builds on Chapter 9's "text topic vs. reader's inference" (→ Project 9). There, you learned not to add your own conclusions. Here, you learn not to let the text's dramatic framing override its scientific conclusions.

4. Distinguish "The Phenomenon" from "The Observation"

This is the most nuanced distinction in this chapter:

THE PHENOMENON (what happens):
  Whales get trapped by ice
  → Happens probably EVERY YEAR
  → NOT alarming
  → Cyclical, natural

THE OBSERVATION (humans witnessing it):
  Being able to see this happen
  → RARE ("ce qui est rare, c'est de pouvoir y assister")
  → Unique opportunity this year
  → "Le côté noir de la nature"

TRAP: Merging the observation's qualities with the phenomenon's assessment
  "Rare to witness" ≠ "rare phenomenon"
  "Dramatic to watch" ≠ "alarming phenomenon"

When the exam describes the phenomenon, use the PHENOMENON'S qualities (annual, not alarming). When it describes the observation, use the OBSERVATION'S qualities (rare, dramatic). Don't cross-contaminate.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Swap Actors Between Paragraphs

The exam's favorite trick in multi-actor texts is assigning Actor A's action to Actor B:

Text SaysTrap ClaimsError
Inuit hope to guide whalesResearchers guided whalesActor swap: Inuit → Researchers
Inuit drilled holesScientists organized expeditionActor swap + action swap
Bears attacked whalesInuit chased bearsActor swap + invented action

Defense: For every option, verify TWO things: (1) Is the ACTOR correct? (2) Is the ACTION correct? Both must match the text.

2. Do NOT Confuse "Wished" with "Done"

This is the chapter's signature error and it applies to two options:

OPTION A (Q29): "les chercheurs les ont guidées"
  TEXT: Inuit "espèrent pouvoir les guider" → HOPED, not done
  TRAP: "ont guidées" → DONE (passé composé)

OPTION C (Q29): "les scientifiques ont organisé une expédition"
  TEXT: Scientists "aimeraient pouvoir" + "ont du mal" → WISHED but FAILED
  TRAP: "ont organisé" → DONE (passé composé)

Both traps convert an unfulfilled wish into a completed action. Always check the verb mood in the text against the verb mood in the option.

3. Do NOT Let the Title Influence Your Assessment

The title is "Un piège pour les baleines" (A trap for the whales). This framing is dramatic — it presents the event as dangerous and alarming. But the text's scientific content says it's "not alarming" and "happens every year."

TITLE:      "A trap for the whales"  → Dramatic, alarming framing
TEXT (P4):  "Nothing alarming"       → Scientific, reassuring conclusion

The title hooks you emotionally.
The text concludes rationally.
Follow the conclusion, not the hook.

This is a more advanced version of the "entry point ≠ subject" principle from Chapter 9 (→ Project 9). The title is the entry point; the scientific assessment is the conclusion.

4. Do NOT Merge "Rare to See" with "Rare to Happen"

Option C in Q31 makes this exact error:

TEXT SAYS:  "ce phénomène se produit sans doute tous les ans"
            = the phenomenon probably happens EVERY YEAR (frequent)
            
            "ce qui est rare, c'est de pouvoir y assister"
            = what's rare is being able to WITNESS it (rarely seen)

OPTION C:   "c'est un phénomène rarissime"
            = it's an extremely rare PHENOMENON (rarely happens)

THE ERROR:  "Rarely witnessed" ≠ "Rarely happens"
            The phenomenon is COMMON.
            The observation is RARE.
            Option C applies "rare" to the wrong element.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Emotional Framing" Override

This text is emotionally charged: trapped whales, brutal bear attacks, Inuit rescue attempts. Candidates who respond to the emotional content will:

  • Think the phenomenon is alarming (it's not)
  • Think rescue expeditions are planned (they're not)
  • Think the text is a manifesto (it's a nature report)
  • Think the event is rare (the event is annual; witnessing it is rare)

The scientific register ALWAYS overrides the emotional register in nature/science texts. When a text provides both emotional description and scientific assessment, the assessment is the text's final word.

Pitfall 2: The "Multiple Expert Voices" Confusion

The text uses three terms for experts:

TermParagraphRole
Un écologiste canadienP1Emotional commentary ("brutal, not pretty")
Les spécialistesP2Scientific context ("happens annually")
Les chercheurs / scientifiquesP4Scientific assessment ("not alarming") + research wish

Candidates might merge these into one "scientist" voice and then attribute any expert's statement to any other expert. But the ecologist's emotional commentary is different from the scientists' reassuring assessment.

Defense: Track which expert says what. Don't transfer the ecologist's "brutal" to the scientists' "not alarming" or vice versa.

Pitfall 3: The "Actor Proximity" Trap

Option D in Q29 says "les Inuits ont chassé les ours polaires." This is tempting because:

  • The Inuit are HELPERS (they try to save whales)
  • The bears are THREATS (they attack whales)
  • It's LOGICAL that helpers would fight threats

But the text never describes any interaction between Inuit and bears. They exist in separate paragraphs and separate timeframes. The exam creates a plausible scenario by combining actors who never interact.

Defense: Only accept actor interactions that are EXPLICITLY described in the text. Logical plausibility is not evidence.

Pitfall 4: The "Direction Reversal" in Option B (Q31)

Option B says ice pushes whales SOUTH. The text says whales went NORTH. This is a simple directional swap, but under time pressure, candidates who remember "whales + ice + movement" might not verify the direction.

Defense: For any option involving direction, movement, or geographic location, verify the EXACT direction/location against the text. North ≠ South. Toward ≠ Away from. Up ≠ Down.
