🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's different from ALL previous projects?

Projects 1-4 dealt with OBJECTIVE texts — facts, descriptions, rules, concepts. The author's personal feelings were irrelevant. You could point to a sentence and say "this is true or false."

This text is SUBJECTIVE. It is one person's OPINION about a film. This changes EVERYTHING about how you read:

Objective Text (Projects 1-4)Subjective Text (Project 5)
The text states FACTSThe text expresses OPINIONS
Right/wrong answers are based on WHAT IS SAIDRight/wrong answers are based on WHAT THE AUTHOR THINKS
Neutral toneEmotional/evaluative tone
"What does the text describe?""What does the author feel?"
Look for INFORMATIONLook for JUDGMENT

This distinction is the single most important skill for the second half of this guide. From here on, many texts will contain opinions, arguments, and subtle positions.

Step 1: Identify the text type

Quick scan reveals:

  • A film title and plot summary
  • Personal pronouns ("moi, j'ai aimé")
  • Evaluative adjectives ("crapuleux," "chaleureux," "simple")
  • A recommendation ("ne laissons pas passer")
  • Comparison to other films/traditions

Verdict: FILM REVIEW / CRITIQUE (critique cinématographique)

Step 3: The "Opinion Decoder" technique (NEW)

In a film review, the author's opinion is NEVER stated in one simple sentence like "I think this film is good." Instead, it's scattered across multiple signals — words, phrases, comparisons, and even what the author DOESN'T say.

Before answering, you need to collect ALL opinion signals and map them on a scale:

THE OPINION SCALE:

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT — Question by Question

QUESTION 25: Dans cet article, l'objectif de l'auteur est de...

What the question really asks: What is the FUNCTION of this text? What is the author TRYING to do?

This question tests whether you understand the DIFFERENCE between:

  • Telling a story
  • Selling a product
  • Presenting a person
  • Evaluating a work

The text does ALL of these partially — it summarizes the plot, it recommends the film, it mentions Agnès Brown — but what is its PRIMARY function?

Let me test each option:

Option A: "faire la critique d'un film" (review a film)

The text:

  1. Summarizes the plot (Dublin 1967, widow, seven children...)
  2. Evaluates the film ("ça marche," "j'ai aimé")
  3. Acknowledges weaknesses ("ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses")
  4. Places it in context ("la spécialité des cinémas italiens et français")
  5. Makes a recommendation ("ne laissons pas passer")

These are ALL elements of a film review/critique. The text follows the classic critique structure:

CRITIQUE STRUCTURE:
Plot summary → Evaluation → Context → Recommendation
     ✅            ✅          ✅           ✅

✅ CORRECT — All elements of a critique are present

Option B: "faire la publicité d'un film" (advertise a film)

A publicity text would:

  • Only say positive things (no acknowledgment of weaknesses)
  • Use superlatives ("le meilleur film de l'année!")
  • Include practical information (release date, cinema listings)
  • NOT include personal opinions ("moi, j'ai aimé")

This text acknowledges a flaw ("ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses") and says it's NOT a masterpiece ("sans espérer le chef-d'œuvre"). An advertisement would NEVER say these things.

But this trap is clever because the text IS positive and DOES recommend the film. The difference between a positive critique and an advertisement is subtle but real:

Critique (even positive)Advertisement
Acknowledges weaknessesHides weaknesses
Personal judgment ("moi, j'ai aimé")Impersonal claims ("le film de l'année")
Nuanced recommendationAbsolute recommendation
Compares to other worksStands alone

❌ ELIMINATED — The text has critique elements that an ad would never include

This introduces a critical distinction:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #16: A POSITIVE review is NOT the same as an advertisement. Reviews acknowledge nuance; ads only promote. When the text says both good AND limiting things, it's a review, not a promotion.

Option C: "présenter une actrice" (present an actress)

Agnès Brown is a CHARACTER, not an actress. The text never mentions the actress who plays the role. It talks about the CHARACTER Agnès Brown (widow, fruit seller, seven children) and the FILM, not the performer.

This is a character-actor confusion trap — assuming that discussing a character means presenting the person who plays them.

❌ ELIMINATED — Agnès Brown is a character, not an actress

Option D: "raconter l'histoire d'Agnès Brown" (tell the story of Agnès Brown)

The text DOES summarize the plot. But plot summary is only the FIRST PART of the text. The MAJORITY of the text is evaluation:

Text SectionContentLength
Plot summaryDublin, widow, children, loan shark, Tom Jones~30%
Critique/evaluation"ça marche," "j'ai aimé," comparisons, recommendation~70%

When the PRIMARY function is evaluation and the plot summary is only a TOOL for the critique, the objective is critique, not storytelling.

❌ ELIMINATED — Plot summary serves the critique, it IS NOT the objective

This introduces another important distinction:

A film critique CONTAINS a plot summary, but the plot summary is a MEANS, not the END. The objective is to EVALUATE, not to narrate.

🎯 Answer: A — faire la critique d'un film

THE CRITICAL LESSON FROM Q26:

The correct answer is NOT found in any single sentence. It is the SYNTHESIS of ALL opinion signals. No single quote says "plutôt bien" — but the PATTERN of all signals together points to exactly this conclusion.

This is why Golden Rule #15 matters: collect ALL signals, THEN judge.

Let me show you what happens if you rely on SINGLE signals:

If you only read...You might conclude...Which would be...
"j'ai aimé" alone"chef-d'œuvre" (he loved it!)❌ Too positive

Single signals mislead. Patterns reveal truth.

QUESTION 27: Quel adjectif qualifie le mieux le film ?

What the question really asks: Which ONE WORD best captures the essence of this film, according to the author?

This is the most nuanced question type in the exam. It requires you to distill the ENTIRE review into a single adjective. Let me test each option against the text's evidence:

Option A: "Subtil" (Subtle)

The text says: "un film qui ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses socio-psychologiques"

"Ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses" = doesn't bother with subtleties. This is the author saying, quite directly, that the film is NOT subtle.

But notice the tone: the author doesn't say this as a criticism. He says it matter-of-factly, then follows with "Et pourtant, ça marche" (And yet, it works). The LACK of subtlety is acknowledged as a characteristic, not condemned as a flaw.

Still, the adjective "subtil" directly contradicts this statement.

❌ ELIMINATED — The text explicitly says the film lacks subtlety

Option B: "Vulgaire" (Vulgar)

The text says: "jamais vulgaire"

"Jamais vulgaire" = NEVER vulgar. The text explicitly denies this quality.

Again, Golden Rule #13 — "vulgaire" appears inside a negation ("jamais"). The exam offers the word itself as an answer, hoping you remember the word but forget the negation.

❌ ELIMINATED — Explicitly negated by "jamais" (Rule #13)

Option C: "Triste" (Sad)

The story involves a widow, debt, and a loan shark — potentially sad elements. But the AUTHOR's description emphasizes:

  • "ça marche" → it works (entertaining)
  • "sans complexes" → uncomplicated (fun)
  • "rythmé comme une ballade irlandaise" → rhythmic (lively)
  • "divertissement chaleureux" → warm entertainment
  • "Heureusement, Agnès s'en sortira" → happy ending

The story COULD be sad, but the author presents it as warm and entertaining, not sad. The tone is uplifting, not melancholic.

This is a critical distinction:

The PLOT might be sad, but the FILM's overall tone (according to the reviewer) is warm and entertaining. The question asks about the FILM, not the plot.

This trap exploits the confusion between story content and film character/tone:

LevelWhat It ContainsTone
PLOT levelWidow, debt, hardshipPotentially sad
FILM levelHow the story is toldWarm, rhythmic, entertaining

❌ ELIMINATED — Confuses plot content with film character

This introduces a vital new principle:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #17: In a review, distinguish between WHAT HAPPENS IN THE STORY (plot content) and HOW THE REVIEWER CHARACTERIZES THE WORK (tone/judgment). Questions about "the film" target the reviewer's characterization, not the plot summary.

Option D: "Simple" (Simple)

The text says:

  • "ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses" → not complicated
  • "sans complexes" → uncomplicated, straightforward
  • "le plaisir simple d'un divertissement chaleureux" → simple pleasure

The word "simple" actually APPEARS in the text ("plaisir simple"). But wait — Golden Rule #2 says correct answers rephrase, and if a word is copied directly, it might be a trap.

However, in this case, "simple" is not just a recycled word. It's the CENTRAL characterization of the entire review. The author builds toward it:

PROGRESSION TOWARD "SIMPLE":
"ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses" → not subtle
     ↓
"sans complexes" → uncomplicated
     ↓
"plaisir SIMPLE" → SIMPLE pleasure
     ↓
"divertissement chaleureux" → warm entertainment

Every evaluative phrase converges on the same idea: SIMPLICITY as a quality, not a deficiency. The author is saying: "This film is simple, and that's perfectly fine."

In this rare case, the text word IS the correct answer because the question literally asks for the best adjective, and the author literally provides it.

✅ CORRECT — The central characterization of the entire review

🎯 Answer: D — Simple

THE LESSON FROM Q27: When Golden Rule #2 has an exception

Golden Rule #2 says correct answers rephrase. But when a question asks "which adjective best describes..." the answer MAY be a word directly from the text — because the question is asking you to IDENTIFY the key word, not to paraphrase.

Rule #2 applies to CONTENT questions ("What does the text say?"). For CHARACTERIZATION questions ("What word best describes...?"), the answer may be the author's own word.

This is not a contradiction of Rule #2 — it's a refinement. The rule still holds for 90% of questions. But characterization/tone questions are the exception.

QUESTION 28: Au départ, Agnès Brown est le nom...

What the question really asks: What was the ORIGINAL form of the Agnès Brown story?

The key sentence:

"Au départ, un roman très populaire puis une série télévisée ont fait d'Agnès Brown une héroïne familière aux Irlandais."

This sentence gives a chronological sequence:

TIMELINE:
Step 1: "un roman" (a novel) → FIRST
Step 2: "puis une série télévisée" (then a TV series) → SECOND
Step 3: [the film being reviewed] → THIRD (current)

The question asks "au départ" = at the beginning / originally. The FIRST step is "un roman."

Now let's test:

Option A: "d'un livre" (a book)

The text says: "un roman très populaire"

Text saysOption A saysRelationship
"un roman" (a novel)"un livre" (a book)PARAPHRASE ✅

A novel IS a book. "Roman" → "livre" is a hypernym paraphrase (specific → general category).

✅ CORRECT — Novel = book (specific → general paraphrase)

Option B: "d'un téléfilm" (a TV movie)

The text mentions "une série télévisée" — but this came SECOND ("puis"), not first ("au départ").

Two problems:

  1. Temporal error: "Série télévisée" was step 2, not step 1. The question asks about the ORIGIN.
  2. Category error: "Série télévisée" (TV series) ≠ "téléfilm" (TV movie). A series has multiple episodes; a TV movie is a single production.

This is Pitfall #8 from Project 3 (Temporal Misassignment) combined with Subtle Substitution (Project 1).

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong chronological position AND wrong format

Option C: "d'une chanson" (a song)

The text mentions "rythmé comme une ballade irlandaise" — but "ballade" here means a folk song/ballad used as a COMPARISON for the film's rhythm. It does NOT mean the story originated as a song.

This is the Comparison-as-Fact trap from Project 3 (Rule #10). The text uses "ballade" as a METAPHOR, and the answer treats it as a literal origin.

Text saysWhat it meansWhat Option C claims
"rythmé comme une ballade"The film has a rhythm LIKE a ballad (comparison)The story started as a song (literal)

❌ ELIMINATED — Metaphorical comparison treated as literal fact (Rule #10)

Option D: "d'une actrice" (an actress)

The text describes Agnès Brown as a CHARACTER ("une héroïne familière aux Irlandais"), not an actress. This is the same character-actor confusion from Q25 Option C.

❌ ELIMINATED — Character ≠ actress

🎯 Answer: A — d'un livre

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

The "Three-Layer Reading" technique for reviews (NEW)

Every film/book/art review has THREE layers. You must separate them:

LAYER 1: FACTUAL INFORMATION
├── Setting (Dublin 1967)
├── Characters (Agnès, 7 children, loan shark, Tom Jones)
├── Origin (novel → TV series → film)
└── Genre (melodrama, entertainment)

LAYER 2: AUTHOR'S EVALUATION
├── Positive signals ("ça marche," "j'ai aimé")
├── Negative signals / limitations ("ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses")
├── Overall position (positive but modest)
└── Recommendation ("ne laissons pas passer")

LAYER 3: CHARACTERIZATION / TONE
├── Key adjectives (simple, chaleureux, sans complexes)
├── Comparisons (ballade irlandaise, cinéma italien/français)
└── Implied qualities (warmth, rhythm, simplicity)

Different questions target different layers:

  • Q28 targets Layer 1 (factual)
  • Q26 targets Layer 2 (evaluation)
  • Q27 targets Layer 3 (characterization)
  • Q25 targets the overall TEXT FUNCTION

Never answer a Layer 2 question with Layer 1 evidence, or vice versa.

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For Film Reviews and Critique Texts Specifically:

1. Collect ALL opinion signals before judging.
Never conclude from one phrase. Map the full pattern (Rule #15).

2. Check the Opinion Scale position.
Is the author very negative, negative, mixed, positive, or very positive? The answer almost always matches the MODERATE positions (plutôt bien / plutôt mauvais) rather than extremes.

3. Separate plot from judgment.
The story may be sad, but the film may be praised. The question asks about the FILM as experienced, not the story as told (Rule #17).

4. Watch for negation traps in evaluative vocabulary.
"Jamais vulgaire" → NOT vulgar. "Sans espérer le chef-d'œuvre" → NOT a masterpiece. Words inside negations are bait (Rule #13).

5. Identify the author's CENTRAL WORD.
In every review, there's usually ONE key word or phrase that captures the author's core judgment. Here it's "simple." Finding this word makes characterization questions immediate.

6. Track chronological sequences with "puis," "ensuite," "au départ."
When the text gives a timeline (novel → TV → film), questions may ask about any point. "Au départ" = the first one.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 14: The Single-Signal Judgment (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 14, 17, 19, 22, 23.

What it is: Basing your understanding of the author's opinion on ONE phrase instead of the full pattern.

How to detect: After choosing your answer, ask: "Is there ANY other phrase in the text that contradicts my choice?"

In this text:

  • "J'ai aimé" alone → might suggest masterpiece
  • "Ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses" alone → might suggest bad film
  • Both together → positive but modest = "plutôt bien"

Defense: Always collect ALL evaluative phrases before deciding.

PITFALL 15: The Plot-Tone Confusion (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 15, 17, 22.

What it is: Confusing the emotional content of the STORY with the critic's characterization of the FILM.

How to detect: When an adjective seems to describe the story rather than the film, ask: "Is the author saying the film IS this way, or just that the story CONTAINS this element?"

In this text: The story involves hardship (potentially sad), but the author describes the FILM as warm, rhythmic, and entertaining (not sad).

Defense: Use the Three-Layer Reading technique. Plot = Layer 1. Critic's judgment = Layer 2. Never cross-contaminate.

PITFALL 16: The Positive Review ≠ Advertisement Confusion (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 15, 17.

What it is: Assuming that because a reviewer likes something, they're advertising it.

How to detect: Check for NUANCE. Does the text acknowledge ANY weakness or limitation? If yes → critique. If only superlatives → advertisement.

In this text: "Ne s'embarrasse pas de finesses" and "sans espérer le chef-d'œuvre" = acknowledged limitations → critique, not ad.

Defense: Apply Rule #16 — reviews have nuance; ads don't.

🌉 BRIDGE TO PHASE 2

Phase 2 (Projects 6-10) introduces a completely new question format: the TEXT À TROUS (gap-fill text).

Project 6 (Bien Recycler pour Limiter les Déchets) will be your first gap-fill challenge. You will learn:
