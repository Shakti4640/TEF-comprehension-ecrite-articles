No single question in our exam set asks directly about tone. But tone understanding underlies every question you have answered so far — and misreading tone is the hidden cause of many wrong answers. This chapter makes the invisible visible.

🧠 THE PROBLEM: TONE IS THE INVISIBLE LAYER

Every text has two layers of meaning:

LAYER 1: CONTENT (what is said)
  → "Ray Kurzweil est une vedette de la prospective scientifique"
  → CONTENT: Kurzweil is famous in the field of scientific futurism
  → This is what Chapters 1-13 taught you to analyze

LAYER 2: TONE (how it is said)
  → The same fact could be stated in many tones:
  
  NEUTRAL:     "Kurzweil est un spécialiste reconnu de la prospective"
  ADMIRING:    "Kurzweil est un visionnaire brillant de la prospective"
  IRONIC:      "Kurzweil est une vedette de la prospective scientifique"
  DISMISSIVE:  "Un certain Kurzweil prétend prédire l'avenir"
  HOSTILE:     "Kurzweil, ce charlatan de la prospective"
  
  The text CHOSE "vedette" (celebrity/star).
  Why "vedette" and not "spécialiste" or "expert"?
  
  "Vedette" carries a subtle AMBIGUITY:
  → It can be POSITIVE (a star, someone brilliant)
  → It can be slightly IRONIC (a celebrity, someone famous 
    rather than necessarily right)
  → The text doesn't resolve this ambiguity — it lets 
    the reader decide
  
  THIS IS TONE. And most students never see it.

Why does tone matter for exam questions?

HOW TONE AFFECTS EXAM ANSWERS:

  Q24 asks about the article's PURPOSE.
  → If the tone is NEUTRAL → purpose is "informer" ✅
  → If the tone is CRITICAL → purpose might be "dénoncer"
  → If the tone is ENTHUSIASTIC → purpose might be "convaincre"
  
  Your ability to identify PURPOSE (Chapter 2) 
  depends ENTIRELY on correctly reading TONE.
  
  Q20 asks for the best TITLE.
  → If the tone is ADMIRING → title should be poetic/positive
  → If the tone is DISMISSIVE → title should be critical
  → If the tone is NEUTRAL → title should be factual
  
  Your ability to choose TITLES (Chapter 10) 
  depends on matching title tone to text tone.
  
  TONE IS NOT A SEPARATE SKILL.
  IT IS THE FOUNDATION BENEATH EVERY OTHER SKILL.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE TONE ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

THE THREE DIMENSIONS OF TONE

Tone is not a single quality. It has three distinct dimensions that you must analyze separately:

╔═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║              THE THREE DIMENSIONS OF TONE                         ║
╠═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║                                                                   ║
║  DIMENSION 1: REGISTER (Level of formality)                      ║
║  → How FORMAL or INFORMAL is the language?                        ║
║  → Academic? Journalistic? Conversational? Literary?              ║
║  → This tells you WHO the text is written for                     ║
║                                                                   ║
║  DIMENSION 2: ATTITUDE (Author's position)                       ║
║  → How does the author FEEL about the subject?                    ║
║  → Neutral? Admiring? Critical? Ironic? Alarmed?                 ║
║  → This tells you WHAT the author thinks                          ║
║                                                                   ║
║  DIMENSION 3: MOVEMENT (Tone changes within the text)            ║
║  → Does the tone SHIFT from beginning to end?                     ║
║  → Neutral → alarming? Admiring → critical?                      ║
║  → This tells you the text's EMOTIONAL TRAJECTORY                 ║
║                                                                   ║
╚═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

Let us analyze all three dimensions for EACH of our three texts.

DIMENSION 2: ATTITUDE

This is where the analysis gets subtle. Let us examine the author's attitude through lexical choices — the specific words chosen when alternatives existed:

ATTITUDE ANALYSIS — TEXTE 101:

  THE TITLE: "Paroles de techno prophète"
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ "Paroles" = words/speech                         │
  │ → Neutral choice                                 │
  │                                                  │
  │ "techno prophète" = technology prophet            │
  │ → "Prophète" is AMBIGUOUS:                       │
  │   → Positive: visionary, seer, wise person       │
  │   → Negative: false prophet, charlatan            │
  │   → Ironic: someone who CLAIMS to see the future │
  │                                                  │
  │ The title doesn't resolve the ambiguity.         │
  │ It lets the reader decide.                       │
  │ THIS IS CHARACTERISTIC OF JOURNALISTIC NEUTRALITY│
  │ — present both possibilities, don't choose.      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PARAGRAPH 1 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  "Considérés par CERTAINS comme un diseur de bonne aventure"
  → "par certains" = by SOME people (not by the journalist)
  → "diseur de bonne aventure" = fortune-teller
  → This is a MOCKING label, but attributed to OTHERS
  → The journalist REPORTS mockery without PERFORMING it
  → ATTITUDE: DISTANCED ✅

  "vedette de la prospective scientifique"
  → "vedette" = star/celebrity
  → Could be admiring (a STAR!) or slightly ironic 
    (more celebrity than scientist?)
  → ATTITUDE: AMBIGUOUS ⚠️

  "Ses prédictions ont le don de diviser"
  → "ont le don de" = have the gift of
  → Slightly IRONIC: a "gift" that causes division
  → But also NEUTRAL: objectively stating the effect
  → ATTITUDE: NEUTRAL WITH SUBTLE IRONY ⚠️

  "Il imagine sans ciller"
  → "sans ciller" = without blinking/flinching
  → Characterizes Kurzweil as BOLD and UNFLINCHING
  → Could be ADMIRING (brave!) or IRONIC (reckless?)
  → ATTITUDE: AMBIGUOUS ⚠️

  PARAGRAPH 2 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  "Selon ses estimations"
  → "Selon" = according to (DISTANCING marker)
  → The journalist attributes, doesn't endorse
  → ATTITUDE: DISTANCED/NEUTRAL ✅
  
  "nous serions capables" / "pourraient avoir fusionné" / 
  "pourrait commencer"
  → CONDITIONAL tense throughout
  → The journalist never states these as facts
  → Maintains DISTANCE from the predictions
  → ATTITUDE: NEUTRAL ✅

  PARAGRAPH 3 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  "Si le rythme continue de s'accélérer, COMME LE PRÉDIT Kurzweil"
  → "comme le prédit" = as Kurzweil predicts
  → Again: ATTRIBUTION, not endorsement
  → ATTITUDE: NEUTRAL ✅
  
  "il est probable que le débat... connaîtra une croissance exponentielle"
  → This is the journalist's OWN analysis (not attributed to Kurzweil)
  → "il est probable" = it is probable
  → The journalist thinks the DEBATE will grow
  → This is a MODERATE claim about the debate, not about the predictions
  → ATTITUDE: ANALYTICALLY NEUTRAL ✅

  OVERALL ATTITUDE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                      │
  │  TEXTE 101 ATTITUDE:                                 │
  │                                                      │
  │  PRIMARILY NEUTRAL with SUBTLE AMBIGUITY              │
  │                                                      │
  │  The journalist:                                     │
  │  → Reports Kurzweil's predictions without endorsing  │
  │  → Uses distancing markers ("selon", conditional)    │
  │  → Presents the FULL range of reactions              │
  │    (disagreement ↔ admiration ↔ skepticism)          │
  │  → Uses vocabulary that is SLIGHTLY AMBIGUOUS        │
  │    ("vedette", "prophète", "sans ciller")            │
  │  → Never explicitly praises OR criticizes            │
  │                                                      │
  │  This is SOPHISTICATED NEUTRALITY:                   │
  │  not bland neutrality (which would use only          │
  │  flat vocabulary) but RICH neutrality that            │
  │  acknowledges complexity without taking sides.       │
  │                                                      │
  │  The subtle ambiguity in word choices ("vedette",    │
  │  "prophète") might hint at GENTLE IRONY —            │
  │  the journalist seems SLIGHTLY amused by             │
  │  Kurzweil's boldness without condemning it.          │
  │                                                      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

DIMENSION 3: MOVEMENT

TONE MOVEMENT — TEXTE 101:

  PARAGRAPH 1: INTRODUCING (neutral + slightly colorful)
  → Colorful vocabulary ("diseur de bonne aventure", 
    "culte du héros", "sans ciller")
  → Sets up the character with flair
  
  PARAGRAPH 2: REPORTING (neutral + distanced)
  → Conditional tense, "selon ses estimations"
  → Driest, most factual paragraph
  → Pure reporting of predictions
  
  PARAGRAPH 3: ANALYZING (neutral + reflective)
  → "il est probable que" = journalist's own analysis
  → Broader perspective: connecting to larger debates
  → Most intellectually engaged paragraph
  
  MOVEMENT: COLORFUL → FACTUAL → ANALYTICAL

  │  └────────┘    └────────┘    └────────┘         │
  │                                                  │

TEXT 2: TEXTE 102 — "LE SAUMON D'ÉLEVAGE"

DIMENSION 1: REGISTER

REGISTER ANALYSIS — TEXTE 102:

  VOCABULARY LEVEL:
  → "chiffre d'affaire" → business terminology ✅
  → "ferme offshore" → industry terminology ✅
  → "poux de mer" → biological/technical ✅
  → "patrimoine génétique" → scientific ✅
  → "dioxydes, pesticides, métaux lourds" → chemistry ✅
  → "écosystème" → ecology ✅
  
  BUT ALSO:
  → "mine d'or" (title) → popular metaphor ⚠️
  → "faramineux" → emphatic/colloquial ⚠️
  → "mis en péril" → dramatic/literary ⚠️
  → "très inquiétante" → emotional ⚠️
  
  SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
  → Mix of data-heavy sentences (Para 1) and 
    opinion-heavy quotes (Para 4)
  → Direct quotes from two different sources
  → Journalistic convention: facts + voices
  
  REGISTER VERDICT:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                  │
  │  JOURNALISTIC REGISTER:                          │
  │  Informative with increasing emotional weight    │
  │                                                  │
  │  Para 1: DATA-HEAVY (numbers, statistics)        │
  │  Para 2-3: PROBLEM-FOCUSED (technical + alarming)│
  │  Para 4: OPINION-HEAVY (expert quote, emotional) │
  │                                                  │
  │  The register SHIFTS from factual to emotional   │
  │  as the text progresses.                         │
  │                                                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

DIMENSION 2: ATTITUDE

ATTITUDE ANALYSIS — TEXTE 102:

  THE TITLE: "Le saumon d'élevage : mine d'or de la Norvège"
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ "mine d'or" = gold mine                          │
  │ → POSITIVE metaphor (profitable, valuable)       │
  │ → But the article will reveal the COSTS           │
  │ → The title creates IRONIC CONTRAST:             │
  │   "gold mine" vs. "pollution nightmare"          │
  │ → The reader expects positivity from the title   │
  │   but gets increasing negativity from the text   │
  │ → THIS IS DELIBERATE JOURNALISTIC TECHNIQUE      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  PARAGRAPH 1 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  "augmente chaque année" → neutral, factual ✅
  "premier producteur mondial" → neutral, factual ✅
  "l'objectif AMBITIEUX" → "ambitieux" = editorial adjective
  → SLIGHTLY evaluative: the journalist judges the goal 
    as ambitious (implies it's very large, perhaps too large)
  → ATTITUDE: MOSTLY NEUTRAL with slight editorial voice ⚠️
  
  "rien que pour le saumon" → "rien que" = just/only
  → Emphasizes HOW MUCH money from salmon alone
  → Slight EMPHASIS suggesting this is remarkable/excessive
  → ATTITUDE: NEUTRAL but EMPHASIZING scale ⚠️

  PARAGRAPH 2 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  "projets FARAMINEUX" → "faramineux" = staggering/enormous
  → NOT neutral — this is an EVALUATIVE adjective
  → "Faramineux" implies excess, something almost too big
  → Compare with neutral alternatives: "grands" (big), 
    "importants" (significant), "majeurs" (major)
  → The journalist CHOSE the most emphatic word
  → ATTITUDE: LEANING TOWARD CONCERN ⚠️
  
  "MAIS l'industrialisation MASSIVE" → pivot + emphatic adjective
  → "Mais" signals the journalist's real focus: problems
  → "Massive" is evaluative: not just big, but EXCESSIVELY big
  → ATTITUDE: INCREASINGLY CONCERNED ⚠️
  
  "MIS EN PÉRIL" → "endangered" = strong, alarming vocabulary
  → Not "pose des difficultés" (creates difficulties — mild)
  → Not "affecte" (affects — neutral)
  → "Mis en péril" = put in DANGER — strong alarm
  → ATTITUDE: ALARMED ⚠️

  PARAGRAPH 3 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  "mettent en péril l'écosystème ET le patrimoine génétique"
  → REPEATS "mettre en péril" from Para 2
  → Repetition = EMPHASIS = the journalist wants you to 
    feel the danger
  → "ET" adds a SECOND victim (genetics on top of ecosystem)
  → ATTITUDE: ESCALATING ALARM ⚠️
  
  "et MÊME des métaux lourds" → "même" = EVEN
  → "Même" is an ESCALATION marker
  → The journalist presents metals as the WORST item
  → The sequence "dioxydes → pesticides → MÊME métaux lourds"
    is DELIBERATELY escalating
  → ATTITUDE: BUILDING ALARM ⚠️
  
  "ne concerne PLUS UNIQUEMENT" → scope expansion
  → "Plus uniquement" = no longer ONLY
  → The danger is SPREADING — not contained to one area
  → ATTITUDE: ALARMED ABOUT EXPANSION ⚠️

  PARAGRAPH 4 — ATTITUDE CLUES:
  
  This paragraph is ENTIRELY Magnen's quote.
  The journalist CHOSE to end with the environmentalist.
  → This is an EDITORIAL CHOICE
  → The last word goes to the most ALARMING voice
  → ATTITUDE: STRUCTURALLY ALIGNED WITH CONCERN ⚠️
  
  "AFFIRME" (not "dit" or "indique")
  → Stronger verb = journalist signals Magnen is making 
    a FORCEFUL claim
  → Compare with Martins: "INDIQUE" (neutral)
  → The journalist gives Magnen's voice MORE FORCE
  → ATTITUDE: SLIGHTLY FAVORING the environmental perspective ⚠️

  OVERALL ATTITUDE:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                      │
  │  TEXTE 102 ATTITUDE:                                 │
  │                                                      │
  │  STRUCTURALLY CONCERNED / INCREASINGLY ALARMED        │
  │                                                      │
  │  The journalist:                                     │
  │  → Starts with neutral economic data                 │
  │  → Uses evaluative adjectives that lean toward       │
  │    concern ("faramineux", "massive")                 │
  │  → Repeats alarm vocabulary ("mis en péril" × 2)     │
  │  → Uses escalation markers ("même", "plus uniquement")│
  │  → Gives MORE space to problems than to profits      │
  │    (3 paragraphs vs. 1)                              │
  │  → Gives the LAST WORD to the environmentalist       │
  │  → Uses stronger attribution verb for Magnen         │
  │    ("affirme") than for Martins ("indique")          │
  │                                                      │
  │  This is NOT overt activism.                         │
  │  This is STRUCTURAL BIAS — the journalist maintains  │
  │  surface neutrality while making editorial choices    │
  │  that LEAN toward environmental concern.             │
  │                                                      │
  │  (Chapter 5 identified this — now we see HOW         │
  │   it works at the vocabulary level)                  │
  │                                                      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

DIMENSION 3: MOVEMENT

TONE MOVEMENT — TEXTE 102:

TEXT 3: L'ALPINISME

DIMENSION 1: REGISTER

REGISTER ANALYSIS — L'ALPINISME:

  VOCABULARY LEVEL:
  → "cime" → literary/elevated ✅
  → "praticable" → formal ✅
  → "extravagante" → literary ✅
  → "dépaysement" → sophisticated ✅
  → "bivouac improvisé" → technical + narrative ✅
  → "versant italien" → geographic/literary ✅
  → "ensevelir" → literary/archaic ✅
  
  SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
  → Long, complex sentences with multiple subordinate clauses
  → "Il faut, pour que le pas décisif soit franchi, 
     l'entrée en scène de Jacques Balmat"
  → Literary construction: subject delayed for dramatic effect
  → Past historic tense ("parut", "eut lieu", "fut arrêtée")

  REGISTER VERDICT:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                  │
  │  LITERARY/EDUCATIONAL REGISTER:                  │
  │  Elevated vocabulary + narrative structure        │
  │                                                  │
  │  This is NOT journalism. It is LITERARY           │
  │  HISTORICAL NARRATIVE — the kind you find         │
  │  in educational books or cultural publications.  │
  │                                                  │
  │  The past historic tense (passé simple)          │
  │  immediately signals: this is WRITTEN language,  │
  │  not spoken language. Formal and literary.       │
  │                                                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

DIMENSION 2: ATTITUDE

ATTITUDE ANALYSIS — L'ALPINISME:

  TOWARD SAUSSURE:
  → "savant Genevois" = Geneva scientist → RESPECTFUL ✅
  → "désireux de faire des observations scientifiques"
    = eager to make scientific observations → ADMIRING ✅
  → "Si la science lui avait fait faire la conquête du 
     Mont Blanc, on peut dire qu'en retour les Alpes 
     l'avaient conquis"
    = Science drove him to conquer; the Alps conquered him
    → POETIC, ADMIRING, PHILOSOPHICAL ✅
  → "comme beaucoup d'entre nous"
    = like many of us → INCLUSIVE, PERSONAL ✅
    → The author includes HIMSELF in those conquered by Alps
  → ATTITUDE TOWARD SAUSSURE: DEEP ADMIRATION ✅

  TOWARD BALMAT:
  → "Il faut, pour que le pas décisif soit franchi, 
     l'entrée en scène de Jacques Balmat"
    = For the decisive step to be taken, Balmat must enter
    → DRAMATIC INTRODUCTION — like a HERO entering a story ✅
  → "C'était un grand coureur de glaciers"
    = He was a great glacier runner → ADMIRING ✅
  → Extended narrative of his solo exploration
    → Most TEXT SPACE = most importance → ELEVATED STATUS ✅
  → "sûr d'avoir trouvé l'accès à la cime"
    = certain he had found the route → CONFIDENT, HEROIC ✅
  → ATTITUDE TOWARD BALMAT: HEROIC ADMIRATION ✅

  TOWARD THE CONQUEST:
  → "Le Mont Blanc était vaincu"
    = Mont Blanc was conquered → TRIUMPHANT ✅
  → "la plus grande «première» des Alpes était accomplie"
    = the greatest first ascent was accomplished → SUPERLATIVE PRAISE ✅
  → ATTITUDE TOWARD CONQUEST: TRIUMPH AND CELEBRATION ✅

  TOWARD ANGEVILLE:
  → "exténuée" = exhausted → SYMPATHETIC ✅
  → Her quote about dying on the summit → DRAMATIC, MOVING ✅
  → ATTITUDE TOWARD ANGEVILLE: ADMIRING HER DETERMINATION ✅

  TOWARD THE FOLLOWERS:
  → "MAIS aucun des amateurs qui suivent les traces 
     de Saussure, ne laisse une trace DURABLE"
    = BUT none of the amateurs leaves a LASTING mark
    → "MAIS" = pivot to criticism ❌
    → "amateurs" = NOT professionals, NOT pioneers ❌
    → "aucun" = NONE — absolute negative judgment ❌
    → "trace durable" = lasting mark — they failed to leave one ❌
  
  → "Ils sont venus là par CURIOSITÉ, peut-être pour la GLOIRE"
    = They came for curiosity, maybe for glory
    → "Curiosité" ≠ science (Saussure's motivation)
    → "Gloire" = glory = self-serving, not knowledge-seeking
    → The author CONTRASTS followers' shallow motivations 
      with pioneers' deep ones ❌
  
  → "MALGRÉ le nombre de ces ascensions, l'exploration 
     du Mont Blanc n'AVANCE PAS"
    = DESPITE the number, exploration DOESN'T ADVANCE

📐 THE TONE DETECTION TOOLKIT

Now let us formalize the techniques for detecting tone in ANY text:

TOOL 1: THE ADJECTIVE AUDIT

THE ADJECTIVE AUDIT:

  Adjectives are the PRIMARY carriers of tone.
  The author's CHOICE of adjective reveals attitude.
  
  TECHNIQUE: List every adjective and classify it:
  
  NEUTRAL ADJECTIVES (no attitude):
  → "premier" (first), "mondial" (global), "norvégien" (Norwegian)
  → These carry no judgment
  
  EVALUATIVE ADJECTIVES (carry attitude):
  → "ambitieux" (ambitious) — implies scale is noteworthy
  → "faramineux" (staggering) — implies excess
  → "massive" (massive) — implies potentially problematic size
  → "tragique" (tragic) — implies emotional judgment
  → "durable" (lasting) — in "aucun ne laisse une trace durable"
    → implies JUDGMENT that followers failed
  
  ESCALATING ADJECTIVES (build intensity):
  → "toxiques" → "lourds" (heavy metals = worst category)
  → "inquiétante" → "TRÈS inquiétante" (intensified with "très")
  
  RULE: Count evaluative adjectives.
  More evaluative adjectives = more opinionated tone.
  
  Texte 101: FEW evaluative adjectives → NEUTRAL ✅
  Texte 102: MANY evaluative adjectives → CONCERNED ⚠️
  L'Alpinisme: MANY evaluative adjectives → PASSIONATE ⚠️

TOOL 2: THE ATTRIBUTION ANALYSIS

THE ATTRIBUTION ANALYSIS:

  HOW does the author handle other people's words?
  
  FULL ATTRIBUTION (neutral):
  → "Selon ses estimations" (according to his estimates)
  → "indique Catarina Martins" (indicates C. Martins)
  → The author DISTANCES from the claim
  
  PARTIAL ATTRIBUTION (leaning):
  → "affirme Jean-Philippe Magnen" (affirms J-P. Magnen)
  → "Affirme" is STRONGER than "indique"
  → The author gives more FORCE to this speaker
  
  NO ATTRIBUTION (owning):
  → "L'industrialisation massive pose des problèmes"
  → The JOURNALIST states this as own assessment
  → No "selon" or quotation marks
  → The author OWNS this claim
  
  RULE: The more the author OWNS claims (without attribution),
  the less neutral the tone.
  
  Texte 101: HEAVY attribution ("selon", conditional) → NEUTRAL
  Texte 102: MIX (journalist owns problems, attributes quotes) → LEANING
  L'Alpinisme: NO attribution (author's own narrative voice) → PERSONAL

TOOL 3: THE STRUCTURAL WEIGHT TEST

THE STRUCTURAL WEIGHT TEST:

  How much SPACE does each perspective get?
  
  TEXTE 101:
  → Kurzweil's predictions: Paragraphs 1-2 (2/3 of text)
  → Reactions to predictions: Paragraph 1 (partial)
  → Broader debate: Paragraph 3 (1/3 of text)
  → BALANCE: Roughly equal space for description and context
  → STRUCTURAL TONE: BALANCED ✅
  
  TEXTE 102:
  → Economic positives: Paragraph 1 (1/4 of text)
  → Problems and concerns: Paragraphs 2-4 (3/4 of text)
  → Industry defense (Martins): ~2 sentences
  → Environmental criticism (Magnen): entire last paragraph
  → IMBALANCE: 3× more space for concerns than for positives
  → STRUCTURAL TONE: LEANING TOWARD CONCERN ⚠️
  
  L'ALPINISME:
  → Pioneers (Saussure, Balmat, conquest): ~60% of text
  → Followers and stagnation: ~40% of text
  → Positive/heroic content: majority
  → Critical content: significant minority, placed at END
  → STRUCTURAL TONE: ADMIRING WITH CRITICAL CODA ⚠️
  
  RULE: Whoever/whatever gets MORE space and the LAST WORD
  is what the author considers most important.

TOOL 4: THE VERB FORCE SCALE

THE VERB FORCE SCALE:

  Verbs carry different levels of FORCE.
  Stronger verbs = stronger tone.
  
  WEAK (neutral):
  → "dit" (says), "indique" (indicates), "note" (notes)
  → "est" (is), "a" (has), "fait" (does)
  
  MEDIUM (slight attitude):
  → "souligne" (underlines), "précise" (specifies)
  → "doit" (must), "pousse" (pushes)
  
  STRONG (clear attitude):
  → "affirme" (affirms), "dénonce" (denounces)
  → "met en péril" (endangers), "menace" (threatens)
  → "vaincu" (conquered), "accomplie" (accomplished)
  
  VERY STRONG (extreme attitude):
  → "exige" (demands), "condamne" (condemns)
  → "détruit" (destroys), "anéantit" (annihilates)
  
  TEXTE 101 VERBS: imagine, prédit, diviser, s'accélérer
  → Mostly MEDIUM → NEUTRAL TONE ✅
  
  TEXTE 102 VERBS: doit s'adapter, pousse, pose des problèmes,
  met en péril, affirme, faut changer
  → Mix of MEDIUM and STRONG → CONCERNED TONE ⚠️
  
  L'ALPINISME VERBS: promet, parut, franchi, conquérir,
  vaincu, accomplie, conquis
  → STRONG verbs (conquest/victory) → PASSIONATE TONE ⚠️

TOOL 5: THE FINAL SENTENCE TEST

THE FINAL SENTENCE TEST:

  The LAST sentence of a text often reveals the author's 
  deepest attitude — the feeling they want to leave 
  with the reader.
  
  TEXTE 101 — LAST SENTENCE:
  "Tant cela fait le lien entre des thèmes extrêmement 
   controversés... et des concepts de science-fiction..."
  → ANALYTICAL: the journalist ends by analyzing connections
  → NEUTRAL: no judgment on whether this is good or bad
  → TONE REVEALED: intellectual curiosity ✅
  
  TEXTE 102 — LAST SENTENCE:
  "il y a une pollution qui est très inquiétante, qui n'est 
   pas seulement sur le saumon d'élevage puisque les eaux 
   sont polluées avec des métaux lourds"
  → ALARMING: "très inquiétante", "polluées", "métaux lourds"
  → URGENT: the pollution is widespread and serious
  → TONE REVEALED: environmental alarm ⚠️
  
  L'ALPINISME — LAST SENTENCE:
  "Dans le reste des Alpes, c'est le silence."
  → DEVASTATING: "silence" = nothing, emptiness, failure
  → REGRETFUL: the promise of exploration went unfulfilled
  → POETIC: short, powerful, resonant
  → TONE REVEALED: profound disappointment ⚠️
  
  RULE: If you can only check ONE thing for tone,
  check the LAST SENTENCE. It's the author's parting gift —
  and it reveals more than any other sentence.

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: READ FOR TONE ON YOUR SECOND PASS

THE TWO-PASS READING STRATEGY:

  FIRST PASS (Chapter 1): Read for STRUCTURE and CONTENT
  → What is this about? How is it organized?
  → Build your structural/chronological map
  
  SECOND PASS (Chapter 14): Read for TONE and ATTITUDE
  → How does the author FEEL about this?
  → What ADJECTIVES carry judgment?
  → Where does the tone SHIFT?
  → What does the LAST SENTENCE reveal?
  
  DO NOT try to read for content AND tone simultaneously.
  Content requires analytical thinking.
  Tone requires EMOTIONAL sensitivity.
  These are different cognitive modes.

RULE 2: MATCH TONE TO QUESTION TYPE

THE TONE-QUESTION CONNECTION:

  PURPOSE QUESTIONS (Q24):
  → Neutral tone → "informer" ✅
  → Critical tone → "dénoncer"
  → Persuasive tone → "convaincre"
  → Showcasing tone → "présenter"
  
  TITLE QUESTIONS (Q20):
  → Admiring tone → poetic/positive title ✅
  → Critical tone → negative/questioning title
  → Neutral tone → factual/descriptive title
  
  TRUE/FALSE QUESTIONS (Q21):
  → Tone tells you what the author EMPHASIZES
  → Emphasized elements are more likely to be 
    tested in true/false questions
  
  INFERENCE QUESTIONS (Q26, Q27):
  → Tone reveals what the author IMPLIES
  → An alarming tone implies the author thinks 
    the situation is dangerous
  → A neutral tone implies the author doesn't 
    take a position

RULE 3: NEVER CONFUSE THE AUTHOR'S TONE WITH A SOURCE'S TONE

THE VOICE SEPARATION RULE:

  TEXTE 102 has THREE voices:
  
  JOURNALIST'S VOICE: "L'industrialisation massive pose des problèmes"
  → His tone: increasingly concerned
  
  MARTINS'S VOICE: «Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts...»
  → Her tone: defensive, minimizing
  
  MAGNEN'S VOICE: «Il faut changer le modèle...»
  → His tone: urgent, alarmed
  
  When analyzing TONE, you must identify WHOSE tone you're reading:
  
  → The JOURNALIST'S tone determines the article's purpose
  → MARTINS'S tone reveals the industry perspective
  → MAGNEN'S tone reveals the environmentalist perspective
  
  THE ARTICLE'S OVERALL TONE = THE JOURNALIST'S TONE
  (not the sources' tones)
  
  But the journalist's CHOICE of which source gets 
  the last word REVEALS his structural preference.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: ASSUMING ALL JOURNALISM IS NEUTRAL

WRONG:
  "This is a newspaper article. Therefore it's neutral.
   Therefore the purpose is 'informer.'"
   
RIGHT:
  "This is a newspaper article. Let me CHECK if it's neutral:
   → Adjective audit: evaluative adjectives present? HOW MANY?
   → Attribution analysis: does the journalist own claims?
   → Structural weight: balanced or imbalanced?
   → Final sentence: neutral or emotional?"
   
  Texte 101 → passes all checks → genuinely NEUTRAL ✅
  Texte 102 → fails structural weight + final sentence → 
  LEANING toward concern (but still "informer" as primary purpose,
  because the journalist REPORTS rather than ARGUES)
  
  JOURNALISM CAN LEAN without being propaganda.
  The key is detecting HOW MUCH it leans.

MISTAKE 2: CONFUSING VOCABULARY REGISTER WITH ATTITUDE

WRONG:
  "The text uses sophisticated vocabulary like 'ingénierie 
   moléculaire' and 'patrimoine génétique.' Therefore the 
   author is impressed/positive about the subject."
   
RIGHT:
  Sophisticated vocabulary = HIGH REGISTER
  High register ≠ positive attitude
  
  A text can use sophisticated vocabulary while being:
  → Critical ("the catastrophic failure of molecular engineering")
  → Neutral ("molecular engineering is a developing field")
  → Enthusiastic ("the revolutionary promise of molecular engineering")
  
  REGISTER tells you WHO the text is for (educated audience).
  ATTITUDE tells you WHAT the author thinks (positive/negative/neutral).
  
  THESE ARE DIFFERENT DIMENSIONS.

MISTAKE 3: READING YOUR OWN EMOTIONS INTO THE TEXT

WRONG:
  "I find Kurzweil's predictions scary/exciting.
   Therefore the article's tone is scary/exciting."
   
RIGHT:
  YOUR emotional response ≠ the AUTHOR'S tone.
  
  You might find eternal life exciting.
  But the journalist reports it NEUTRALLY (conditional tense).
  
  You might find salmon pollution alarming.
  The journalist IS somewhat alarmed (evaluative adjectives).
  
  You might find Alpine history boring.
  The author is PASSIONATE about it (heroic vocabulary).
  
  RULE: Detect the AUTHOR'S emotions from TEXTUAL EVIDENCE,
  not from YOUR emotional reaction to the content.

MISTAKE 4: IGNORING TONE SHIFTS

WRONG:
  "Texte 102 starts with positive economic data.
   Therefore the article's tone is positive."
   
RIGHT:
  The article's tone STARTS positive and BECOMES alarmed.
  The FINAL tone (alarmed) is more representative 
  than the INITIAL tone (positive).
  
  WHY? Because the positive beginning is SETUP.
  The alarm is the MAIN MESSAGE.
  
  RULE: When tone shifts, the ENDING tone usually 
  represents the author's true attitude.
  
  (This is the same as Chapter 4's Last Paragraph Rule —
   the ending carries the author's most important point.)

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "ONE WORD" OVER-READING

DANGER: Building your entire tone analysis on ONE word.
        
  "The text uses 'vedette' — this proves the journalist 
   is being ironic about Kurzweil!"
   
  ONE ambiguous word ≠ definitive tone identification.
  
  You need MULTIPLE consistent signals:
  → "vedette" (ambiguous) + "selon" (distancing) + 
    conditional tense (hedging) + presents all reactions 
    (balanced) = NEUTRAL WITH SUBTLE AMBIGUITY
  
  Not: "vedette" alone = IRONIC
  
  RULE: Tone identification requires PATTERN recognition
  across multiple signals, not single-word analysis.

PITFALL 2: THE QUOTATION TONE CONFUSION

DANGER: Attributing a quoted source's tone to the journalist.
        
  Magnen: «pollution qui est TRÈS INQUIÉTANTE»
  → Magnen's tone: ALARMED
  
  Student: "The article's tone is alarmed because 
  it says 'très inquiétante.'"
  
  BUT: The journalist QUOTES Magnen.
  The journalist doesn't SAY "très inquiétante" himself.
  
  HOWEVER: The journalist CHOSE to include and END WITH 
  this alarmed quote — which IS a structural editorial choice.
  
  NUANCED READING:
  → The journalist's OWN words: concerned (evaluative adjectives)
  → The journalist's STRUCTURAL choice: alarming (ends with Magnen)
  → Overall article tone: concerned → alarming trajectory
  → But the MOST alarming words belong to Magnen, not the journalist
  
  RULE: Always distinguish between the journalist's OWN tone 
  and the tone of quoted sources.
