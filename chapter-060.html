Chapter 8: ANATOMIE D'UN PLAT — UNMASKING CONSUMER DECEPTION

🐟 Texte 69 | Phase 2: Easy | Core Patterns

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Avez-vous remarqué que votre tranche de saumon en super promo est souvent plus colorée qu'une tranche de saumon sauvage qui, elle, tend plutôt vers le rose pâle du fait des crustacés chargés naturellement de caroténoïdes que notre poisson mangeait auparavant ?

Car les saumons d'élevage ont changé de régime et sont aujourd'hui gavés de protéines végétales de soja. Résultat : les saumons domestiques sont tout gris. Pour attirer les consommateurs il fallait donc ajouter un cocktail d'additifs chimiques comme l'astaxanthine, également utilisée dans les autobronzants ou l'alimentation des poules pour redonner le jaune d'œuf. Pire, le saumon norvégien, qui constitue 80% de notre consommation, est également plein de pesticides. Agglomérés dans leurs fermes piscicoles, ils sont envahis par les poux de mer, d'où la nécessité de les épouiller avec ces produits phytosanitaires intégrés dans leur nourriture, mais susceptibles d'augmenter les risques de cancer s'ils sont ingérés par l'homme.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's different from all previous projects?

Projects 1-7 dealt with texts where the logic was either directly stated (Projects 1-5) or structurally implied (Projects 6-7, gap-fill). In those texts, you could always find a sentence that directly or almost directly matched the answer.

This text introduces a fundamentally different challenge: IMPLICIT CAUSE-EFFECT CHAINS. The text describes a sequence of facts, and you must CONNECT them yourself. The text never says "the industrials color the fish" — it describes a chain of events that IMPLIES this conclusion.

This is the difference between:

One sentence = one answerMultiple sentences = one inference

Step 3: The "Cause-Effect Chain Mapping" technique (EVOLVED from Project 2)

In Project 2, we introduced basic cause-effect chains. This text requires a MORE COMPLEX version — a multi-step chain where each step leads to the next:

THE SALMON DECEPTION CHAIN:

STEP 1: NATURAL STATE
Wild salmon eat crustacés (shellfish)
→ Crustacés contain caroténoïdes (natural pigment)
→ Wild salmon = naturally PINK

STEP 2: FARMING CHANGE
Farmed salmon eat protéines végétales de soja (soy protein)
→ Soy has NO caroténoïdes
→ Farmed salmon = GREY

STEP 3: COSMETIC FIX
Grey salmon won't sell
→ Industrials add additifs chimiques (astaxanthine)
→ Farmed salmon = ARTIFICIALLY COLORED (more colored than wild!)

STEP 4: PARASITE PROBLEM
Farmed salmon in crowded farms
→ Invaded by poux de mer (sea lice)
→ Need pesticides to remove lice
→ Pesticides in salmon's food

STEP 5: HEALTH RISK
Pesticides in salmon
→ Humans eat salmon
→ Susceptibles d'augmenter les risques de cancer

This is the COMPLETE chain. No single sentence contains all of it. The text presents each step, and YOU must assemble the chain.

Why this matters for the exam:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #27: In investigative/scientific texts, the correct answer often SUMMARIZES a multi-step chain that no single sentence expresses. You must ASSEMBLE the chain from scattered evidence across the text, then match the assembled conclusion to the answer choices.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT — Question by Question

QUESTION 17: Cet article traite...

What the question really asks: What is the OVERALL TOPIC of this article?

This is a "global comprehension" question — it tests whether you understood the BIG PICTURE, not a specific detail.

The big picture:

The text describes:

  1. What farmed salmon are fed (soy)
  2. What chemicals are added to color them (astaxanthine)
  3. What pesticides are used to treat them (phytosanitaires)
  4. What health risks this poses (cancer)

ALL of these are PRACTICES in salmon farming. The text is an exposé of what happens INSIDE the salmon farming industry.

Now let's test each option:

Option A: "Des pratiques en vigueur dans l'élevage de saumons"
(The practices in use in salmon farming)

"Pratiques en vigueur" = practices currently in use.
"Élevage de saumons" = salmon farming.

The text describes: feeding practices (soy), coloring practices (astaxanthine), treatment practices (pesticides). ALL are current practices in salmon farming.

Text ContentMatches "pratiques en vigueur dans l'élevage"?
Soy-based feeding✅ Farming practice
Chemical coloring✅ Farming practice
Pesticide treatment✅ Farming practice

Everything fits under this umbrella.

✅ STRONG CANDIDATE

Option B: "Des exigences alimentaires des consommateurs de saumons"
(The food requirements of salmon consumers)

"Exigences alimentaires" = dietary/food demands.
"Des consommateurs" = of consumers (humans who eat salmon).

The text mentions consumers once: "Pour attirer les consommateurs il fallait donc ajouter..."

But this sentence describes what PRODUCERS do TO attract consumers — not what consumers DEMAND. The text never discusses consumer preferences, requirements, or behavior. Consumers are mentioned as the TARGET of the deception, not as actors making demands.

This is the Role Confusion trap from Project 4 (Pitfall #13) — swapping who ACTS and who is ACTED UPON.

Role in TextActorAction
Subject (who acts)Industrials/farmersColor the fish, add pesticides
Object (acted upon)ConsumersAre targeted/deceived

Option B claims the text is about consumers' demands. The text is actually about producers' practices.

❌ ELIMINATED — Role confusion (text is about producers' actions, not consumers' demands)

Option C: "De l'absence de contrôle sanitaire dans l'élevage des saumons"
(The absence of health inspections in salmon farming)

"Absence de contrôle sanitaire" = lack of health regulation/inspection.

The text describes harmful practices (pesticides, chemicals) — but does it say there's NO CONTROL? Does it say inspections are absent?

No. The text never mentions regulations, inspections, or their absence. It describes WHAT happens, not WHETHER it's regulated.

This is a LOGICAL LEAP trap — the reader thinks: "These practices are harmful → they must be unregulated → the text is about lack of regulation." But this is YOUR deduction, not the text's content.

This is Golden Rule #7 from Project 2 in action — don't import outside reasoning. The text describes practices. It does NOT discuss regulation.

❌ ELIMINATED — Logical leap (harmful practices ≠ absent controls — the text doesn't discuss regulation)

This distinction is subtle but critical:

What the Text DOESWhat the Text DOES NOT
Describes farming practicesDiscuss whether practices are legal
Lists chemicals and pesticides usedMention any regulatory body
Warns about health risksSay there are no controls

Option D: "Des nouvelles contraintes alimentaires des saumons sauvages"
(New dietary constraints of wild salmon)

"Saumons sauvages" = WILD salmon.

The text discusses wild salmon only in the FIRST sentence — as a COMPARISON point. The entire article is about FARMED salmon ("saumons d'élevage," "saumons domestiques"). Wild salmon are mentioned to show the contrast (natural pink vs. artificial color).

This is a Subject Swap trap — the text is about farmed salmon, and the answer claims it's about wild salmon.

Text's actual subjectOption D's claimed subject
Saumons d'élevage (farmed)Saumons sauvages (wild)

❌ ELIMINATED — Subject swap (farmed ≠ wild)

🎯 Answer: A — Des pratiques en vigueur dans l'élevage de saumons

THE LESSON FROM Q17: Global comprehension requires UMBRELLA thinking

For "cet article traite de..." questions, the correct answer must be an UMBRELLA that covers ALL the text's content, not just one part.

UMBRELLA TEST:

Option A: "Pratiques dans l'élevage"
├── Feeding practices (soy) ✅ COVERED
├── Coloring practices (astaxanthine) ✅ COVERED  
├── Treatment practices (pesticides) ✅ COVERED
└── Health implications ✅ COVERED (consequence of practices)

Option C: "Absence de contrôle sanitaire"  
├── Feeding practices → NOT about control ❌
├── Coloring practices → NOT about control ❌
└── Only the health risk part partially relates ⚠️

Option A covers EVERYTHING. Option C covers only a fraction. The correct "topic" answer always has the WIDEST accurate coverage.

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #28: For "What is this article about?" questions, apply the UMBRELLA TEST — the correct answer must cover ALL major content areas, not just one aspect. An answer that only covers part of the text, even if accurate for that part, is too narrow.

(Sell a tasteless but toxin-free fish)

Two problems:

Problem 1: "sans toxines" (toxin-free)
The text says the salmon contains pesticides that are "susceptibles d'augmenter les risques de cancer." A fish that can increase cancer risk is NOT toxin-free. Direct contradiction.

Problem 2: "sans saveur" (tasteless)
The text never discusses TASTE. It discusses COLOR (grey vs. pink) and HEALTH (pesticides, cancer). Taste is never mentioned.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Sans toxines" contradicts pesticide/cancer information + "sans saveur" is fabricated

THE MASTER LESSON FROM Q18: Decoding technical vocabulary through context

The text uses several technical terms. You don't need to know them — the text EXPLAINS them through context:

Technical TermHow the Text Explains It
CaroténoïdesDescribed as being in crustacés (shellfish) and giving salmon its color → pigment
AstaxanthineCompared to "autobronzants" (self-tanners) → coloring agent
Produits phytosanitairesUsed to "épouiller" (de-louse) the fish → pesticide/treatment
Poux de mer"Envahis par les poux de mer" → parasites that invade fish

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #29: When a text uses technical vocabulary, look for CONTEXTUAL CLUES that explain it — comparisons ("également utilisée dans..."), purpose statements ("afin de..."), or parallel descriptions. You don't need prior knowledge; the text provides the decoder.

Here's a systematic approach to decoding unknown words:

TECHNICAL WORD DECODER:

1. Look for COMPARISONS
   "comme..." / "également utilisée dans..." / "similaire à..."
   → The comparison object reveals the function
   
2. Look for PURPOSE
   "afin de..." / "pour..." / "d'où la nécessité de..."
   → The purpose reveals what the word DOES
   
3. Look for RESULTS
   "Résultat:" / "ce qui provoque..." / "d'où..."
   → The result reveals the word's EFFECT
   
4. Look for ETYMOLOGY
   "phyto-" = plant / "pisci-" = fish / "auto-" = self
   → Greek/Latin roots can help

QUESTION 19: Les traitements administrés aux poissons servent à...

What the question really asks: What is the PURPOSE of the pesticide treatments?

The key passage:

"Agglomérés dans leurs fermes piscicoles, ils sont envahis par les poux de mer, d'où la nécessité de les épouiller avec ces produits phytosanitaires intégrés dans leur nourriture"

Let me decode this sentence step by step:

CHAIN FOR Q19:

FACT 1: "Agglomérés dans leurs fermes piscicoles"
(Crowded together in their fish farms)
         ↓ CAUSE
FACT 2: "ils sont envahis par les poux de mer"
(they are invaded by sea lice)
         ↓ CONSEQUENCE → NEED
FACT 3: "d'où la nécessité de les épouiller"
(hence the need to de-louse them)
         ↓ METHOD
FACT 4: "avec ces produits phytosanitaires intégrés dans leur nourriture"
(with these phytosanitary products mixed into their food)

The PURPOSE of the treatment: to remove sea lice (parasites) from the fish.

Key vocabulary decoding:

  • "Poux de mer" = sea LICE → parasites
  • "Épouiller" = to de-LOUSE → remove lice/parasites
  • "Produits phytosanitaires" = pesticides/treatments

Now let's test:

Option A: "Les faire grossir plus rapidement"
(Make them grow faster)

The text never mentions growth, size, or speed of development. The treatments are for LICE, not for growth.

This is a common real-world association trap (Pitfall #6 from Project 2) — in the real world, farming often involves growth hormones, so a reader might assume treatments = growth enhancement. But this text specifically says treatments = de-lousing.

❌ ELIMINATED — Common association trap (farming treatments ≠ growth in this text)

Option B: "Améliorer la qualité de leur chair"
(Improve the quality of their flesh)

The text presents the treatments as a PROBLEM, not an improvement. The pesticides are "susceptibles d'augmenter les risques de cancer" — they HARM quality for human consumption. The treatments are a necessary evil to deal with parasites, not a quality enhancement.

This trap inverts the value judgment — the text presents treatments as harmful, and the answer presents them as beneficial.

❌ ELIMINATED — Value inversion (treatments are harmful, not beneficial)

Option C: "Les débarrasser de leurs parasites"
(Rid them of their parasites)

Text saysOption C saysRelationship
"envahis par les poux de mer" (invaded by sea lice)"leurs parasites" (their parasites)PARAPHRASE ✅
"les épouiller" (de-louse them)"les débarrasser" (rid them)PARAPHRASE ✅

Double paraphrase:

  • "Poux de mer" (sea lice) → "parasites" (general term for the same thing)
  • "Épouiller" (de-louse) → "débarrasser" (rid/remove)

Both are perfect hypernym paraphrases — specific terms replaced by general terms.

"poux de mer"  →  "parasites"      (specific → general)

✅ CORRECT — Double hypernym paraphrase

❌ ELIMINATED — Fabrication (reproduction never mentioned)

THE LESSON FROM Q19: Hypernym paraphrasing

This question introduces a specific type of paraphrase that will be increasingly common:

HYPERNYM PARAPHRASE: The text uses a SPECIFIC term, and the correct answer uses a GENERAL term that includes it.

Specific (text)General (answer)Relationship
Poux de mer (sea lice)ParasitesSea lice ARE parasites
Épouiller (de-louse)Débarrasser (rid/remove)De-lousing IS a form of removal
Roman (novel)Livre (book)A novel IS a book (Project 5, Q28)
Chute ou choc (fall or impact)AccidentsFalls and impacts ARE accidents (Project 2, Q14)

Notice: this is the REVERSE of how we usually think. We expect the text to be general and the answer to be specific. But in Compréhension Écrite, it's often the opposite — the text is specific (using precise vocabulary) and the answer is general (using umbrella terms).

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #30: The correct answer frequently uses HYPERNYM PARAPHRASING — replacing the text's specific terms with broader general terms. "Sea lice" becomes "parasites." "Novel" becomes "book." "Fall or impact" becomes "accident." Train yourself to recognize when a general term ENCOMPASSES a specific one.

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For Investigative/Scientific Consumer Texts Specifically:

1. Map the COMPLETE cause-effect chain before answering.
Don't stop at one step. Follow the chain from the FIRST cause to the FINAL consequence.

2. Decode technical terms from CONTEXT, not from memory.
Use comparisons, purpose statements, and results as decoders (Rule #29). You don't need a science degree.

3. When the answer seems "too simple," check if it's a synthesis.
The correct answer to Q18 ("they color the fish") seems almost too obvious. But it's not IN the text as a single statement — it's the CONCLUSION of a five-step chain. Simple-sounding answers can be the product of complex reasoning.

4. Watch for HYPERNYM paraphrasing.
When the text uses specific terms (poux de mer, astaxanthine) and the answer uses general terms (parasites, additifs), that's likely correct — not vague.

5. Distinguish the text's TOPIC from its IMPLICATION.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 23: The Implication-as-Topic Trap (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 14, 17, 21, 23.

What it is: An answer that describes a LOGICAL IMPLICATION of the text's content, rather than the actual TOPIC of the text.

How to detect: Ask: "Does the text DISCUSS this, or do I CONCLUDE this?"

In this text:

  • The text DISCUSSES farming practices (feeding, coloring, treating)
  • I CONCLUDE that controls are absent — but the text never discusses controls

Defense: Apply the Umbrella Test (Rule #28). The correct topic must cover ALL major content areas. "Absence of controls" doesn't cover feeding practices, coloring methods, or specific chemicals.

PITFALL 24: The Value Inversion Trap (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 17, 21, 23.

What it is: An answer that presents a HARMFUL element as BENEFICIAL, or a NEGATIVE practice as POSITIVE.

How to detect: Check the TEXT'S value judgment. If the text presents something as a problem, the answer shouldn't present it as an improvement.

In this text: Pesticide treatments are presented as harmful (cancer risk). Option B for Q19 says they "improve quality" — a value inversion.

Defense: Always check: "Does the text present this positively or negatively?"

PITFALL 25: The Subject Swap (NEW — refined from Project 4)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 11, 14, 17.

What it is: The answer claims the text is about Subject B when it's actually about Subject A. Subject B may appear in the text but only as a reference point.

How to detect: Ask: "Is this the MAIN subject or just a COMPARISON/REFERENCE point?"

In this text: Wild salmon = comparison point. Farmed salmon = main subject. Option D for Q17 swaps them.

Defense: Identify the main subject in the FIRST reading. Usually, the subject that gets the MOST description is the main one.
