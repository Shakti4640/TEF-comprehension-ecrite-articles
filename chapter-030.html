CHAPTER 4: GRANDE ENQUÊTE DE CONSOMMATION

Analyzing a Commercial Survey — Detecting Who Benefits, What's Required, and What's Promised

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Texte 53 : Grande enquête de consommation

Savez-vous que plus de 7 nouveaux produits sur 10 disparaissent dans l'année suivant leur commercialisation car ils ne correspondent pas aux besoins des consommateurs ? Nous avons décidé d'effectuer un grand sondage afin de déterminer les raisons pour lesquelles des milliers de nouveaux produits meurent chaque année. En remplissant le questionnaire ci-joint, vous pourrez aider les entreprises à mieux cibler les besoins et les attentes des consommateurs à l'égard de nombreux produits. Pour notre étude, il est impératif que vous remplissiez la case « fiche signalétique » en mentionnant votre identité et votre domiciliation. Ce sondage ne vous prendra que quelques minutes et si vous nous le renvoyez dans les délais indiqués vous pourrez bénéficier d'offres spéciales et de bons de réduction sur les nouveaux produits préférés des ménages. Vous serez de même inscrit(e) sur une liste de consommateurs de marque. Tous les nouveaux produits sur le marché des cosmétiques vous seront automatiquement offerts. Vous n'êtes pas obligé(e) de répondre à toutes les questions. Si vous n'avez pas d'avis particulier sur telle ou telle question, passez à la suivante. Vos réponses sont confidentielles et personnelles et ne pourront en aucun cas être utilisées ou divulguées à de tierces personnes à des fins publicitaires. Nous vous remercions par avance pour votre participation à cette enquête.

Questions:

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH THIS TEXT

Step 1: Recognize the Text Type — A New Category

Apply the text type identification skill (→ Project 2):

SignalWhat You SeeDiagnosis
Direct address throughout"Savez-vous...", "vous pourrez...", "Vos réponses..."Text speaks directly to YOU
Call to action"En remplissant le questionnaire ci-joint"Wants you to DO something
Promises/rewards"offres spéciales", "bons de réduction", "automatiquement offerts"Offers incentives for participation
Obligation language"il est impératif que"Sets conditions
Reassurance language"confidentielles", "ne pourront en aucun cas"Tries to overcome your hesitation

Conclusion: This is a solicitation text — specifically, a survey invitation. It's a hybrid:

PROMOTIONAL (like Ch 2):  It's selling you on participating
INFORMATIONAL (like Ch 3): It explains a system (the survey process)
NEW ELEMENT:              It creates an EXCHANGE — your data for their rewards

This is the first text where you must understand a transactional relationship: the text wants something FROM you and offers something IN RETURN.

Step 2: Map the Text Architecture

This text has no paragraph breaks — it's one continuous block. This is deliberate. When there are no visual breaks, you must create logical breaks yourself. This is a new mapping challenge.

Read through once and identify the functional segments:

SEGMENT 1: THE PROBLEM (lines 1-2)
    "7 out of 10 new products disappear"
    "They don't match consumer needs"
    → Establishes WHY the survey exists

SEGMENT 2: THE PURPOSE (lines 2-3)
    "Determine the reasons products fail"
    "Help companies better target consumer needs"
    → States WHO benefits and HOW

SEGMENT 3: THE OBLIGATION (lines 3-4)
    "Il est impératif" → you MUST fill in identity + address
    → States what is REQUIRED for validity

SEGMENT 4: THE REWARDS (lines 4-7)
    "Offres spéciales" + "bons de réduction"
    "Liste de consommateurs de marque"
    "Cosmetics products automatically sent"
    → States what you GET in exchange

SEGMENT 5: THE REASSURANCE (lines 7-9)
    "Not obliged to answer ALL questions"
    "Skip if no opinion"
    "Confidential, never shared for advertising"
    → Removes your objections/fears

SEGMENT 6: THE CLOSING (line 9)
    "Merci par avance"
    → Polite closure

Why this mapping matters more than ever: With no paragraph breaks, the exam can pull questions from ANY part of the text. Your mental segmentation is the ONLY navigation system you have.

Step 3: Identify the Core Transactional Logic

This is the chapter's key new concept. The text describes an exchange:

WHAT THEY WANT FROM YOU:          WHAT THEY OFFER YOU:
├── Fill out the questionnaire     ├── Special offers
├── Provide name + address         ├── Discount coupons  
│   (MANDATORY)                    ├── Placement on brand list
└── Return within deadline         └── Free cosmetics products
                                   
    CONDITIONS:                    GUARANTEES:
    ├── Identity = impératif       ├── Confidentiality
    ├── Return on time             ├── No advertising use
    └── (Answering all = NOT       └── No sharing with third parties
         required)

Understanding this structure means you can instantly answer questions about:

  • Who benefits? → Follow the purpose: "aider les entreprises"
  • What's required? → Find the "impératif" condition
  • What do you get? → List the rewards
  • What's the goal? → Find the "afin de" statement

Step 4: Detect the Linguistic Registers of Obligation

This text uses different levels of obligation. Recognizing these levels is a critical new skill:

French ExpressionLevelMeaning
"il est impératif que"MANDATORYYou absolutely must do this
"si vous nous le renvoyez"CONDITIONALIf you do this, then you get that
"vous pourrez bénéficier"POSSIBLEYou may benefit (not guaranteed)
"vous n'êtes pas obligé(e)"OPTIONALYou don't have to do this
"ne pourront en aucun cas"ABSOLUTE PROHIBITIONThis will never happen

The exam LOVES testing whether you can distinguish these levels. A "mandatory" element is different from an "optional" one, and a "conditional" reward is different from a "guaranteed" one.

🧩 HOW TO SOLVE EACH QUESTION

QUESTION 21: Cette enquête va servir...

Step 1: Locate the zone

"Va servir" = will serve / will be useful to. This is a WHO BENEFITS question. Go to Segment 2 (the purpose).

Step 2: Find the relevant sentence

"En remplissant le questionnaire ci-joint, vous pourrez aider les entreprises à mieux cibler les besoins et les attentes des consommateurs"

The sentence explicitly names the beneficiary: les entreprises (companies/businesses).

But wait — let's think deeper. The text says consumers can "help companies." Doesn't that mean it also serves consumers, since companies will better target their needs? Let's test this reasoning against the options.

Step 3: Test each option

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — aux entreprisesThe text directly says "aider les entreprises à mieux cibler." Companies are the explicit, named beneficiary of the survey. The survey's data goes to them.✅ DIRECT MATCH
B — aux publicitairesThe text actually says the OPPOSITE: "ne pourront en aucun cas être utilisées... à des fins publicitaires." The data will NOT be used for advertising purposes. Advertisers are explicitly excluded.❌ DIRECTLY CONTRADICTED
C — aux consommateursSUBTLE TRAP. Yes, consumers might eventually benefit if companies make better products. But the survey doesn't directly serve consumers — it serves companies. Consumers are the data source, not the beneficiary. The text says you help companies, not that the survey helps you.❌ INDIRECT BENEFICIARY TRAP
D — aux cosmétologuesThe text mentions cosmetics products as a REWARD for participating, not as the survey's purpose. Cosmetics is in the incentive section, not the purpose section.❌ SEGMENT CONFUSION (reward ≠ purpose)

✅ ANSWER: A — aux entreprises

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Identified a "who benefits" question → went to purpose segment
  2. Found the explicit beneficiary: "aider les entreprises"
  3. Defeated Trap B by finding the direct contradiction ("à des fins publicitaires" = excluded)
  4. Defeated Trap C by distinguishing direct beneficiary from indirect beneficiary
  5. Defeated Trap D by separating the purpose segment from the reward segment

Critical new concept — Direct vs. Indirect Beneficiary:

DIRECT BENEFICIARY:   Who receives the survey's output? → Companies
INDIRECT BENEFICIARY: Who might eventually gain? → Consumers (better products)
DATA SOURCE:          Who provides the information? → You (the survey respondent)

The question asks "va servir" (will serve) = DIRECT beneficiary = Companies

This distinction will appear repeatedly in future chapters. The exam almost always asks about the direct beneficiary.

QUESTION 22: Pour qu'elle soit valable, il faut...

Step 1: Locate the zone

"Pour qu'elle soit valable" = for it to be valid. This is a REQUIREMENT question. Go to Segment 3 (the obligation).

Step 2: Find the relevant sentence

"Pour notre étude, il est impératif que vous remplissiez la case « fiche signalétique » en mentionnant votre identité et votre domiciliation."

Key terms:

  • Il est impératif = mandatory / required
  • Identité = your name (identity)
  • Domiciliation = your address (where you live)

Now translate this into the options' language: "donner votre nom et adresse" = "mentionner votre identité et votre domiciliation." These are synonyms.

Step 3: Test each option

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — répondre à toutes les questionsDIRECT CONTRADICTION. The text explicitly says: "Vous n'êtes pas obligé(e) de répondre à toutes les questions." Answering all questions is NOT required — it's optional. This is the exam's most dangerous trap here because many candidates assume surveys require complete answers.❌ EXPLICITLY CONTRADICTED
B — garder votre anonymatOPPOSITE OF WHAT'S REQUIRED. The text demands your identity and address. Anonymity would make the survey invalid, not valid. This is the exact opposite of the requirement.❌ OPPOSITE TRAP
C — donner votre nom et adresse"Identité" = nom (name). "Domiciliation" = adresse (address). "Il est impératif que vous remplissiez... identité et domiciliation" = you must give your name and address.✅ SYNONYM MATCH
D — la renvoyer rapidementThe text mentions "dans les délais indiqués" (within the indicated deadlines), but this is a condition for receiving REWARDS, not for the survey's validity. The deadline is in Segment 4 (rewards), not Segment 3 (obligation). Also, "rapidement" (quickly) ≠ "dans les délais" (within the deadline).❌ SEGMENT CONFUSION + WORD DISTORTION

✅ ANSWER: C — donner votre nom et adresse

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Identified a requirement question → found the "impératif" marker
  2. Matched "identité + domiciliation" → "nom et adresse" (synonym paraphrase)
  3. Defeated Trap A by finding the explicit contradiction ("pas obligé de répondre à toutes")
  4. Defeated Trap B by recognizing it as the OPPOSITE of the requirement
  5. Defeated Trap D by separating validity conditions (Segment 3) from reward conditions (Segment 4)

New skill activated — Obligation Level Sorting:

This question tested whether you can distinguish between:

MANDATORY (for validity):    Provide name + address
CONDITIONAL (for rewards):   Return within deadline
OPTIONAL (your choice):      Answer all questions OR skip some
PROHIBITED (guaranteed):     Data shared for advertising

The exam placed a conditional requirement (Trap D) and an explicitly optional element (Trap A) alongside the mandatory one. Only by sorting obligation levels could you isolate the correct answer.

QUESTION 23: En échange, vous...

Step 1: Locate the zone

"En échange" = in exchange. This is a REWARD question. Go to Segment 4.

Step 2: Find the relevant sentences

Segment 4 contains multiple rewards. List them all:

  1. "vous pourrez bénéficier d'offres spéciales"
  2. "et de bons de réduction sur les nouveaux produits préférés des ménages"
  3. "Vous serez de même inscrit(e) sur une liste de consommateurs de marque"
  4. "Tous les nouveaux produits sur le marché des cosmétiques vous seront automatiquement offerts"

Now, what do these rewards actually mean in simpler terms?

Reward in TextPlain Meaning
Offres spécialesSpecial deals
Bons de réductionDiscount coupons = reduced prices
Liste de consommateurs de marqueVIP consumer list
Produits cosmétiques offertsFree cosmetics

The question asks what you get "en échange." Let's test each option.

Step 3: Test each option

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — gagner des produits ménagersVOCABULARY TRAP. The text says "produits préférés des ménages" (products preferred by households). "Ménages" = households. "Produits ménagers" = household cleaning products (like bleach, detergent). These are completely different things. "Produits préférés des ménages" means "products that households prefer" — NOT "household cleaning products."❌ COMPOUND NOUN TRAP
B — pourrez bénéficier de tarifs préférentiels"Bons de réduction" = discount coupons = reduced prices = tarifs préférentiels (preferential/reduced rates). Also, "offres spéciales" typically involve better pricing. This is a valid paraphrase.✅ MATCH
C — profiter de soins de beautéThe text mentions cosmetics products being sent to you, not beauty treatments (soins de beauté). Receiving a product ≠ receiving a service. Cosmetics are products you use at home; beauty treatments are services performed on you.❌ PRODUCT ≠ SERVICE CONFUSION

✅ ANSWER: B — pourrez bénéficier de tarifs préférentiels

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Located the reward segment (Segment 4)
  2. Listed all rewards mentioned
  3. Matched "bons de réduction" + "offres spéciales" → "tarifs préférentiels"
  4. Defeated Trap A with precise vocabulary: "produits des ménages" ≠ "produits ménagers"
  5. Defeated Trap C by distinguishing product (cosmétiques) from service (soins de beauté)

Deep dive into Trap A — The Compound Noun Trap:

This is one of the most devious traps in the French language exam. Here's why:

TEXT:    "produits préférés des ménages"
         products preferred by households
         = the products that families like most

TRAP:    "produits ménagers"  
         household products
         = cleaning supplies (bleach, mops, detergent)

The word "ménages" appears in both, but the grammatical structure changes the meaning entirely:

ExpressionStructureMeaning
produits des ménagesproducts of householdsProducts that households buy
produits ménagershousehold productsProducts FOR the household (cleaning)

One is a genitive construction (belonging to households), the other is an adjective (related to housekeeping). Same root word, completely different meaning. This trap exploits candidates who recognize the word "ménage" without analyzing its grammatical function.

QUESTION 24: Ce sondage est effectué pour...

Step 1: Locate the zone

"Est effectué pour" = is conducted for / the purpose is. This is a PURPOSE question. Go to Segments 1 and 2.

Step 2: Find the relevant sentences

Segment 1: "plus de 7 nouveaux produits sur 10 disparaissent... car ils ne correspondent pas aux besoins des consommateurs"

Segment 2: "afin de déterminer les raisons..." + "aider les entreprises à mieux cibler les besoins et les attentes des consommateurs"

The core purpose: mieux cibler les besoins et les attentes = better target needs and expectations.

In other words: understand what consumers want so products can match that demand = mieux cibler la demande (better target the demand).

Step 3: Test each option

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — mieux cibler la demande"Mieux cibler les besoins et les attentes des consommateurs" = "mieux cibler la demande." "Besoins et attentes" (needs and expectations) IS the demand. Perfect paraphrase.✅ MATCH
B — tester vos connaissancesThis is a consumer survey, not a knowledge test or quiz. Nothing in the text suggests testing anyone's knowledge.❌ WRONG TEXT TYPE (survey ≠ test)
C — vendre des produits de marqueThe text mentions "consommateurs de marque" (brand consumers) as a REWARD category, not as the survey's purpose. Selling is not mentioned as the goal.❌ SEGMENT CONFUSION (reward ≠ purpose) — same pattern as Trap D in Q21
D — augmenter l'offreThe problem isn't too FEW products — it's that products DON'T MATCH consumer needs. The text says 70% of new products disappear because they miss the target. The solution is better targeting, not more products.❌ MISDIAGNOSIS OF THE PROBLEM

✅ ANSWER: A — mieux cibler la demande

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Located the purpose statement ("afin de")
  2. Identified the core goal: match products to consumer needs
  3. Matched "besoins et attentes" → "demande" (demand = needs + expectations)
  4. Defeated Trap D by understanding the problem correctly (mismatch, not shortage)

Important distinction — Trap D's logic error:

THE PROBLEM:   Products don't match needs (quality mismatch)
TRAP D SAYS:   Increase the supply (quantity increase)

These address completely different issues:
- Quality mismatch → Solution: better targeting (Option A)
- Quantity shortage → Solution: more products (Option D)

The text describes a quality problem. Option D offers a quantity solution.

✅ WHAT SHOULD BE DONE

1. Segment Continuous Texts Manually

When a text has no paragraph breaks (like this one), your first job is to create your own segments. Use these markers to find segment boundaries:

Marker in TextSignalsSegment Type
"Savez-vous que..."Attention-grabbing questionProblem/Context
"Nous avons décidé... afin de"Purpose statementPurpose/Goal
"il est impératif que"Obligation languageRequirement
"si vous... vous pourrez"Conditional rewardReward/Exchange
"Vous n'êtes pas obligé(e)"Permission/relaxationReassurance
"Nous vous remercions"Closing formulaClosure

Each segment answers a different type of question. Your mental segmentation becomes your paragraph structure.

2. Build a "Who-Gets-What" Table for Transactional Texts

Whenever a text describes an exchange (survey, offer, contract, promotion), immediately build this table:

WHO GIVES WHAT:                    WHO GETS WHAT:
├── You give: survey answers       ├── You get: discounts, free products
├── You give: identity + address   ├── You get: VIP list placement
└── You give: time ("quelques      └── Companies get: consumer data
    minutes")                          to improve targeting

This table answers most questions before you even read them.

3. Identify the "Afin de" / "Pour" Purpose Markers

French texts signal their purpose with specific constructions. Add these to your cause-effect marker vocabulary (building on → Project 3):

Purpose MarkerExampleTranslation
afin de + infinitiveafin de déterminer les raisonsin order to determine
pour + infinitivepour mieux ciblerto better target
dans le but de(not in this text, but common)with the goal of
en vue de(coming in later chapters)with a view to

When you see these markers, you've found the text's stated purpose. Highlight them mentally.

4. Sort Obligation Levels Immediately

When a text contains instructions, demands, or suggestions, sort them by obligation level as you read:

LEVEL 1 — MANDATORY:    "il est impératif que" → MUST do
LEVEL 2 — CONDITIONAL:  "si vous... vous pourrez" → IF you do, THEN you get
LEVEL 3 — OPTIONAL:     "vous n'êtes pas obligé(e)" → you DON'T HAVE TO
LEVEL 4 — PROHIBITED:   "ne pourront en aucun cas" → NEVER, UNDER NO CIRCUMSTANCES

The exam will test whether you confuse these levels. A mandatory element is not optional, and a conditional reward is not guaranteed.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Confuse the Data Source with the Beneficiary

This is the deepest conceptual trap in this text. The survey COLLECTS data from consumers and DELIVERS value to companies. Many candidates think: "The survey is about consumer needs, so it serves consumers." Wrong.

WRONG LOGIC:  Survey is about consumers → serves consumers
RIGHT LOGIC:  Survey collects FROM consumers → serves companies WITH that data

The farmer who milks a cow serves himself, not the cow.
The survey that polls consumers serves companies, not consumers.

Rule: The beneficiary is who RECEIVES the output, not who PROVIDES the input.

2. Do NOT Assume "All Questions Required" for Surveys

This is a cultural/common-sense trap. Most people assume that for a survey to be valid, you must answer every question. The text explicitly contradicts this assumption:

"Vous n'êtes pas obligé(e) de répondre à toutes les questions."

Never let assumptions override explicit text statements. This is the same principle from Chapter 1 (→ Project 1: "Do NOT answer from general knowledge"), now applied to procedural texts.

3. Do NOT Equate "Mentioned in the Text" with "Relevant to This Question"

Every single wrong option in this text uses a word or concept that genuinely appears in the text:

Wrong OptionBorrows From Text
"publicitaires" (Q21-B)"à des fins publicitaires"
"répondre à toutes les questions" (Q22-A)"Vous n'êtes pas obligé de répondre à toutes"
"produits ménagers" (Q23-A)"produits préférés des ménages"
"produits de marque" (Q24-C)"consommateurs de marque"

Every trap option is anchored in the text's vocabulary. The words are real; the application is wrong. This means you can NEVER select an answer just because "I saw those words in the text." You must verify that the words mean the same thing in the option as they do in the text AND that they answer the specific question asked.

4. Do NOT Read "Rapidement" into "Dans les Délais"

Trap D in Q22 says "la renvoyer rapidement" (return it quickly). The text says "dans les délais indiqués" (within the indicated deadlines). These are NOT the same:

"rapidement" = quickly, fast (subjective speed)
"dans les délais" = within the deadline (objective timeframe)

A deadline could be 3 months away — that's not "quickly."
"Quickly" implies urgency; "within the deadline" implies compliance.

Moreover, returning within the deadline is a condition for REWARDS, not for VALIDITY. The question asks about validity. Even if the wording matched, it would be answering the wrong question.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Reverse Meaning" Trap

Option B in Q22 says "garder votre anonymat" (keep your anonymity). The text says "il est impératif... votre identité et votre domiciliation." This is the exact opposite — you must REVEAL your identity, not hide it.

Why candidates fall for it: Many people associate surveys with anonymity. In their experience, surveys are anonymous. So "garder votre anonymat" feels correct based on prior experience, even though the text explicitly demands the opposite.

Defense: The text overrides your experience. Always. Every time. Without exception.

Pitfall 2: The "Compound Noun vs. Genitive" Confusion

This was analyzed in Q23 (Trap A), but it deserves a broader framework because this trap type will recur:

ExpressionGrammarMeaning
produits ménagersNoun + AdjectiveCleaning products
produits des ménagesNoun + de + NounProducts bought by households
consommateurs de marqueNoun + de + NounBrand/premium consumers
produits de marqueNoun + de + NounBrand-name products

The same base words create completely different meanings depending on grammatical construction. The exam exploits this systematically.

Defense: When you see a multi-word expression in an option, check whether the grammatical structure matches the text's structure, not just whether the individual words appear.

Pitfall 3: The "Segment Bleed" Problem

Without paragraph breaks, your brain stores the entire text as one continuous stream. When a question asks about the purpose (Segment 2), your brain may retrieve information from the rewards (Segment 4) or the reassurance (Segment 5) because everything is stored together.

Example: Q24 asks about the survey's purpose. Your brain might retrieve "consommateurs de marque" from Segment 4 and connect it to "vendre des produits de marque" (Option C). But that detail is a REWARD, not a PURPOSE.

Defense: After identifying the question's theme, mentally isolate the correct segment. Ask yourself: "Is this detail from the PURPOSE section or from the REWARD section?" If you can't tell, re-read the text with your segment markers in mind.

Pitfall 4: The "Obvious Answer" Skepticism

Q21 asks who the survey serves. Option A says "aux entreprises." This feels almost too obvious — the text literally says "aider les entreprises." Many candidates think: "That's too easy. The exam must want a more sophisticated answer." They then drift toward Option C ("aux consommateurs") because it feels more nuanced.

Reality check: Sometimes the correct answer IS the obvious one. The exam doesn't always set the most complex option as the answer. If the text directly states something and an option directly paraphrases it, that's your answer. Don't overthink.

Rule: Direct text evidence ALWAYS beats "sophisticated" reasoning. If the text says X and an option says X, choose X — even if it feels too simple.

🌉 BRIDGE TO PROJECT 5

You've now handled four text types: factual (Ch 1), promotional (Ch 2), informational report (Ch 3), and transactional/survey (Ch 4). In all four, the text's tone was relatively neutral or positive — no one was criticizing, judging, or expressing a personal opinion about quality.

In Chapter 5 (Dans la Chaleur du Mélodrame — Reading a Simple Film Review), you'll encounter your first opinion text. This is fundamentally different because:

  • The author has a personal position (likes or dislikes the film)
  • The text contains subjective judgments ("ça marche," "j'ai aimé")
  • You must distinguish the author's voice from the film's content
  • You'll need to detect tone — is the review positive, negative, or mixed?

The segmentation skill from this chapter will transfer directly: a review also has identifiable segments (plot summary → personal opinion → comparison → recommendation). But the new challenge is that the correct answer depends on detecting attitude, not just locating facts.

Phase 1 concludes with Chapter 5. After that, you'll have all the foundational tools needed for Phase 2's more complex challenges.
