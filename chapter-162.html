QUESTION 24: Cet article nous apprend...

This is a "what does the article tell us" question — similar to the "subject" question from Project 5, but asking for the FACTUAL content rather than the thematic subject.

First, identify the CORE FACTS of the article:

CORE FACTS:
1. A group of eco-activists ("le Clan du néon") exists
2. They TARGET neon signs on shops in cities
3. They TURN OFF these signs to fight light pollution
4. They operate in Paris and provincial cities
5. Some shopkeepers haven't turned their signs back on

ELIMINATE:

Choice A: "Qu'une panne générale a privé Paris d'électricité pendant une heure"

"panne générale" = general power outage/blackout
"a privé Paris d'électricité" = deprived Paris of electricity
"pendant une heure" = for one hour

Let me check each element:

"Panne générale" → The text describes TARGETED actions on individual 
signs, NOT a general blackout. They turn off neon signs one by one.

"Pendant une heure" → The text mentions WWF's call to "éteindre les 
lumières pendant une heure" — but that's the WWF EVENT, not what 
happened in Paris. The clan worked for FIVE HOURS ("Cinq heures après").

"Privé Paris d'électricité" → They made streets "plus sombres" (darker), 
not without electricity. They only turned off NEON SIGNS, not all power.

❌ ELIMINATED. This answer inflates targeted actions into a citywide blackout. Three errors: "panne générale" (it was targeted, not general), "privé d'électricité" (only neons, not all power), and "pendant une heure" (the WWF event was one hour, but the clan worked for five). This combines Degree Trap (Project 2 — targeted → general) and Detail Hijack (Project 1 — "une heure" from the WWF context is misapplied).

Choice C: "Qu'une manifestation nocturne contre la pollution a eu lieu à Paris"

"manifestation" = demonstration, protest march
"nocturne" = nighttime
"contre la pollution" = against pollution

Was this a "manifestation"? Let's check:

A "manifestation" in French typically means:
→ A public demonstration or protest
→ People marching with signs/banners
→ A visible, organized public event

What the text describes:
→ A small group (~10 people) in DISGUISE (perruques, lunettes)
→ Operating DISCREETLY (human ladder, flipping switches)
→ No march, no signs, no public spectacle
→ This is DIRECT ACTION, not a demonstration

❌ ELIMINATED. This is a Category Error trap — the answer assigns the wrong TYPE of action. The text describes direct action (covert operation to turn off signs), not a manifestation (public demonstration). The activists are disguised precisely BECAUSE they're NOT demonstrating publicly — they're acting covertly.

🆕 NEW TRAP TYPE: Category Error

The answer describes the correct general area (environmental activism) but assigns the wrong CATEGORY of action. A "manifestation" is a public protest; "direct action" is a covert operation. Same cause, different method. Always check: does the text describe THIS specific type of action?

Choice D: "Que des petits commerces sont victimes d'actes de vandalisme"

"victimes" = victims
"actes de vandalisme" = acts of vandalism

Let me check the text's FRAMING of the action:

TEXT FRAMING:
"pacifistes fantaisistes" = whimsical pacifists
"la fibre écologiste" = ecological convictions
"invité à renoncer au néon" = INVITED to give up neon
"pollution lumineuse" = light pollution (the PROBLEM they fight)

VANDALISM FRAMING:
→ Destructive intent
→ Damage to property
→ Criminal motivation
→ Victims suffering harm

The text frames the activists as POSITIVE — "pacifistes" (pacifists), fighting pollution, INVITING shop owners to change. No destruction, no damage. The switch is designed to be operated (it's there for firefighters). They're not breaking anything.

❌ ELIMINATED. This is a Framing Reversal trap — the answer reverses the text's framing entirely. The text presents activists as POSITIVE agents fighting pollution. The answer presents them as CRIMINALS committing vandalism. Same action, opposite interpretation. The text's vocabulary ("pacifistes," "invité," "écologiste") clearly establishes a positive frame.

🆕 NEW TRAP TYPE: Framing Reversal

The answer takes an action described POSITIVELY in the text and reframes it NEGATIVELY (or vice versa). The text says "pacifist eco-activists invite change" → the answer says "vandals victimize shops." Always check: HOW does the text frame the action — positively or negatively?

💡 KEY INSIGHT: The Emotion Precision Principle

WHEN A QUESTION ASKS ABOUT FEELINGS:

STEP 1: Find the EXACT emotion word in the text
STEP 2: Determine its INTENSITY (mild, moderate, intense)
STEP 3: Determine its DIRECTION (positive, negative, neutral)
STEP 4: Match BOTH intensity AND direction to the answer choices

"jouissance" = INTENSE + POSITIVE
"jubiler" = INTENSE + POSITIVE → MATCH ✓
"se féliciter" = MILD + POSITIVE → intensity mismatch ✗
"se plaindre" = MILD + NEGATIVE → both mismatch ✗
"rechigner" = MODERATE + NEGATIVE → both mismatch ✗

🎯 STEP 4: WHAT COULD BE DONE

Strategy 1: The Action Sequence Map (NEW — for narrative/action texts)

Whenever a text describes a PHYSICAL PROCESS or OPERATION:

HOW TO BUILD AN ACTION SEQUENCE MAP:
1. Read the text and number each ACTION in order
2. For each step, note: WHO does it? WHERE? WITH WHAT? WHAT HAPPENS?
3. Mark which steps are INSIDE vs OUTSIDE
4. Mark which steps are MAIN ACTIONS vs SECONDARY actions
5. When a question asks "what does the intervention consist of,"
   point to the MAIN action, not the secondary ones

THIS TEXT'S HIERARCHY:
MAIN ACTION: Lowering the switch → cutting power to neon sign
SECONDARY: Leaving a sticker → signing the action
SECONDARY: Informing the owner → communicating the message

✅ STEP 5: WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

Rules for Action Narrative Texts

1. Build the Action Sequence Map BEFORE answering

Number every step. Note WHERE each action happens (inside/outside), WHO does it, and WHAT the result is. This prevents you from confusing steps or inventing locations.

2. For emotion questions, find the EXACT word and measure its intensity

Don't approximate. "Jouissance" is not "satisfaction." "Jubiler" is not "se féliciter." The exam tests whether you can distinguish INTENSE emotions from MILD ones.

3. For impact questions, measure SCALE precisely

QUANTIFIER PRECISION:
"certains" / "quelques" = some / a few → LIMITED
"plusieurs" = several → MODERATE  
"beaucoup" / "nombreux" = many → LARGE
"la plupart" / "la majorité" = most → VAST
"tous" / "la totalité" = all → COMPLETE

If the text says "certains" and the answer says "nombreux" → WRONG.
If the text says "certains" and the answer says "quelques" → MATCH.

4. Check the text's FRAMING before evaluating answers

If the text frames activists positively ("pacifistes"), any answer calling them vandals is wrong. Framing guides interpretation.

5. Distinguish MAIN actions from SECONDARY actions

The sticker is a secondary action (signature). The switch is the main action (intervention). When the question asks "l'intervention consiste à," answer with the MAIN action.

❌ STEP 6: WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

❌ DON'T confuse "turning off a sign" with "closing a shop"

A neon sign is exterior advertising. Turning it off doesn't close the business. Don't extrapolate consequences the text doesn't describe.

❌ DON'T import your OPINION about the action

You might personally think these activists ARE vandals. But the TEXT frames them positively. In CE, you answer based on what the TEXT says, not what you believe.

❌ DON'T pick the emotion that's "in the right direction" but wrong intensity

"Se féliciter" IS positive, and the activists ARE positive. But "se féliciter" is too MILD for "jouissance." Direction alone isn't enough — intensity must match too.

❌ DON'T inflate the scale of impact

"Certains" (some) is not "nombreux" (many) and certainly not "la corporation des commerçants" (the entire profession). Respect the text's quantifiers.

❌ DON'T confuse the WWF event with the clan's specific action

The WWF called for lights off for ONE hour. The clan worked for FIVE hours. The WWF event is CONTEXT; the clan's operation is the SUBJECT. Don't merge them.
