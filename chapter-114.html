À la cantine, des baladeurs plutôt que des frites

Manger des carottes râpées à la cantine du collège plutôt que des pâtes ou des hamburgers pour gagner des baladeurs à la mode dont rêvent tous les adolescents, des jeux vidéo, des livres ou des places de cinéma, c'est désormais possible pour les 30 000 élèves d'une trentaine d'établissements scolaires de Glasgow en Écosse où la municipalité a trouvé ce moyen original de lutter contre l'obésité des jeunes.

Certains plats au menu des cantines scolaires permettent d'acquérir un certain nombre de points : une salade verte ou un fruit valent 15 points, un hamburger n'en vaut que 4 et une assiette de frites n'en donne aucun. Les élèves ont reçu des cartes magnétiques sur lesquelles sont stockés les points de leur menu lorsqu'ils passent à la caisse. Lorsqu'on a 4 000 points, on gagne un baladeur, 1500 points donnent droit à un bon d'achat de 15 euros sur un site de vente de livres sur internet. 850 points à deux places de cinéma. Katie, 13 ans, élève du collège trouve cette idée formidable « avant, tout le monde se précipitait sur les pizzas et chips. Maintenant, on mange des trucs plus sains. J'ai déjà gagné deux places de cinéma, je vais continuer », raconte-t-elle. Le menu sain, le plus populaire, comprend aujourd'hui une soupe, un sandwich, un yaourt et du lait pour 1.80 euros. Il rapporte 40 points. Il suffit d'en ingurgiter 100 pour gagner un baladeur d'une valeur de 280 euros.

Un site internet a même été créé pour permettre aux élèves de vérifier leurs points.

La municipalité promet de dépenser plus de 60 000 euros pour acheter les prix pour les 12 prochains mois, considérant que c'est là la meilleure solution pour lutter contre le surpoids des adolescents.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

Chapters 6-9 dealt with texts about lifestyles, disasters, predictions, and political proposals. None of them described a WORKING MECHANISM — a system with inputs, processes, and outputs.

Chapter 10 is the first mechanism text — it describes HOW something works, step by step, with precise numbers. This creates a fundamentally different reading challenge:

PREVIOUS TEXT TYPES:              THIS TEXT TYPE:
  Lifestyle feature (Ch.6)         MECHANISM DESCRIPTION
  News report (Ch.7)               - Inputs: food choices
  Prediction (Ch.8)                - Process: points accumulated
  Political proposal (Ch.9)        - Outputs: prizes won
                                   - Numbers: precise quantities
                                   - Goal: behavior change

The New Core Concept: Mechanism Mapping

A mechanism text describes a SYSTEM. You need to understand the system's components:

MECHANISM ANATOMY:

1. CONTEXT    → Where and why the system exists
2. INPUT      → What goes INTO the system (food choices)
3. PROCESS    → How the system WORKS (points calculation)
4. OUTPUT     → What comes OUT (prizes)
5. GOAL       → What the system aims to ACHIEVE (reduce obesity)
6. SCALE      → Numbers, quantities, costs

Questions about mechanism texts test whether you can:

SkillWhat It Tests
Identify the system's PURPOSEWhy does this system exist? (goal)
Track numerical precisionExact points, prices, quantities
Understand the logic flowInput → Process → Output
Distinguish METHOD from GOALThe points system is the METHOD. Reducing obesity is the GOAL.
Avoid numerical distortionsOptions may mix up numbers or change conditions

The New Core Concept: Reading Numbers Within Text

This text is DENSE with numbers. Unlike previous texts where numbers were secondary details, here numbers ARE the content:

NUMBERS IN THIS TEXT:

30,000 students in 30 schools
15 points for salad or fruit
4 points for a hamburger
0 points for fries
4,000 points = 1 baladeur (music player)
1,500 points = 15€ book voucher
850 points = 2 cinema tickets
1.80€ = price of healthy menu
40 points per healthy menu
100 healthy menus = 1 baladeur (280€ value)
60,000€ budget for 12 months of prizes

The trap with numbers: options can MIX UP which number goes with which item, or change the CONDITIONS under which a number applies.

Skill 1 — Distinguishing METHOD from DIET from COMPETITION:

  A contest where participants compete against each other
  → Students compete against each other for prizes
  → The text describes individual accumulation, not competition ❌

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Distinguishing SUFFICIENT from NECESSARY (the chapter's most critical logical skill):

LOGICAL DISTINCTION:

"Il SUFFIT d'en ingurgiter 100"
  "suffit" = it's SUFFICIENT / it's enough
  = 100 healthy menus is ONE WAY to reach 4,000 points
  = It WORKS, but it's not the ONLY way

"OBLIGATOIREMENT commander 100 menus sains"
  "obligatoirement" = necessarily / you MUST
  = 100 healthy menus is the ONLY WAY
  = No other path exists

SUFFICIENT ≠ NECESSARY

SUFFICIENT: "This path works" (there may be other paths too)
NECESSARY: "This is the only path" (no alternatives exist)

The text says 100 menus is SUFFICIENT (one valid method).
Option C says 100 menus is NECESSARY (the only method).
These are logically different.

THE CORRECT ANSWER focuses on the UNDERLYING REQUIREMENT:
  4,000 points (regardless of how you get them)
  = Option A: "cumuler au moins 4,000 points"

This is a fundamental logical distinction that appears throughout comprehension exams:

SUFFICIENCY vs. NECESSITY:

"Il suffit de X"     = X is enough (but not the only way)
"Il faut X"          = X is required
"Obligatoirement X"  = X is mandatory (the only way)
"Au moins X"         = at least X (minimum threshold)

"Il suffit" signals: this is ONE method, not the ONLY method.
"Obligatoirement" signals: this is the ONLY method.

When the text uses "suffit" and an option uses "obligatoirement,"

Skill 3 — Preventing number-context confusion:

Option D: "ajouter 1.80 euros chaque fois"

The text says: "une soupe, un sandwich, un yaourt et du lait pour 1.80 euros"
  → 1.80€ is the PRICE of the healthy menu

Option D says: "ajouter 1.80€" = ADD 1.80€

The 1.80€ is what you PAY for the food.
It's not an ADDITIONAL charge on top of something else.
Option D treats a PRICE as a SURCHARGE.

PRICE ≠ SURCHARGE
"pour 1.80€" = costs 1.80€ (price)
"ajouter 1.80€" = add 1.80€ extra (surcharge)

This is a new type of numerical distortion:

TRAP TYPE: "NUMBER-CONTEXT SWAP"

The text assigns a number to one context (price of a menu).
An option moves that number to a different context (surcharge per purchase).

The NUMBER is correct (1.80€).
The CONTEXT is wrong (price → surcharge).

Same number + different context = different meaning.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Sufficient → Necessary" Conversion (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: The text says "it's enough to do X" (sufficient condition). An option says "you must do X" (necessary condition). Students don't notice the logical difference.

THE MECHANISM:

TEXT: "Il SUFFIT d'en ingurgiter 100"
  = Eating 100 is ENOUGH
  = 100 is ONE valid path to 4,000 points
  = Other paths exist (e.g., eating 267 hamburgers at 15 pts each... wait)
  
Actually, let me recalculate:
  Hamburger = 4 pts
  4,000 ÷ 4 = 1,000 hamburgers (possible but absurd)
  
  Or mix: 50 menus (2,000 pts) + 133 salads (1,995 pts) ≈ 4,000 pts
  
The point is: MULTIPLE PATHS exist to 4,000 points.
100 healthy menus is just the SIMPLEST calculation.

OPTION C: "OBLIGATOIREMENT commander 100 menus sains"
  = You MUST order exactly 100 healthy menus
  = No other path is valid

SUFFICIENT ≠ NECESSARY
"Enough" ≠ "Must"
"One way" ≠ "The only way"

Defense: When you see "il suffit de" in the text, immediately FLAG any option containing "obligatoirement," "nécessairement," or "il faut absolument." These convert sufficiency into necessity.

Pitfall #2: The "Number-Context Swap" Trap

What happens: An option takes a correct number from the text but places it in the wrong context.

EXAMPLES IN THIS TEXT:

NUMBER: 60,000€
  CORRECT CONTEXT: annual budget for buying prizes
  WRONG CONTEXT: value of a single prize (Option B)

NUMBER: 1.80€
  CORRECT CONTEXT: price of the healthy menu
  WRONG CONTEXT: surcharge added per purchase (Option D)

NUMBER: 100
  CORRECT CONTEXT: number of healthy menus for a baladeur
  WRONG CONTEXT: could be confused with "100 points"

The NUMBER is always FROM the text (so it looks legitimate).
The CONTEXT is changed (so the meaning is distorted).

Defense: For every number in an option, verify TWO things:

  1. Is this number IN the text? (usually yes — the trap uses real numbers)
  2. Does the option assign it to the SAME context as the text? (often no)

Pitfall #3: The "Goal Reversal" Trap

What happens: An option states the OPPOSITE of the system's purpose.

TEXT:     "lutter CONTRE l'obésité" (fight AGAINST obesity)
OPTION D: "FAVORISANT l'obésité"    (PROMOTING obesity)

contre ↔ favorisant = ANTONYMS
  contre = against
  favorisant = promoting/fostering

This reverses the system's ENTIRE PURPOSE.
Under time pressure, students see "obésité" in both
and think they match, without checking the DIRECTION.

Defense: Always check the DIRECTION word (against/for, fight/promote, reduce/increase) in addition to the TOPIC word (obesity, weight, health).

Pitfall #4: The "Comprehensiveness Competition"

What happens: Two options are both factually true, but one is more comprehensive than the other. Students pick the narrower one because it's more specific (and specificity usually helps).

BOTH TRUE:
  C: "all foods don't earn the same points" (comprehensive — covers all data)
  D: "some foods earn no points" (specific — covers only fries)

In MOST exam questions, specificity helps (it's more precise).
But when the question asks about "ce système" (the system as a whole),
COMPREHENSIVENESS wins over specificity.

THE RULE:
  "What characterizes this system?" → comprehensive answer
  "What specific detail is true?" → specific answer
  
Match your answer's SCOPE to the question's SCOPE.
