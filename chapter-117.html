📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le français a colonisé l'anglais

Henriette Walter, linguiste, vient de publier un essai sur les relations tumultueuses entre l'anglais et le français.

Journaliste : À vous lire, sans Jeanne d'Arc, le français occuperait aujourd'hui la place de l'anglais dans le monde.

H. Walter : Presque ! En boutant les Anglais hors de France, Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française ses chances d'expansion mondiale. Si son intervention n'avait pas eu lieu, le roi d'Angleterre, Henri V, aurait été couronné à Reims et serait devenu roi de France à la place du dauphin. Le français aurait pu devenir la langue des deux pays réunis en un seul royaume.

Journaliste : Car, pendant très longtemps, les Anglais ont parlé le français ?

H. Walter : Henri IV, en 1395, est le premier roi en trois siècles et demi à parler l'anglais. Jusqu'au XIVe siècle, le français domine, c'est la langue élégante ; la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long. Les tribunaux anglais n'ont cessé de siéger en français qu'en 1731.

Journaliste : Donc, le français a colonisé l'anglais ?

H. Walter : Exactement. Les Anglais, eux, auraient vraiment des raisons de se plaindre ! Ouvrez un dictionnaire anglais : plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française, alors que les 55 000 mots du Robert, 2 500 à peine sont anglais. Il faut rétablir la vérité !

Journaliste : Quand les Français commencent-ils à être attirés par l'anglais ?

H. Walter : Au XVIIIe siècle, parce que l'élite était en admiration devant le régime parlementaire britannique. La première apparition du franglais, nous la devons aux sans-culottes. Ensuite, il y a eu le sport, au début du XIXe siècle, et puis la mode. Avec la mondialisation, le langage qui s'élabore est constitué en grande partie de français par le biais du latin. Dans l'informatique, par exemple, 80 % du vocabulaire est d'origine latine. Mais qui le sait ?

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

Chapters 11-12 featured a SINGLE authorial voice — one journalist writing one article. You tracked ONE person's opinion. Chapter 13 introduces the interview format, where TWO voices alternate:

INTERVIEW FORMAT ANATOMY:

INTRODUCTION:  Brief context (who is being interviewed, why)
EXCHANGE 1:    Journalist asks → Expert answers
EXCHANGE 2:    Journalist asks → Expert answers
EXCHANGE 3:    Journalist asks → Expert answers
EXCHANGE 4:    Journalist asks → Expert answers

TWO DISTINCT VOICES:
  JOURNALIST: Frames questions, summarizes, sometimes provokes
  EXPERT:     Provides knowledge, makes claims, gives evidence

The critical challenge: WHO says WHAT?

In a regular article, everything is from one source. In an interview, the journalist's questions sometimes contain SUMMARIES or INTERPRETATIONS of the expert's position — and these may be imprecise. The expert's answers contain the ACTUAL claims.

ATTRIBUTION DANGER ZONES:

JOURNALIST'S QUESTIONS often contain:
  → Simplified summaries of the expert's thesis
  → Leading phrases that may exaggerate
  → Provocative reframings to get a reaction

EXPERT'S ANSWERS contain:
  → The actual, nuanced claims
  → Supporting evidence (dates, numbers, examples)
  → Qualifications and corrections

RULE: When a question says "Selon H. Walter,"
      look at what H. WALTER says (her answers),
      not what the JOURNALIST says about her.

The New Core Concept: Following an Argument Across Exchanges

Unlike a continuous text where an argument develops paragraph by paragraph, an interview's argument is FRAGMENTED across Q&A pairs. Each exchange covers one aspect:

ARGUMENT MAP OF THIS INTERVIEW:

Exchange 1: The "Jeanne d'Arc thesis"
  → Without Jeanne d'Arc, French might have become
     the world language instead of English

Exchange 2: Historical dominance of French in England
  → English kings spoke French for 350 years
  → French = language of power/elegance

Exchange 3: French vocabulary in English
  → More than half of English words come from French
  → Only 2,500 French words come from English

Exchange 4: French attraction to English
  → Started in 18th century (admiration for British parliament)
  → Then sport (19th century), then fashion
  → Modern globalized language is mostly Latin-based

Questions may ask about ANY exchange. You must navigate to the RIGHT exchange to find the answer.

The New Core Concept: Historical Claims and Counter-Intuitive Logic

This interview makes claims that go AGAINST common assumptions:

COMMON ASSUMPTION vs. TEXT'S CLAIM:

ASSUMPTION: English has invaded French (anglicisms in French)
CLAIM:      French invaded English FIRST (much more extensively)

ASSUMPTION: Jeanne d'Arc saved French culture
CLAIM:      Jeanne d'Arc PREVENTED French from becoming a world language

ASSUMPTION: English dominance is natural/inevitable
CLAIM:      English dominance is partly accidental (result of Jeanne d'Arc)

These counter-intuitive claims are CENTRAL to the interview.
Questions test whether you understand the REVERSAL of expectations.

STEP 2: Read the text with radar ON — EXCHANGE-BY-EXCHANGE MAPPING

I'll map each exchange, separating the JOURNALIST's framing from WALTER's actual claims:

Introduction:

"Henriette Walter, linguiste, vient de publier un essai sur les relations tumultueuses entre l'anglais et le français."

🏷️ CONTEXT: Linguist, new book, about French-English relations.
🏷️ TONE: "Tumultueuses" = tumultuous/stormy → the relationship is CONFLICTUAL.

Exchange 1: The Jeanne d'Arc Thesis

Journalist: "À vous lire, sans Jeanne d'Arc, le français occuperait aujourd'hui la place de l'anglais dans le monde."

🏷️ JOURNALIST'S SUMMARY: Without Jeanne d'Arc, French would occupy English's current position (= French would be the world language).
🏷️ GRAMMAR: "Occuperait" = conditional → this is a HYPOTHETICAL scenario, not a fact.

H. Walter: "Presque ! En boutant les Anglais hors de France, Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française ses chances d'expansion mondiale."

🏷️ WALTER'S CLAIM: By expelling the English from France, Jeanne d'Arc caused French to LOSE its chance of global expansion.

🚨 CRITICAL LOGIC:

THE CAUSAL CHAIN (Walter's argument):

1. Before Jeanne d'Arc: England and France could have MERGED
   → One kingdom, one king, speaking FRENCH
   → French would become the language of BOTH countries

2. Jeanne d'Arc expels the English from France
   → The two countries SEPARATE
   → England develops its OWN language (English)
   → English eventually becomes the world language

3. CONCLUSION: Jeanne d'Arc, by saving France's independence,
   inadvertently PREVENTED French from becoming global
   → She is "responsible" for ENGLISH becoming the world language

THE PARADOX:
  Jeanne d'Arc = French national hero
  BUT: her victory led to ENGLISH global dominance
  She saved France but lost French the world

"Si son intervention n'avait pas eu lieu, le roi d'Angleterre, Henri V, aurait été couronné à Reims et serait devenu roi de France..."

🏷️ HYPOTHETICAL: Conditionnel passé = what WOULD HAVE happened without Jeanne d'Arc.

"Le français aurait pu devenir la langue des deux pays réunis en un seul royaume."

🏷️ HYPOTHETICAL CONCLUSION: French COULD HAVE become the language of both countries united in one kingdom.

Exchange 2: Historical French Dominance in England

Journalist: "Car, pendant très longtemps, les Anglais ont parlé le français ?"

🏷️ JOURNALIST: Confirming the historical premise — English people spoke French for a long time.

H. Walter: "Henri IV, en 1395, est le premier roi en trois siècles et demi à parler l'anglais."

🏷️ FACT: The first English king to speak English (in 350 years!) was Henri IV in 1395.
🏷️ IMPLICATION: For 350 years before that, English kings spoke FRENCH, not English.

"Jusqu'au XIVe siècle, le français domine, c'est la langue élégante ; la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long."

🏷️ FACT: Until the 14th century, French dominated in England.
🏷️ SIGNIFICANCE: French = language of elegance and POWER.

🚨 IDIOM: "Avoir le bras long" = to have a long arm = to have CONNECTIONS/INFLUENCE/POWER.

IDIOM: "AVOIR LE BRAS LONG"

LITERAL: to have a long arm
IDIOMATIC: to have power, influence, connections
           (your "arm" reaches far = you can affect things)

IN CONTEXT:
  "la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long"
  = speaking French proves you have power/influence
  = French was the language of the POWERFUL
  = If you spoke French in England, you were IMPORTANT

"Les tribunaux anglais n'ont cessé de siéger en français qu'en 1731."

🏷️ FACT: English courts only stopped using French in 1731.
🏷️ GRAMMAR: "N'ont cessé... qu'en 1731" → "ne...que" = ONLY in 1731 (from Ch.4). Courts stopped using French ONLY in 1731 = they used French until 1731 = remarkably late.

Exchange 3: French Vocabulary in English

Journalist: "Donc, le français a colonisé l'anglais ?"

🏷️ JOURNALIST: Provocative summary — French "colonized" English.

H. Walter: "Exactement. Les Anglais, eux, auraient vraiment des raisons de se plaindre !"

🏷️ WALTER: Confirms and adds humor — the English would have reason to COMPLAIN (about French linguistic invasion), not the French about English.

"Ouvrez un dictionnaire anglais : plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française"

🏷️ CLAIM: More than one in two English words = MORE THAN 50% of English vocabulary comes from French.

🚨 CRITICAL DATA:

VOCABULARY STATISTICS:

ENGLISH dictionary: "plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française"
  = more than 1 in 2 = MORE THAN 50% from French

FRENCH dictionary (Robert): "2,500 à peine sont anglais"
  = barely 2,500 out of 55,000 = about 4.5% from English

COMPARISON:
  French → English: >50% of English words
  English → French: ~4.5% of French words

WALTER'S POINT: French influenced English FAR MORE
than English influenced French. The "invasion" went
the OTHER direction from what people think.

"alors que les 55 000 mots du Robert, 2 500 à peine sont anglais."

🏷️ CONTRAST: Only 2,500 out of 55,000 French words come from English. "À peine" = barely/hardly.

Exchange 4: French Attraction to English

Journalist: "Quand les Français commencent-ils à être attirés par l'anglais ?"

🏷️ JOURNALIST: Asks about the BEGINNING of French interest in English. "Commencent-ils" = when did they START?

H. Walter: "Au XVIIIe siècle, parce que l'élite était en admiration devant le régime parlementaire britannique."

🏷️ ANSWER: 18th century. Reason: French elite ADMIRED the British PARLIAMENTARY SYSTEM.

🚨 KEY DETAIL FOR Q27: The FIRST reason = admiration for British POLITICAL system.

CHRONOLOGY OF FRENCH INTEREST IN ENGLISH:

1. 18th century: British parliamentary system (POLITICAL admiration)
   → "l'élite en admiration devant le régime parlementaire"
   → This is the FIRST/INITIAL reason

2. French Revolution era: Sans-culottes (first "franglais")
   → "La première apparition du franglais"

3. Early 19th century: Sport
   → "Ensuite, il y a eu le sport"

4. Later: Fashion (mode)
   → "et puis la mode"

5. Modern era: Globalization + IT
   → "Avec la mondialisation... l'informatique"

"La première apparition du franglais, nous la devons aux sans-culottes."

🏷️ FACT: First franglais came from the French Revolution era (sans-culottes = revolutionary movement).

"Ensuite, il y a eu le sport, au début du XIXe siècle, et puis la mode."

🏷️ SEQUENCE: Sport (early 19th century) → fashion (later).

"Avec la mondialisation, le langage qui s'élabore est constitué en grande partie de français par le biais du latin."

🏷️ MODERN CLAIM: The globalized language being created is LARGELY French-derived through Latin.

"Dans l'informatique, par exemple, 80 % du vocabulaire est d'origine latine."

🏷️ EXAMPLE: 80% of IT vocabulary comes from Latin (which connects to French).

🚨 WALTER'S FINAL POINT: Even in modern technology, the vocabulary is more Latin (= related to French) than people realize. Her rhetorical question "Mais qui le sait ?" (But who knows this?) implies: nobody recognizes French's ongoing influence.

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 24

"Selon H. Walter, Jeanne d'Arc pourrait être tenue responsable de..."

The relevant exchange:

"En boutant les Anglais hors de France, Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française ses chances d'expansion mondiale."

WALTER'S ARGUMENT:

Jeanne d'Arc → expelled English from France
  → France and England remained SEPARATE
  → English developed independently
  → English (not French) became the world language

THEREFORE: Jeanne d'Arc is responsible for
  FRENCH losing its chance → which means
  ENGLISH gaining its chance → which means
  ENGLISH becoming the world language

She is responsible for "la large expansion de l'ANGLAIS"
  NOT for "la faible place de l'anglais" (that's the opposite)
OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) l'abandon de la royauté en France"Abandon de la royauté" = abandonment of monarchy. The text discusses kings and kingdoms but NEVER says Jeanne d'Arc caused France to abandon monarchy. She actually SAVED the French monarchy (the dauphin became king thanks to her).❌ ELIMINATED
B) la faible place de l'anglais en France"Faible place de l'anglais" = the weak position of English in France. This is the OPPOSITE of the argument. Walter says Jeanne d'Arc caused English to become DOMINANT worldwide. English's position is STRONG, not weak. And the argument is about GLOBAL language, not just France.❌ ELIMINATED
C) l'importance de la royauté en Angleterre"Importance de la royauté en Angleterre" = importance of English monarchy. The text mentions the English king (Henri V) but doesn't argue Jeanne d'Arc made the English monarchy important. This is an off-topic claim.❌ ELIMINATED
D) la large expansion de l'anglais dans le monde"Large expansion de l'anglais dans le monde" = the wide expansion of English in the world. Walter argues: Jeanne d'Arc prevented French from becoming global → English filled that space → English expanded worldwide. Jeanne d'Arc is thus INDIRECTLY responsible for English's global expansion.✅ CORRECT

Answer: D

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Following counter-intuitive causal chains (the chapter's core skill):

COUNTER-INTUITIVE CAUSAL CHAIN:

INTUITIVE expectation:
  "Jeanne d'Arc saved France → she helped French"
  → You'd expect her to be responsible for something GOOD for French

ACTUAL argument:
  "Jeanne d'Arc saved France → BUT this prevented French
   from becoming global → English became global instead"
  → She's responsible for something GOOD for ENGLISH

The causal chain has an UNEXPECTED twist:
  Saving France (good for France) →
  Separating France from England (neutral) →
  English developing independently (consequence) →
  English becoming the world language (unexpected result)

To follow this, you must:
  1. Accept the counter-intuitive premise
  2. Follow the chain to its logical conclusion
  3. Not substitute your expectations for the text's argument

Skill 2 — Distinguishing "faible" from "large" in the context:

Option B is the most dangerous trap because it uses a PLAUSIBLE-SOUNDING claim:

TRAP ANALYSIS — OPTION B:

"la faible place de l'anglais en France"
  = the weak position of English in France

This sounds like it could be related to Jeanne d'Arc:
  She expelled the English → English became less present in France
  → English has a "faible place" in France

But THIS IS NOT WALTER'S ARGUMENT.

Walter's argument is about GLOBAL language dominance, not France specifically:
  "chances d'expansion MONDIALE" = global expansion chances
  "la place de l'anglais DANS LE MONDE" = English's place IN THE WORLD

The scale is GLOBAL, not national.
And the direction is EXPANSION (strong), not weakness (faible).

Option B:  "faible" (weak) + "en France" (national scale) → WRONG
Option D:  "large expansion" (strong) + "dans le monde" (global scale) → CORRECT

Skill 3 — Attribution precision in interviews:

The question says: "Selon H. WALTER"
  → Look at WALTER's statements, not the journalist's

JOURNALIST says: "le français occuperait la place de l'anglais"
  → This is the journalist's SUMMARY

WALTER says: "Jeanne d'Arc fait perdre à la langue française
              ses chances d'expansion mondiale"
  → This is WALTER's actual claim

The JOURNALIST focuses on: what French WOULD HAVE been
WALTER focuses on: what French LOST (and thus what English GAINED)

The correct answer comes from WALTER's framing, not the journalist's.

This reinforces the attribution-restricted reading from Chapter 7, now applied to interviews:

ATTRIBUTION IN INTERVIEWS:
  "Selon H. Walter" → use ONLY Walter's answers
  "D'après le journaliste" → use ONLY the journalist's questions
  "Selon cet article" → use BOTH

Always match the attribution in the question
to the speaker in the text.

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 25

"Avant le XIVe siècle, quand un Anglais parlait français, cela signifiait qu'il..."

The relevant text:

"Jusqu'au XIVe siècle, le français domine, c'est la langue élégante ; la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long."

WHAT SPEAKING FRENCH INDICATED:

"langue élégante" = language of elegance
"le bras long" = power/influence/connections

Speaking French in England BEFORE the 14th century meant:
  → You were ELEGANT (cultured, refined)
  → You had POWER (le bras long = influence)
  → You were part of the ELITE (court, aristocracy, law)
OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) était étranger"Étranger" = foreigner. Speaking French didn't mean you were foreign — French was the DOMESTIC language of the English elite. English aristocrats spoke French as their NATIVE court language. Being French-speaking in England meant being ENGLISH elite, not foreign.❌ ELIMINATED
B) était royaliste"Royaliste" = royalist (supporter of monarchy). The text says kings spoke French, but "royaliste" means supporting the monarchy politically. Speaking French indicated SOCIAL STATUS, not political allegiance.❌ ELIMINATED
C) avait du pouvoir"Avait du pouvoir" = had power. Text says: "la parler prouve qu'on a le bras long." "Le bras long" = power/influence. "Avait du pouvoir" directly translates this idiom.✅ CORRECT
D) venait d'un milieu populaire"Milieu populaire" = working-class/common background. The OPPOSITE is true — French was the language of the ELITE, not the common people. The common people spoke English; the powerful spoke French.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: C

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Idiom-to-literal translation (reinforcing Ch.11-12):

IDIOM: "avoir le bras long"
LITERAL: to have a long arm
MEANING: to have power, influence, connections

OPTION C: "avait du pouvoir" (had power)

"le bras long" → "du pouvoir"
IDIOM → LITERAL EQUIVALENT

This is the THIRD idiom-based question in Phase 3:
  Ch.11: "faire avaler la pilule" → deceive
  Ch.12: "s'avoir à l'œil" → monitor each other
  Ch.13: "avoir le bras long" → have power ← CURRENT

PATTERN: Phase 3 consistently tests idiom comprehension.
The correct answer LITERALIZES the idiom.

Skill 2 — Understanding sociolinguistic implications:

SOCIOLINGUISTIC LOGIC:

In medieval England:
  ELITE spoke French (kings, courts, aristocracy)
  COMMONERS spoke English (ordinary people)

Therefore:
  Speaking French = you're part of the elite = you have POWER
  Speaking English = you're common = you're ordinary

This is the INVERSE of modern expectations:
  TODAY: speaking English = global power/influence
  THEN: speaking French = domestic power/influence

The text's argument REVERSES modern assumptions.

Skill 3 — Eliminating the "opposite" option:

Option D says speaking French meant coming from a "milieu populaire" (working-class). This is the EXACT OPPOSITE of the text:

TEXT: French = language of the elite, of power
OPTION D: French = language of the common people

EXACT OPPOSITE → immediate elimination

This is the most straightforward antonym trap:
  Elite ↔ Populaire
  Power ↔ Ordinary

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 26

"D'après Henriette Walter,"

The relevant text:

"plus d'un terme sur deux est d'origine française" (about English)
"les 55 000 mots du Robert, 2 500 à peine sont anglais" (about French)

WALTER'S VOCABULARY CLAIMS:

ENGLISH: "plus d'un terme sur deux" = more than 1 in 2 = >50%
  → More than HALF of English vocabulary comes from French

FRENCH: "2,500 à peine" out of 55,000 = ~4.5%
  → Barely 4.5% of French vocabulary comes from English
OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) le français est menacé par l'anglais"Menacé par l'anglais" = threatened by English. Walter's ENTIRE argument is that French influenced English FAR MORE than the reverse. Her thesis is that the perceived "threat" is exaggerated. She doesn't say French is threatened — she says French already WON the vocabulary war.❌ ELIMINATED
B) peu de mots anglais viennent de la langue française"Peu de mots anglais viennent du français" = few English words come from French. The text says the OPPOSITE: "plus d'un terme sur deux" = MORE THAN HALF. More than half ≠ few. This directly contradicts Walter.❌ ELIMINATED
C) une majorité de mots français sont d'origine anglaise"Majorité de mots français sont d'origine anglaise" = most French words come from English. The text says "2,500 à peine" out of 55,000 = about 4.5%. That's a TINY fraction, not a majority.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: D

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Fraction-to-proportion translation:

TEXT:     "plus d'un terme SUR DEUX"
          = more than one OUT OF two
          = more than 1/2
          = more than 50%

OPTION D: "la MOITIÉ du vocabulaire"
          = HALF of the vocabulary
          = 50%

"un sur deux" = "la moitié" → SYNONYM
  (one in two = half)

Strictly, "PLUS d'un sur deux" = MORE than half
"La moitié" = exactly half

But "la moitié" is the CLOSEST match available.
The slight imprecision (">50%" simplified to "50%")
is acceptable when all other options are clearly wrong.

This is another application of the "closest match" principle from Chapter 7.

Skill 2 — Detecting the "reversed statistic" trap:

Options B and C each take ONE of Walter's statistics and REVERSE it:

REVERSAL TRAP ANALYSIS:

OPTION B: "peu de mots ANGLAIS viennent du FRANÇAIS"
  TEXT: MORE THAN HALF of English words come from French
  → B reverses "more than half" to "few" → OPPOSITE

OPTION C: "majorité de mots FRANÇAIS sont d'origine ANGLAISE"
  TEXT: Only 2,500 out of 55,000 French words come from English
  → C reverses the tiny fraction into a "majority" → OPPOSITE

Both options use the CORRECT language pair (English/French)
but REVERSE the proportion (many→few, few→majority).

This is a DOUBLE reversal trap:
  B reverses the English statistic (many→few)
  C reverses the French statistic (few→many)
  D correctly states the English statistic (half)

Skill 3 — Distinguishing Walter's THESIS from a common assumption:

Option A says "le français est menacé par l'anglais" (French is threatened by English). This is a COMMON ASSUMPTION that Walter is explicitly ARGUING AGAINST:

COMMON ASSUMPTION (what most people think):
  "English is invading French! French is threatened!"

WALTER'S COUNTER-THESIS:
  "Actually, French invaded English first, and much more extensively.
   English should be the one complaining, not French."
  "Il faut rétablir la vérité!" (We must set the record straight!)

Option A restates the COMMON ASSUMPTION.
Walter's interview REFUTES this assumption.
Picking Option A means siding with what Walter CONTRADICTS.

This introduces a new trap type for interview texts:

TRAP TYPE: "COMMON ASSUMPTION vs. EXPERT'S COUNTER-THESIS"

In interviews, experts often challenge common beliefs.
Wrong options may restate the COMMON BELIEF.
Correct options reflect the EXPERT'S ACTUAL CLAIM.

COMMON BELIEF: French is threatened by English
EXPERT CLAIM: French influenced English more (>50% of English vocabulary)

Option A = common belief → WRONG
Option D = expert's claim → CORRECT

Skill 1 — Chronological precision: "commencé" = FIRST item:

"Les Français ont COMMENCÉ à s'intéresser..."

Skill 2 — Eliminating chronologically later items:

  A: sport → 19th century (3rd in sequence)
  B: IT → modern era (5th in sequence)
  C: fashion → later (4th in sequence)

Only D describes the FIRST influence:
  D: political system → 18th century (1st in sequence)

Skill 3 — Synonym matching "régime parlementaire" = "système politique":

TEXT:     "régime parlementaire britannique"

  Both describe: the form of government/political organization

  "Système" is more general (political system)
  General encompasses specific → acceptable match

  Both mean: British / in Great Britain

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Common Assumption" Trap (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: An expert interview challenges a widely held belief. An option restates that belief. Students pick it because it "sounds right" — it matches what they already believe.

THE MECHANISM:

COMMON BELIEF: "English is invading French. French is under threat."
  → Most people think this
  → It's discussed in media frequently
  → Option A says exactly this: "le français est menacé par l'anglais"

EXPERT'S COUNTER-THESIS:
  → French influenced English FAR more (>50% of English vocabulary)
  → Only 4.5% of French words come from English
  → "Les Anglais auraient vraiment des raisons de se plaindre!"
  → "Il faut rétablir la vérité!"

The expert EXPLICITLY contradicts the common belief.
Picking the common belief = ignoring the expert's argument.

Defense: In expert interviews, ask: "Is this option what the EXPERT says, or what EVERYONE ELSE thinks?" The expert's counter-thesis is almost always the correct answer.

Pitfall #2: The "Reversed Statistic" Trap

What happens: The text provides precise statistics about two languages. Options reverse which statistic applies to which language.

TEXT:
  English: >50% of words from French (many)
  French: ~4.5% of words from English (few)

OPTION B: "few English words come from French" → REVERSES "many" to "few"
OPTION C: "majority of French words from English" → REVERSES "few" to "majority"
OPTION D: "half of English vocabulary is French origin" → CORRECT

The trap swaps the proportions:
  Many (text) → Few (option) for English
  Few (text) → Many (option) for French

Both options use REAL elements from the text
but SWAP the proportions between languages.

Defense: When the text gives statistics about TWO things, create a simple table:

LANGUAGE    | PROPORTION FROM THE OTHER
English     | >50% from French (MANY)
French      | ~4.5% from English (FEW)

Then check each option against this table.

Pitfall #3: The "Chronological Later Item" Trap

What happens: The question asks about the FIRST reason. The text lists a sequence. An option describes a LATER item in the sequence. Students pick it because it's also mentioned in the text.

SEQUENCE: politics → revolution → sport → fashion → IT

"Commencé" (began) → FIRST item → politics

Options:
  A: sport (3rd) ← later item
  B: IT (5th) ← later item
  C: fashion (4th) ← later item
  D: politics (1st) ← CORRECT

All four ARE in the text. Only one is FIRST.

Defense: When the question uses "commencé," "d'abord," or "initialement," ONLY the FIRST item in the sequence is correct. Eliminate everything that came later.

Pitfall #4: The "Scale Confusion" Trap

What happens: The argument operates at one SCALE (global). An option operates at a different scale (national). Students confuse the two.

WALTER'S ARGUMENT:
  Scale: GLOBAL ("expansion mondiale," "dans le monde")
  Claim: Jeanne d'Arc prevented French from becoming the WORLD language

OPTION B:
  Scale: NATIONAL ("en France")
  Claim: Jeanne d'Arc caused the weak position of English IN FRANCE

GLOBAL ≠ NATIONAL
  "dans le monde" (worldwide) ≠ "en France" (in France)

The argument is about WORLD language dominance,
not about one language's presence in one country.

Defense: Check the SCALE in both the text and the option. Are they talking about the same geographic scope?

Bridge to Chapter 14:

Chapters 11-13 dealt with traditional text formats — articles, literary features, interviews. All had CONTINUOUS prose where you read from beginning to end.

Chapter 14 introduces a COMPLETELY NEW FORMAT: the texte à trous (gap-fill text). In "Et les OGM?" (Texte 33), you'll encounter a text with GAPS where words or phrases have been removed. For each gap, you must select the correct option from four choices.

This is a fundamentally different skill set:

TRADITIONAL TEXT:  Read → Understand → Answer questions ABOUT the text
TEXTE À TROUS:    Read → Identify what's MISSING → Select the right piece

The challenge shifts from COMPREHENSION to CONSTRUCTION:
  You must understand the text's LOGIC well enough
  to REBUILD it from fragments.

You'll need to master:

  1. Logical connectors — "même si," "grâce à," "malgré," "à cause de" — which connector fits the logical relationship?
  2. Coherence and cohesion — Does the inserted phrase make the text flow naturally?
  3. Concession vs. cause vs. consequence — Understanding logical relationships between clauses

This is the most significant FORMAT change in the entire guide. Welcome to the texte à trous.
