Les revenants — Réalisé par Robin Campillo

Les morts vivants de Robin Campillo ne surgissent pas la nuit mais par un bel après-midi d'été, quittant tranquillement leur cimetière en un cortège pédestre à dominante beige et pastel. Ils ont l'âge qu'ils avaient à leur mort mais sans les stigmates d'une maladie ou d'un accident. La vision presque radieuse d'un phénomène pour le moins effrayant est l'une des bonnes idées de ce film : l'image possède la clarté supposée rassurante des pubs télé du matin valorisant le troisième âge, mais la lenteur extrême des mouvements, la fixité des regards distillent à dose infime une part de grotesque et un profond malaise. Nombre de séquences montrent d'ailleurs les mesures concrètes de la municipalité pour répondre rationnellement à l'événement : accueil, hébergement, suivi et réinsertion professionnelle des ex-morts. Mais dans l'intimité des foyers, c'est l'hébétude face au prodige que constitue la présence d'un être dont on avait fait le deuil. Ainsi de l'épouse du maire, d'un enfant unique dont les parents avaient vidé la chambre ou d'un accidenté de la route à nouveau dans le lit de son ex-compagne. Le film fait songer au premier épisode d'un feuilleton expérimental que l'auteur aurait tourné avant d'écrire les suivants. Plusieurs hypothèses prometteuses sont développées. Il y a le thriller. Il y a la rêverie plastique et feutrée, parfois envoûtante, du metteur en scène autour de ses créatures, de leur déplacement, de leurs accents. Il y a aussi la métaphore politique du réfugié, de l'indésirable et il n'est pas non plus interdit de sourire, avec le cinéaste plutôt que sur son dos, de la malveillance de certains revenants. Dommage qu'aucune de ces directions, également intéressantes, ne s'impose. Que faire des revenants, population insaisissable cette belle chimère de cinéma ? Le casse-tête semble s'être imposé de façon aussi aiguë au cinéaste qu'à ses mortels circonspects.

🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

Welcome to the Final Chapter of Phase 3

This chapter introduces the film review — a text genre you haven't encountered yet. Film reviews are unique because they operate on THREE simultaneous levels:

LEVEL 1: NARRATIVE    → What happens in the film (the story)
LEVEL 2: AESTHETIC    → How the film looks and feels (the craft)
LEVEL 3: EVALUATION   → What the critic thinks about it (the judgment)

Each question may target a different level. Confusing levels — answering a story question with a judgment answer, or vice versa — leads to errors.

NEW CONCEPT 1: Genre Identification from Description

The text never SAYS "this is a fantasy film." Instead, it DESCRIBES what happens, and you must INFER the genre from the description. This requires understanding what defines each genre:

HORROR:        Designed to FRIGHTEN. Monsters, violence, terror, darkness.
               Key markers: fear, blood, screaming, darkness, threat.

DRAMA:         Realistic stories about human emotions and relationships.
               Key markers: no supernatural elements, everyday settings.

FANTASTIQUE:   Supernatural events in an otherwise normal world.
               Key markers: impossible events (dead returning), 
               realistic setting, no scientific explanation.

SCIENCE-FICTION: Future technology, space, scientific explanations.
                 Key markers: technology, scientific concepts, future setting.

The film shows DEAD PEOPLE COMING BACK TO LIFE in a NORMAL TOWN. There's no technology explaining it (not sci-fi), no terror or horror (not horror), and the supernatural element disqualifies it as pure drama. It's fantastique — the supernatural invading the ordinary.

NEW CONCEPT 2: Metaphor and Allegory in Film Criticism

The critic writes:

"Il y a aussi la métaphore politique du réfugié, de l'indésirable"

This means: the returning dead REPRESENT refugees and unwanted people. The film isn't LITERALLY about immigration — it uses the supernatural as a METAPHOR for a real social issue.

Understanding that a critic reads films on both literal AND figurative levels is essential for answering evaluation questions.

NEW CONCEPT 3: The Mixed Verdict

Most student-level texts present clear opinions: this is good, or this is bad. Film reviews are different — they often present mixed verdicts:

POSITIVE: "bonnes idées," "prometteuses," "envoûtante," "belle chimère"
NEGATIVE: "Dommage qu'aucune de ces directions ne s'impose," "casse-tête"

MIXED VERDICT: The film has interesting ideas but fails to commit to one.
               It's promising but unresolved. Good parts, weak whole.

Recognizing a mixed verdict prevents you from choosing answers that are either entirely positive OR entirely negative.

QUESTION 1: Le film de Robin Campillo appartient à quel genre ?

The genre identification method:

Let's test the film against each genre's defining characteristics:

Test for HORROR (A):

Horror requires: fear, terror, threat, darkness, violence, monsters

Film shows:
  ✗ Dead come back during a "bel après-midi d'été" (beautiful summer afternoon)
  ✗ They leave the cemetery "tranquillement" (calmly)
  ✗ Colors are "beige et pastel" (soft, gentle)
  ✗ No violence, no threat described
  ✗ Vision is "presque radieuse" (almost radiant)
  ✗ They undergo "réinsertion professionnelle" (job reintegration!)

VERDICT: This is the OPPOSITE of horror. Dead people returning peacefully
         in pastel colors and getting jobs is anti-horror.

Test for DRAMA (B):

Drama requires: realistic events, no supernatural elements, human emotions

Film shows:
  ✗ DEAD PEOPLE COME BACK TO LIFE → supernatural event
  ✗ This is impossible in reality → not realistic
  ✓ Human emotions ARE present (hébétude, malaise)
  ✓ Realistic setting (town, municipality, homes)

VERDICT: The supernatural element DISQUALIFIES pure drama.
         Drama deals with possible events. Dead returning is impossible.

Test for FANTASTIQUE (C):

Fantastique requires: supernatural event in an otherwise normal world,
                      no scientific/technological explanation

Film shows:
  ✓ DEAD PEOPLE RETURN → supernatural event
  ✓ Normal town setting (municipality, homes, jobs)
  ✓ No scientific explanation given
  ✓ Realistic reactions to impossible events (bureaucratic management)
  ✓ Mix of ordinary and extraordinary

VERDICT: PERFECT MATCH. Supernatural event (dead returning) disrupts
         an ordinary world. No explanation. Classic fantastique.

Test for SCIENCE-FICTION (D):

Science-fiction requires: technology, scientific concepts, future setting,
                          scientific explanation for events

Film shows:
  ✗ No technology mentioned
  ✗ No scientific explanation for the dead returning
  ✗ No future setting — contemporary town
  ✗ No robots, aliens, space, or advanced science

VERDICT: Nothing science-fictional in this film.

🔬 THE GENRE IDENTIFICATION METHOD

When a text describes a film without labeling its genre, use this elimination framework:

STEP 1: Is there a SUPERNATURAL element? (dead returning, magic, ghosts)
         YES → eliminates DRAMA (realistic only)
         NO → could be drama

STEP 2: Is there a SCIENTIFIC/TECHNOLOGICAL explanation?
         YES → SCIENCE-FICTION
         NO → not sci-fi

STEP 3: Is the TONE frightening, dark, violent?
         YES → HORROR
         NO → not horror

STEP 4: Does the supernatural invade an otherwise NORMAL world?
         YES → FANTASTIQUE
         NO → check other genres

For this film:
  ✅ Supernatural (dead return) → not drama
  ❌ No science → not sci-fi
  ❌ Not frightening (pastel, calm) → not horror
  ✅ Normal world disrupted → FANTASTIQUE ✅

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #29: To identify a film's genre from a review, test the described events against each genre's DEFINING characteristics. Supernatural without science = fantastique. Supernatural with terror = horror. Supernatural with technology = sci-fi. No supernatural = drama.

This question contains one of the subtlest traps in this entire guide. BOTH "malaise" and "hébétude" appear in the text. But they describe DIFFERENT people's reactions:

│  RELATIVES/PROCHES│  "l'HÉBÉTUDE face au prodige"        │

│   the film)       │  loved one alive again               │

The question asks about "proches" (relatives) → the answer is "hébétude" → which maps to "stupéfaction" (C).

If the question had asked "What feeling does the film create in the viewer?" → the answer would have been "malaise" (D).

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #30: In film reviews, emotions exist on TWO levels: emotions felt BY CHARACTERS within the film, and emotions felt BY SPECTATORS watching the film. When a question specifies WHO feels the emotion ("leurs proches" = the characters), match the emotion to the correct level. Don't confuse the spectator's aesthetic experience with the character's narrative experience.

🚨 NEW CONCEPT: The "Dommage" Pivot in Reviews. In film/book/art criticism, the word "dommage" (a pity / unfortunately) marks the TURN from positive to negative. Everything before "dommage" acknowledges qualities. Everything after reveals the critic's main complaint. The main complaint is the CORE of the evaluation.

The answer must reflect the CORE criticism (after "dommage"), not just the positive acknowledgments (before "dommage"):

CORE CRITICISM: "aucune de ces directions ne s'impose"
                = no guiding direction dominates

PARAPHRASED AS: "n'a pas su choisir une idée-phare pour guider l'intrigue"
                = couldn't choose a guiding idea for the plot

This is a Type 2 paraphrase (full phrase replacement):

"aucune de ces directions"      → "une idée-phare" (negated: pas su choisir)
"ne s'impose"                   → "pour guider l'intrigue"
"dommage"                       → "n'a pas su" (implies regret/failure)
"également intéressantes"       → [acknowledged but not the focus]

🎯 WHAT COULD BE DONE

The "Three-Level Film Review" Reading Method

When reading a film review, track THREE levels:

LEVEL 1: NARRATIVE (what happens)
  → Characters, events, plot, setting
  → Answers "what is the story about?" questions

LEVEL 2: AESTHETIC (how it looks/feels)
  → Visual style, rhythm, tone, atmosphere
  → Answers "what genre?" and "what feeling?" questions

LEVEL 3: EVALUATION (what the critic thinks)
  → Praise, criticism, mixed verdict
  → Answers "what does the critic say?" questions

EACH QUESTION targets a SPECIFIC level.
Don't answer a Level 1 question with Level 3 information.

The "Mixed Verdict" Detection Method

For film criticism, look for these structural markers:

POSITIVE ZONE markers:
  "bonnes idées," "prometteuses," "intéressant," "envoûtante"

PIVOT markers:
  "dommage que," "malheureusement," "cependant," "toutefois,"
  "le problème est que," "en revanche"

NEGATIVE ZONE markers:
  "ne s'impose pas," "casse-tête," "manque de," "échoue à"

MIXED VERDICT = positive markers BEFORE pivot + negative markers AFTER

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For genre identification questions:

  1. Look for supernatural elements — dead returning, magic, impossible events
  2. Check the tone — is it frightening (horror) or unsettling but not terrifying (fantastique)?
  3. Check for technology — science-fiction requires scientific/technological elements
  4. Use the elimination flowchart — test each genre's criteria systematically

For "best summary" questions:

  1. Extract the MAIN plot events from the narrative level
  2. Apply the three criteria — accuracy, completeness, tone (from Chapter 4)
  3. Reject summaries with invented events — "founding a new city" never happens
  4. Reject summaries with wrong tone — "revolt" and "farce" don't match the text's calm, bewildered atmosphere

For emotion questions in film reviews:

  1. Identify WHO feels the emotion — characters or spectators?
  2. Match the emotion to the correct person/level
  3. Don't confuse aesthetic effects (spectator) with narrative emotions (characters)

For critical evaluation questions:

  1. Find the PIVOT word — "dommage," "malheureusement," "cependant"
  2. The CORE criticism comes AFTER the pivot
  3. The positive acknowledgments come BEFORE the pivot
  4. The answer should reflect the CORE criticism, not just the positive parts

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #61: The "Genre by Association" Trap 🧟

"Morts vivants" (undead/zombies) → Student thinks → HORROR

But genre is determined by TREATMENT, not by subject matter alone. Dead people CAN appear in:

  • Horror (terrifying zombies in the dark)
  • Fantasy (peaceful returns in daylight)
  • Comedy (funny ghost stories)
  • Drama (grief and mourning themes)

The TREATMENT here is calm, pastel, bureaucratic — the opposite of horror.

How to avoid it: Don't classify by SUBJECT (dead people = horror). Classify by TREATMENT (how the subject is presented). Peaceful, unexplained supernatural = fantastique.

PITFALL #62: The "Two Emotions, Wrong Assignment" Trap 🎭

TEXT mentions TWO emotions:
  "malaise"    → felt by SPECTATORS (aesthetic level)
  "hébétude"   → felt by RELATIVES (narrative level)

Q3 asks about RELATIVES ("proches")
  → Correct: hébétude → stupéfaction (C)
  → TRAP: malaise (D) — right text, wrong target

Both words are in the text. Both describe real emotions. But they apply to DIFFERENT people. The question specifies "proches" → only "hébétude" applies.

How to avoid it: When a film review mentions multiple emotions, TAG each one with its RECIPIENT: who feels this? Spectators? Characters? Critics? Then match the question's specified recipient.

PITFALL #63: The "Promising = Successful" Confusion ⭐

TEXT says: "Plusieurs hypothèses PROMETTEUSES" (promising)
          + "DOMMAGE qu'aucune ne s'impose" (but none dominates)

Student thinks: "Promising" means "good" → the film is good → choose positive answer

"Promising" means it has POTENTIAL — not that it DELIVERS. The critic says the film promises much but doesn't fulfill that promise. "Promising but unresolved" is a CRITICISM wrapped in a compliment.

How to avoid it: In reviews, "promising" followed by "dommage" or "mais" is a MIXED verdict. Don't read "promising" as "successful." Read the FULL sentence: "promising BUT none dominates" = potential unfulfilled.

PITFALL #64: The "Casse-tête = Resolution" Inversion 🧩

TEXT:    "Le CASSE-TÊTE semble s'être imposé de façon aussi aiguë 
         au cinéaste qu'à ses mortels circumspects"
         = The PUZZLE defeated the filmmaker as much as his characters

TRAP D:  "le problème a été SUBTILEMENT RÉSOLU"
         = the problem was subtly resolved

"Casse-tête" (puzzle/headache) + "s'être imposé au cinéaste" (imposed itself on the filmmaker) = the puzzle DEFEATED the filmmaker. He couldn't solve it. This is the OPPOSITE of "subtly resolved."

TEXT:    Problem → DEFEATS filmmaker (unresolved)
TRAP D:  Problem → RESOLVED by filmmaker (success)
→ COMPLETE INVERSION

How to avoid it: "Casse-tête" is ALWAYS negative — it means a problem that's DIFFICULT or UNSOLVABLE. If the text says something is a "casse-tête" for the filmmaker, it means the filmmaker STRUGGLED with it, not that they elegantly solved it.
