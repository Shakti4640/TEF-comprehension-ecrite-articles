📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le nouveau sport à la mode en Europe ? Grimper aux arbres... généalogiques. Anglais et Français y sont particulièrement accros. En témoignent la multiplication de sites Web dédiés à cette quête identitaire ou encore l'affluence record, 15 000 visiteurs, de la 4e Biennale de généalogie, en décembre à Paris. Des deux côtés de la Manche, ce boom n'a pas échappé aux producteurs, prompts à avaler les tendances pour les régurgiter sous forme de concepts télé. Comme souvent, la BBC a ouvert le feu, aidée par des people. L'émission « Who Do You Think You Are ? » (« Qui pensez-vous être ? »), l'automne dernier, mettait en scène des personnalités lancées sur les traces de leurs ancêtres. Au final, de bonnes audiences, qui ont donné des idées aux chaînes françaises. Gérard Louvin reprend donc le format, rebaptisé « Tous cousins » et attendu fin juin sur France 2. « C'est l'occasion de redécouvrir les habitués des plateaux, qui ont déjà tout raconté chez Drucker », commente le producteur. De son côté, Jean-Pierre Pernaut cogiterait sur un pilote d'émission spéciale titrée « Comme un air de famille ». Il devrait en assurer la présentation avant l'été. La généalogie, alibi pour caser des célébrités en promo ? Certes, mais pas seulement. Les chaînes ont aussi senti venir le vent de la « psychogénéalogie », nouvelle thérapie fondée sur l'exploration de l'histoire familiale. De quoi faire frétiller le petit écran, friand de récits à valeur émotionnelle ajoutée. Le 13 mars, « Zone interdite » a déjà décrypté cet épiphénomène. Sur la BBC4, la série documentaire « Family Ties », (« Les liens familiaux »), en octobre, a suivi des anonymes dans leur enquête transgénérationnelle. Et dans leur découverte de secrets enfouis : frères et sœurs cachés, oncle sanguinaire, bébé assassiné. En Allemagne on va même jusqu'à placer devant les caméras des hommes et des femmes qui recourent au test ADN pour s'assurer des origines de leurs enfants. Mais le pire est à voir avec une émission américaine « qui est ton papa ». Une jeune femme adoptée à la naissance doit reconnaître son géniteur parmi huit candidats. Si elle désigne le bon elle repart avec son paternel et gagne 100 000 dollars. On finit par se demander si la bonne question n'est pas « qui est ton papa ? » mais « ça intéresse qui ? »

🚨 WHY THIS TEXT DEMANDS A NEW SKILL SET

In Project 19, you navigated a LONG text with a single CHRONOLOGICAL thread (history of alpinism). Time moved forward in one direction.

Project 20 introduces THREE simultaneous challenges:

Challenge 1: GEOGRAPHICAL MULTIPLICATION

The text discusses FOUR countries doing the same thing differently:

COUNTRY MAP:
UK ──────── BBC: "Who Do You Think You Are?" (celebrities)
France ──── France 2: "Tous cousins" / Pernaut: "Comme un air de famille"
Germany ─── DNA tests on camera
USA ──────── "Qui est ton papa?" (reality show)

If you confuse which country does what, you fail Q19, Q20, and Q21.

Challenge 2: ESCALATION STRUCTURE

The text is NOT neutral. It builds from harmless to horrifying, and the author's tone shifts accordingly:

ESCALATION LADDER:
Level 1: Genealogy as hobby (websites, biennale) ──── NEUTRAL ✓
Level 2: Celebrity genealogy on TV (UK, France) ──── MILDLY IRONIC
Level 3: Family secrets revealed (BBC4 documentary) ── UNCOMFORTABLE
Level 4: DNA paternity tests on camera (Germany) ──── DISTURBING
Level 5: Guess-your-daddy game show (USA) ─────────── OUTRAGEOUS ✗

The author uses signal words at each escalation step. Detecting these is critical.

Challenge 3: IRONIC CLOSURE

The text ends with:

"On finit par se demander si la bonne question n'est pas « qui est ton papa ? » mais « ça intéresse qui ? »"

This rhetorical question IS the author's verdict. It means: "At this point, who even cares?" — expressing disgust disguised as a question. Understanding this irony is essential for Q21.

QUESTION 18: Français et Anglais...

The target zone:

"Le nouveau sport à la mode en Europe ? Grimper aux arbres... généalogiques. Anglais et Français y sont particulièrement accros. En témoignent la multiplication de sites Web dédiés à cette quête identitaire ou encore l'affluence record, 15 000 visiteurs, de la 4e Biennale de généalogie"

The text's message: French and English people are RECENTLY and INTENSELY enthusiastic about genealogy.

Now let's test:

OptionAnalysisVerdict

🎯 Answer: D

THE CRITICAL LESSON — Metaphor vs. Literal Reading

Option B falls into a trap that becomes increasingly dangerous in Phase 4:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #15: In journalistic texts, metaphors are NEVER meant literally. "Grimper aux arbres généalogiques" means "doing genealogy research," NOT "building family trees." When an answer takes a metaphor literally, it is ALWAYS wrong.

The metaphor detection checklist:

Clue that it's a metaphorExample in this text
The image seems absurd if taken literally"Climbing genealogical trees" — you don't physically climb paper trees
It's in the opening sentenceJournalists use catchy metaphors to HOOK readers
It's immediately followed by an explanation"Anglais et Français y sont particulièrement accros" explains the metaphor

BONUS LESSON — The "Communes" Trap (Option C)

Option C is the most dangerous wrong answer here. Let's examine why:

What the text saysWhat Option C adds
French AND English are interested in genealogyFrench and English search for their common roots
Each nation does its own thingThey're doing it TOGETHER
Parallel interestSHARED interest

The word "communes" transforms the meaning entirely. The text describes two countries with the SAME hobby, not two countries doing the hobby TOGETHER. This is a Subtle Addition Trap — one word added that changes everything.

This connects directly to Golden Rule #1 (Project 1): watch for words that AREN'T in the text.

THE CRITICAL LESSON — Direction Reversal at Phase 4

Option A is a Phase 4 direction reversal trap — the most sophisticated version of this trap type:

What the text saysWhat Option A claims
BBC created → France copiedBritish producers copied a French concept
Direction: UK → FranceDirection: France → UK

This is NOT a simple lie. It uses the CORRECT verb ("repris" = copied) and the CORRECT concept (one country copying another). It just reverses WHO copied WHOM.

How to detect direction reversals:

Step 1: Identify the ACTORS (BBC = British, Louvin = French)
Step 2: Identify the ACTION DIRECTION (who did it first?)
Step 3: Check: does the answer preserve or reverse the direction?

PHASE 4 AWARENESS: At this level, traps don't just use wrong words — they use the RIGHT words in the WRONG direction.

THE CRITICAL LESSON — Tense and Modality Traps (NEW — Phase 4)

This question introduces TWO new trap mechanisms that are devastating at this level:

TRAP TYPE A: Tense Manipulation

What the text saysWhat the option claims
"Family Ties" a suivi (past — already happened)sera proposée (future — hasn't happened yet)
Reality: DONEClaim: NOT YET DONE

TRAP TYPE B: Modality Manipulation

What the text saysWhat the option claims
"Tous cousins" IS on France 2"aurait pu faire l'objet" (COULD HAVE been a show)
Reality: EXISTSClaim: HYPOTHETICAL

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #16: The exam manipulates VERB TENSES and MODALITIES to turn real events into hypothetical ones (or vice versa). Always check: does the text present this as DONE, HAPPENING, or HYPOTHETICAL? Does the answer match?

The Tense-Modality Verification Table:

Text verb formMeaningMatching answer verbs
Passé composé ("a suivi")Completed past"a eu lieu," "s'est produit"
Présent ("reprend")Currently happening"fait," "est," "concerne"
Futur ("devrait")Planned future"sera," "va"
Conditionnel ("aurait pu")Hypothetical"pourrait," "serait susceptible"

If the text uses passé composé but the answer uses conditionnel → MISMATCH → WRONG.

THE CRITICAL LESSON — The Escalation Attribution Trap

Option C is the signature trap of this text and introduces a critical Phase 4 skill:

The text builds an ESCALATION:

UK (harmless) → France (mild) → UK/BBC4 (uncomfortable) → Germany (disturbing) → USA (THE WORST)

The author EXPLICITLY marks the escalation:

  • Germany: "on va même jusqu'à" (they go even as far as)
  • USA: "le pire est à voir" (the WORST is)

Option C says Germany inspires "le pire." But the text CLEARLY assigns "le pire" to the USA.

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #17: In texts with escalation across multiple entities (countries, people, events), the exam will MISATTRIBUTE the peak. The worst/best/first/last is assigned to the WRONG entity. Always verify: WHO does the text say is at the extreme?

The Escalation Verification Method:

Step 1: Identify the escalation markers
         → "même" = even (escalation step)
         → "le pire" = the worst (peak)
         → "le plus" = the most (superlative)

Step 2: Note which ENTITY each marker is attached to
         → "même" → Germany
         → "le pire" → USA

Step 3: When an answer attributes a peak to an entity, CHECK
         → Does the text assign that peak to THAT entity?
         → Option C assigns "le pire" to Germany → TEXT says "le pire" = USA → WRONG

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 13: The Escalation Misattribution (NEW — Phase 4)

What it is: The text ranks entities on a scale (harmless → worst). The exam assigns the extreme position to the WRONG entity.

In this text:

  • Text: "le pire est à voir avec une émission américaine"
  • Option C (Q21): "inspire le pire des programmes télévisuels allemands"
  • The word "pire" is CORRECT but attached to the WRONG country

Detection method: When you see a superlative in an answer ("le pire," "le meilleur," "le premier"), immediately ask: "Which entity does the TEXT assign this superlative to?"

PITFALL 14: The Tense-Modality Swap (NEW — Phase 4)

What it is: The answer changes the verb tense or modality, turning a real past event into a future possibility, or a confirmed fact into a hypothesis.

In this text (two instances):

InstanceText saysAnswer claimsThe swap
Q20, Option B"Family Ties" a suivi (PAST)sera proposée (FUTURE)Past → Future
Q20, Option D"Tous cousins" on France 2 (REAL)aurait pu faire l'objet (HYPOTHETICAL)Real → Hypothetical

Detection method: Circle the VERB in the answer option. Ask: "Does this tense/modality match what the text actually says?"

PITFALL 15: The Subtle Addition (Reinforced from Q18)

What it is: The answer adds ONE word that changes the entire meaning, and that word is NOT in the text.

In this text:

  • Text: French AND English are interested in genealogy (parallel interest)
  • Option C (Q18): searching for their common roots ("communes" = shared)

"Communes" is the assassin word. Without it, the option would be arguably correct. WITH it, it claims the French and English are searching for SHARED roots — which the text never says.

Detection method: When an answer seems almost perfect, identify the ONE word that might not be in the text. Ask: "Does the text actually say THIS specific word or concept?"
