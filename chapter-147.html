Nuisance sonore : Si, à force d'entendre chaque jour les mêmes bruits

Si, à force d'entendre chaque jour les mêmes bruits, on peut finir par ne plus y prêter attention, notre organisme, lui, ne s'y habitue pas et il y réagit silencieusement.

Pétarade des deux-roues, circulation intense, tapage nocturne des voisins, marteau-piqueur dans la rue, le bruit est omniprésent dans notre quotidien.

En France, 12 % de la population vit dans des zones ou l'intensité de bruit est excessive, notamment aux abords d'aéroports et d'aérodromes, en bordures de voies ferrées ou de routes à fort Trafic. Démunis face à ces agressions sonores, nombreux sont ceux qui n'hésitent plus à porter plainte. D'autant que ces nuisances se répercutent insidieusement sur la santé : troubles du sommeil, augmentation du rythme cardiaque et de la tension artérielle, perte de concentration et de mémorisation, fatigue physique et nerveuse sont autant d'effets néfastes essentiellement attribués au bruit. L'écoute de musique amplifiée lors de concerts ou dans des discothèques n'est pas non plus étrangère à ces effets secondaires. Or, curieusement, aucune mise en garde n'informe les usagers des risques qu'ils courent. Les chiffres du secrétariat d'Etat à la santé parlent pourtant d'eux-mêmes : 30 000 à 50 000 jeunes présentent déjà des altérations graves du système auditif. « La presbyacousie, une perte de l'audition due au vieillissement, commence habituellement après 70 ans, mais dans les générations futures, on deviendra un « vieux auditif » dès 40 ans », prévient un spécialiste. On l'aura compris : la protection est de mise ! Les pouvoirs publics l'ont confirmé en promulguant une loi restreignant les nuisances sonores dues à la construction et à l'aménagement de routes et de voies ferrées nouvelles à proximité d'habitations. Mais cela ne suffit pas et des initiatives fleurissent çà et là pour limiter les dégâts.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

Why This Text Is the Culmination of Phase 3

In Chapter 16 (Brocantes), you learned to navigate a cultural phenomenon through multiple stakeholder lenses. That text was warm, poetic, celebratory — a phenomenon everyone enjoyed, with mild tensions between groups.

This text is completely different in nature. It's a problem-analysis article — a text that:

  1. Identifies a PROBLEM (noise pollution)
  2. Describes its CONSEQUENCES (health effects)
  3. Shows the VICTIMS' RESPONSE (legal action)
  4. Introduces EXPERT EVIDENCE (specialist quote)
  5. Describes the INSTITUTIONAL RESPONSE (new law)
  6. Delivers a VERDICT (insufficient — more needed)

This is the first text where you must simultaneously track three different knowledge domains woven into a single argument:

DOMAIN 1: HEALTH
├── Sleep disorders
├── Cardiac acceleration
├── Blood pressure increase
├── Concentration/memory loss
├── Physical/nervous fatigue
└── Hearing degradation (30,000-50,000 young people)

DOMAIN 2: LAW
├── Citizens filing complaints ("porter plainte")
├── Government law restricting construction noise
└── Law targets roads and railways near housing

DOMAIN 3: SOCIETY
├── 12% of population in excessive noise zones

├── Gap between legal action and actual needs

Each question will target a specific domain or intersection — your job is to identify which one and respond accordingly.

The Opening Paradox — Your Interpretive Key

"Si, à force d'entendre chaque jour les mêmes bruits, on peut finir par ne plus y prêter attention, notre organisme, lui, ne s'y habitue pas et il y réagit silencieusement."

This opening sentence is the thesis of the entire article. It establishes:

  • Surface illusion: We THINK we get used to noise (psychological adaptation)
  • Hidden reality: Our BODY never adapts (physiological damage)
  • Core message: Noise is dangerous BECAUSE we stop noticing it

Everything that follows — the health effects, the statistics, the expert warning, the insufficient laws — all flow from this paradox. If you understand this opening, you understand the article.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 28: Quel titre convient le mieux à cet article ?

This is a "best title" question — the most demanding global comprehension question type. You encountered simpler versions in Chapters 6 and 12. Here, all four options are plausible-sounding titles that use vocabulary from the noise/health domain. The challenge is finding the one that captures the WHOLE article, not just one section.

Step 1: Summarize the article in ONE sentence.

Before checking options, force yourself to complete this sentence:

"This article is mainly about ____________________."

My answer: "This article is mainly about the multiple harmful consequences of noise pollution, which keep cascading despite people's psychological habituation and despite insufficient institutional responses."

The key concept: CONSEQUENCES — the article is DOMINATED by listing effects of noise on health, hearing, and society.

Step 2: Apply the "Coverage Test" to each option.

For "best title" questions, the winning option must cover the MAXIMUM amount of the article's content. An option that only covers one paragraph or one aspect is too narrow.

Option A — "Nuisances sonores : conséquences en cascade"

Let's test coverage:

  • "Nuisances sonores" → covers the TOPIC (noise pollution) ✓
  • "conséquences en cascade" → covers the STRUCTURE (one consequence leading to another: noise → sleep disorders → cardiac problems → concentration loss → fatigue → hearing damage → premature aging of hearing) ✓

Does this title miss anything important? The law and the "not enough" verdict are secondary elements — the BULK of the article (approximately 70%) is about consequences. A title doesn't need to capture 100% — it needs to capture the dominant thrust.

✅ STRONG CANDIDATE

Option B — "Pollution sonore : développement des protections"

  • "Pollution sonore" → covers the TOPIC ✓
  • "développement des protections" → implies protections are GROWING/DEVELOPING

But wait — the article's verdict is the OPPOSITE: "cela ne suffit pas" (it's not enough). The law exists but is INSUFFICIENT. Initiatives are emerging but described as scattered ("çà et là"). The article's message is that protections are INADEQUATE, not that they're developing well.

This title contradicts the article's conclusion.

❌ ELIMINATED — Contradicts the article's "not enough" verdict

This is a critical trap. The article DOES mention protections (the law, local initiatives). But the article's JUDGMENT on those protections is negative — they're insufficient. A title that presents protections as "developing" gives an optimistic spin that the article explicitly rejects.

Option C — "Recherche : un nouveau budget pour l'acoustique"

  • "Recherche" → the article mentions NO research
  • "nouveau budget" → the article mentions NO budget
  • "acoustique" → the article is about noise EFFECTS, not acoustics as a science

This is entirely invented. Nothing in the text discusses research funding or acoustics budgets.

❌ ELIMINATED — Complete invention

Option D — "Régulation du bruit ambiant : de nouvelles lois à l'étude"

  • "Régulation du bruit" → partially covers the topic
  • "nouvelles lois à l'étude" → implies laws are BEING STUDIED (planned, not yet enacted)

But the text says the law has already been ENACTED: "Les pouvoirs publics l'ont confirmé en promulguant une loi" — "promulguant" means officially enacting/issuing. The law EXISTS. It's not "under study."

Furthermore, this title suggests the article is mainly about REGULATION — but regulation occupies only the last two sentences. The article is overwhelmingly about CONSEQUENCES.

❌ ELIMINATED — Misrepresents both the law's status and the article's focus

✅ ANSWER: A

The Lesson Here: The "Best Title" Decision Matrix

For "best title" questions, apply this three-step test:

TEST 1: COVERAGE — Does the title capture the DOMINANT theme?
         → If it only covers one paragraph → TOO NARROW → eliminate

TEST 2: ACCURACY — Does the title match the text's TONE and VERDICT?
         → If it gives an optimistic spin when the text is critical → INACCURATE → eliminate

TEST 3: FACTUAL TRUTH — Does the title state things the text actually says?
         → If it mentions things not in the text → INVENTED → eliminate

Option A passes all three tests. Options B, C, and D each fail at least one.

RULE (building on Chapter 12): The best title captures the article's DOMINANT theme AND its TONE. A title that covers the right topic but misrepresents the author's judgment is wrong.

QUESTION 29: Les victimes du bruit mentionnées dans cet article sont...

This question targets the SOCIETY domain — specifically, how the victims are described. The phrase "mentionnées dans cet article" reminds you: answer from the TEXT, not from general knowledge.

Step 1: Locate everything the text says about what VICTIMS DO.

"Démunis face à ces agressions sonores, nombreux sont ceux qui n'hésitent plus à porter plainte."

Key elements:

  • "Démunis" = helpless, defenseless
  • "n'hésitent plus" = no longer hesitate (implies they USED to hesitate but NOW they don't)
  • "porter plainte" = file a complaint / take legal action
  • "nombreux" = many (growing number implied by "plus")

Step 2: Check each option.

Option A — "régulièrement informées des protections existantes"

The text says the OPPOSITE: "curieusement, aucune mise en garde n'informe les usagers des risques qu'ils courent." — NO warning informs users. People are NOT informed.

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct contradiction

This is a reversal trap — the answer states the exact opposite of what the text says. The text emphasizes the ABSENCE of information; Option A claims REGULAR information exists.

Option B — "atteintes de troubles psychologiques irréversibles"

The text mentions: troubles du sommeil, cardiac acceleration, blood pressure, concentration loss, fatigue, hearing damage. These are PHYSICAL and NEUROLOGICAL effects, not specifically "troubles psychologiques." Moreover, the text never uses the word "irréversibles."

❌ ELIMINATED — Mischaracterizes the type of effects + adds "irréversible" which isn't in the text

Be careful here: concentration loss and fatigue COULD be classified as partly psychological. But the text presents them as physical/physiological consequences of noise, not as psychological disorders. And the word "irréversibles" is an invention — the text describes effects but never claims they're irreversible.

Option C — "de plus en plus nombreuses à saisir les tribunaux"

Let's map this:

  • "de plus en plus nombreuses" ↔ "nombreux... n'hésitent plus" (the "plus" implies an increasing trend — they DIDN'T USED TO file complaints, now they DO) ✓
  • "saisir les tribunaux" ↔ "porter plainte" (filing complaints = bringing matters to court) ✓

"Saisir les tribunaux" is a paraphrase of "porter plainte." The increasing trend is captured by "de plus en plus" matching the implication of "n'hésitent plus" (a change from past hesitation to present action).

✅ CORRECT

Option D — "fortement invitées à consulter des spécialistes"

The text never invites anyone to consult specialists. A specialist SPEAKS in the article, but the text doesn't advise victims to go see one. This confuses the specialist's ROLE (providing expert testimony to readers) with a medical RECOMMENDATION (advising patients).

❌ ELIMINATED — Confuses expert testimony with medical advice

✅ ANSWER: C

The Lesson Here: The "n'hésitent plus" Construction

The French construction "ne... plus" (no longer) is a hidden trend indicator. It tells you that something CHANGED over time:

BEFORE: People hesitated to file complaints
NOW:    People no longer hesitate
IMPLICATION: More and more people are taking legal action

The correct answer translates this implication into explicit language: "de plus en plus nombreuses." This is a paraphrase of an implication, not a paraphrase of a direct statement — a higher-level skill.

RULE: "Ne... plus" implies a CHANGE. The correct answer will often make this change EXPLICIT. "N'hésitent plus à porter plainte" = "de plus en plus nombreuses à saisir les tribunaux."

QUESTION 30: Le spécialiste intervenant dans cet article mentionne...

This is an expert citation interpretation question — you must isolate the specialist's SPECIFIC claim from the rest of the article.

Step 1: Locate the specialist's exact quote.

« La presbyacousie, une perte de l'audition due au vieillissement, commence habituellement après 70 ans, mais dans les générations futures, on deviendra un « vieux auditif » dès 40 ans », prévient un spécialiste.

This is a PRECISE, bounded quote. Everything between the quotation marks is the specialist's claim. Everything outside is the author's.

Step 2: Decode the specialist's claim.

The specialist says:

  • NORMALLY: hearing loss from aging (presbyacousie) starts after 70 years old
  • IN THE FUTURE: it will start at 40 years old
  • CONCLUSION: hearing will deteriorate 30 years EARLIER than normal

The key concept: PREMATURE degradation — the same condition arriving much sooner than it should.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "une stabilisation du nombre de troubles auditifs"

"Stabilisation" = leveling off, staying the same. The specialist describes the OPPOSITE — an ACCELERATION and WORSENING of the problem (from 70 to 40). Nothing is stabilizing.

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct contradiction of the trend described

Option B — "une dégradation plus précoce du système auditif"

  • "dégradation" ↔ "perte de l'audition" (hearing loss) ✓
  • "plus précoce" ↔ "dès 40 ans" instead of "après 70 ans" (earlier) ✓
  • "système auditif" ↔ "audition" / "presbyacousie" ✓

This perfectly captures the specialist's message: hearing degradation is happening EARLIER (more precociously).

✅ CORRECT

Option C — "un vieillissement important de la population atteinte"

"Vieillissement de la population atteinte" = aging of the affected population. This would mean the VICTIMS are getting OLDER. But the specialist says the OPPOSITE — the affected population is getting YOUNGER (40 instead of 70).

❌ ELIMINATED — Reverses the direction of the trend

This is a brilliantly crafted trap. The word "vieillissement" APPEARS in the specialist's quote ("perte de l'audition due au vieillissement"). But in the quote, "vieillissement" describes the CAUSE of presbyacousie (aging causes hearing loss). In Option C, "vieillissement" describes the VICTIMS (the affected population is aging). Same word, completely different application.

This is the "keyword match" trap from Chapter 1, but now operating at a much more sophisticated level — the word is not just used differently, it's applied to a different SUBJECT within the same semantic field.

Option D — "une prise de conscience accrue des jeunes générations"

"Prise de conscience" = growing awareness. The specialist says NOTHING about young people becoming more aware. He says young people will SUFFER earlier — that's a medical prediction, not a sociological observation about awareness.

❌ ELIMINATED — Confuses medical prediction with sociological awareness

✅ ANSWER: B

The Lesson Here: Isolating Expert Quotes

When a question asks about a specific person's statement, you must:

STEP 1: Find the EXACT quotation marks
STEP 2: Read ONLY what's inside them
STEP 3: Ignore everything the AUTHOR says around the quote
STEP 4: Translate the quote's core message into simple language
STEP 5: Find the option that matches YOUR simple translation

The specialist's quote, simplified: "People will lose their hearing 30 years earlier than normal."

Option B, simplified: "The hearing system will deteriorate earlier."

Perfect match.

RULE: Expert citation questions have a BOUNDED answer zone — the quotation marks. Anything outside the quotes is irrelevant. Anything inside is your ONLY evidence.

QUESTION 31: De la lecture de cet article, on peut conclure...

This is the ultimate inference question — the most demanding question type in Phase 3. "On peut conclure" means you must SYNTHESIZE the entire article into one concluding statement. You're not looking for a detail; you're looking for the article's final message.

Step 1: What is the article's FINAL message?

The last two sentences are critical:

"Les pouvoirs publics l'ont confirmé en promulguant une loi restreignant les nuisances sonores dues à la construction et à l'aménagement de routes et de voies ferrées nouvelles à proximité d'habitations. Mais cela ne suffit pas et des initiatives fleurissent çà et là pour limiter les dégâts."

The structure:

ACTION TAKEN:     A law was enacted
VERDICT:          "Mais cela ne suffit pas" = BUT it's not enough
CONSEQUENCE:      Scattered initiatives trying to compensate
OVERALL MESSAGE:  Current measures are INSUFFICIENT

Step 2: Confirm with the article's overall trajectory.

OPENING:  Noise is everywhere, body doesn't adapt
MIDDLE:   Devastating health effects, people filing complaints
          No warnings for music venue users
          30,000-50,000 young people already damaged
EXPERT:   Problem will get worse (hearing loss at 40)
END:      Law exists but "ne suffit pas"
          Local initiatives "çà et là" (here and there = scattered, incomplete)

TRAJECTORY: Problem → Consequences → Inadequate Response

Step 3: Check each option against this trajectory.

Option A — "qu'une majorité de gens vit dans un environnement trop bruyant"

The text says: "12 % de la population vit dans des zones où l'intensité de bruit est excessive."

12% is NOT a "majorité" (majority). A majority would be over 50%. This option dramatically exaggerates the statistic.

❌ ELIMINATED — 12% ≠ majority

This trap tests whether you remember the SPECIFIC number from the text. The article gives an impression of widespread noise (motorcycles, traffic, neighbors, jackhammers), which might make you FEEL like "a majority" is affected. But the text gives a precise figure: 12%. Your feeling doesn't override the data.

Option B — "que les constructions de chemins de fer et de routes sont limitées"

The text says: "une loi restreignant les nuisances sonores dues à la construction et à l'aménagement de routes et de voies ferrées nouvelles"

The law restricts the NOISE from construction — not the construction ITSELF. Option B says construction is limited. The text says construction NOISE near housing is limited. This is a crucial distinction:

TEXT:     law restricts [NOISE] from [construction of roads/railways]
OPTION B: [construction of roads/railways] is limited

The law targets NOISE, not CONSTRUCTION. Building roads and railways continues — it's just that the noise produced must be controlled near homes.

❌ ELIMINATED — Confuses the target of the law (noise) with construction itself

This is an exceptionally subtle trap. The words "construction," "routes," and "voies ferrées" all appear in both the text and Option B. The sentence structure is similar. But the OBJECT being limited is different: noise vs construction.

Option C — "que les problèmes cardiaques liés au bruit sont en augmentation"

The text mentions "augmentation du rythme cardiaque et de la tension artérielle" — cardiac acceleration and blood pressure increase. These are SYMPTOMS caused by noise. But the text does NOT say these problems are "en augmentation" (increasing over time). It says noise CAUSES these symptoms — it doesn't say the number of cardiac problems is GROWING.

TEXT:     Noise causes → increased heart rate (a medical effect)
OPTION C: Cardiac problems are increasing (a statistical trend)

A medical EFFECT (noise makes your heart beat faster) is not the same as an epidemiological TREND (more people having heart problems over time).

❌ ELIMINATED — Confuses a medical symptom with an epidemiological trend

Option D — "que les efforts pour réduire le bruit restent en deçà des besoins"

Let's map this:

  • "les efforts pour réduire le bruit" ↔ "promulguant une loi" + "des initiatives fleurissent çà et là" (efforts exist) ✓
  • "restent en deçà des besoins" ↔ "cela ne suffit pas" (below what's needed) ✓
  • "en deçà" = below, short of, less than required

"Efforts remain below what's needed" is a perfect paraphrase of "it's not enough."

✅ CORRECT

✅ ANSWER: D

The Lesson Here: The "On peut conclure" Framework

"Conclusion" questions always test whether you understand the article's FINAL VERDICT — not a detail, not one section, but the overall message when everything is considered.

The framework:

STEP 1: Read the article's LAST two sentences — they almost always contain the verdict
STEP 2: Identify the TRAJECTORY: Is the text optimistic, pessimistic, or mixed?
STEP 3: Find the option that matches the VERDICT + TRAJECTORY
STEP 4: Eliminate options that:
         - Exaggerate statistics (Option A: 12% → "majority")
         - Distort the target of an action (Option B: noise → construction)
         - Confuse categories (Option C: symptom → trend)

RULE: "On peut conclure" = What is the author's FINAL JUDGMENT? Look at the last paragraph, find the verdict word ("ne suffit pas," "insuffisant," "en deçà"), and match it.

Strategy 1: The Three-Domain Color Coding

When a question asks about "the specialist," you go to 🔴. When it asks about "victims' response," you check 🔵. When it asks "what can we conclude," you synthesize all three.

In problem-analysis articles, the author always delivers a VERDICT. Train yourself to spot these verdict markers:

In this text: "Mais cela ne suffit pas et des initiatives fleurissent çà et là"

Strategy 3: The Implication-Explicit Translation

Building on the skill from Chapter 16 (Duality Law), Phase 3 texts increasingly require you to translate implications into explicit statements:

What the Text IMPLIESHow the Correct Answer STATES It

Strategy 4: The "Precision Audit" for Statistics

When the text gives you a NUMBER, that number will almost certainly be tested — often through exaggeration or distortion. Build a mental "precision file":

NUMBERS IN THIS TEXT:
- 12% → of population in excessive noise zones
- 30,000-50,000 → young people with hearing damage
- 70 ans → normal onset of presbyacousie
- 40 ans → predicted future onset

Then check: does the answer MATCH or DISTORT these numbers?

  • Option A says "majorité" → 12% is NOT a majority → DISTORTION
  • Option B says "constructions limitées" → law limits NOISE, not construction → DISTORTION
  • Option C says "en augmentation" → text says noise CAUSES symptoms, not that cases are RISING → DISTORTION

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

DO #1: Read the opening and closing sentences FIRST for problem-analysis texts

In this genre, the first sentence gives you the THESIS and the last sentence gives you the VERDICT:

FIRST: "notre organisme ne s'y habitue pas" → body doesn't adapt = noise is dangerous
LAST:  "cela ne suffit pas" → current measures are insufficient

With these two anchors, you already understand the article's MESSAGE before reading the middle.

DO #2: Separate the specialist's quote from the author's commentary

As established in the Q30 analysis: quotation marks are your boundary. The specialist said what's INSIDE the quotes. The author interprets OUTSIDE the quotes. Don't mix them.

INSIDE QUOTES (specialist):  "presbyacousie... après 70 ans... dès 40 ans"
OUTSIDE QUOTES (author):     "prévient un spécialiste" / "On l'aura compris"

DO #3: Verify that numbers match EXACTLY

When an answer option quantifies something, go back to the text and check the EXACT number. "12%" ≠ "majority." "30,000-50,000" ≠ "millions." Even small exaggerations invalidate an answer.

DO #4: Distinguish between a SYMPTOM and a TREND

This is a new skill for medical/scientific texts:

  • SYMPTOM: "Noise causes increased heart rate" (a physiological effect)
  • TREND: "Heart problems are increasing" (a statistical evolution over time)

The text can describe symptoms without implying a trend. Option C exploited this confusion.

DO #5: Track "mais" at the END of the article

In Chapter 16, you learned the Duality Law for stakeholder positions. Here, "mais" operates at the ARTICLE level:

[Government took action] MAIS [it's not enough]
                          ↓
VERDICT = insufficiency

The "mais" in the last paragraph almost always signals the author's TRUE position — what comes AFTER "mais" is what they really think.

❌ WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

DON'T #1: Let the FEELING of the text override SPECIFIC data

The article creates an ATMOSPHERE of pervasive noise (motorcycles, traffic, neighbors, jackhammers). This atmosphere makes you FEEL like everyone is affected. But the DATA says 12%. Don't let emotional impact substitute for factual precision.

DON'T #2: Confuse what the law TARGETS with what it PREVENTS

The law restricts noise from construction near housing. It does NOT prevent construction. It does NOT limit the building of roads or railways. It controls the ACOUSTIC IMPACT of building them. This distinction is the heart of Option B's trap.

LAW TARGETS:    Noise levels during construction
LAW DOES NOT:   Ban or limit construction projects

DON'T #3: Assume "mentioned" means "increasing"

The text mentions cardiac symptoms. It does NOT say they're increasing. The text mentions hearing damage in young people. It does NOT say the NUMBER is growing year over year. Presence in the text ≠ trend over time.

DON'T #4: Apply the specialist's words to the wrong subject

"Vieillissement" in the quote describes the CAUSE of hearing loss (aging causes presbyacousie). In Option C, "vieillissement" describes the VICTIMS (the affected population is aging). Same word, different subject. Always check: WHO or WHAT is the word describing?

DON'T #5: Ignore the difference between "à l'étude" and "promulguée"

Option D says "lois à l'étude" (laws being studied/planned). The text says "promulguant une loi" (enacting a law). A law "à l'étude" doesn't exist yet. A "promulguée" law already exists. Verb choice determines the law's status — and the answer's validity.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: The "Atmospheric Exaggeration" Trap

YOUR BRAIN: "The text describes noise EVERYWHERE — motorcycles, traffic,
             neighbors, construction — surely a majority is affected!"
REALITY:    Text gives a precise figure: 12%. That's far from a majority.
LESSON:     Vivid description creates IMPRESSIONS.
            Statistics provide FACTS.
            Always choose facts over impressions.

PITFALL 2: The "Object Displacement" Trap

YOUR BRAIN: "The text mentions a law about construction and roads → 
             Option B says construction is limited → match!"
REALITY:    The law limits NOISE from construction, not construction itself.
            "restreignant les nuisances sonores dues à la construction"
            ≠ "les constructions sont limitées"
LESSON:     Identify what the VERB actually targets.
            "Restreindre les nuisances" ≠ "restreindre les constructions"
            The object of the verb is EVERYTHING.

PITFALL 3: The "Symptom → Trend" Category Error

YOUR BRAIN: "Text says noise causes heart rate increase → 
             Option C says cardiac problems are increasing → same thing!"
REALITY:    CAUSE (noise → heart rate up) ≠ TREND (cardiac problems rising)
            A medical effect is not the same as an epidemiological trend.
LESSON:     "X causes Y" does NOT mean "Y is increasing."
            It means "when X occurs, Y happens."
            Check: is the text describing a MECHANISM or a STATISTIC?

PITFALL 4: The "Keyword Recycling" Trap (Advanced)

YOUR BRAIN: "The word 'vieillissement' is in the specialist's quote →
             Option C mentions 'vieillissement' → must be right!"
REALITY:    In the quote: "vieillissement" = CAUSE (aging causes hearing loss)
            In Option C: "vieillissement" = DESCRIPTION OF VICTIMS (older people)
            Same word, applied to DIFFERENT ELEMENTS of the sentence.
LESSON:     A word can appear in both the text and an option
            but describe DIFFERENT things.
            Always check: what is the word's SUBJECT or OBJECT?

PITFALL 5: The "Optimistic Spin" Title Trap

YOUR BRAIN: "The text mentions a law and initiatives → 
             Option B says 'development of protections' → sounds right!"
REALITY:    The text's verdict is NEGATIVE: "cela ne suffit pas"
            A title saying "development of protections" gives a POSITIVE spin
            that contradicts the article's critical tone.
LESSON:     The best title must match the article's TONE.
            If the article is critical, the title cannot be optimistic.
            Check: does the title match the VERDICT or contradict it?

PITFALL 6: The "ne... plus" Implication Blindness

YOUR BRAIN: "The text says people 'n'hésitent plus à porter plainte' →
             they file complaints → so what?"
REALITY:    "ne... plus" = NO LONGER = implies they USED TO hesitate
            This means the NUMBER of complaints is GROWING
            "de plus en plus nombreuses à saisir les tribunaux" captures this TREND
LESSON:     "Ne... plus" is a HIDDEN TREND INDICATOR.
            It tells you something CHANGED over time.
            The correct answer often makes this change EXPLICIT.
