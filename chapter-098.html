📖 START → COMPLETE ARTICLE

Les relations entre la société actuelle et les sciences

Les relations entre la société actuelle et les sciences et techniques évoluent. Des minorités constituées autour d'un rejet de celles-ci tentent d'imposer leur loi et d'interdire progressivement tout débat public sérieux des scientifiques ne partageant pas leur opinion. Ces derniers sont de plus en plus interpelés s'ils abordent publiquement et de façon non idéologique des questions relatives aux ondes électromagnétiques, aux nanotechnologies, au nucléaire, au gaz de schiste. Il devient difficile de recruter dans les disciplines concernées. Les organismes de recherche ont été conduits à donner une forte priorité aux études portant sur les risques, même ténus, de ces techniques, réduisant ainsi leur potentiel de compréhension et d'innovation. Or c'est la science et la technologie qui, par l'élaboration de nouveaux procédés et dispositifs, améliorent les conditions de vie des hommes, et protègent l'environnement.

Notre modèle est en difficulté suite à sa perte de compétitivité. Comment imaginer remonter la pente sans innover ? Comment innover si la liberté de créer est systématiquement contestée et si la méfiance envers les chercheurs est généralisée ? Les scientifiques doivent pouvoir s'exprimer dans leurs domaines d'expertise car la démocratie même est menacée si elle est incapable d'entendre des expertises, même contraires à la pensée dominante.

D'après Libération

The "invisible chain" problem

In concrete texts, causes and effects are VISIBLE:

"She was fined for smoking → She contacted the Sécurité routière → They told her it was unprecedented"

In abstract texts, causes and effects are INVISIBLE — they must be RECONSTRUCTED by the reader:

"Minorities reject science → Scientists are silenced → Recruitment drops → Research shifts to risk studies → Innovation potential decreases → Competitiveness suffers → Democracy is threatened"

This chain is NEVER stated as a single sentence. It is DISTRIBUTED across the entire text, with each link appearing in a different sentence. Your job is to see the CHAIN, not just the individual sentences.

The text's architecture — A Seven-Link Causality Chain

LINK 1: Minorities formed around rejection of science
         ↓ (impose their law)
LINK 2: Scientists silenced if they discuss topics non-ideologically
         ↓ (interpelés, débat interdit)
LINK 3: Recruitment becomes difficult in affected disciplines
         ↓ (fewer researchers)
LINK 4: Research organizations prioritize risk studies over innovation
         ↓ (reduced potential)
LINK 5: Science and technology CAN'T improve life/protect environment
         ↓ (Or... sentence reverses to remind us of science's positive role)
LINK 6: Competitiveness declines without innovation
         ↓ (Comment remonter la pente?)
LINK 7: Democracy is threatened if expertise can't be heard
         ↓ (AUTHOR'S THESIS)

Each question targets a SPECIFIC LINK in this chain:

QUESTION 20 — What happens to scientific debates?

The question: Selon l'article, les débats sur la science sont...

This is a "Selon l'article" question — it asks what the text DESCRIBES as the current state of scientific debates.

Step 1: Locate the description of scientific debates.

"Des minorités constituées autour d'un rejet de celles-ci tentent d'imposer leur loi et d'interdire progressivement tout débat public sérieux des scientifiques ne partageant pas leur opinion."

"Ces derniers sont de plus en plus interpelés s'ils abordent publiquement et de façon non idéologique des questions relatives aux ondes électromagnétiques, aux nanotechnologies, au nucléaire, au gaz de schiste."

Key information extracted:

  • Minorities try to impose their views and ban serious debate
  • Scientists are challenged if they discuss topics without ideological bias
  • The debates are being shut down by ideological pressure, not economic or technical factors

The core problem: ideology is contaminating scientific debate. Scientists can't speak freely because minority groups enforce an ideological line.

Step 2: What is "politiquement correct" and does it match?

"Les dérives du politiquement correct" = the excesses of political correctness.

Political correctness, in this context, means: a dominant ideological position that suppresses dissenting views. The text describes exactly this:

  • Minorities "impose their law"
  • Scientists are "challenged" if they speak "non-ideologically"
  • The goal is to "ban serious public debate"

This is the DEFINITION of political correctness taken to its extreme: an ideological orthodoxy that silences opposing voices.

Step 3: Test each option systematically.

OptionWhat it claimsText evidenceVerdict
A) pollués par les dérives du politiquement correctDebates are contaminated by ideological orthodoxy"Imposer leur loi," "interdire tout débat," "de façon non idéologique" = ideological pressure silencing dissent✅ Perfect match — ideological minorities polluting scientific discourse
B) freinés par des considérations d'ordre économiqueDebates are slowed by economic factorsThe text NEVER mentions economics as a factor hindering debates. Economics appears later (competitiveness), but as a CONSEQUENCE, not a cause of debate suppression❌ No economic factors mentioned as debate obstacles
C) limités à des problèmes de rivalité interdisciplinairesDebates are reduced to rivalry between disciplinesThe text describes ideological groups vs. scientists, NOT rivalry between different scientific disciplines❌ The conflict is ideology vs. science, not physics vs. chemistry
D) orientés abusivement vers les applications techniquesDebates are pushed toward technical applicationsThe text says research is pushed toward RISK STUDIES ("études portant sur les risques"), not toward technical applications. These are opposite directions: risk studies = caution; applications = innovation❌ Close but inverted — research is pushed toward RISK assessment, not applications

Step 4: Verify option D's subtlety — the most dangerous trap.

Option D is the most seductive wrong answer because it involves a partial truth:

  • The text DOES say research priorities have been distorted
  • But the distortion is toward risk studies (defensive, cautious), not toward technical applications (proactive, innovative)
  • "Applications techniques" would mean more PRACTICAL use of science — the text says the opposite: less innovation, more caution

This is a direction inversion trap (learned in Chapter 18). The text says research is being pushed toward CAUTION. Option D says it's being pushed toward APPLICATION. These are opposite vectors.

✅ Answer: A

🔑 New technique: READING "POLITICAL CORRECTNESS" AS IDEOLOGICAL PRESSURE

In French academic and journalistic discourse, "le politiquement correct" or "la pensée dominante" refers to an ideological consensus that suppresses dissent. When a text describes:

  • Groups trying to "impose their law" on discourse
  • Scientists being silenced for non-conforming views
  • Debate being "banned" or restricted

...this is the phenomenon of political correctness — ideological orthodoxy limiting free expression.

The text never uses the phrase "politiquement correct" — but the CONCEPT it describes IS political correctness. This is a concept paraphrase: the answer names the concept; the text describes its symptoms.

TEXT describes:     symptoms (silencing, banning, challenging)
ANSWER names:       the concept (politiquement correct)
YOUR JOB:           recognize that the symptoms = the concept

The question: Cette situation est due à...

"Cette situation" = the pollution of scientific debates described in Q20. The question asks: WHO is responsible?

Step 1: Identify the AGENTS in the text.

"Des minorités constituées autour d'un rejet de celles-ci tentent d'imposer leur loi..."

The agents are:

  • Minorities (des minorités)
  • Constituted around rejection (constituées autour d'un rejet)
  • They try to impose their law (tentent d'imposer leur loi)

These are small groups (minorities), organized around opposition to science (rejet), who use pressure tactics to silence debate.

QUESTION 22 — What is the CONSEQUENCE?

The question: Elle a pour conséquence...

"Elle" = the situation described (ideological pressure on science). The question asks: what RESULTS from this pressure?

Step 1: Locate the CONSEQUENCES in the text.

The text lists TWO direct consequences:

Consequence 1: "Il devient difficile de recruter dans les disciplines concernées."
= Hard to recruit researchers in affected fields

Consequence 2: "Les organismes de recherche ont été conduits à donner une forte priorité aux études portant sur les risques, même ténus, de ces techniques, réduisant ainsi leur potentiel de compréhension et d'innovation."
= Research organizations forced to prioritize risk studies → REDUCING their potential for understanding and innovation

The key phrase is: "réduisant leur potentiel de compréhension et d'innovation" — reducing their potential for UNDERSTANDING (= fundamental research) and INNOVATION.

Step 2: Test each option.

OptionWhat it claimsText evidenceVerdict
A) un renforcement abusif des contrôles techniquesMore technical controlsThe text says research is pushed toward RISK STUDIES, not that technical controls on products/processes are increased. Risk studies ≠ technical controls❌ Confuses "studying risks" with "controlling technology"

Step 3: Understand the critical vocabulary mapping.

This question hinges on understanding what "compréhension" means in a SCIENTIFIC context:

compréhensionUnderstanding (a person understands something)Fundamental research (understanding how nature works)

The text says both are reduced: fundamental research AND applied research suffer. Option D captures the first (fundamental research). No option captures both simultaneously, so D is the best available match.

Step 4: Why option B is the subtlest trap.

Option B is tempting because the text DOES mention the environment: "protègent l'environnement." Your brain connects: "If science protects the environment, and science is weakened, then the environment is at risk."

This reasoning is LOGICALLY VALID — but it's an INFERENCE, not a stated consequence. The text says:

STATED: Research potential for understanding and innovation is REDUCED
IMPLIED: This COULD eventually harm the environment

The question asks for a CONSEQUENCE ("elle a pour conséquence"), meaning what the text says HAS happened or IS happening — not what MIGHT happen. The stated consequence is the research setback (option D), not the environmental risk (option B).

🔑 Rule: "A pour conséquence" asks for STATED consequences, not logical inferences. Even if your reasoning is valid, if the text doesn't explicitly state it as a consequence, it's wrong.

✅ Answer: D

🔑 New technique: DISTINGUISHING STATED CONSEQUENCES FROM LOGICAL INFERENCES

In abstract argumentation texts, the author builds a causality chain where each link implies the next. But not all links are EXPLICITLY STATED as consequences:

LINK 4: Research shifts to risk studies → REDUCES innovation potential
         ↑ EXPLICITLY STATED as a consequence

LINK 5: If innovation decreases → environment may suffer
         ↑ LOGICALLY IMPLIED but NOT stated as a current consequence

LINK 6: If competitiveness declines → economy suffers
         ↑ PRESENTED AS A RHETORICAL QUESTION, not a stated fact

When a question asks "what is the consequence," select only what the text STATES. When a question asks "what does the author argue/believe," you CAN select logical implications.

This distinction will be tested aggressively in Phase 5.

QUESTION 23 — What does the AUTHOR argue?

The question: Selon l'auteur...

This is the most important question in the chapter. It asks for the author's THESIS — not what the text describes, but what the author BELIEVES and ARGUES.

Step 1: Locate the author's argumentative voice.

In this text, the author's DESCRIPTIVE voice dominates paragraph 1 (describing the situation). The author's ARGUMENTATIVE voice emerges in paragraph 2:

"Notre modèle est en difficulté suite à sa perte de compétitivité. Comment imaginer remonter la pente sans innover ? Comment innover si la liberté de créer est systématiquement contestée et si la méfiance envers les chercheurs est généralisée ?"

These are rhetorical questions — they don't seek answers; they ASSERT a position:

  • "How can we recover without innovating?" = We CANNOT recover without innovating
  • "How can we innovate if freedom is contested?" = We CANNOT innovate without freedom

And then the THESIS — the final sentence:

"Les scientifiques doivent pouvoir s'exprimer dans leurs domaines d'expertise car la démocratie même est menacée si elle est incapable d'entendre des expertises, même contraires à la pensée dominante."

The word "doivent" (must) is a PRESCRIPTIVE verb — it tells us what SHOULD happen. This is the author's recommendation, not a description of reality.

The word "car" (because) introduces the REASON for this recommendation: democracy is threatened.

The THESIS is: Scientific independence is a matter of democratic survival. Or more precisely: The freedom of scientists to express expertise is essential for democracy.

🔑 New technique: THE PREMISE vs. THESIS DISTINCTION

This is the chapter's signature technique and the most important skill for Phase 5.

When "Selon l'auteur" appears in a question, the exam is asking for the author's THESIS — the position they DEFEND, the conclusion they DRAW, the argument they MAKE.

The exam will always include a trap option that describes a PREMISE — a fact the author mentions as part of building their argument, but which is NOT the argument itself.

How to tell them apart:

FeaturePREMISETHESIS
Verb moodIndicative (describes reality)Prescriptive ("doit," "il faut") or conditional ("serait menacée")
Position in textUsually early (sets up the argument)Usually late (concludes the argument)
FunctionEstablishes a situationDraws a conclusion or makes a recommendation
Can stand alone?Yes (it's a fact)Only makes sense in the context of the argument

In this text:

  • "La méfiance est généralisée" = PREMISE (indicative, early, describes a fact)
  • "La démocratie même est menacée" = THESIS (conditional, late, draws a conclusion)

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Premise Masquerading as Thesis" Trap

What happens: You read "la méfiance envers les chercheurs est généralisée" in the text, see option A in Q23 ("la défiance envers les scientifiques est croissante"), and think: "Yes! The author says this!"

Why it fails: The author does MENTION this — as a FACT, not as their ARGUMENT. The question asks "Selon l'auteur" — what does the author BELIEVE, CONCLUDE, or ADVOCATE? The author USES the fact of widespread distrust to BUILD their argument that scientific independence is a democratic issue. The distrust is the FOUNDATION, not the BUILDING.

The structural test:

"Distrust is widespread" → SO WHAT? → "Democracy is threatened" → THEREFORE → "Scientists must speak freely"

Option A captures the "SO WHAT?" starting point
Option B captures the "THEREFORE" conclusion

"Selon l'auteur" always asks for the conclusion, not the starting point.

🔑 Rule: When "Selon l'auteur" appears, look for the LAST major claim in the text — the one everything else leads to. That's the thesis. Everything before it is scaffolding.

Pitfall 2: The "Valid Inference" Trap

What happens: You reason: "If science is weakened, and science protects the environment, then the environment is at risk." This is LOGICALLY VALID. So you select option B for Q22.

Why it fails: The question asks "Elle a pour conséquence" — what CONSEQUENCE does the text STATE? Not what you can DEDUCE, but what the text SAYS.

The text STATES: "réduisant ainsi leur potentiel de compréhension et d'innovation" = reducing research potential.
The text IMPLIES: this could harm the environment eventually.

The precision test:

STATED CONSEQUENCE:  "reducing potential for understanding and innovation"
                     = Research setback (option D) ✅

IMPLIED CONSEQUENCE: "if innovation is reduced, environment could suffer"
                     = Environmental risk (option B) ❌ (valid but not stated)

🔑 Rule: In "conséquence" questions, only select what the text EXPLICITLY describes as a result. Valid inferences are not stated consequences.

Pitfall 3: The "Abstract Vocabulary Panic"

What happens: You encounter terms like "nanotechnologies," "gaz de schiste," "ondes électromagnétiques," "compétitivité," and feel lost because you don't fully understand the scientific or economic concepts.

Why it doesn't matter: You do NOT need to understand nanotechnology or shale gas to answer the questions. These terms appear as EXAMPLES in a LIST. The list's FUNCTION is to show that scientists are silenced on MULTIPLE topics. You need to understand the FUNCTION of the list, not the content of each item.

The abstraction test:

REPLACE specific terms with generic labels:

"questions relatives aux [TOPIC A], [TOPIC B], [TOPIC C], [TOPIC D]"
= Scientists are challenged on VARIOUS SCIENTIFIC TOPICS

The specific topics don't matter. The PATTERN matters:
scientists + challenged + multiple topics = systematic silencing

🔑 Rule: In abstract texts, scientific or technical terms usually appear in LISTS or as EXAMPLES. You need to understand the LIST'S PURPOSE, not each item's technical meaning.

Pitfall 4: Misreading "Or" as English "Or"

What happens: You see "Or c'est la science et la technologie qui..." and read "Or" as the English conjunction "or" (alternative). You think the sentence offers a choice.

Why it fails: In French, "Or" is NOT "or." It means "Yet" / "However" / "Now" — it introduces a contrasting or corrective statement:

ENGLISH "or":   Choice between alternatives (A or B)
FRENCH "Or":    Contrasting connector (Yet / However / Now then)

In this text:

"...réduisant ainsi leur potentiel de compréhension et d'innovation. Or c'est la science et la technologie qui améliorent les conditions de vie..."

= "...reducing their potential for understanding and innovation. YET it is science and technology that improve living conditions..."

"Or" here creates a CONTRAST: science is being weakened (previous sentence), BUT science is what improves life (this sentence). The contrast highlights the IRONY and DANGER of the situation.

🔑 Rule: French "Or" ≠ English "or." French "Or" = "Yet/However/Now then." It introduces a contrast or corrective. Always read it as a pivot in the argument.
