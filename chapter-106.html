Chapter 2: "Déjeuner sur l'herbe" — Recognizing Text Purpose

(Texte 47 — Phase 1: Very Easy — Foundation Thinking)

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Déjeuner sur l'herbe

Partie de campagne chic, casse-croûte urbain entre amis, pique-nique ludique pour les enfants ou encore barbecue au jardin pour toute la tribu : les thèmes ne manquent pas pour profiter du plaisir de la cuisine au grand air. Les recettes fraîches et festives non plus !

L'ouvrage Cuisine de plein air publié par les éditions Solar vous propose de célébrer l'été sous toutes ses facettes : pour chaque occasion, des idées inventives qui actualisent les traditionnels du pique-nique (cakes salés, terrines, salades, quiches, sandwichs, grillades et brochettes) ou qui s'accordent à la tendance du « snacking » (dips, bouchées, crostinis, tapas et tartines).

En plus des recettes et menus, découvrez des conseils pratiques qui vous aideront à élaborer un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer...), à préparer vos plats à l'avance ou encore à vous équiper judicieusement... Bref, tous les ingrédients pour réussir vos repas et déjeuners sur l'herbe.

QUESTIONS:

Question 14 : cet article a pour objectif

  • A) d'opposer la cuisine des villes à celle des campagnes
  • B) de faire la promotion d'un livre de recettes
  • C) de proposer une découverte des déjeuners en plein air
  • D) de comparer les modes culinaires du monde

Question 15 : les plats évoqués

  • A) sont conçus pour éviter le grignotage.
  • B) ont pour origine des traditions anciennes.
  • C) présentent l'avantage d'être vite préparés.
  • D) marquent la modernisation du pique-nique.

Question 16 : l'article donne

  • A) des listes d'ingrédients à utiliser.
  • B) des idées pour organiser un cocktail.
  • C) des propositions de repas à thèmes.
  • D) des conseils pour une bonne nutrition.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

In Chapter 1, every question asked you to locate explicit information in the text:

  • "What IS the product?" → The text told you directly
  • "What color is where?" → The text described it explicitly
  • "Who is the target audience?" → The text stated it clearly

Chapter 2 introduces a fundamentally different question type:

"Cet article a pour objectif..." (This article aims to...)

This question does NOT ask what the text says. It asks WHY the text exists — its communicative purpose. The text never says "Mon objectif est de faire la promotion d'un livre." You must infer the purpose from the text's structure, vocabulary, and content.

The New Core Concept: Communicative Intent

Every text is written for a reason. In Compréhension Écrite, texts fall into these categories:

PURPOSE CATEGORIES:
┌──────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ INFORMER         │ Neutral reporting of facts/events       │
│ PERSUADER        │ Trying to change your opinion           │
│ PROMOUVOIR       │ Selling/advertising a product or idea   │
│ DIVERTIR         │ Entertaining the reader                 │
│ DÉCRIRE          │ Painting a picture of a situation       │
│ COMPARER         │ Setting two things side by side         │
│ CRITIQUER        │ Expressing negative judgment            │
│ CONSEILLER       │ Giving practical recommendations        │
└──────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────┘

The key question for this chapter: How do you tell the difference between a text that INFORMS about outdoor cooking and a text that PROMOTES a book about outdoor cooking?

Answer: You look for THE SOURCE. If a text talks about outdoor cooking in general, it informs. If a text talks about outdoor cooking by pointing you to a specific product (a book, an event, a brand), it promotes.

Applying the Foundation Method (from Chapter 1)

We still use the same 5-step method. No change:

STEP 1: Read question stems → What am I looking for?
STEP 2: Read text with radar → Scan for answers
STEP 3: Read all four options → Consider each one
STEP 4: Match evidence → Find text support
STEP 5: Eliminate wrong answers → Confirm by exclusion

STEP 2: Read the text with radar ON

Let me now map the text paragraph by paragraph, looking for PURPOSE signals:

Paragraph 1:

"Partie de campagne chic, casse-croûte urbain entre amis, pique-nique ludique pour les enfants ou encore barbecue au jardin pour toute la tribu : les thèmes ne manquent pas..."

This paragraph sets the scene — outdoor eating occasions. It's enthusiastic and inviting. The tone is promotional (positive adjectives: chic, ludique, festives).

Paragraph 2:

"L'ouvrage Cuisine de plein air publié par les éditions Solar vous propose..."

🚨 KEY SIGNAL: The text names a specific book (Cuisine de plein air) and a specific publisher (éditions Solar). This is the DNA of a promotional text. A general informational article about outdoor cooking would NOT mention a specific product.

"des idées inventives qui actualisent les traditionnels du pique-nique"

🚨 KEY WORD: "actualisent" — modernize/update. The dishes MODERNIZE traditional pique-nique food. This will matter for Q15.

"ou qui s'accordent à la tendance du « snacking »"

🚨 KEY WORD: "tendance" — trend. These dishes follow modern trends.

Paragraph 3:

"En plus des recettes et menus, découvrez des conseils pratiques..."

🚨 KEY SIGNAL: The imperative "découvrez" (discover!) is a direct address to the reader, typical of promotional/advertising language. The text is TELLING you to buy and explore this book.

"un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer...)"

🚨 KEY DETAIL: Thematic picnics with specific examples. This will matter for Q16.

Text Map Summary:

PARAGRAPH 1: Sets the scene (outdoor eating = wonderful!)     → HOOK
PARAGRAPH 2: Introduces THE BOOK + what it offers              → PRODUCT
PARAGRAPH 3: Lists additional features of THE BOOK             → SELL

OVERALL PURPOSE: PROMOTE a specific book about outdoor cooking

STEP 3, 4, 5: Solve each question

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 14

"Cet article a pour objectif..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) d'opposer la cuisine des villes à celle des campagnesThe text mentions both urban ("casse-croûte urbain") and rural ("partie de campagne") eating. But it does NOT oppose them. It lists them as EQUAL options. "Opposer" means to set against each other — that's not happening here.❌ ELIMINATED
B) de faire la promotion d'un livre de recettesThe text names a specific book (Cuisine de plein air), a specific publisher (éditions Solar), describes the book's contents enthusiastically, and uses promotional language ("découvrez," "vous propose"). This IS a book promotion.✅ CORRECT
C) de proposer une découverte des déjeuners en plein airThis is the most dangerous trap. The text IS about outdoor eating. But is its PURPOSE to help you "discover" outdoor dining? No — its purpose is to sell you a BOOK about outdoor dining. The outdoor dining is the TOPIC, not the OBJECTIVE.❌ ELIMINATED
D) de comparer les modes culinaires du mondeThe text mentions tex-mex, tapas, crostinis (international foods). But it doesn't COMPARE culinary traditions. It simply lists them as options in the book. Listing ≠ comparing.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: B

🔴 CRITICAL LESSON: TOPIC ≠ PURPOSE

This is the single most important distinction in this chapter:

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│   TOPIC = What the text talks ABOUT                     │
│   PURPOSE = WHY the text was written                    │
│                                                         │
│   TOPIC of this text: outdoor cooking / pique-nique     │
│   PURPOSE of this text: promoting a specific book       │
│                                                         │
│   Option C describes the TOPIC.                         │
│   Option B describes the PURPOSE.                       │
│                                                         │
│   The question asks for "objectif" = PURPOSE.           │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

If the question asked "De quoi parle cet article?" (What is this article about?), then C might be valid. But "a pour objectif" asks about the GOAL — and the goal is to promote a book.

How to detect purpose in future texts:

  • Does the text name a specific product, brand, book, or event? → Likely promotional
  • Does the text use imperative verbs addressed to the reader (découvrez, essayez, venez)? → Likely promotional
  • Does the text use enthusiastic adjectives without any criticism? → Likely promotional

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 15

"Les plats évoqués..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) sont conçus pour éviter le grignotage"Grignotage" = snacking/nibbling. The text actually EMBRACES snacking: "la tendance du snacking (dips, bouchées, crostinis, tapas et tartines)." The dishes align WITH snacking, not against it.❌ ELIMINATED
B) ont pour origine des traditions anciennesThe text mentions "traditionnels du pique-nique" but then says they are being UPDATED ("actualisent"). The emphasis is on modernization, not on ancient origins.❌ ELIMINATED
C) présentent l'avantage d'être vite préparésThe text mentions "préparer vos plats à l'avance" (prepare in advance), which implies advance preparation, NOT quick preparation. Nothing says these dishes are fast to make.❌ ELIMINATED
D) marquent la modernisation du pique-niqueText says: "des idées inventives qui actualisent les traditionnels du pique-nique" — inventive ideas that MODERNIZE traditional picnic food. Also: "s'accordent à la tendance du snacking" — aligned with the TREND. This IS modernization.✅ CORRECT

Answer: D

The critical reading skill here: The keyword is "actualisent" — to update, to modernize. This single verb is the key to the correct answer. Options B and D are almost mirror opposites:

  • B says the dishes come from old traditions (looking backward)
  • D says the dishes modernize the tradition (looking forward)

The text does BOTH — it mentions traditions AND their modernization. But the EMPHASIS is on updating, not preserving. The words "inventives," "actualisent," and "tendance" all point forward.

A new skill for your toolkit — Detecting Emphasis:

When a text mentions two things (tradition + modernization),
the CORRECT answer reflects what the text EMPHASIZES,
not just what it MENTIONS.

SIGNAL WORDS for emphasis:
- Adjectives of novelty: inventif, nouveau, original, moderne
- Verbs of change: actualiser, moderniser, renouveler, transformer
- Trend language: tendance, en vogue, à la mode

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 16

"L'article donne..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) des listes d'ingrédients à utiliserThe text lists DISHES (cakes salés, terrines, tapas...), not INGREDIENTS. Dishes ≠ ingredients. A cake salé is a finished dish; flour, eggs, and cheese are ingredients.❌ ELIMINATED
B) des idées pour organiser un cocktailThe text is about pique-niques and outdoor meals, not cocktail parties. A cocktail is a formal standing reception — completely different context.❌ ELIMINATED
C) des propositions de repas à thèmesText says: "un pique-nique thématique (buffet tex-mex, grillades de fruits de mer...)" — thematic picnic ideas with specific examples. This IS "propositions de repas à thèmes."✅ CORRECT
D) des conseils pour une bonne nutritionThe text gives "conseils pratiques" about organization and equipment, NOT nutritional advice. Nothing discusses health, calories, or dietary balance.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: C

The critical reading skill here: The word "thématique" in the text maps directly to "à thèmes" in option C. This is one of those cases where the text and the correct answer use near-synonyms:

TEXT:        "pique-nique thématique"
OPTION C:    "repas à thèmes"

thématique = à thèmes  ← SYNONYM MATCH
pique-nique ≈ repas    ← APPROXIMATE MATCH (acceptable)

This is the opposite of the traps in Chapter 1. In Chapter 1, same-looking words meant different things (ambassadeur ≠ ambassades). Here, different-looking words mean the same thing (thématique = à thèmes). You need BOTH skills:

CHAPTER 1 SKILL: Same word ≠ same meaning (beware false friends)
CHAPTER 2 SKILL: Different words = same meaning (recognize synonyms)

Also notice Option A's trap: the text DOES list food items. But dishes (cakes, terrines, quiches) are not ingredients (flour, butter, eggs). This tests whether you understand the vocabulary hierarchy:

INGREDIENT → used to make a → DISH → served as part of a → MEAL → organized around a → THEME
   (farine)                    (quiche)                    (pique-nique)                 (tex-mex)

The text operates at the DISH, MEAL, and THEME levels. Option A asks about the INGREDIENT level, which the text never reaches.

✏️ WHAT COULD BE DONE

Strategy: The "Why Does This Text Exist?" Test

Before answering ANY question about a text's purpose, ask yourself:

"If I had to explain to a friend why someone wrote this text, what would I say?"

For this text, the honest answer is:

"Someone at éditions Solar or a journalist working with them wrote this to make people want to buy the book Cuisine de plein air."

You would NOT say:

"Someone wrote this so people could discover outdoor dining." (That's the topic, not the purpose.)
"Someone wrote this to compare world cuisines." (That's a minor detail, not the purpose.)

This "explain to a friend" test cuts through complexity instantly.

Strategy: Identify the "Commercial DNA"

Promotional texts leave fingerprints. Here's a checklist:

PROMOTIONAL TEXT CHECKLIST:
☑ Names a specific product/book/event/brand
☑ Names a publisher/company/organizer
☑ Uses imperative verbs (découvrez, essayez, profitez)
☑ Uses "vous" to address the reader directly
☑ Uses enthusiastic language without any criticism
☑ Ends with an invitation or call to action

This text checks ALL SIX boxes:

  • ☑ Cuisine de plein air (specific book)
  • ☑ éditions Solar (specific publisher)
  • ☑ "découvrez" (imperative)
  • ☑ "vous propose," "vous aideront" (direct address)
  • ☑ No negative word anywhere in the text
  • ☑ "tous les ingrédients pour réussir" (closing invitation)

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Topic Trap" (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: A question asks for the text's PURPOSE. One option describes the TOPIC. You pick the topic because it "feels right" — the text IS about that topic, after all.

Example: Q14 — Option C says "proposer une découverte des déjeuners en plein air." The text IS about outdoor dining. But the PURPOSE is to sell a book.

How to detect it:

ASK: "Could this purpose exist WITHOUT a specific product/brand/book?"

Option C: "proposer une découverte des déjeuners en plein air"
→ Yes, you could write about outdoor dining without mentioning any book.
→ But THIS text revolves around a specific book.
→ Therefore, C describes a GENERAL topic, not THIS text's specific purpose.

Option B: "faire la promotion d'un livre de recettes"
→ This can ONLY be true if the text mentions a specific book.
→ The text DOES mention "Cuisine de plein air" by "éditions Solar."
→ Therefore, B is the SPECIFIC purpose of THIS text.

Rule: When one option is GENERAL (could apply to many texts) and another is SPECIFIC (only applies to THIS text), the SPECIFIC option is almost always correct.

Pitfall #2: The "Vocabulary Level Confusion"

What happens: You confuse words that belong to different levels of the same category.

Example: Q16 — Confusing "dishes" with "ingredients."

This confusion appears in many future texts too. Here's the hierarchy to memorize:

FOOD VOCABULARY HIERARCHY:

Level 1 - INGREDIENTS:  farine, beurre, sel, tomates, poulet
Level 2 - DISHES:       quiche, cake salé, terrine, salade
Level 3 - MEALS:        déjeuner, dîner, pique-nique, brunch
Level 4 - THEMES:       tex-mex, fruits de mer, végétarien
Level 5 - OCCASIONS:    anniversaire, fête, sortie entre amis

When a question asks about one level, don't answer with another level.

Pitfall #3: The "Emphasis Blindness"

What happens: The text mentions both A and B, but emphasizes B. You pick A because it was mentioned first or because it's more familiar.

Example: Q15 — The text mentions traditions ("les traditionnels du pique-nique") AND modernization ("actualisent," "tendance"). You might pick B (traditions) because it appears first. But the text EMPHASIZES modernization — the traditions are mentioned only to show how they're being UPDATED.

How to detect emphasis:

COUNT the signal words:

TRADITION signals:    "traditionnels" → 1 word
MODERNIZATION signals: "inventives" + "actualisent" + "tendance" + "snacking" → 4 words

The text gives FOUR signals for modernization vs. ONE for tradition.
Modernization wins.

Pitfall #4: The "Positive Absence" Error

What happens: You pick an option because the text DOESN'T contradict it, even though the text doesn't SUPPORT it either.

Example: Q16, Option D — "des conseils pour une bonne nutrition." The text mentions "conseils pratiques." You might think: "Well, it doesn't say the advice ISN'T about nutrition..." But absence of contradiction ≠ presence of support. The text specifies what the advice IS about: "élaborer un pique-nique thématique," "préparer vos plats à l'avance," "vous équiper judicieusement." These are about ORGANIZATION and EQUIPMENT, not nutrition.

Rule: The correct answer must be actively supported by the text, not merely "not contradicted."

Bridge to Chapter 3:

Chapters 1 and 2 dealt with texts that either DESCRIBE a product or PROMOTE a book. Neither text gave you direct advice on what to do.
