🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

Chapter 14 introduced gap-filling with logical connectors — short linking words.
Chapter 15 introduced gap-filling with thematic sentences — full statements matching a paragraph's theme.
Chapter 16 introduces gap-filling within an argumentative skeleton — each gap is a PIECE of an argument, and the gaps TOGETHER build a complete case.

GAP-FILL EVOLUTION:

Ch.14: [connector] links two existing ideas
  → Same si, mais, dès lors que

Ch.15: [sentence] fits a paragraph's theme
  → Thematic compatibility + pronoun anchors

Ch.16: [argument piece] constructs a logical case
  → Each gap DEPENDS on the previous gap's content
  → The gaps together form an ARGUMENT CHAIN
  → Missing one piece breaks the entire case

The New Core Concept: The Argument Skeleton

This text builds a CASE: obesity is a workplace problem, not just a personal one. The argument has a specific structure:

ARGUMENT SKELETON:

PREMISE:      Obesity was once personal → now it's a workplace concern
EVIDENCE 1:   Obesity causes health problems → employees take sick leave
EVIDENCE 2:   Obesity reduces productivity → employers lose money
CONCLUSION:   Employers should address employee health
SOLUTION:     Health programs at work → proven results

Each GAP fills a piece of this skeleton:
  Gap 36: PREMISE (the shift from personal to workplace)
  Gap 37: EVIDENCE 1 (sick leave consequence)
  Gap 38: EVIDENCE 2 (productivity consequence)
  Gap 39: CONCLUSION (employer responsibility)
  Gap 40: SOLUTION (programs work)

The New Core Concept: Cross-Gap Dependencies

Unlike Chapters 14-15 where each gap was relatively independent, Chapter 16's gaps are INTERDEPENDENT:

CROSS-GAP DEPENDENCIES:

Gap 36 establishes: obesity = workplace concern
  → Gap 37 must follow: what's the workplace CONSEQUENCE?

Gap 37 establishes: employees take more sick leave
  → Gap 38 must add: ANOTHER workplace consequence

Gap 38 establishes: reduced productivity
  → Gap 39 must conclude: what should employers DO?

Gap 39 establishes: employers should act
  → Gap 40 must show: the programs WORK

If Gap 36 says obesity is PERSONAL (not workplace),
then Gaps 37-40 about workplace consequences make no sense.

THE CHAIN MUST BE CONSISTENT:
  Personal → workplace concern → sick leave → productivity loss
  → employer action → programs work → proven results

The New Core Concept: The "Direction of the Argument" Check

This text argues ONE DIRECTION consistently: obesity is BAD for workplaces. Every gap must support this direction:

ARGUMENT DIRECTION: OBESITY = NEGATIVE FOR WORKPLACES

Every gap must show obesity as:
  ✅ A problem (not a benefit)
  ✅ Causing negative consequences (not positive ones)
  ✅ Requiring employer action (not being ignored)
  ✅ Solvable through programs (not hopeless)

Options that REVERSE this direction are wrong:
  ❌ "obese employees are more resistant to illness" (positive)
  ❌ "obese employees are more productive" (positive)
  ❌ "programs don't work" (hopeless)

✅ SOLVING GAP 36

Context sandwich:

BEFORE: "Considérée il y a très peu comme un problème personnel,"
  = Recently considered a personal problem,

AFTER:  "l'obésité est liée à un très grand nombre de problèmes de santé."
  = obesity is linked to a very large number of health problems

LOGICAL RELATIONSHIP:
  The opening creates a CONTRAST:
    BEFORE (recently): obesity = personal problem
    NOW: obesity = [GAP 36]
    
  The sentence structure is:
    "Considérée [as X in the past], [GAP = now Y]"
    = temporal contrast: past vs. present
    
  What changed?
    Personal problem → workplace/corporate concern
    
  The GAP must express the CURRENT status:
    obesity is NOW a concern for BUSINESSES/EMPLOYERS
OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) l'obésité est assimilée aujourd'hui à une faute professionnelle"Assimilée à une faute professionnelle" = equated with a professional offense/fault. This is too EXTREME — obesity isn't a "fault" or "offense." The text treats obesity as a HEALTH PROBLEM that affects work, not as a punishable offense. This overstates the shift.❌ ELIMINATED (too extreme)
B) l'obésité est en passe de devenir une préoccupation des entreprises"En passe de devenir" = on the way to becoming. "Préoccupation des entreprises" = a concern for companies. Past: personal problem → Present: becoming a company concern. This is a MEASURED shift, not extreme. It matches the text's tone (informational, not accusatory).✅ CORRECT
C) l'obésité est délaissée aujourd'hui au profit d'autres préoccupations"Délaissée au profit d'autres" = neglected in favor of other concerns. This means obesity is being IGNORED today — but the entire text argues the OPPOSITE: obesity is getting MORE attention from employers, not less.❌ ELIMINATED (contradicts argument direction)
D) l'obésité se transforme en une préoccupation personnelle de chaque salarié"Préoccupation personnelle de chaque salarié" = personal concern of each employee. But the BEFORE text already says obesity WAS personal. The GAP should show the SHIFT from personal to CORPORATE. Option D keeps it personal — there's no shift.❌ ELIMINATED (no contrast with "before")

Answer: B

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "temporal contrast" structure:

TEMPORAL CONTRAST STRUCTURE:

"Considérée il y a très peu comme X, [GAP = now Y]"
  = Once considered X, [now Y]

This structure REQUIRES:
  X ≠ Y (there must be a CHANGE)
  X = "problème personnel" (personal problem)
  Y = something DIFFERENT from personal

Option D: Y = "préoccupation personnelle" (personal concern)
  → X = personal, Y = personal → NO CHANGE → NO CONTRAST
  → Violates the temporal contrast structure ❌

Option B: Y = "préoccupation des entreprises" (company concern)
  → X = personal, Y = corporate → CHANGE ✅ → CONTRAST ✅

Skill 2 — Degree calibration (new):

DEGREE CALIBRATION:

The text is an INFORMATIONAL article, not a polemic.
The tone is measured, factual, evidence-based.

Option A: "faute professionnelle" (professional offense)
  → VERY STRONG language → accusatory → polemic tone
  → Doesn't match the text's measured, informational tone

Option B: "en passe de devenir une préoccupation" (on the way to becoming a concern)
  → MODERATE language → observational → informational tone
  → Matches the text's measured approach

DEGREE CALIBRATION:
  Match the INTENSITY of the option to the TONE of the text.
  Informational text → moderate language
  Polemic text → strong language
  
  "Faute professionnelle" is too strong for this informational text.
  "Préoccupation des entreprises" matches the tone.

This is a new skill:

NEW SKILL: TONE-INTENSITY MATCHING

Before accepting an option, ask:
  "Does this option's INTENSITY match the text's TONE?"

TONE SCALE:
  Neutral/informational → moderate vocabulary
  Analytical → measured, qualified vocabulary
  Polemic/editorial → strong, accusatory vocabulary
  
If the text is informational but the option is accusatory,
there's a TONE MISMATCH → likely wrong.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "par exemple" reverse-engineering (similar to Ch.15's "donc"):

"PAR EXEMPLE" REVERSE-ENGINEERING:

"Par exemple" AFTER a gap means:
  What follows is a SPECIFIC EXAMPLE of the gap's GENERAL claim

EXAMPLE: Back pain → absenteeism (specific)
GENERAL CLAIM: Obese employees take more sick leave (general)

The example ILLUSTRATES the claim:
  Obesity → back pain → absenteeism = more sick leave
  
  GENERAL: more obesity → more sick leave (Gap 37)
  SPECIFIC: back pain → #1 cause of absenteeism (example)

The gap must be the GENERAL statement that the example supports.

Skill 2 — Eliminating options that REVERSE the argument direction:

ARGUMENT DIRECTION: Obesity = NEGATIVE for workplaces

Options A, C, D all present obesity as POSITIVE:
  A: "more resistant to illness" → positive
  C: "least absent" → positive
  D: "better handle stress" → positive

Only Option B presents obesity as NEGATIVE:
  B: "more sick leave" → negative

THREE options go AGAINST the argument direction.
ONE option follows it.
This is a strong pattern in argumentative texte à trous.

RULE FOR ARGUMENTATIVE TEXTS:
  If the text argues "X is bad," ALL gaps must support "X is bad."
  Options saying "X is good" contradict the argument → eliminate them.

This creates a powerful elimination strategy:

THE "DIRECTION FILTER":

STEP 1: Identify the text's argument DIRECTION
  (obesity = negative for workplaces)

STEP 2: For each gap, immediately eliminate options
  that go AGAINST this direction

STEP 3: Among remaining options, choose the one
  that best fits the specific context

This often eliminates 2-3 options instantly,
leaving only 1-2 candidates to evaluate carefully.

Context sandwich:

BEFORE: "l'obésité est l'une des principales causes de ce type de douleur. 
         En outre,"
  = obesity is one of the main causes of this pain. Furthermore,

AFTER:  "Et ça, aucun employeur n'en veut. Car l'obésité a un coût."

ANCHORS:
  1. "En outre" = furthermore/moreover (ADDITION connector)

     → Previous consequence: sick leave (Gap 37)
     → New consequence: something ADDITIONAL

  2. "Et ÇA, aucun employeur n'en veut"
     → "ça" = THAT → refers to what the gap states
     → Whatever the gap says, employers DON'T WANT IT
     → The gap must describe something NEGATIVE for employers

  3. "Car l'obésité a un COÛT"
     → Because obesity has a COST
     → The gap relates to the FINANCIAL impact of obesity

LOGICAL CHAIN:
  Evidence 1 (Gap 37): More sick leave
  + "En outre" (furthermore)
  Evidence 2 (Gap 38): [ANOTHER negative workplace consequence]
  → Employers don't want this → obesity has a cost
  
  What workplace consequence ADDS to sick leave AND costs employers money?
  → REDUCED PRODUCTIVITY (less work output)

Answer: C

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "aucun employeur n'en veut" absurdity test (critical):

THE ABSURDITY TEST:

"Et ÇA, aucun employeur n'en veut"
  = And THAT, no employer wants

This sentence AFTER the gap means:
  Whatever the gap describes, employers REJECT it.

TEST EACH OPTION:
  A: "more efficient" → employers don't want efficiency? → ABSURD ❌
  B: "more diligent" → employers don't want diligence? → ABSURD ❌
  C: "less productive" → employers don't want low productivity → LOGICAL ✅
  D: "great concentration" → employers don't want focus? → ABSURD ❌

Only a NEGATIVE consequence makes sense with "aucun employeur n'en veut."
Employers REJECT negative things (low productivity).
Employers WANT positive things (efficiency, diligence, concentration).

THREE options create ABSURD sentences.
ONE option creates a LOGICAL sentence.

This is an extremely powerful technique:

THE ABSURDITY TEST:

When text AFTER a gap expresses a reaction (positive or negative),
insert each option and ask: "Does this reaction make sense?"

If the reaction is NEGATIVE ("no one wants this"):
  → The gap must describe something NEGATIVE
  → Positive options create absurd sentences

If the reaction is POSITIVE ("everyone welcomes this"):
  → The gap must describe something POSITIVE
  → Negative options create absurd sentences

The absurdity test can eliminate 3 out of 4 options instantly.

Skill 2 — Cross-gap consistency check:

CROSS-GAP CONSISTENCY:

Gap 37 established: obese employees take MORE sick leave (more absence)
Gap 38 must be CONSISTENT with this:

Option B: "particularly diligent/present"
  → CONTRADICTS Gap 37 (more sick leave = MORE absent, not more present)
  → Cross-gap INCONSISTENCY

Option C: "less productive"
  → CONSISTENT with Gap 37 (sick leave + low productivity = double problem)
  → Cross-gap CONSISTENCY

RULE: Each gap must be CONSISTENT with previous gaps.
If Gap N establishes X, Gap N+1 cannot contradict X.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "AINSI" forward confirmation:

"AINSI" FORWARD CONFIRMATION:

"Ainsi" = thus/therefore (consequence marker)

AFTER: "Standard Life et Unilever ont AINSI constaté une amélioration"
  = These companies THUS observed improvement

"Ainsi" means: what follows is a CONCRETE EXAMPLE of the gap's claim.

If the gap says "programs produce good results" (Option A),
then "thus, these companies observed improvement" CONFIRMS it.

If the gap says "programs can't change habits" (Option C),
then "thus, these companies observed improvement" CONTRADICTS it.

"Ainsi" creates a CONFIRMATION relationship between gap and after.
The gap and the final sentence must be CONSISTENT.

Skill 2 — End-of-text consistency (reinforcing Ch.14's concluding logic):

END-OF-TEXT CONSISTENCY:

The FINAL sentence of the text is:
  "nette amélioration de l'alimentation de leurs salariés"
  = clear improvement in employees' diet
  → POSITIVE ending

The gap (second-to-last position) must LEAD TO this positive ending.

Options B, C, D are all NEGATIVE or DOUBTFUL:
  B: malnutrition (negative health outcome)
  C: programs can't change habits (pessimistic)
  D: bosses hesitate (doubt about adoption)

Only Option A is POSITIVE:
  A: programs produce good results quickly (optimistic)

NEGATIVE gap → "ainsi" → POSITIVE final sentence → INCONSISTENT ❌

Strategy: The Argument Direction Filter

For argumentative texte à trous, identify the argument's DIRECTION first, then filter all options:

THE DIRECTION FILTER:

STEP 1: Identify the text's overall argument
  "Obesity is a workplace problem that employers should address"

STEP 2: Determine the DIRECTION (positive/negative about the subject)
  Obesity = NEGATIVE for workplaces

STEP 3: For each gap, immediately eliminate options that go AGAINST direction
  Options saying obesity is POSITIVE → eliminate
  Options saying programs DON'T work → eliminate (contradicts solution)

STEP 4: Among remaining options, use context to select the best fit

Strategy: The Cross-Gap Consistency Check

After solving each gap, verify it's consistent with previous gaps:

CROSS-GAP CONSISTENCY CHAIN:

Gap 36: Obesity becoming a workplace concern ✅

Gap 38: Less productivity (another negative) ✅ — consistent with sick leave

When text after a gap expresses a reaction, test whether each option creates an absurd statement:

Insert each option for "ça":

  D: "great concentration" → no employer wants focus? → ABSURD

The absurdity test eliminates 3/4 options instantly.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Reversed Argument Direction" Trap (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: The text argues "X is bad." Multiple options say "X is good." Students pick a "good" option because it sounds reasonable in isolation.

THE MECHANISM:

TEXT ARGUES: Obesity is BAD for workplaces
  (causes sick leave, costs money, reduces productivity)

TRAP OPTIONS (sound reasonable in isolation):
  "Obese employees are more resistant to illness" — plausible!
  "Obese employees are more diligent" — sounds nice!
  "Obese employees show great concentration" — positive!

These are REASONABLE statements on their own.
But they CONTRADICT the text's argument.

In a texte à trous, the gap must FIT the argument.
A "nice" statement that contradicts the argument is WRONG.

DEFENSE: Always check the argument direction FIRST.
  "Is this text arguing X is good or bad?"
  Then eliminate all options going the wrong direction.

Pitfall #2: The "Absurdity Blindness" Trap

What happens: Students insert an option without reading what comes AFTER, creating an absurd statement that they don't notice.

THE MECHANISM:

Gap 38 + After: "[obese workers are more efficient]. And THAT, no employer wants."
  = No employer wants more efficient workers → ABSURD

Students who don't read AFTER the gap miss the absurdity.
They see "more efficient" and think: "That sounds right."
But the AFTER text makes it absurd.

DEFENSE: ALWAYS read the complete sentence (before + gap + after)
  as a WHOLE. If it sounds absurd, the option is wrong.

Pitfall #3: The "Wrong Actor" Trap

What happens: The text's argument points to one actor (employers) as responsible. Options shift responsibility to a different actor (employees, government).

THE MECHANISM:

TEXT'S ACTOR: Employers should address obesity
  Evidence: workplace costs, workplace programs, workplace report

TRAP OPTIONS:
  A: "employees should solve it themselves" → wrong actor
  D: "government should take responsibility" → wrong actor

These options might be TRUE in general:
  Yes, individuals should also address their health.
  Yes, governments should also fund prevention.

But the TEXT argues specifically for EMPLOYER action.
The gap must reflect the TEXT's argument, not general truth.

DEFENSE: Ask "WHO does the TEXT say should act?"
  Not "Who SHOULD act in general?"

Pitfall #4: The "Temporal Contrast Failure" Trap

What happens: A temporal contrast structure ("once X, now Y") requires X ≠ Y. An option provides Y ≈ X, destroying the contrast.

THE MECHANISM:

BEFORE: "Considérée comme un problème PERSONNEL" (once personal)
GAP: should show the CHANGE (now corporate)

Option D: "préoccupation PERSONNELLE de chaque salarié" (personal concern)
  → PERSONAL → PERSONAL = no change = no contrast

The structure DEMANDS change. Option D provides sameness.

DEFENSE: In "once X, now Y" structures,
  verify that Y is DIFFERENT from X.
