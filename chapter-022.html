🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What makes this article COMPLETELY DIFFERENT from Chapters 18–21

Every gap-fill text you've seen so far has been analytical or argumentative:

  • Chapter 18: Statistics about French holidays
  • Chapter 19: A worker's testimony about overtime
  • Chapter 20: A sociological analysis of mobile phones
  • Chapter 21: Practical advice about washing machines

They all followed logical structures — enumeration, cause-effect, problem-solution. The connectors were your guides.

This text is NONE of those. This is narrative journalism — a story told like a short film. The journalist takes you on a JOURNEY:

SCENE 1: You're on Route 66, you see an old Mercedes, you smell frying
SCENE 2: A sticker explains the mystery — biodiesel!
SCENE 3: The journalist traces the source — a garage
SCENE 4: The garage owner is thriving — sold his business
SCENE 5: The fuel supply chain — restaurants give away used oil

The gaps are not logical connectors anymore. They are NARRATIVE MOMENTS:

GapNarrative function
Gap 36The revelation — what's actually strange about the scene
Gap 37The reaction — the journalist's "aha!" moment
Gap 38The transition — following the trail to a specific place
Gap 39The character introduction — how the owner feels
Gap 40The practical bridge — connecting the business to fuel supply

THE NARRATIVE CINEMA METHOD (New technique for this chapter)

Think of this text as a movie scene. Each gap is a camera shot or a character reaction. To fill it, ask:

1. What SENSE is being activated? (sight, smell, sound, touch?)
2. What EMOTION is the character/narrator feeling?
3. What INFORMATION has just been revealed?
4. What LOGICAL NEXT STEP would the story take?

This is fundamentally different from connector logic. Here, you need storytelling instinct.

THE IDIOM CHALLENGE

Question 39 presents four idiomatic body-language expressions. These are FIGURATIVE — their meaning is NOT their literal translation. If you don't know what "se frotter les mains" means, you cannot solve this question through logic alone.

This is why this chapter introduces a critical vocabulary tool: The French Body Idiom Decoder.

QUESTION 36 — The Sensory Revelation Gap

What comes BEFORE the gap:

"...une vieille Mercedes avance lentement, et ça sent la friture derrière le vieux break. L'impression d'étrangeté de la scène ne réside pas dans ce que l'on voit :"

Let me break this down piece by piece:

FrenchMeaningSignificance
"ça sent la friture"it smells like frying/deep-fryingA SMELL has been established
"L'impression d'étrangeté"The impression of strangenessSomething is WEIRD
"ne réside pas dans ce que l'on voit"does NOT lie in what one SEESThe strangeness is NOT visual
":" (colon)Here's what it actually isThe gap REVEALS the source of strangeness

The logical deduction:

The strangeness is NOT in what you SEE → it must be in another sense
The text already mentioned SMELL ("ça sent la friture")
Therefore: the strangeness IS the smell

This is a process of elimination based on senses:

SenseMentioned in text?"Not in what you see" eliminates it?
Sight (voir)"ce que l'on voit"❌ EXPLICITLY eliminated
Smell (sentir)"ça sent la friture"✅ Already introduced — this is it!
Sound (entendre)Not mentionedPossible but not set up
Touch (toucher)Not mentionedIrrelevant here

Now let's test each choice:

ChoiceSenseContentVerdict
A "Les gros pneus sont toutes lisses"👁️ SIGHT (you SEE smooth tires)The text JUST said strangeness is NOT in what you see!❌ Directly contradicts the setup
B "C'est plutôt l'odeur, écœurante !"👃 SMELL"It's rather the smell, nauseating!" Matches "ça sent la friture" and contrasts with "not sight."✅ CORRECT
C "Le véhicule fait un bruit assourdissant"👂 SOUND (deafening noise)Technically not eliminated by "not sight." But the text established SMELL, not sound. Also, "deafening noise" doesn't connect to the biodiesel sticker that follows.❌ Wrong sense, no narrative connection
D "La voiture dégage un gros nuage de fumée"👁️ SIGHT (you SEE a cloud of smoke)Again, the text said NOT in what you see!❌ Directly contradicts the setup

The double-confirmation:

The text set up the answer BEFORE the gap with "ça sent la friture." This is what I call a narrative echo — the gap echoes and amplifies something already hinted at.

HINT: "ça sent la friture" (it smells like frying)
GAP: "C'est plutôt l'odeur, écœurante !" (It's the smell!)
ECHO: The smell of frying IS the nauseating odor

Let's verify:

"...et ça sent la friture derrière le vieux break. L'impression d'étrangeté de la scène ne réside pas dans ce que l'on voit : C'est plutôt l'odeur, écœurante ! Un autocollant plaqué sur la vitre arrière précise : « Je roule au biodiesel »."

Translation: ...and it smells like frying behind the old station wagon. The impression of strangeness does not lie in what you see: It's rather the smell, nauseating! A sticker on the rear window specifies: "I run on biodiesel."

✅ Mystery (smell) → Revelation (it IS the smell) → Explanation (biodiesel sticker). Cinematic storytelling.

✅ Answer 36: B

🔧 NEW Technique: THE SENSORY ELIMINATION TEST
When a text explicitly excludes one sense ("ne réside pas dans ce que l'on voit"), immediately eliminate ALL answer choices that rely on that sense. Then identify which ALTERNATIVE sense was set up earlier in the text.

QUESTION 37 — The Reaction Gap

What comes BEFORE the gap:

"Un autocollant plaqué sur la vitre arrière précise : « Je roule au biodiesel »."

A sticker says "I run on biodiesel."

What comes AFTER the gap:

"Il n'y a plus qu'à remonter la filière."

Translation: "All that's left is to trace back the supply chain."

The narrative moment:

We've just had a MYSTERY (why does it smell like frying?) followed by a CLUE (biodiesel sticker). The gap must be the narrator's REACTION to the clue.

MYSTERY: Strange frying smell behind an old Mercedes
CLUE: Sticker says "I run on biodiesel"
GAP 37 = NARRATOR'S REACTION to the clue
NEXT ACTION: "All that's left is to trace back the supply chain"

The narrator discovered the answer to the mystery. What's the natural human reaction when a mystery is solved?

ChoiceReaction typeContentFits the narrative?
A "Ah…. tout s'explique."💡 UNDERSTANDING"Ah... everything is explained!"YES — the sticker solves the mystery. "Aha!" moment.
B "On a du mal à y croire."😮 DISBELIEF"It's hard to believe"The sticker is a simple, clear explanation. Why would it be hard to believe?
C "Quel avenir pour le pétrole ?"🤔 PHILOSOPHICAL"What future for oil?"A general question about the oil industry. Too abstract for this narrative moment.
D "Encore un militant pour le nucléaire !"😤 SARCASM"Another nuclear activist!"Biodiesel has NOTHING to do with nuclear energy. Factually wrong.

Testing the FLOW:

With A: Sticker says "biodiesel." AH, EVERYTHING IS EXPLAINED! 
        Now let's trace back the supply chain.
        → Mystery solved → Curiosity to learn more → Investigation begins
        → PERFECT narrative arc ✅

With B: Sticker says "biodiesel." IT'S HARD TO BELIEVE.

        → But if it's hard to believe, why immediately trace the chain?
        → You'd expect doubt or verification first, not action ❌

With C: Sticker says "biodiesel." WHAT FUTURE FOR OIL?

Let's verify:

"Un autocollant plaqué sur la vitre arrière précise : « Je roule au biodiesel ». Ah…. tout s'explique. Il n'y a plus qu'à remonter la filière."

Translation: A sticker on the rear window specifies: "I run on biodiesel." Ah... everything is explained. All that's left is to trace back the supply chain.

✅ Clue → Understanding → Action. Classic journalistic investigation rhythm.

✅ Answer 37: A

🔧 NEW Technique: THE NARRATIVE EMOTION TEST
In narrative journalism, gaps between events are REACTIONS. Ask:

  • What just happened? (a clue was revealed)
  • What emotion does this trigger? (understanding, surprise, confusion?)
  • What action follows? (investigation, retreat, celebration?)
  • The reaction must BRIDGE the event and the action logically.

✅ Answer 38: A

🔧 Technique reinforced: THE "99% RIGHT" DETECTOR
When an answer choice feels perfect EXCEPT for one detail, that one detail is the trap. ALWAYS cross-check specific facts:

  • Nationalities (Asian vs. German)
  • Numbers (thousands vs. millions)
  • Directions (north vs. south)
  • Time periods (weeks vs. months)

One wrong detail = wrong answer. No exceptions.

QUESTION 39 — The Idiomatic Expression Gap

This is the signature question of Chapter 22. It tests a skill no previous chapter has required: decoding French body-language idioms.

What comes BEFORE the gap:

"L'endroit fait commerce de filtres trafiqués à poser uniquement sur les voitures de la marque allemande."

The garage sells modified filters for German cars.

What comes AFTER the gap:

"Il a de quoi être enchanté : il vient de revendre sa petite entreprise à une maison plus importante."

Translation: He has every reason to be delighted: he has just resold his small business to a larger company.

Two anchors:

  1. "Il" — masculine singular → the gap must introduce a MALE person

All four choices introduce "Le propriétaire" (the owner — masculine singular ✅). So the pronoun test doesn't help eliminate. We need the idiom test.

QUESTION 40 — The Supply Chain Bridge Gap

What comes BEFORE the gap:

"Le garage livre environ deux voitures par jour et vend sur internet les kits d'installation des nouveaux filtres dans le monde entier."

The garage delivers about two cars per day and sells installation kits worldwide on the internet.

What comes AFTER the gap:

"À Los Angeles, certains restaurants commencent d'ailleurs à donner gratuitement leurs huiles de fritures usagées !"

Translation: In Los Angeles, some restaurants are even starting to give away their used frying oils for free!

The narrative logic:

The text has explained:

  1. Cars run on biodiesel (vegetable oil)
  2. A garage converts Mercedes to use this fuel
  3. The business is booming

Now, the LAST piece of the puzzle: WHERE do users get the vegetable oil to run their cars?

BEFORE: The garage sells the conversion kits (the HARDWARE)
GAP 40 = [the FUEL supply question]
AFTER: Restaurants give away used frying oil for free (the FUEL answer)

The gap must RAISE the question of fuel supply, so the AFTER sentence can ANSWER it.

The word "d'ailleurs" in the AFTER sentence is critical:

ConnectorMeaningFunction
d'ailleursmoreover / besides / as a matter of factAdds supporting evidence to the previous statement

"D'ailleurs" means: "And by the way, here's something that CONFIRMS what was just said." So whatever the gap says, the restaurant oil giveaway is PROOF or SUPPORT for it.

ChoiceContentDoes the restaurant info support it?Verdict
A "Malheur à ceux qui aiment trop les frites !""Woe to those who love fries too much!"Humorous but irrelevant. Restaurants giving away oil doesn't prove anything about fry lovers.❌ Non-sequitur
B "Ça va faire le bonheur d'un tas de petits commerces.""It will make lots of small businesses happy."Restaurants giving away oil = good for small businesses? Partially logical, but the text isn't about small businesses benefiting.❌ Wrong beneficiary focus
C "Aux utilisateurs, ensuite, de se fournir en huile végétale.""Up to the users, then, to supply themselves with vegetable oil."Users need oil → "d'ailleurs, restaurants give it away free!" = See, getting oil is easy! PERFECT support.✅ CORRECT
D "Mais la question du prix pour les utilisateurs reste posée.""But the price question for users remains.""Mais" (but) introduces a PROBLEM. Then "d'ailleurs" would need to support that problem. But restaurants giving oil FOR FREE actually SOLVES the price problem. Contradiction!❌ "Free oil" contradicts "price remains a question"

The deep analysis of Choice D — the clever trap:

With D: "The price question remains. MOREOVER, restaurants give away oil for free."
  → If the price question "remains," the AFTER sentence should show WHY it's hard.
  → But restaurants giving oil FOR FREE means the price problem is SOLVED.
  → "D'ailleurs" would need to SUPPORT "price remains a problem"
  → Free oil CONTRADICTS "price remains a problem"
  → Logical incoherence ❌
With C: "Up to users to get vegetable oil. MOREOVER, restaurants give it away free."
  → Users need to find oil → Here's great news: restaurants give it for free!
  → "D'ailleurs" supports the feasibility of self-supply
  → Logical coherence ✅

Let's verify:

"Le garage livre environ deux voitures par jour et vend sur internet les kits d'installation des nouveaux filtres dans le monde entier. Aux utilisateurs, ensuite, de se fournir en huile végétale. À Los Angeles, certains restaurants commencent d'ailleurs à donner gratuitement leurs huiles de fritures usagées !"

Translation: The garage delivers about two cars per day and sells installation kits worldwide on the internet. Up to the users, then, to supply themselves with vegetable oil. In Los Angeles, some restaurants are even starting to give away their used frying oils for free!

✅ Business model (hardware) → User responsibility (fuel) → Easy access (free oil). Complete supply chain story.

✅ Answer 40: C

🔧 NEW Technique: THE "D'AILLEURS" SUPPORT TEST
When the AFTER sentence contains "d'ailleurs" (moreover/besides), it provides SUPPORTING EVIDENCE for the gap. Your gap answer must state something that the AFTER sentence CONFIRMS, ILLUSTRATES, or STRENGTHENS — never something it CONTRADICTS.

GAP: [claim X]
AFTER: "d'ailleurs" + [evidence that supports X]

If the evidence CONTRADICTS X → your gap answer is WRONG
If the evidence CONFIRMS X → your gap answer is CORRECT

🎯 WHAT COULD BE DONE (Strategic Options for Narrative Gap-Fill)

Strategy A: Cinema Method (RECOMMENDED for narrative texts)

1. Read the WHOLE text first, ignoring gaps — feel the STORY
2. Identify the narrative arc (mystery → clue → investigation → resolution)
3. For each gap, ask: "What SCENE MOMENT is this?"
4. Choose the answer that advances the STORY naturally
Time: 5-6 minutes | Accuracy: Very High

Strategy B: Anchor-First Method (from Chapter 18)

1. For each gap, read BEFORE and AFTER sentences
2. Find anchors (pronouns, key words, connectors)
3. Match answers to anchors
Time: 5-6 minutes | Accuracy: High but may miss narrative flow

For narrative texts, Strategy A is superior. The story's FLOW is the primary guide. Anchors are secondary checks.

For analytical/argumentative texts (Chapters 18-21), Strategy B remains superior. Logic and connectors are the primary guides.

You cannot decode idiomatic answer choices through logic alone. When the four choices are all idioms, you either know them or you don't. Refer to the idiom bank provided in Question 39 above. Learn them by heart.

3. Use "D'ailleurs" as a Confirmation Arrow

Whenever you see "d'ailleurs" in a sentence near a gap:

"D'ailleurs" = CONFIRMATION ARROW
  ↓
Points FROM the gap TOWARD the supporting evidence
  ↓
The gap states a CLAIM
The "d'ailleurs" sentence provides PROOF

If the proof CONTRADICTS your gap answer → WRONG
If the proof CONFIRMS your gap answer → CORRECT

4. Cross-check sensory details

In narrative texts, sensory details are PLANTED earlier in the text to prepare for later revelations:

"ça sent la friture" (planted early)
    ↓
"C'est plutôt l'odeur" (revealed in the gap)
    ↓
"Je roule au biodiesel" (explained after the gap)

Always ask: "Was this sense (smell, sound, sight) mentioned EARLIER in the text?"

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #1: The Sense Mismatch 👁️ vs 👃

Text: "The strangeness is NOT in what you SEE"
Choice A: "The big tires are smooth" (SIGHT)
Choice D: "The car emits a big cloud of smoke" (SIGHT)

TWO of the four choices use SIGHT — the very sense the text EXCLUDED. These are double traps designed to catch students who don't read "ne réside pas dans ce que l'on voit" carefully.

How to detect it: When the text explicitly eliminates a sense, mark ALL choices using that sense as ❌ IMMEDIATELY. You'll often eliminate 2 out of 4 instantly.

PITFALL #2: The Emotional Mismatch 😊 vs 😤

AFTER: "Il a de quoi être enchanté" (reason to be delighted)
Choice A: "serre les poings" (clenches fists = ANGER)

The emotion in the text is POSITIVE. The idiom in the choice is NEGATIVE. This mismatch should be caught instantly — but only if you KNOW what the idiom means.

How to detect it: Decode the idiom FIRST. If you don't know it, use ELIMINATION: if three idioms are clearly negative/neutral and one is positive, and the text requires positive → choose the positive one.

PITFALL #3: The "Mais + D'ailleurs" Collision 💥

Gap: "Mais la question du prix reste posée" (BUT the price question remains)
AFTER: "d'ailleurs... donner gratuitement" (MOREOVER... give away for free)

"Mais" raises a PROBLEM. "D'ailleurs" adds SUPPORTING EVIDENCE. But "free oil" SOLVES the problem rather than supporting it. The two connectors pull in opposite directions.

How to detect it: Read the gap + AFTER as one continuous thought. If they contradict each other, the gap answer is wrong.

PITFALL #4: The "Correct Category, Wrong Detail" Trap 🎯

Choice D for Gap 38: "garage réservé aux modèles asiatiques"
  ✅ Correct category (it IS a garage)
  ✅ Correct narrative function (a place to investigate)
  ❌ Wrong detail: "asiatiques" vs. "marque allemande"

Everything about this answer FEELS right — except one word. This is the deadliest trap in narrative gap-fill because your storytelling instinct says "yes!" while one factual detail says "no."

How to detect it: After choosing an answer that FEELS right, do a DETAIL SCAN: check every adjective, nationality, number, and proper noun against the surrounding text. One mismatch = wrong answer.
