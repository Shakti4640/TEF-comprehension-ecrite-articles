Quotidien gratuit

Trois nouveaux projets de quotidien gratuit sont en chantier. Arrivés en Belgique il y a dix ans, les gratuits ont fragilisé leurs confrères payants. Les éditeurs et les investisseurs s'inclinent devant leur succès, en tentant de répliquer. Dernier en date, le président du groupe de communication Habas a décidé de lancer son quotidien gratuit avant l'été. Son originalité ? Cette multiplication de projets a une explication très simple : alors que certains quotidiens perdent de l'argent, les gratuits en gagnent, grâce à leur large distribution et à leur marché publicitaire croissant, qui assurent leur rentabilité.

Prochaine étape, le développement d'hebdos et de mensuels gratuits ; la sixième chaîne de télévision va investir dans les mensuels « Homme en ville » et « Femme en ville ». De quoi fragiliser encore un peu plus la presse traditionnelle, et surtout ses circuits de distribution.

This text about free newspapers is SHORT (only ~130 words) but DENSE. Every sentence carries either a CAUSE, an EFFECT, or a CONSEQUENCE. Your job is to untangle the chain.

The Core Challenge: CAUSE-EFFECT CHAINS

Unlike previous texts where you extracted facts or tracked evolution, this text presents a logical argument:

CAUSE 1:    Free papers have large distribution + growing ad market
                ↓
EFFECT 1:   Free papers are profitable (they make money)
                ↓
EFFECT 2:   Paid papers lose money (they're weakened)
                ↓
REACTION:   Paid press tries to replicate (launches own free papers)
                ↓
NEXT STEP:  Free format expands to weeklies and monthlies
                ↓
CONSEQUENCE: Traditional press weakened even further

Each question targets a DIFFERENT LINK in this chain. If you understand the full chain before answering, every question becomes straightforward.

The New Skill: "BEST TITLE" Questions

For the first time, you'll face a question that asks: "Which title best fits this text?"

This is the HARDEST type of global comprehension question because:

  1. A title must capture the MAIN ARGUMENT, not a detail
  2. A title must reflect the TONE of the article (neutral? alarmed? humorous?)
  3. A title must be ACCURATE — not too broad, not too narrow
  4. ALL four options will sound plausible — you must find the MOST accurate

The strategy:

STEP 1: Summarize the text in ONE sentence (your own words)
STEP 2: Identify the text's DOMINANT PERSPECTIVE (who is this about?)
STEP 3: Match your summary to the option that captures both 
         the CONTENT and the PERSPECTIVE

The New Skill: FUTURE TREND IDENTIFICATION

The last paragraph of this text shifts from PRESENT to FUTURE:

"Prochaine étape, le développement d'hebdos et de mensuels gratuits..."

"Prochaine étape" (next step) is a future marker — like the temporal anchors in Chapter 3, but pointing FORWARD instead of backward. When a question asks about "un avenir proche" (near future), you must ONLY use information from sentences with future markers.

PRESENT markers:    "sont en chantier," "a décidé de lancer," "perdent de l'argent"
FUTURE markers:     "prochaine étape," "va investir," "de quoi fragiliser"

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 14: Quel titre convient le mieux à ce texte ?

Step 1: Apply the "Best Title" method.

First, my one-sentence summary:

"Free newspapers are multiplying, succeeding financially, and increasingly threatening the traditional paid press."

Second, the dominant perspective:

The article is written FROM THE PERSPECTIVE of the threat to paid press. Key phrases: "ont fragilisé leurs confrères payants," "de quoi fragiliser encore un peu plus la presse traditionnelle."

The text keeps returning to the IMPACT ON PAID PRESS. The free papers are the CAUSE, but the paid press is the SUBJECT of concern.

Step 2: Check each option.

Option A — "Ils sont fous, ces gratuits."

"Ils sont fous" = They're crazy. This title is humorous/colloquial. But is the article's tone humorous? Let's check:

The article uses neutral, analytical language: "sont en chantier," "explication très simple," "rentabilité." There's no humor, irony, or playful language. The tone is factual and slightly alarmed about the paid press, not humorous.

Also, "fous" implies the free papers are doing something irrational. But the text says the OPPOSITE — their strategy is highly rational and profitable.

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong tone (humorous vs. analytical) + wrong characterization (irrational vs. strategic)

This is a TONE MISMATCH IN TITLE TRAP — a title whose REGISTER doesn't match the article's tone. The article is serious and analytical; this title is colloquial and humorous. In Chapter 2, you learned tone detection for reviews. Now apply it to titles.

Option B — "Les gratuits se font concurrence."

"Les gratuits se font concurrence" = Free papers compete with EACH OTHER.

Does the article describe competition BETWEEN free papers? Let's check:

The text mentions ONE instance of avoiding competition: the Habas group distributes in the evening "pour éviter la concurrence frontale avec les autres quotidiens." But "les autres quotidiens" refers to OTHER DAILY PAPERS (both free and paid), and the strategy is to AVOID competition, not to intensify it.

More importantly, the article's MAIN ARGUMENT is about free papers threatening PAID press — not about free papers competing with each other. Competition between free papers is at most a secondary detail, not the central theme.

❌ ELIMINATED — Describes a secondary detail, not the main argument; misidentifies the competitive dynamic

This is a DETAIL-AS-TITLE TRAP — elevating a minor point (one company's evening distribution strategy) to the main theme. A title must capture the WHOLE article, not one paragraph's detail.

Option C — "Les quotidiens gagnent des lecteurs."

"Les quotidiens gagnent des lecteurs" = Daily papers gain readers.

Which daily papers? If this refers to PAID dailies, the text says the opposite — they're losing money and being weakened. If this refers to FREE dailies, the text doesn't discuss readership numbers — it discusses PROFITABILITY.

Furthermore, the text's perspective is about THREAT to paid press, not about growth of readership.

❌ ELIMINATED — Contradicts the text's message (paid press is LOSING, not gaining) and misses the central argument

Option D — "Nouvelle alerte pour la presse payante."

"Nouvelle alerte" = New alarm/warning. "Pour la presse payante" = for the paid press.

Let's match to my one-sentence summary:

My summaryOption D
Free papers threatening paid press"alerte pour la presse payante"
Multiplying / expanding"nouvelle" (new = latest development)
Increasingly"alerte" (alarm = growing concern)

And the text's dominant perspective:

  • "ont fragilisé leurs confrères payants" (weakened their paid counterparts)
  • "de quoi fragiliser encore un peu plus la presse traditionnelle" (further weaken traditional press)

The article IS an alarm signal about the paid press. Every paragraph returns to this theme:

  • P1: Paid press weakened, tries to replicate
  • P2: Free papers profitable, paid papers losing money
  • P3: Further expansion will weaken paid press even more

"Nouvelle alerte" captures the ESCALATING nature of the threat (new projects, new formats, further weakening).

✅ CORRECT — Captures the main argument (threat to paid press), the perspective (alarm), and the progression (new/escalating)

QUESTION 15: La presse payante réagit en...

Step 1: Identify the question type.

"La presse payante réagit en..." = "The paid press reacts by..." → This asks about the REACTION of paid press to the threat from free papers. This targets the middle of the cause-effect chain.

CAUSE: Free papers succeed → EFFECT: Paid press weakened → REACTION: ???

Step 2: Locate the paid press's reaction in the text.

Paragraph 1:

"Les éditeurs et les investisseurs s'inclinent devant leur succès, en tentant de répliquer. Dernier en date, le président du groupe de communication Habas a décidé de lancer son quotidien gratuit avant l'été."

Key expressions:

  • "s'inclinent devant leur succès" = bow to their success (acknowledge they're winning)
  • "tentant de répliquer" = trying to replicate/respond
  • "lancer son quotidien gratuit" = launch HIS OWN free daily

The reaction is clear: paid press publishers launch their own free papers.

They don't fight the trend — they JOIN it. "S'inclinent" (bow) implies SUBMISSION to the model, not resistance. "Répliquer" means to respond by COPYING the successful formula.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "diversifiant leurs ressources financières"

"Diversifiant leurs ressources financières" = diversifying their financial resources.

The text doesn't mention financial diversification strategies. It describes a specific action: launching a free paper. This is too VAGUE and GENERIC compared to what the text actually says.

❌ ELIMINATED — Too vague; text describes a specific action (launching free papers), not generic financial diversification

Option B — "investissant dans la distribution"

"Investissant dans la distribution" = investing in distribution.

The text mentions distribution twice:

  • "large distribution" (as a cause of free papers' success)
  • "ses circuits de distribution" (as something the traditional press will lose)

But neither mentions PAID PRESS investing in distribution as a REACTION. The paid press's reaction is to launch their OWN free papers — not to improve their distribution networks.

❌ ELIMINATED — Distribution is mentioned as a feature of free papers' success, not as a reaction of paid press

This is a CROSS-SUBJECT ATTRIBUTION TRAP — information that applies to one subject (free papers have large distribution) is applied to another subject (paid press invests in distribution). In Chapter 5, you learned about Group Attribution Errors. This is the same mechanism applied to economic actors instead of people.

Option C — "s'alliant avec d'autres quotidiens"

"S'alliant avec d'autres quotidiens" = forming alliances with other daily papers.

The text mentions NO alliances, partnerships, or mergers between papers. Pure invention.

❌ ELIMINATED — Invented action (no alliances mentioned)

Option D — "publiant son propre gratuit"

Option D saysText says
publiant"lancer" / "tentant de répliquer"
son propre gratuit"son quotidien gratuit"

"Publiant son propre gratuit" (publishing their own free paper) paraphrases "lancer son quotidien gratuit" (launch his free daily) and "tentant de répliquer" (trying to replicate).

  • "Publiant" ≈ "lancer" (both mean putting out/releasing a publication)
  • "Son propre gratuit" ≈ "son quotidien gratuit" (their own free paper)

✅ CORRECT — Direct paraphrase of the paid press's reaction described in the text

✅ ANSWER: D

🔬 THE REACTION-IN-CHAIN LESSON:

The cause-effect chain:
  Free papers succeed → Paid press weakened → PAID PRESS REACTS → ???
                                                      ↓
  Text says: "s'inclinent... tentant de répliquer... lancer son quotidien gratuit"
                                                      ↓
  Answer: "publiant son propre gratuit" ✅

RULE: When a question asks about a REACTION, find the link in the 
      chain that shows RESPONSE TO the preceding cause.
      The reaction must be attributed to the RIGHT ACTOR (paid press, 
      not free press).

QUESTION 16: Dans un avenir proche, il y aura...

Step 1: Identify the question type.

"Dans un avenir proche" = "In the near future" → This asks about FUTURE DEVELOPMENTS. You must use ONLY information from sentences with future markers.

This is the Future Trend Identification skill introduced in this chapter. Apply your Temporal Anchoring from Chapter 3, but pointed FORWARD.

Step 2: Locate future-oriented sentences.

Paragraph 3 contains ALL the future information:

"Prochaine étape, le développement d'hebdos et de mensuels gratuits ; la sixième chaîne de télévision va investir dans les mensuels « Homme en ville » et « Femme en ville ». De quoi fragiliser encore un peu plus la presse traditionnelle, et surtout ses circuits de distribution."

Future developments:

  1. Free weeklies and monthlies will be developed ("hebdos et mensuels gratuits")
  2. TV channel 6 will invest in two monthly magazines ("Homme en ville" + "Femme en ville")
  3. Further weakening of traditional press and its distribution circuits

Key vocabulary:

  • "hebdos" = hebdomadaires = weeklies (magazine format)
  • "mensuels" = monthlies (magazine format)
  • These are NOT daily papers — they are MAGAZINES (weekly/monthly publication rhythm)

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "un gratuit sur les médias"

"Un gratuit sur les médias" = a free paper ABOUT media/the media industry.

The text mentions "la sixième chaîne de télévision" — a TV channel that will invest in free monthlies. But the TV channel is the INVESTOR, not the SUBJECT of the publication. "Homme en ville" and "Femme en ville" are lifestyle magazines, not media industry publications.

❌ ELIMINATED — Confuses the INVESTOR (TV channel) with the CONTENT of the publications

This is an INVESTOR-CONTENT CONFUSION TRAP — because a TV channel (media company) invests in the magazines, the answer assumes the magazines are ABOUT media. But a TV channel can invest in lifestyle magazines — the investor's industry doesn't determine the publication's topic.

Option B — "des gratuits au format magazine"

Option B saysText says
des gratuitsgratuits (free publications)
au format magazinehebdos et mensuels

Are "hebdos et mensuels" magazines? Let's think:

QUOTIDIEN = daily newspaper (format: newspaper)
HEBDOMADAIRE = weekly publication (format: typically magazine)
MENSUEL = monthly publication (format: typically magazine)

The text's current topic is free DAILY papers (quotidiens gratuits). The "prochaine étape" is expanding to free WEEKLY and MONTHLY publications. Weekly and monthly publications are typically in MAGAZINE format — hence "format magazine."

"Des gratuits au format magazine" captures the SHIFT from daily newspaper format to weekly/monthly magazine format.

✅ CORRECT — "hebdos et mensuels" = publications in magazine format; "gratuits" = free

Option C — "une émission sur la sixième chaîne"

"Une émission sur la sixième chaîne" = a TV show on channel 6.

The text says channel 6 will "investir dans les mensuels" — INVEST IN magazines, not create a TV show. The TV channel is using its money to enter the FREE PRESS market, not creating broadcast content.

❌ ELIMINATED — "investir dans les mensuels" (invest in magazines) ≠ "une émission" (a TV show)

This is a MEDIUM CONFUSION TRAP — because the actor is a TV channel, the answer assumes the product is a TV show. But the text explicitly says the TV channel is investing in PRINTED publications (mensuels), not broadcast content.

Option D — "de nouvelles formes de distribution"

"De nouvelles formes de distribution" = new forms of distribution.

The text mentions distribution circuits will be affected: "surtout ses circuits de distribution." But this describes a CONSEQUENCE (distribution circuits will be weakened), not a future development that's being planned. The text doesn't say new distribution methods will be created.

❌ ELIMINATED — Distribution is mentioned as something that will be AFFECTED, not something new that will be CREATED

This is a CONSEQUENCE-AS-PLAN TRAP — taking a SIDE EFFECT mentioned in the text and presenting it as a planned DEVELOPMENT. The text says distribution circuits will be further weakened (passive consequence), not that new distribution forms will be developed (active plan).

✅ ANSWER: B

DO #5: Distinguish PLANNED developments from SIDE EFFECTS

  → Someone is DOING this on purpose

  "De quoi fragiliser encore un peu plus la presse traditionnelle"

"What will happen?" questions → include both

❌ WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

DON'T #1: Choose a title based on a single detail

WRONG: "The article mentions competition → title about competition!"
RIGHT: "Competition is mentioned ONCE in passing. The MAIN theme is 
        the threat to paid press, repeated in ALL three paragraphs."

A title must capture the REPEATED, DOMINANT theme, not a one-time mention.

DON'T #2: Assume the actor's identity determines the product's nature

WRONG: "TV channel 6 invests → must be a TV show"
RIGHT: "TV channel 6 invests in MENSUELS (monthly magazines) → 
        it's printed publications, not broadcast content"

Read WHAT is being produced, not just WHO produces it.

DON'T #3: Confuse a CONSEQUENCE with a PLAN

WRONG: "Distribution circuits will be weakened → new distribution forms will be created"
RIGHT: "Distribution weakening is a SIDE EFFECT, not a planned development"

"De quoi fragiliser" = "enough to weaken" (consequence)
≠ "On va développer" = "they will develop" (plan)

DON'T #4: Apply information about one actor to another actor

From Chapter 5's Group Attribution Error, now applied to economic actors:

WRONG: "Free papers have large distribution → paid press invests in distribution"
RIGHT: "Large distribution is WHY free papers succeed. Paid press REACTS by 
        launching their own free papers — not by improving distribution."

Each actor has their OWN actions. Don't swap them.

DON'T #5: Choose a title with the wrong TONE

ARTICLE TONE:  Analytical, factual, slightly concerned
WRONG TITLE:   "Ils sont fous, ces gratuits!" (humorous, colloquial)
RIGHT TITLE:   "Nouvelle alerte pour la presse payante" (concerned, analytical)

The title's register must match the article's register.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: The Tone Mismatch in Title Trap (NEW — Core concept of Chapter 6)

MECHANISM:  A title uses a tone (humorous, dramatic, colloquial) that doesn't 
            match the article's analytical/neutral tone
EXAMPLE:    Article is analytical → Title "Ils sont fous, ces gratuits!" is humorous
DEFENSE:    Match the title's REGISTER to the article's REGISTER
            Analytical article → analytical title
            Humorous article → humorous title

PITFALL 2: The Detail-as-Title Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  A minor detail from the text is elevated to the main theme in a title
EXAMPLE:    One company avoids competition with morning papers (detail)
            → Title: "Les gratuits se font concurrence" (makes it the main theme)
DEFENSE:    A title must capture what ALL paragraphs discuss, not what 
            ONE sentence mentions

PITFALL 3: The Cross-Subject Attribution Trap (NEW — builds on Chapter 5 Group Attribution)

MECHANISM:  Information about Subject A is used to describe Subject B's actions
EXAMPLE:    Free papers have "large distribution" (Subject A's advantage)
            → "Paid press invests in distribution" (attributed to Subject B)
DEFENSE:    Track which information belongs to which actor/subject

PITFALL 4: The Investor-Content Confusion Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  The investor's industry is assumed to determine the product's content
EXAMPLE:    TV channel (media) invests → assumed to create media content
            Reality: TV channel invests in lifestyle MAGAZINES
DEFENSE:    Read WHAT is being produced, not just WHO produces it

PITFALL 5: The Medium Confusion Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  The actor's primary medium is assumed for the new product
EXAMPLE:    TV channel → assumed to create TV show
            Reality: TV channel creates printed magazines
DEFENSE:    The text specifies the format ("mensuels") — trust the text, 
            not your assumptions about the actor

PITFALL 6: The Consequence-as-Plan Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  A passive consequence is presented as an active planned development
EXAMPLE:    Text: "de quoi fragiliser ses circuits de distribution" (consequence)
            Trap: "de nouvelles formes de distribution" (planned development)
DEFENSE:    Distinguish "this will HAPPEN as a result" from "someone PLANS to do this"

🌉 BRIDGE TO CHAPTER 7

In Chapter 6, you mapped a simple cause-effect chain in an economic text: free papers succeed → paid press weakened → paid press reacts → free format expands. The chain was LINEAR — each link led clearly to the next.

In Chapter 7 (Jeune Fumeur), you'll face a text about cigarette marketing strategies targeting young people. This introduces:

  • STRATEGY IDENTIFICATION: The text describes deliberate TACTICS used by cigarette companies. Unlike Chapter 6's market dynamics (which happen somewhat naturally), these are INTENTIONAL strategies. Questions will test whether you can identify WHAT the strategy is, WHO deploys it, and HOW it works.
  • INSTITUTIONAL SOURCE READING: The text cites INPES (health prevention institute) and Harvard's Health Affairs. Questions will test whether you can distinguish what the JOURNALIST says from what the INSTITUTIONS say.
  • "SUBJECT OF THIS ARTICLE" QUESTIONS: A global comprehension question that asks what the article is fundamentally ABOUT — similar to "Best Title" but in statement form. You'll need to distinguish the MAIN subject from secondary topics.

Your Cause-Effect Chain (Chapter 6) evolves into Strategy-Method-Target analysis. Your ability to separate perspectives (journalist vs. institution vs. company) builds on Chapter 5's Multi-Stakeholder reading.
