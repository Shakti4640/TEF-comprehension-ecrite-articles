🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

In Chapter 1, you read a product description — the beer text described features neutrally.
In Chapter 2, you identified a promotional text — the book text aimed to sell something.

Chapter 5 combines both: it's a product review. A review DESCRIBES features AND EVALUATES them. The text doesn't just say what the TX8 does — it says the TX8 is "le plus utile," "la plus intéressante," "très abordable." These are judgments, not neutral facts.

Why does this matter? Because questions will test whether you can:

  1. Identify the SUBJECT vs. the OBJECTS it interacts with — The TX8 controls TVs and hi-fi systems, but it IS a remote control. Don't confuse the controller with the controlled.
  2. Separate evaluation from fact — "Very practical" is the reviewer's opinion. "Available in specialist shops" is a fact. Both can be tested.
  3. Detect antonyms hidden in options — An option might use the OPPOSITE of what the text says (inabordable vs. abordable), hoping you'll confuse them.

The New Core Concept: The Subject-Object Confusion

This text introduces a trap that doesn't exist in simpler texts: the product INTERACTS with other products. The TX8 is a universal remote that CONTROLS televisions and hi-fi systems. The text mentions ALL THREE:

THE TX8 (subject)  ──controls──►  Television (object 1)
                   ──controls──►  Hi-fi system (object 2)

The question "What is the product?" tests whether you identify
the SUBJECT (TX8 = remote) or get confused by the OBJECTS (TV, hi-fi)

This seems obvious here, but in more complex texts (Chapters 12+), the subject-object confusion becomes much harder to detect. Building the skill now is essential.

The New Core Concept: Evaluative Language

A review text uses evaluative vocabulary — words that express the author's JUDGMENT:

EVALUATIVE VOCABULARY IN THIS TEXT:

POSITIVE evaluations:
  "le plus utile"           → most useful
  "la plus intéressante"    → most interesting
  "très abordable"          → very affordable
  "très simple et claire"   → very simple and clear
  "un grand nombre"         → a large number

NEGATIVE evaluations:
  "le plus méconnu"         → most unknown/underappreciated
  "difficile à trouver"     → hard to find

MIXED signals:
  Positive product + limited availability = great but hard to get

Questions can test evaluative language in two ways:

  • Directly: "Is the TX8 practical?" (Yes — the text evaluates it positively)
  • By antonym trap: An option uses the OPPOSITE of the evaluation (e.g., "inabordable" when the text says "abordable")

✅ SOLVING QUESTION 24

"L'appareil dont il est question est..."

OptionAnalysisVerdict
A) Un ordinateur"Ordinateur" = computer. The text never mentions computers. The TX8 controls TVs and hi-fi systems. A computer is a completely different device.❌ ELIMINATED
B) Une chaîne hi-fi"Chaîne hi-fi" is mentioned in the text, but as something the TX8 CONTROLS, not as what the TX8 IS. The hi-fi is the OBJECT, not the SUBJECT.❌ ELIMINATED
C) Une télécommandeThe text says: "concentrer toutes les caractéristiques des télécommandes classiques en un seul appareil." Later: "la télécommande chez Solips." The TX8 IS a remote control.✅ CORRECT
D) Un poste de télévisionLike the hi-fi, the TV is something the TX8 CONTROLS. The text says "votre poste de télévision" — YOUR television, which the TX8 operates. The TV is the OBJECT.❌ ELIMINATED

Answer: C

The critical reading skill here — The Subject-Object Distinction:

This question is the purest test of the subject-object confusion trap. Let me formalize it:

TRAP TYPE: "SUBJECT-OBJECT CONFUSION"

The text mentions THREE types of devices:
  1. The TX8 itself (SUBJECT — the product being reviewed)
  2. Television (OBJECT — controlled by the TX8)
  3. Hi-fi system (OBJECT — controlled by the TX8)

Options B and D are OBJECTS, not the SUBJECT.
Option C is the SUBJECT.

HOW THE TRAP WORKS:
The text mentions "poste de télévision" and "chaîne hi-fi" prominently.
Students who skim might think one of these IS the product.
But these are what the product CONTROLS, not what it IS.

THE DEFINING SENTENCE:
"Il s'agit d'un appareil qui permet de concentrer toutes les
caractéristiques des TÉLÉCOMMANDES classiques en un seul appareil."

This sentence tells you: it's a device that COMBINES remote controls.
Therefore, it IS a remote control.

A simple test to apply in future texts:

THE "WHAT DOES IT DO?" TEST:

Ask: "Does the product DO something TO this device,
      or IS it this device?"

TX8 → CONTROLS a TV       → TX8 is NOT a TV
TX8 → CONTROLS a hi-fi    → TX8 is NOT a hi-fi
TX8 → IS a remote control  → TX8 IS a remote control

The verb matters:
  "controls/operates/manages" → it's the SUBJECT acting on OBJECTS
  "is/constitutes/represents" → it IS that thing

Answer: D

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "Transferred Context" Trap (new and critical):

Option C is the most dangerous trap in this question. Let me dissect it:

TRAP TYPE: "TRANSFERRED CONTEXT"

The text mentions "grande surface" in this sentence:
  "votre poste de télévision acheté un soir de promotion en grande surface"

This tells you where YOUR TV was bought.
It does NOT tell you where the TX8 is sold.

Option C takes "grande surface" from the TV context
and TRANSFERS it to the TX8 context.

The "grande surface" applies to: TV (where it was bought)
The "grande surface" does NOT apply to: TX8 (where it's sold)

TWO DIFFERENT SUBJECTS, SAME LOCATION WORD:
  TV → bought in grande surface     ✅ (text confirms)
  TX8 → bought in grande surface    ❌ (text contradicts)

This is a more sophisticated version of the "word hijack" trap from Chapter 1. In Chapter 1, the word "ambassadeur" was hijacked from its brand context to a diplomatic context. Here, "grande surface" is hijacked from the TV's purchase context to the TX8's purchase context.

WORD HIJACK EVOLUTION:
Chapter 1: Same word, different MEANING (ambassadeur/ambassade)
Chapter 5: Same word, different SUBJECT (grande surface for TV ≠ grande surface for TX8)

Skill 2 — Abstract "marché" vs. physical "marché":

Option A exploits a vocabulary ambiguity:

"marché" has TWO meanings in French:

MEANING 1 (abstract): "the market" = commercial marketplace
  "les huit proposées sur le marché"
  = the eight [remotes] offered on the market
  = available commercially

MEANING 2 (physical): "a market" = outdoor bazaar/market stall
  "sur les marchés"
  = at the outdoor markets

Option A uses MEANING 2.
The text uses MEANING 1.

Same word, completely different meaning.

This connects directly to Chapter 1's "word hijack" lesson, but now at a higher level — the same word in the SAME text has a different meaning than the one in the option.

Skill 3 — Reinforcing "ne...que" from Chapter 4:

"elle n'est disponible que dans quelques boutiques de spécialistes"

"ne...que" = ONLY (restriction, not negation)

= available ONLY in specialist shops
= NOT available elsewhere
= limited distribution

This "ne...que" simultaneously:
  CONFIRMS: sold in specialist shops → supports Option D
  DENIES: sold anywhere else → eliminates Options A, B, C

One grammatical structure ("ne...que") both confirms the right answer AND eliminates the wrong ones. This is why understanding "ne...que" (from Chapter 4) is so powerful.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "Antonym Swap" Trap (new and critical):

This question contains TWO antonym traps:

TRAP TYPE: "ANTONYM SWAP"

TRAP 1 (Option A):
  TEXT says:    "abordable"     (affordable)
  OPTION says:  "inabordable"   (unaffordable)
  → EXACT OPPOSITE

TRAP 2 (Option C):
  TEXT says:    "très simple et claire" (very simple and clear)
  OPTION says:  "difficile à utiliser"  (difficult to use)
  → CONCEPTUAL OPPOSITE

HOW THE TRAP WORKS:
The option uses a word that LOOKS similar to the text's word
(abordable / inabordable differ by only two letters: "in-")
but means the EXACT OPPOSITE.

Under time pressure, your eye sees "abordable" in both
the text and the option, and your brain registers a "match."
But the prefix "in-" REVERSES the meaning entirely.

This is a CRITICAL trap type that will appear throughout the exam. Let me catalog the most common French antonym prefixes:

FRENCH ANTONYM PREFIXES — MEMORIZE THESE:

in- / im- / il- / ir-:
  abordable → inabordable     (affordable → unaffordable)
  possible → impossible        (possible → impossible)
  légal → illégal              (legal → illegal)
  responsable → irresponsable  (responsible → irresponsible)

dé- / dés-:
  faire → défaire              (do → undo)
  accord → désaccord           (agreement → disagreement)
  agréable → désagréable       (pleasant → unpleasant)

mal- / mé-:
  heureux → malheureux         (happy → unhappy)
  content → mécontent          (content → discontent)
  connu → méconnu              (known → unknown)

The defense: Whenever you see a word in an option that RESEMBLES a word in the text, check for prefix changes. If the prefix differs, the meaning might be REVERSED.

Skill 2 — Synonym bridging for evaluation:

TEXT:     "le plus utile"          (most useful)
OPTION B: "très pratique"          (very practical)

"utile" ≈ "pratique"  → SYNONYM
"le plus" ≈ "très"    → Both express HIGH DEGREE

The correct answer rephrases the evaluation using synonyms.
It does NOT copy the exact words.

This reinforces the pattern established in every chapter: correct answers REPHRASE; they don't copy.

REPHRASING PATTERN ACROSS CHAPTERS:
Ch.1: "citron vert" → "goût de citron" (ingredient → taste)
Ch.2: "thématique" → "à thèmes" (adjective → prepositional phrase)
Ch.3: "teintes qui flattent" → "couleurs qui vont bien" (flatter → suit)
Ch.4: "logé" → "héberger" (housed → accommodated)
Ch.5: "le plus utile" → "très pratique" (most useful → very practical) ← NEW

Skill 3 — Distinguishing "hard to FIND" from "hard to USE":

Option C says the TX8 is "difficile à utiliser" (hard to use).
The text says it's "difficile à trouver" (hard to find).

"difficile à TROUVER"  = hard to FIND    (distribution problem)
"difficile à UTILISER"  = hard to USE     (usability problem)

These are completely different:
  Finding it = WHERE it's sold (availability)
  Using it = HOW it works (ease of use)

The text says FINDING it is hard.
The text says USING it is easy ("très simple et claire").

Option C swaps "trouver" for "utiliser" — changing the meaning entirely.

This is another variant of the word hijack: same adjective ("difficile à"), different verb (trouver/utiliser), completely different meaning.

TRAP TYPE: "VERB SWAP IN FIXED EXPRESSION"

"difficile à + VERB" is a common French structure.
The VERB changes the entire meaning:

  difficile à trouver    = hard to find
  difficile à utiliser   = hard to use
  difficile à comprendre = hard to understand
  difficile à croire     = hard to believe

If the text says "difficile à X" and an option says "difficile à Y,"
check whether X and Y are the SAME verb.
If not, the option changes the meaning.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Reading RESTRICTIVE adverbs in options:

Notice the adverbs in each option:

Option A: "principalement" = mainly
Option B: "seulement"      = only
Option C: "uniquement"     = exclusively
Option D: "surtout"        = especially/mainly

B and C use ABSOLUTE restrictors (only, exclusively)
A and D use RELATIVE restrictors (mainly, especially)

ABSOLUTE restrictors are easier to disprove:
  "ONLY specialists" → If the text addresses non-specialists too, B is wrong
  "EXCLUSIVELY engineers" → If the text addresses non-engineers too, C is wrong

RELATIVE restrictors are safer:
  "MAINLY users" → This allows for some other readers too
  → Harder to disprove → More likely to be correct

This is a valuable test-taking insight:

INSIGHT: OPTIONS WITH ABSOLUTE LANGUAGE ARE EASIER TO ELIMINATE

Words like "seulement," "uniquement," "jamais," "toujours,"
"tous," "aucun" make ABSOLUTE claims.

A single counterexample disproves an absolute claim.

Words like "surtout," "principalement," "en général,"
"souvent," "la plupart" make RELATIVE claims.

Relative claims are harder to disprove and more likely correct.

WHEN IN DOUBT: prefer options with relative language
               over options with absolute language.

  • "largement diffusé" → CONTRADICTS ⚠️ availability

Whenever an option contains a word that LOOKS like a word in the text, check the prefix:

STEP 1: Spot the similar-looking word

STEP 2: Check the prefix

STEP 3: Compare meanings
  abordable = affordable ≠ inabordable = unaffordable
  → OPPOSITE → ELIMINATE

Make this a HABIT for every question. It takes two seconds and prevents one of the most common errors.

Strategy: The "Which Subject?" Check for Transferred Context

When an option mentions something from the text, verify it applies to the RIGHT subject:

"Grande surface" appears in the text.
BUT: it applies to where YOUR TV was bought,
     NOT to where the TX8 is sold.

BEFORE accepting an option, ask:
"Does this detail apply to THE PRODUCT BEING DISCUSSED,

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Antonym Prefix" Trap (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: An option uses a word that is the EXACT OPPOSITE of the text's word, differing only by a small prefix. Under time pressure, you see the root word, register a "match," and pick the wrong answer.

The mechanism:

YOUR BRAIN'S PROCESS (wrong):
  1. Text says "abordable"
  2. Option says "inabordable"
  3. Brain sees "abordable" in both → MATCH!
  4. You pick the option → WRONG

YOUR BRAIN'S PROCESS (correct):
  1. Text says "abordable"
  2. Option says "inabordable"
  3. Brain checks prefix: "in-" = REVERSAL
  4. abordable ≠ inabordable → MISMATCH
  5. You eliminate the option → CORRECT

Defense: ALWAYS read the FULL word, including prefixes. When you see a familiar root word in an option, SLOW DOWN and check for prefix modifications.

Common antonym pairs in Compréhension Écrite:

abordable / inabordable         (affordable / unaffordable)
connu / méconnu / inconnu       (known / underappreciated / unknown)
efficace / inefficace           (effective / ineffective)
suffisant / insuffisant         (sufficient / insufficient)
favorable / défavorable         (favorable / unfavorable)
croissant / décroissant         (increasing / decreasing)
prévisible / imprévisible       (predictable / unpredictable)
réversible / irréversible       (reversible / irreversible)

Pitfall #2: The "Transferred Context" Trap

What happens: A detail from the text is CORRECTLY associated with Object A, but an option TRANSFERS it to Object B.

The mechanism:

TEXT: "votre TV acheté en grande surface"    (TV → grande surface)
TRAP: "la TX8 vendue en grande surface"      (TX8 → grande surface)

The DETAIL (grande surface) is real — it IS in the text.
The ASSOCIATION is false — it applies to the TV, not the TX8.

This is harder to catch than fabricated details (Ch.4)
because the information IS in the text. It's just attached
to the WRONG subject.

Defense: For every option, ask TWO questions:

  1. "Is this detail IN the text?" → Yes, "grande surface" is mentioned.
  2. "Does it apply to THE THING being asked about?" → No, it applies to the TV, not the TX8.

Both questions must be answered YES for the option to be correct.

Pitfall #3: The "Verb Swap" Trap

What happens: An option copies a structure from the text but changes the KEY VERB, altering the meaning.

The mechanism:

TEXT:     "difficile à TROUVER"    (hard to FIND → availability issue)
OPTION:   "difficile à UTILISER"   (hard to USE → usability issue)

Same structure: "difficile à + infinitive"
Different verb: trouver ≠ utiliser
Different meaning: finding ≠ using

The structure LOOKS the same, which tricks your pattern-matching brain.
But the VERB is what carries the actual meaning.

Defense: When you see a matching structure between text and option, zoom in on the VERB. The verb is the engine of meaning in French sentences.

Pitfall #4: The "Absolute Quantifier" Trap

What happens: An option uses an absolute word (only, exclusively, all, never) that makes a claim too strong to be supported by the text.

The mechanism:

Option B: "SEULEMENT aux spécialistes"  (ONLY to specialists)
Option C: "UNIQUEMENT aux ingénieurs"   (EXCLUSIVELY to engineers)

These absolute claims require the text to explicitly EXCLUDE
all other readers. The text doesn't do this.

The text addresses "vous" — anyone who owns a TV and hi-fi.
That's a much broader audience than "only specialists."

Defense: When you see "seulement," "uniquement," "tous," "aucun," "jamais," "toujours" in an option, treat it as a RED FLAG. Ask: "Does the text REALLY make such an absolute claim?" Usually, it doesn't.

Bridge to Phase 2, Chapter 6:

Phase 1 texts were SHORT (one paragraph to half a page) and DIRECT (information explicitly stated). Phase 2 texts are LONGER and require INFERENCE — understanding what the text IMPLIES without stating it.

Chapter 6 introduces "Voyageurs sans bagages" (Texte 28) — a journalistic article about people who commute 200km daily between a provincial town and Paris. This text is TWICE as long as anything you've seen so far, and the questions require you to:

  1. Understand a METAPHORICAL TITLE — "Voyageurs sans bagages" doesn't literally mean travelers without luggage. What does it REALLY mean?
  2. Infer ATTITUDES from behavior — The text describes what commuters DO; the questions ask how they FEEL.
  3. Read SOCIAL COMMENTARY — The journalist isn't just reporting facts; they're commenting on a lifestyle trend. Can you detect the commentary beneath the reporting?

This is the leap from explicit comprehension (the text SAYS it) to implicit comprehension (the text IMPLIES it). Welcome to Phase 2.
