CHAPTER 7: THE 6000-DOLLAR GREEN CARD SCAM

📰 Texte 82 — Fraud Narrative | Phase 2: Core Patterns

📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

La carte verte à 6000 dollars

Depuis toujours, Stanley rêvait d'aller travailler aux USA. Après quelques voyages sur le continent de ses rêves, le jeune homme avait décidé de s'expatrier. Malheureusement, il lui manquait la Carte Verte, plus communément appelée Green Card.

Un jour comme Stanley voit une petite annonce qui concerne la recherche d'un manager de restaurant, il envoie son CV. Une certaine Sophie le contacte rapidement pour lui dire que son profil est parfait et qu'elle a des facilités pour obtenir une Green Card. Il faut passer par un avocat sur place qui aurait la possibilité d'obtenir la carte en peu de temps. Toutefois, pour bénéficier de cette aide, Stanley doit verser 3000 dollars. Après avoir pris les précautions d'usage, Stanley accepte de payer l'argent demandé par Sophie. Mais cette dernière prétend qu'il doit régler 6000 dollars au total pour résoudre certains problèmes administratifs. Stanley doit emprunter de l'argent à ses parents pour régler la facture.

Quand il se rend à l'Ambassade des USA, pour vérifier la validité de son document, il apprend qu'il s'agit d'un faux. Aussitôt, il contacte le restaurant. La place était prise depuis longtemps et la véritable propriétaire du restaurant explique à Stanley qu'elle avait elle-même été victime d'un détournement de son annonce.

D'après Stop arnaques

🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's NEW in this chapter

Chapter 6 introduced inference from a static description (a sealed room with security measures). This chapter introduces narrative tracking — following a STORY that unfolds across time, with multiple characters playing different roles.

NEW CONCEPT 1: The Chronological Narrative

Unlike previous articles that described a situation or event, this text tells a STORY with a timeline:

STEP 1: Stanley dreams of the USA
STEP 2: Stanley sees a job ad, sends CV
STEP 3: Sophie contacts him, offers help
STEP 4: Stanley pays $3,000
STEP 5: Sophie demands $6,000 total
STEP 6: Stanley borrows from parents, pays
STEP 7: Embassy reveals the document is FAKE
STEP 8: Restaurant owner reveals the ad was HIJACKED

You must follow this sequence precisely. Mixing up the order or confusing who does what leads to wrong answers.

NEW CONCEPT 2: The Three-Character Map

This story has three distinct characters with three different roles:

STANLEY          = VICTIM (lost money, got a fake document)
SOPHIE           = SCAMMER (took money, provided nothing real)
RESTAURANT OWNER = BYSTANDER/SECONDARY VICTIM (her ad was stolen)

Each question targets a different character or relationship. You must keep them straight.

QUESTION 17: Cet article donne des informations sur...

This is a global theme question (like Chapter 4's title question and Chapter 6's Q17).

Mental summary of the article:

WHO:  Stanley (victim) tried to get a Green Card
WHAT: He was scammed by Sophie who took $6,000 for a fake document
HOW:  Through a fake job ad and false promises
RESULT: Lost money, got a worthless fake, the job never existed
SOURCE: "Stop arnaques" — a publication about scams

The article's TRUE purpose: To WARN readers about the RISKS of trying to get a Green Card through unofficial channels.

Now let's test each answer:

ChoiceAnalysisVerdict
A — la manière d'obtenir facilement une Carte Verte"Facilement" = easily. The article shows the OPPOSITE: Stanley's attempt to get a Green Card "easily" through Sophie resulted in a scam. The article doesn't TEACH you how to get one easily — it WARNS about the danger of trying.❌ Opposite of the article's message
B — le temps à prévoir pour recevoir une Carte Verte"Le temps à prévoir" = the time to plan for. The article never discusses how LONG the official process takes. Time is not the subject.❌ Not the article's topic
C — les tarifs pratiqués lorsqu'on a besoin d'une Carte Verte"Les tarifs pratiqués" = the prices charged. While $3,000 and $6,000 are mentioned, these are SCAM prices, not legitimate tariffs. The article doesn't inform about real costs — it shows how a scammer exploited someone's need.❌ Confuses scam amounts with legitimate pricing information
D — les risques possibles quand on demande une Carte Verte"Les risques possibles" = the possible risks. The entire article IS about a risk that materialized: Stanley tried to get a Green Card and was scammed. Source = "Stop arnaques." The article serves as a WARNING about risks.✅ CORRECT

✅ Answer: D

🔬 THE "ARTICLE PURPOSE" ANALYSIS

This question tests whether you understand the article's PURPOSE, not just its content. Let's distinguish:

CONTENT = What HAPPENS in the story
  → Stanley gets scammed trying to buy a Green Card

PURPOSE = WHY the article was written / WHAT it teaches the reader
  → To warn readers about the RISKS of seeking Green Cards 
    through unofficial channels

The content tells a specific story. The purpose draws a GENERAL lesson from that story.

CONTENT level: "Stanley lost $6,000 to Sophie"
PURPOSE level: "There are risks when you seek a Green Card"

Q17 asks about "informations sur" — what INFORMATION does the article provide? The INFORMATION it provides is about RISKS. The specific story of Stanley is the EXAMPLE that illustrates those risks.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #12: When a question asks what the article "gives information about" or "has as its theme," look for the GENERAL LESSON, not just the specific story. The story is the vehicle; the lesson is the destination.

How to identify the general lesson:

  1. Check the source ("Stop arnaques" = about scams = about risks)
  2. Ask: "If I were to WARN a friend using this article, what would I say?"
  3. Your warning = the article's general lesson = the answer

QUESTION 18: En faisant appel aux services de Sophie, Stanley...

The question asks: What was the CONSEQUENCE for Stanley of using Sophie's services?

Let's track Stanley's losses through the narrative:

BEFORE Sophie:
  - Stanley had his savings
  - Stanley had his time
  - Stanley had hope

DURING Sophie's "services":
  - Paid $3,000 (his own money)
  - Paid $3,000 more (borrowed from parents) = $6,000 total
  - Waited for the process

AFTER discovering the truth:
  - Lost $6,000 (money gone)
  - Got a FAKE document (worthless)
  - The job never existed (time wasted)
  - Still doesn't have a Green Card (back to square one)

Summary of consequences: He lost BOTH his money ($6,000) AND his time. He gained NOTHING.

Now let's test each answer:

ChoiceAnalysisVerdict
A — a pu s'expatrier aux USA"A pu s'expatrier" = was able to move to the USA. The document was FAKE. He COULDN'T expatriate. The scam failed him completely.❌ Opposite of reality
B — a perdu son temps et son argent"A perdu son temps" = lost his time. "Et son argent" = and his money. He spent time dealing with Sophie, paid $6,000, and got NOTHING real in return. Both time and money were lost.✅ CORRECT
C — a dépensé deux fois plus d'argent"Deux fois plus" = twice as much. Sophie first asked for $3,000, then demanded $6,000 total. So $6,000 = 2 × $3,000. Mathematically, he paid double the initial amount. This is FACTUALLY TRUE about the money... BUT it doesn't capture the FULL consequence. It only talks about money, not time. And more importantly, the REAL consequence isn't just paying double — it's that he paid AND got nothing.❌ Partially true but incomplete — and misses the main point
D — a obtenu rapidement sa carte Verte"A obtenu" = obtained. He obtained a FAKE, not a real Green Card. "Rapidement" = quickly. Sophie promised speed, but the result was a worthless fake.❌ Confuses the promise with reality

✅ Answer: B

🔬 THE "PARTIAL TRUTH" TRAP — Why Answer C is wrong despite being mathematically correct

This is one of the most sophisticated traps in this guide so far. Let's examine it carefully:

Answer C: "a dépensé deux fois plus d'argent"

Is this mathematically true?
  Sophie asked for $3,000 first → then $6,000 total
  $6,000 ÷ $3,000 = 2 → YES, it's double

So why is it WRONG?

Three reasons:

Reason 1: It's INCOMPLETE.
The question asks what happened to Stanley overall ("En faisant appel aux services de Sophie, Stanley..."). The answer should capture the FULL consequence. Spending double is only PART of the story — he also lost ALL his money AND his time AND got nothing real.

Reason 2: It implies a NEUTRAL outcome.
"A dépensé deux fois plus" (spent twice as much) sounds like an inconvenience, not a catastrophe. It could mean "the service cost more than expected but he still got it." In reality, he got NOTHING — the money was pure loss.

Reason 3: Answer B is MORE COMPLETE.
"A perdu son temps et son argent" captures BOTH dimensions of the loss (time + money) AND uses the word "perdu" (lost) which correctly reflects that the money is GONE with zero return.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #13: When two answers seem partially correct, choose the one that is MORE COMPLETE and captures the FULL picture. A partially true answer that misses the main point is still WRONG.

This is a refinement of the "best title" completeness criterion from Chapter 4 — but applied to detail questions.

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For narrative/story-based articles:

  1. Build the timeline as you read — mentally number each event (Step 1, Step 2...)
  2. Tag each character's role — Victim? Perpetrator? Bystander? Authority?
  3. Separate PROMISES from REALITY — What was claimed vs. what actually happened
  4. Watch for "signal words" that indicate deception ("prétend," "une certaine," conditional tense)

For "consequence" questions ("En faisant appel à... Stanley..."):

  1. Look at the END of the story for consequences, not the middle
  2. The consequence = what the character LOST or GAINED in the end
  3. Choose the MOST COMPLETE answer (Rule #13)

For questions about what a character SAID or REVEALED:

  1. Go to the EXACT paragraph where that character speaks
  2. Read their words/actions precisely
  3. Don't mix up what CHARACTER A said with what CHARACTER B did

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #21: The "Promise = Reality" Confusion 🎭

PROMISE: Sophie said she could get a Green Card quickly
REALITY: The document was a fake

Student who confuses them → picks "a obtenu rapidement sa carte Verte" (D)

This is the most common trap in scam/deception narratives. The exam places the SCAMMER'S PROMISES as answer choices to see if you've understood that the promises were LIES.

How to avoid it: When reading a scam story, mentally label every claim by Sophie as [LIE]:

"Son profil est parfait"                    [LIE]
"Elle a des facilités pour obtenir..."      [LIE]
"Un avocat aurait la possibilité..."        [LIE]
"Il doit régler 6000 dollars pour résoudre..." [LIE]

Then when you see these claims in answer choices, you KNOW they're traps.

PITFALL #22: The "Partial Truth" Trap 🧩

Answer C: "a dépensé deux fois plus d'argent"

TRUE: He did pay double the initial amount ($3,000 → $6,000)
INCOMPLETE: This misses the MAIN point — he lost everything for nothing

A partial truth is not the BEST answer. In multiple choice, you must choose the MOST ACCURATE and MOST COMPLETE answer.

How to detect it: Ask yourself: "Does this answer capture the ESSENCE of what happened, or just one mathematical detail?" If it's just a detail, there's probably a better answer.

PITFALL #23: The "Character Crossover" Trap 🔀

Answer D (Q19): "Sophie avait bien transmis le dossier de candidature"

This answer CONNECTS Sophie to the restaurant owner — as if Sophie 
actually forwarded Stanley's application to the real restaurant.

But in reality:

Sophie ←──── NO CONNECTION ────→ Restaurant owner
  │                                    │
  │ Scammed Stanley                    │ Was also a victim
  │ using the restaurant's ad          │ of her ad being stolen
  │                                    │
  └── Never contacted the restaurant ──┘

The exam creates FALSE connections between characters who never interacted.

How to avoid it: For each answer, ask: "In the story, did these two characters actually communicate or interact?" If not, the answer is fabricating a relationship.

PITFALL #24: The "Wrong Crime Type" Trap 🏠

Answer B (Q19): "victime d'un grave cambriolage" (victim of a serious burglary)

The restaurant owner WAS a victim — but of AD HIJACKING, not BURGLARY.
What happenedWhat the trap says
détournement d'annonce (ad hijacking)cambriolage (burglary)
Digital/fraudulent crimePhysical break-in crime

Both are crimes. Both have victims. But they're COMPLETELY different types of crime.

How to avoid it: Match the SPECIFIC type of crime, not just the general concept of "being a victim." The text says "détournement de son annonce" — this is fraud/identity theft, not physical theft.

  • Instead of tracking characters, you'll track logical chains: "X causes Y which leads to Z"
  • Questions will test whether you can distinguish between a recommendation (what you SHOULD do) and a prohibition (what you should NOT do)
  • You'll encounter scientific reasoning embedded in everyday language: how TV affects eating → how eating affects weight → how exercise affects fat
  • The concept of cause-effect chains extends Chapter 6's inference skill into the scientific domain

The new skill: Causal Chain Reading — following multi-step logical sequences where each effect becomes the cause of the next step.
