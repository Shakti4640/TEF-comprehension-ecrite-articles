🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's NEW in this chapter

Every article so far contained ONE voice — one narrator, one journalist, one character. This chapter introduces THREE separate speakers in ONE text. This creates a completely new type of challenge.

NEW CONCEPT 1: Multi-Speaker Attribution

Each question asks "Quelle personne..." (Which person...). You must:

  1. Read what EACH person says
  2. Understand their INDIVIDUAL relationship with chocolate
  3. Match the question's description to the CORRECT person
  4. NOT confuse what Person A said with what Person B said

This is like a detective lineup — three suspects, and you must identify who matches each description.

NEW CONCEPT 2: Temporal Distinction (Past vs. Present)

One critical trap in this text involves TIME. One speaker talks about what they DID in the past (ate too much) but no longer does. Another talks about what they DO now. Confusing past behavior with present behavior is a major error.

PAST behavior:    "Des folies, j'en AI FAIT" (I DID crazy things)
PRESENT behavior: "Je me DROGUE au chocolat" (I'm addicted NOW)
STOPPED behavior: "C'est FINI, depuis trois ans" (It's OVER)

NEW CONCEPT 3: The "Aucune des trois" Option

Every question includes option D — "Aucune des trois" (None of the three). This means: it's possible that NO speaker matches the description. This option keeps you honest — you can't just assign every description to someone. Sometimes the answer is "nobody said that."

Applying the method (Rule #1)

STEP 1: Read all questions first

Q11: "dit avoir mangé TROP de chocolat?" → WHO admits PAST EXCESS?
Q12: "mange le PLUS et le PLUS RÉGULIÈREMENT?" → WHO eats the MOST NOW?
Q13: "dit ne manger qu'À L'OCCASION?" → WHO eats OCCASIONALLY?

Three questions, three different consumption patterns:

Q11 → PAST EXCESS (who overdid it?)
Q12 → CURRENT MAXIMUM CONSUMPTION (who eats the most right now?)
Q13 → OCCASIONAL CONSUMPTION (who eats only sometimes?)

Critical observation: Q11 asks about the PAST ("avoir mangé" = having eaten). Q12 asks about the PRESENT ("mange" = eats). This temporal distinction is THE key to avoiding confusion.

✅ HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 11: Quelle personne dit avoir mangé TROP de chocolat ?

The critical phrase: "dit avoir mangé trop" = says they ATE too much. This is about ADMITTING PAST EXCESS.

Let's check each speaker:

Charles Dulvat:

"Je me drogue au chocolat... trois tablettes par semaine"

Charles eats A LOT. But does he say he eats TOO MUCH? Let's look carefully:

  • He says "je me drogue" (I'm addicted) — but this is said with PRIDE, not regret
  • He says "cela revient moins cher que la viande" — he JUSTIFIES his consumption
  • He says "c'est une récompense" — he sees it as POSITIVE
  • He NEVER says "I ate too much" or expresses regret

Charles doesn't ADMIT excess — he CELEBRATES his consumption. He doesn't think it's "too much."

Alexandra Vaccariello:

"Je n'exagère pas ma consommation"

She explicitly says she does NOT overdo it. She controls her consumption. She's proud of being moderate and gourmet. She definitely does NOT say she ate too much.

Charly Tankoua:

"Des folies, j'en ai fait... Après avoir mangé beaucoup de chocolat, j'ai eu énormément de caries... j'ai arrêté toute consommation"

Let's decode this:

  • "Des folies, j'en ai fait" = I did crazy things (= EXCESS, ADMITTED)
  • "Après avoir mangé beaucoup" = after having eaten A LOT (= ADMITS quantity)
  • "j'ai eu énormément de caries" = I got tons of cavities (= NEGATIVE CONSEQUENCE)
  • "j'ai arrêté" = I stopped (= REACTION to excess)

Charly EXPLICITLY admits past excess. He says he went overboard ("folies"), ate too much ("beaucoup"), suffered consequences (cavities), and stopped.

Now let's test each answer:

🔬 THE "EATING A LOT ≠ ADMITTING EXCESS" DISTINCTION

This is the critical insight of Q11. Let's visualize the difference:

CHARLES:                              CHARLY:
├─ Eats 3 bars/week                   ├─ Used to eat a lot
├─ Calls himself "addicted"           ├─ Calls it "des folies" (crazy)

The question asks who SAYS they ate too much — not who ACTUALLY eats the most. Charles eats the most RIGHT NOW, but he doesn't THINK it's too much. Charly ate a lot IN THE PAST and ADMITS it was excessive.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #18: "Doing something a lot" ≠ "Admitting you did too much." The question asks about SELF-ASSESSMENT, not objective measurement. Look for words of REGRET or NEGATIVE JUDGMENT ("folies," "trop," "beaucoup" + negative consequence) — not just large quantities.

🔬 THE TEMPORAL TRAP — Why Charly is NOT the answer

Students might think: "Charly used to eat a LOT — more than anyone. So isn't he the one who ate the most?"

But the question asks "mange" (PRESENT tense) — who EATS the most NOW. Charly USED TO eat a lot but STOPPED three years ago. His current consumption is ZERO.

QUESTION TENSE: "mange" = PRESENT
CHARLY'S STATUS: "c'est fini" = PAST (stopped)
→ MISMATCH: question asks about NOW, Charly's excess was THEN

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #19: Always match the TENSE of the question to the TENSE of the evidence. If the question uses present tense ("mange"), the answer must be about CURRENT behavior. Past behavior doesn't count — even if it was more extreme.

Let's check each speaker against "occasional consumption":

Charles Dulvat:

"trois tablettes par semaine" / "je me drogue"

Three bars a week is NOT occasional. It's REGULAR and HEAVY. Charles is the opposite of occasional.

Charles: REGULAR HEAVY consumer ≠ occasional ❌

Alexandra Vaccariello:

"je n'exagère pas" / "très souvent"

She doesn't overdo it — but she buys "très souvent" (very often). "Very often" is NOT "only occasionally." She's moderate in QUANTITY but FREQUENT in consumption.

Alexandra: MODERATE but FREQUENT consumer ≠ occasional ❌

Charly Tankoua:

"c'est fini" / "je le refuse systématiquement" / "arrêté toute consommation"

Charly doesn't eat chocolate AT ALL. Not occasionally, not sometimes — NEVER. He "refuses systematically" and has "stopped all consumption."

Charly: ZERO consumer ≠ occasional ❌

  • Charles eats A LOT regularly

  • Charly eats NOTHING

"Occasionally" would be someone who eats chocolate once in a while — maybe at a party, maybe once a month. None of the three matches this description.

🔬 WHY "AUCUNE DES TROIS" IS THE CORRECT ANSWER

This is the first time in this guide that the correct answer is "None of the above." Let's understand why students hesitate with this option:

Common mistake 1: Students often pick Alexandra because she's the most "moderate." But moderate ≠ occasional.

MODERATE: "Je n'exagère pas" = I don't overdo it (controls quantity)
OCCASIONAL: "qu'à l'occasion" = only sometimes (low frequency)

Alexandra is MODERATE in QUANTITY but FREQUENT ("très souvent")
The question asks about FREQUENCY, not quantity

Common mistake 2: Students avoid "Aucune des trois" because it feels like a "trick answer" or a "give up" answer. But it's a legitimate option that tests whether you can resist forcing a match that doesn't exist.

Common mistake 3: Students might pick Charly because he eats "little" (zero). But zero ≠ occasional. "Occasionally" implies SOME consumption. Charly has NONE.

FREQUENCY SPECTRUM:
Never ──── Occasionally ──── Often ──── Always

Charly: NEVER (zero)
Alexandra: OFTEN ("très souvent")
Charles: ALWAYS (addicted, 3 bars/week)

"Occasionally" falls between Never and Often.
NOBODY occupies this zone.
→ "Aucune des trois" ✅

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #20: When "Aucune des trois" / "None of the above" is an option, it CAN be the correct answer. Don't force a match that doesn't exist. If no speaker matches the description, "none" is the right choice. Always verify each speaker INDIVIDUALLY before eliminating this option.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #37: The "Quantity vs. Admission" Confusion 📊

QUESTION: "Who SAYS they ate too much?"

Charles: EATS the most → but DOESN'T SAY it's too much (proud)
Charly:  ATE a lot in the past → SAYS it was "des folies" (admits excess)

TRAP: Choosing the biggest current eater instead of the one who ADMITS excess

The question asks about SELF-ASSESSMENT ("dit avoir mangé trop") — not about OBJECTIVE measurement. Charles objectively eats more than anyone, but he doesn't JUDGE it as excessive. Charly explicitly calls his past consumption "des folies" and stopped because of negative consequences.

How to avoid it: Focus on the word "dit" (says). The question isn't "Who eats the most?" but "Who SAYS they ate too much?" Look for words of judgment: "folies," "trop," "exagérer," "regretter."

PITFALL #38: The "Present vs. Past" Tense Trap ⏰

Q12 asks: "mange" (present tense) = who eats the most NOW?
TRAP: Choosing Charly because he used to eat the most

Charly's consumption timeline:
  PAST: ██████████ (very high — "des folies")
  NOW:  ░░░░░░░░░░ (zero — "c'est fini")

Charles's consumption timeline:
  PAST: ████████ (not discussed, presumably high)
  NOW:  ██████████ (very high — 3 bars/week)

The question uses present tense → the answer must reflect present reality.

How to avoid it: Apply Rule #19 — always match the question's tense to the speaker's tense. If the question says "mange" (present), check what each person does NOW, not what they DID before.

PITFALL #39: The "Moderate ≠ Occasional" Semantic Trap 📐

MODERATE:   Controls QUANTITY (how much per time)
            → "je n'exagère pas" ✓ Alexandra

OCCASIONAL: Controls FREQUENCY (how often)
            → "qu'à l'occasion" = only sometimes

Alexandra: moderate quantity BUT "très souvent" (very often)
         = moderate amount, high frequency
         ≠ occasional (which would mean low frequency)

These are TWO DIFFERENT dimensions:

           LOW frequency     HIGH frequency
LOW qty    Occasional ✓      Light regular
HIGH qty   Rare binge        Heavy regular

Alexandra is in the "Light regular" zone — low quantity but high frequency. "Occasional" would be low quantity AND low frequency. Different zone entirely.

How to avoid it: Separate QUANTITY from FREQUENCY. "Moderate" describes quantity. "Occasional" describes frequency. Don't confuse them.

PITFALL #40: The "None of the Above" Avoidance 🚫

Many students have a psychological resistance to choosing "None of the above." It feels like:

  • "I must be missing something"
  • "One of them MUST match"
  • "This is a trick to make me look dumb"

But "Aucune des trois" is a VALID answer that tests a specific skill: the ability to recognize when NO option fits.

How to overcome it: After checking all three speakers, if NONE matches, trust your analysis. Ask yourself: "Did I check EACH speaker carefully? Did ANY of them match?" If the answer is "I checked all three and none matches" → choose D with confidence.

              Present tense question ("mange") → present behavior only.
              Past tense question ("avoir mangé") → past behavior.

              Verify EVERY speaker individually before eliminating it.
              Don't force a match that doesn't exist.

NEW CONCEPT: Frequency vs. Quantity Distinction
             MODERATE = controls quantity (how much)
             OCCASIONAL = controls frequency (how often)
             These are DIFFERENT dimensions — don't confuse them.

             ACTIVE consumer (present tense)
             FORMER consumer (past tense, stopped)
             These are DIFFERENT states — match to question's tense.
