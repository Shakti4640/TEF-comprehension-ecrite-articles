CHAPTER 14: LA SOCIÉTÉ FINDUS

Understanding Behavioral Research — Declared vs. Actual Behavior and Social Desirability Bias

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Texte 54 : La société Findus

La société Findus a décidé de lancer une expérimentation pour tenter de mieux comprendre les habitudes alimentaires des Français. Dix familles ont été sélectionnées, cinq à Paris et cinq à Lyon. Elles ont accepté que soient installées deux caméras dans leur appartement, l'une à proximité de la télévision, l'autre dans la cuisine. Deux fois par jour, entre 12 heures et 14 heures puis de 18 heures à 22 heures, leur quotidien sera filmé. Au moment où le consommateur semble difficile à appréhender, à analyser, et où le débat sur la progression de l'obésité place les sociétés de l'agroalimentaire en première ligne, la société Findus a choisi l'analyse in vivo pour s'approcher d'une réalité qu'elle espère plus juste. « Nous souhaitons observer les différences entre la vie rêvée des gens et leur vie réelle », explique le directeur du marketing de Findus. Le plus souvent, le développement de nouveaux produits s'effectue suite à des études déclaratives. Il y a également les enquêtes du Credoc sur les habitudes alimentaires. Or les Français déclarent, à 80 %, manger ensemble, en famille. Il y a un filtre social qui fait que souvent les gens répondent en disant non pas ce qu'ils font, mais ce qu'ils aimeraient faire. Cuisiniers, commerciaux, chefs de produits, responsables du marketing se partageront les travaux d'observation et d'analyse. « Il y a une grande différence culturelle entre les milieux du marketing ou de la publicité et la France de tous les jours ».

LE MONDE, mars 2005

Step 3: Identify the Core Concept — Declared Behavior vs. Actual Behavior

This is the chapter's defining analytical concept and the most important idea introduced since the expectation/reality pattern (→ Project 11). It builds on that pattern but goes deeper:

CHAPTER 11 PATTERN (Expectation vs. Reality):
  "People EXPECTED X" → "In fact, Y happens"
  External observers made wrong predictions about user behavior.

CHAPTER 14 CONCEPT (Declared vs. Actual):
  "People DECLARE X about themselves" → "But they actually DO Y"
  The SUBJECTS THEMSELVES misrepresent their own behavior.
  
  KEY DIFFERENCE: In Ch 11, outside observers were wrong.
                  In Ch 14, people are wrong about THEMSELVES.

The text provides the most explicit explanation of this concept:

"Il y a un filtre social qui fait que souvent les gens répondent en disant non pas ce qu'ils font, mais ce qu'ils aimeraient faire."

Translation: There is a social filter that makes people respond by saying not what they do, but what they would like to do.

This is called social desirability bias in research methodology: the tendency to present oneself in a favorable light when answering questions.

The text's example:

DECLARED BEHAVIOR:   80% of French people say they eat together as a family
ACTUAL BEHAVIOR:     (Unknown — that's what the cameras will reveal)
FINDUS'S HYPOTHESIS: The declared 80% is probably inflated by social desirability

"Vie rêvée" (dreamed life) = what people WISH they did
"Vie réelle" (real life)    = what people ACTUALLY do

Step 4: Understand WHY Findus Uses Cameras

The text describes a chain of reasoning that explains the methodology:

PROBLEM CHAIN:

STEP 1: Findus needs to develop new food products
STEP 2: Product development requires understanding consumer habits
STEP 3: Current understanding comes from "études déclaratives" (surveys)
STEP 4: Surveys are unreliable because of social desirability bias
STEP 5: People say what they'd LIKE to do, not what they ACTUALLY do
STEP 6: Therefore, Findus needs DIRECT OBSERVATION (cameras)
STEP 7: Cameras capture ACTUAL behavior, bypassing the "social filter"

CONCLUSION: Better understanding of REAL habits 
            → better product development
            → better adapted products

This chain connects the research method to the business goal: cameras → real behavior → better products. The exam will test whether you understand this connection.

QUESTION 2: Que pense la société Findus à propos de l'attitude des Français face aux enquêtes ?

Step 1: Identify the question type

"Que pense Findus" = what does Findus think. "L'attitude des Français face aux enquêtes" = French people's attitude toward surveys. This asks about Findus's ASSESSMENT of how French people respond to traditional surveys. Go to Segment 3 (the problem with existing methods).

Step 2: Find the relevant evidence

The Findus marketing director explains the problem:

"Il y a un filtre social qui fait que souvent les gens répondent en disant non pas ce qu'ils font, mais ce qu'ils aimeraient faire."

And the data point:

"Or les Français déclarent, à 80%, manger ensemble, en famille."

The key concept: people respond to surveys with socially desirable answers rather than truthful ones. They filter their responses through social norms.

Now, what is this behavior called? The French people are not LYING deliberately. They're unconsciously adjusting their answers to match social expectations. They CENSOR themselves — presenting an idealized version of their behavior rather than the real one.

"S'autocensurer" = to self-censor = to filter one's own responses through social norms.

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "Ils y répondent rarement"
(They rarely respond to them.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text doesn't say French people REFUSE to participate in surveys. It says they participate BUT give filtered answers. The problem isn't participation rate — it's answer quality. The 80% statistic shows people DO respond (they declared eating together). The issue is WHAT they respond, not WHETHER they respond.French people DO respond. The problem is their answers are filtered, not that they refuse.❌ PARTICIPATION ≠ ACCURACY — wrong dimension of the problem

Option B: "Ils s'autocensurent souvent"
(They often self-censor.)

TestReasoningVerdict
"Filtre social" (social filter) = self-censorship mechanism. "Non pas ce qu'ils font, mais ce qu'ils aimeraient faire" = they filter out their real behavior and replace it with idealized behavior. "S'autocensurer" (self-censor) perfectly describes this process: unconsciously filtering one's own responses to match social expectations. "Souvent" (often) matches "souvent les gens répondent en disant...""Filtre social" = self-censorship. "Souvent" matches "souvent." Exact conceptual match.✅ MATCH

Option C: "Ils décrivent leur vie réelle"
(They describe their real life.)

TestReasoningVerdict
DIRECT OPPOSITE. The entire point of the text is that French people do NOT describe their real life in surveys. They describe their "vie rêvée" (dreamed life), not their "vie réelle" (real life). The marketing director explicitly says: "Nous souhaitons observer les différences entre la vie rêvée des gens et leur vie réelle." The study exists BECAUSE surveys capture the dreamed life, not the real one.The text's core argument is that surveys capture "vie rêvée," NOT "vie réelle." This option states the exact opposite.❌ DIRECT OPPOSITE — the text's entire thesis is that people DON'T describe real life

This is the most straightforward "opposite trap" in this chapter. The text exists to make the point that surveys DON'T capture real life. An option saying they DO is the text's antithesis.

Option D: "Ils répondent souvent négativement"
(They often respond negatively.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text says people respond with POSITIVE self-images (eating together as a family = positive social behavior). The social filter makes people present themselves FAVORABLY, not negatively. If anything, the bias is toward POSITIVE responses, not negative ones. The 80% claiming to eat together as a family is a POSITIVE declaration.Social desirability bias produces POSITIVE responses, not negative ones. The direction of the bias is wrong.❌ WRONG DIRECTION — bias is toward positive self-presentation, not negativity

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Located the "filtre social" explanation in Segment 3
  2. Understood the concept: people filter responses through social norms
  3. Matched "filtre social" + "non pas ce qu'ils font, mais ce qu'ils aimeraient faire" → "s'autocensurer"
  4. Defeated Trap C by recognizing it as the direct opposite of the text's thesis
  5. Defeated Trap D by identifying the correct direction of the bias (positive, not negative)

The Direction of Adaptation framework:

This is a crucial new concept that applies to any text about a company studying its market:

WHO ADAPTS TO WHOM?

COMPANY → CONSUMER (Market-driven):
  "mieux comprendre les habitudes" → "mieux adapter ses produits"
  Company studies consumers to change its OWN products
  = What this text describes

CONSUMER → COMPANY (Product-driven):
  "développer le goût" → "apprendre à accommoder"
  Company educates consumers to accept its products
  = NOT what this text describes

THE TEST: Is the company LEARNING or TEACHING?
  Learning from consumers → Market-driven (Options A) ✓
  Teaching consumers      → Product-driven (Options C, D) ✗

2. Distinguish Context from Purpose in Research Texts

Research texts typically contain BOTH contextual information (why the research matters) and purpose information (what the research aims to achieve). The exam tests whether you can separate them:

CONTEXT (WHY it matters):
  - Obesity debate
  - Food companies under pressure
  - Consumer hard to understand
  → BACKGROUND that motivates the study

PURPOSE (WHAT it aims to do):
  - Better understand eating habits
  - Observe real behavior vs. declared behavior
  - Improve product development
  → GOAL of the study

CONTEXT ≠ PURPOSE
Obesity is the REASON Findus acts.
Better products is the GOAL of Findus's action.

When an option mixes context with purpose, it's usually wrong. The purpose is what the study will PRODUCE, not what motivated its creation.

3. Determine the Direction of Adaptation

For any text about a company studying consumers:

DIRECTION CHECK:
  □ Is the company trying to CHANGE ITSELF to fit consumers?
    → Market-driven = "adapter ses produits"
    
  □ Is the company trying to CHANGE CONSUMERS to fit its products?
    → Product-driven = "développer le goût," "apprendre à accommoder"
    
  □ Which direction does THIS text describe?
    → Look for: "comprendre," "observer," "appréhender" = LEARNING (market-driven)
    → Look for: "enseigner," "éduquer," "informer" = TEACHING (product-driven)

In this text, EVERY verb related to the study is about LEARNING: "comprendre" (understand), "observer" (observe), "appréhender" (grasp), "s'approcher de la réalité" (get closer to reality). These are all INWARD-directed verbs — the company is absorbing information. No OUTWARD-directed verbs (teach, inform, educate) appear.

4. Track Study Stage (Design → Execution → Results)

When reading about research, immediately determine WHAT STAGE the study is at:

STAGE 1 — DESIGN/LAUNCH:
  Markers: "a décidé de lancer," "ont été sélectionnées," "seront filmés"
  Status: Planning and setup phase
  Results: DON'T EXIST YET

STAGE 2 — EXECUTION:
  Markers: "sont en cours de," "les données montrent"
  Status: Data collection in progress
  Results: EMERGING

STAGE 3 — ANALYSIS:
  Markers: "l'analyse révèle," "les résultats indiquent"
  Status: Data being interpreted
  Results: BEING PROCESSED

STAGE 4 — RESULTS:
  Markers: "l'enquête montre que," "le résultat est"
  Status: Findings published
  Results: AVAILABLE

This text is at Stage 1 (design/launch). Key markers: "a décidé de lancer" (decided to launch), "ont été sélectionnées" (have been selected), "sera filmé" (will be filmed — FUTURE tense). Any option that implies results exist is wrong.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Confuse the Study's Topic with the Study's Method

This is the deepest conceptual trap in title-selection questions:

STUDY'S TOPIC:   French eating habits (what is being studied)
STUDY'S METHOD:   Camera observation (how it's being studied)
TEXT'S SUBJECT:   The method and its rationale (why this approach was chosen)

The TEXT is about the METHOD, not the TOPIC.
A title about "eating habits" alone misses the METHOD dimension.
A title about "understanding the ritual of meals" captures BOTH.

Option A ("new ways of eating") describes EATING HABITS — the study's topic. But the text isn't about eating habits themselves; it's about HOW TO STUDY them. The correct title (B) captures the ACT OF UNDERSTANDING ("mieux appréhender"), not just the subject being understood.

2. Do NOT Confuse "Social Filter" with "Refusal to Participate"

Option A in Q2 says French people "rarely respond" to surveys. The text says they DO respond — but with filtered answers. These are completely different problems:

LOW PARTICIPATION:     People don't answer surveys (quantity problem)
FILTERED RESPONSES:    People answer but inaccurately (quality problem)

The text describes a QUALITY problem, not a QUANTITY problem.
80% of French people DID declare something — they PARTICIPATED.
But what they declared doesn't match reality.

3. Do NOT Assume a Study About Food = a Study About Obesity

The obesity debate is mentioned as CONTEXT — it explains why Findus feels pressure to understand consumers better. But the study itself isn't about obesity:

CONTEXT:   Obesity debate → pressure on food companies
RESPONSE:  Findus launches study on eating habits
PURPOSE:   Better understand real habits → develop better products

The study is triggered BY obesity concerns
but is not ABOUT obesity.
It's ABOUT eating habits and product development.

This is the same principle as Chapter 9's "entry point ≠ subject" (→ Project 9) and Chapter 10's "emotional register ≠ scientific assessment" (→ Project 10). The dramatic framing (obesity) is not the text's actual subject (research methodology).

4. Do NOT Reverse the Direction of Who Learns From Whom

This was the core trap in Options C and D of Q3. The study is designed for Findus to learn FROM consumers. It is NOT designed for Findus to teach consumers:

FINDUS LEARNS FROM CONSUMERS:
  Observe → Understand → Adapt products
  = The text's actual description

FINDUS TEACHES CONSUMERS:
  Inform → Educate → Change preferences
  = NOT in the text

Rule: When a text describes a company conducting research, the company is ALWAYS the learner and the consumer is ALWAYS the subject. Any option that reverses this (company teaches, consumer learns) contradicts the fundamental nature of market research.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Résultat d'une Enquête" Stage Confusion

Option C in Q1 implies the article presents survey RESULTS. But the study is being LAUNCHED — cameras are being installed, families have been selected, filming WILL happen (future tense: "sera filmé"). No results exist.

The word "résultat" is powerfully misleading because:

  • Candidates expect articles about studies to present results
  • The text IS about a study → "therefore it must present results" (wrong assumption)
  • The text actually presents the study's DESIGN, not its FINDINGS

Defense: Check verb tenses. If the text uses future tense for the study ("sera filmé," "se partageront"), the study hasn't happened yet. No results are available.

FUTURE TENSE MARKERS in this text:
  "sera filmé"        → will be filmed (not yet done)
  "se partageront"    → will share (not yet done)
  
These confirm: STAGE 1 (design/launch), NOT Stage 4 (results)

Pitfall 2: The "TV Near Camera" = "TV Influences Food" Misreading

The text mentions a camera near the TV. Some candidates build this logical chain:

CANDIDATE'S CHAIN:  Camera near TV → studying TV's effect on eating → 
                    "Influence de la télévision sur l'alimentation"

But the text's actual logic is:

TEXT'S LOGIC:  People often eat near the TV → 
               camera near TV captures mealtime behavior →
               one of TWO observation points (TV + kitchen)

The camera placement is a METHODOLOGICAL DECISION about where to observe, not a RESEARCH HYPOTHESIS about TV's influence. The other camera is in the kitchen — by the same logic, should we conclude the study is about "the influence of kitchens on eating"?

Pitfall 3: The "Opposite as Correct" Illusion for Q2

Option C in Q2 says French people "describe their real life" in surveys. This is the EXACT OPPOSITE of the text's thesis. Yet some candidates choose it because:

  1. The text discusses "vie réelle" (real life)
  2. The option uses "vie réelle" (real life)
  3. The candidate matches the WORDS without matching the MEANING

The text says Findus wants to discover the "vie réelle" BECAUSE surveys DON'T capture it. The option says surveys DO capture it. Same words, opposite meanings.

TEXT:     "vie réelle" = what cameras will reveal (UNKNOWN from surveys)
OPTION C: "vie réelle" = what surveys already capture (CLAIMED by option)

Same phrase, opposite claims about its source.

Defense: Don't match words — match MEANINGS. When an option uses the text's exact vocabulary, be EXTRA suspicious. Check whether the option's CLAIM matches the text's CLAIM, not just whether the option's WORDS match the text's WORDS.

Pitfall 4: The "Self-Censorship Sounds Too Negative" Hesitation

Some candidates hesitate on Option B ("s'autocensurent") because "censorship" sounds harsh — as if French people are being accused of deliberate deception. They prefer softer options.

But "s'autocensurer" doesn't mean "lie." It means unconsciously filter one's responses through social norms. The "auto-" prefix means "self-" — it's done to oneself, not by an external force. And the text explicitly describes this mechanism: "un filtre social qui fait que souvent les gens répondent en disant non pas ce qu'ils font, mais ce qu'ils aimeraient faire."

The "filtre social" IS self-censorship. It's not deliberate lying — it's unconscious self-editing. "S'autocensurer" is the precise term for this behavior.

"S'AUTOCENSURER" in this context:
  ≠ Deliberately lying
  ≠ Refusing to answer
  = Unconsciously filtering responses through social expectations
  = Presenting an idealized version of behavior
  = "Filtre social"
