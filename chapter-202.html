📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

Texte 102 : Le saumon d'élevage : mine d'or de la Norvège.

La consommation de saumon augmente chaque année, et la Norvège, premier
producteur mondial, doit s'adapter pour répondre à la demande quitte à
industrialiser toujours plus sa production. Multiplier par cinq la
production d'ici 2050, voilà l'objectif ambitieux de la Norvège. Les
exportations norvégiennes ont atteint un record en 2016 avec un chiffre
d'affaire de 7,5 milliards d'euros, rien que pour le saumon. C'est la
France qui se trouve en tête du classement des consommateurs de produits
marins norvégiens : nous consommons en moyenne 2,3 kilos de saumon
norvégien par an.

Cette augmentation constante de la demande pousse le pays scandinave à
mettre en place des projets faramineux, comme par exemple une ferme
offshore en haute mer capable d'accueillir plus d'un million de saumons.
Mais l'industrialisation massive de l'élevage pose des problèmes pour les
saumons sauvages. Le saumon sauvage mis en péril par le saumon industriel.

L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes en ce qui concerne le saumon :
la prolifération des poux de mer et l'évasion des saumons d'élevage. Ces
deux conséquences de l'élevage à très grande échelle mettent en péril
l'écosystème et le patrimoine génétique des saumons sauvages. «Nous
faisons beaucoup d'efforts d'un point de vue technique pour trouver des
solutions pour réduire les traitements chimiques et les remplacer par des
techniques naturelles,» indique Catarina Martins, directrice environnement
de la compagnie Marine Harvest. Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés à des
produits toxiques. Le saumon pourrait aussi contenir des produits toxiques
comme des dioxydes, des pesticides et même des métaux lourds. En 2013, la
Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation de saumon modérée pour les
enfants et les femmes enceintes. Mais le risque, dû notamment à la
pollution des eaux, ne concerne plus uniquement le saumon d'élevage.

«Aujourd'hui même le saumon bio est pollué par les métaux lourds et
notamment le mercure», affirme Jean-Philippe Magnen, ancien élu Europe
Ecologie Les Verts pour qui «Il faut changer le modèle industriel
d'élevage, aujourd'hui il y a une pollution qui est très inquiétante, qui
n'est pas seulement sur le saumon d'élevage puisque les eaux sont polluées
avec des métaux lourds.»


Question 1 : L'article explique :
 - Les pratiques utilisées en vigueur dans l'élevage du saumon

Question 2 : Les éleveurs :
 - Améliorent la couleur des saumons pour le rendre plus appétissant

Question 3 : Les méthodes utilisées servent à :
 - Eliminer parasites présentes dans les saumons

🧠 THE PROBLEM: THE VOICE CONFUSION CRISIS

Chapter 5 introduced the distinction between facts, opinions, and quotes. That was at a foundational level — recognizing THAT different voices exist in a text.

Chapter 21 goes far deeper. The problem is not just that different voices exist. The problem is that in a well-written journalistic article, those voices are woven together so seamlessly that you cannot tell them apart under time pressure.

Look at what happens in Texte 102:

THE VOICE WEAVING IN TEXTE 102:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Read this passage and try to identify WHO is speaking:

  "Ces deux conséquences de l'élevage à très grande 
   échelle mettent en péril l'écosystème et le patrimoine 
   génétique des saumons sauvages. «Nous faisons beaucoup 
   d'efforts d'un point de vue technique pour trouver des 
   solutions pour réduire les traitements chimiques et les 
   remplacer par des techniques naturelles,» indique 
   Catarina Martins, directrice environnement de la 
   compagnie Marine Harvest. Des saumons qui peuvent être 
   exposés à des produits toxiques. Le saumon pourrait 
   aussi contenir des produits toxiques comme des dioxydes, 
   des pesticides et même des métaux lourds."

  NOW: Who said what?
  
  ❓ "mettent en péril l'écosystème" — Who says this?
  ❓ "Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts" — Who says this?
  ❓ "Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés" — Who says this?
  ❓ "Le saumon pourrait aussi contenir" — Who says this?

  IF YOU CANNOT INSTANTLY ANSWER THESE FOUR QUESTIONS,
  YOU HAVE THE VOICE CONFUSION PROBLEM.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Here is why this matters for exam questions:

WHY VOICE CONFUSION CAUSES WRONG ANSWERS:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  SCENARIO 1: A question asks what the ARTICLE explains.
  
  If you attribute Catarina Martins' quote to the journalist,
  you might think the article EXPLAINS that efforts are 
  being made → wrong. The ARTICLE explains the problems.
  MARTINS claims efforts are being made.
  
  SCENARIO 2: A question asks what BREEDERS do.
  
  If you attribute the journalist's reporting to the breeders,
  you might think breeders ACKNOWLEDGE the pollution →
  wrong. The JOURNALIST reports the pollution.
  Martins (representing breeders) talks about EFFORTS.
  
  SCENARIO 3: A question asks about methods used.
  
  If you confuse Martins' claims about REDUCING chemicals
  with the journalist's description of USING chemicals,
  you might think the methods are about "natural techniques"
  → wrong. The current methods ARE chemical treatments.
  Natural techniques are the DESIRED replacement.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

📌 Every time you misattribute a claim to the wrong voice, you shift the meaning of the text. And shifted meaning = wrong answers.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE VOICE IDENTIFICATION SYSTEM

THE FOUR VOICES IN TEXTE 102

Every journalistic article about a controversial topic contains up to four distinct voices. Texte 102 has all four:

THE FOUR VOICES:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  VOICE 1: THE JOURNALIST (NARRATOR)
  ──────────────────────────────────
  → The person who WROTE the article
  → NEUTRAL reporting tone
  → Uses third person ("la Norvège," "le saumon")
  → States facts, presents data, describes situations
  → Does NOT take sides explicitly
  → Identified by: ABSENCE of attribution markers
  
  VOICE 2: THE INDUSTRY EXPERT (CATARINA MARTINS)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Directrice environnement, Marine Harvest
  → DEFENSIVE tone (acknowledges problems, emphasizes efforts)
  → Uses first person plural ("Nous faisons...")
  → Represents the INDUSTRY perspective
  → Identified by: «guillemets» + "indique Catarina Martins"
  
  VOICE 3: THE ENVIRONMENTAL CRITIC (JEAN-PHILIPPE MAGNEN)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Ancien élu Europe Ecologie Les Verts
  → ALARMED tone (emphasizes danger, demands change)
  → Uses first person singular viewpoint
  → Represents the ENVIRONMENTAL perspective
  → Identified by: «guillemets» + "affirme Jean-Philippe Magnen"
  
  VOICE 4: THE INSTITUTIONAL VOICE (NORWAY/GOVERNMENT)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Not a named person but an institutional actor
  → Referenced indirectly by the journalist
  → "la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation modérée"
  → Represents OFFICIAL positions
  → Identified by: Country/institution as subject + past tense

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: THE ATTRIBUTION TRACKING TOOLKIT

TOOL 1: THE GUILLEMET DETECTOR

The most reliable voice indicator in French journalism is the guillemet (« »):

THE GUILLEMET RULE:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ANYTHING INSIDE «guillemets» = SOMEONE ELSE'S WORDS
  → NOT the journalist
  → A QUOTED person or source
  
  ANYTHING OUTSIDE guillemets = THE JOURNALIST'S WORDS
  → The journalist's reporting, description, analysis
  
  IN TEXTE 102:

  «Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts d'un point de vue 
   technique pour trouver des solutions pour réduire 
   les traitements chimiques et les remplacer par des 
   techniques naturelles,»
  → INSIDE guillemets → Catarina Martins speaking
  
  "Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés à des produits 
   toxiques."
  → OUTSIDE guillemets → Journalist reporting
  
  «Aujourd'hui même le saumon bio est pollué par les 
   métaux lourds et notamment le mercure»
  → INSIDE guillemets → Magnen speaking
  
  "En 2013, la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation 
   de saumon modérée"
  → OUTSIDE guillemets → Journalist reporting
  
  THIS RULE IS 100% RELIABLE.
  If it's in guillemets, it's a quote.
  If it's not, it's the journalist.
  No exceptions.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

TOOL 2: THE ATTRIBUTION TAG DECODER

After (or before) every quote, the journalist includes an attribution tag — a short phrase that identifies WHO is speaking and HOW they are speaking:

ATTRIBUTION TAG ANATOMY:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  TAG 1: "indique Catarina Martins, directrice 
          environnement de la compagnie Marine Harvest"

  BREAKDOWN:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                  │
  │  SPEECH VERB: "indique" (indicates/states)       │
  │  → NEUTRAL verb                                  │
  │  → Does not suggest agreement or disagreement    │
  │  → The journalist is simply reporting what she   │
  │    said without judgment                         │
  │                                                  │
  │  NAME: "Catarina Martins"                        │
  │  → Identifies the speaker                        │
  │                                                  │
  │  TITLE: "directrice environnement"               │
  │  → Environmental director                        │
  │  → Her JOB = managing environmental issues       │
  │  → She has a PROFESSIONAL OBLIGATION to defend   │
  │    the company's environmental record             │
  │                                                  │
  │  ORGANIZATION: "compagnie Marine Harvest"        │
  │  → Marine Harvest = one of the world's largest   │
  │    salmon farming companies                      │
  │  → She speaks FOR the industry                   │
  │  → Her words represent the COMPANY'S position    │
  │                                                  │
  │  BIAS INFERENCE:                                 │
  │  → She has financial interest in defending       │
  │    industrial salmon farming                     │
  │  → Her quote about "efforts" is a DEFENSE        │
  │  → She acknowledges the problem (chemicals)      │
  │    but emphasizes the solution (natural methods)  │
  │                                                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  TAG 2: "affirme Jean-Philippe Magnen, ancien élu 
          Europe Ecologie Les Verts"

  BREAKDOWN:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                  │
  │  SPEECH VERB: "affirme" (affirms/asserts)        │
  │  → STRONGER than "indique"                       │
  │  → Suggests conviction, certainty                │
  │  → The journalist chose a verb that conveys      │
  │    Magnen's ASSERTIVE tone                       │
  │                                                  │
  │  NAME: "Jean-Philippe Magnen"                    │
  │  → Identifies the speaker                        │
  │                                                  │
  │  FORMER ROLE: "ancien élu"                       │
  │  → FORMER elected official                       │
  │  → "Ancien" = no longer in office                │
  │  → He speaks from EXPERIENCE, not current power  │
  │                                                  │
  │  POLITICAL PARTY: "Europe Ecologie Les Verts"    │
  │  → The GREEN party                               │
  │  → Environmentalist political movement           │
  │  → His ENTIRE political identity is built on     │
  │    environmental protection                      │
  │                                                  │
  │  BIAS INFERENCE:                                 │
  │  → He has POLITICAL interest in highlighting     │
  │    environmental problems                        │
  │  → His quote about pollution is an ATTACK on     │
  │    the industrial model                          │
  │  → He wants SYSTEMIC CHANGE, not just improvements│
  │                                                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The speech verb matters enormously:

SPEECH VERB SPECTRUM:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NEUTRAL VERBS (journalist doesn't signal opinion):
  → "dit" (says)
  → "indique" (indicates) ← MARTINS
  → "explique" (explains)
  → "précise" (specifies)
  → "note" (notes)
  
  ASSERTIVE VERBS (journalist signals strong conviction):
  → "affirme" (affirms/asserts) ← MAGNEN
  → "soutient" (maintains/argues)
  → "insiste" (insists)
  → "assure" (assures)
  
  DISTANCING VERBS (journalist signals doubt):
  → "prétend" (claims/pretends)
  → "avance" (puts forward)
  → "estime" (estimates/considers)
  → "suggère" (suggests)
  
  CRITICAL VERBS (journalist signals disapproval):
  → "dénonce" (denounces)
  → "déplore" (deplores)
  → "s'alarme" (raises alarm)
  → "regrette" (regrets)

  THE JOURNALIST'S CHOICE OF VERB REVEALS THEIR STANCE:
  
  "indique" for Martins → NEUTRAL presentation
  "affirme" for Magnen → STRONGER, more assertive
  
  The journalist gives Magnen the STRONGER verb.
  This subtly signals that Magnen speaks with MORE 
  conviction — without the journalist explicitly 
  taking sides.

TOOL 3: THE VOICE TRANSITION DETECTOR

The most dangerous moment for voice confusion is the transition — where one voice ends and another begins, or where a quote ends and the journalist resumes:

VOICE TRANSITION MAP FOR PARAGRAPH 3:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  LINE 1-2: JOURNALIST describes two problems
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  "L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes..."
  → VOICE 1 (journalist)
  → You are reading the JOURNALIST'S analysis
  
           ┌─── TRANSITION 1 ───┐
           │  guillemet opens «  │
           └────────────────────┘
  
  LINE 3-5: MARTINS speaks about efforts
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  «Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts...»
  → VOICE 2 (Martins)
  → You are reading HER WORDS, not the journalist's
  
           ┌─── TRANSITION 2 ───────────────┐
           │  guillemet closes »             │
           │  attribution tag follows        │
           │  "indique Catarina Martins..."  │
           └─────────────────────────────────┘
  
  LINE 6: JOURNALIST resumes with a transition sentence
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  "Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés à des 
   produits toxiques."
  → VOICE 1 (journalist) — BACK to journalist
  → This is NOT Martins speaking anymore
  → No guillemets = journalist
  
  LINE 7-8: JOURNALIST continues with details
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  "Le saumon pourrait aussi contenir des produits 
   toxiques comme des dioxydes, des pesticides et 
   même des métaux lourds."
  → VOICE 1 (journalist)
  → The journalist is providing ADDITIONAL information
    that Martins did NOT mention
  → This is the journalist's OWN reporting, not a quote

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  ★ CRITICAL CONFUSION POINT ★
  
  Students often think that EVERYTHING after Martins' 
  quote is still "what Martins said."
  
  WRONG: Martins talked about REDUCING chemicals.
  The journalist THEN talks about TOXIC PRODUCTS.
  These are DIFFERENT claims by DIFFERENT voices.
  
  Martins: "We're trying to replace chemicals with 
            natural techniques" (DEFENSIVE)
  
  Journalist: "Salmon can contain dioxides, pesticides, 
               and heavy metals" (ALARMING)
  
  The journalist's continuation is actually MORE 
  ALARMING than what Martins admitted to.
  
  THIS SHIFT IS DELIBERATE.
  The journalist lets Martins defend herself, THEN 
  immediately presents worse information that Martins 
  did NOT address.

DO 3: DISTINGUISH BETWEEN "THE ARTICLE SAYS" AND "A PERSON IN THE ARTICLE SAYS"

This is the single most tested distinction in comprehension questions:

THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  "THE ARTICLE SAYS" = The journalist's reporting
  → Facts, data, descriptions, analysis
  → EVERYTHING outside guillemets
  → This is the ARTICLE'S position
  
  "A PERSON IN THE ARTICLE SAYS" = An expert's opinion
  → Claims, arguments, defenses, criticisms
  → EVERYTHING inside guillemets
  → This is THAT PERSON'S position
  
  EXAM QUESTION PATTERN:
  
  "L'article explique..." → Answer must come from 
  JOURNALIST'S reporting (Voice 1)
  
  "Selon l'expert..." → Answer must come from 
  THAT EXPERT'S words (Voice 2 or 3)
  
  "Les éleveurs..." → Answer must come from what 
  the text says ABOUT breeders (could be any voice)
  
  DANGER: Attributing Martins' defense to "the article"
  
  If a question asks "What does the article explain?"
  and you answer with Martins' quote about efforts,
  you are attributing Voice 2 to the article.
  
  THE ARTICLE does not say efforts are being made.
  MARTINS says efforts are being made.
  THE ARTICLE reports that MARTINS says this.
  
  There is a LAYER between the article and Martins:
  
  Article → reports that → Martins claims → efforts
  
  The article's OWN explanation is about PROBLEMS,
  not about efforts.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: TREATING QUOTES AS THE ARTICLE'S OPINION

WRONG LOGIC:
  "Martins says they're making efforts to reduce chemicals.
   Therefore, the article says efforts are being made."
  
REALITY:
  The article REPORTS that Martins CLAIMS efforts are 
  being made. The article does not ENDORSE this claim.
  
  In fact, the article IMMEDIATELY AFTER the quote 
  presents information that UNDERMINES Martins' defense:
  → Toxic substances (dioxides, pesticides, heavy metals)
  → Government health warnings for children and pregnant women
  → Pollution extending beyond farmed salmon
  
  THE JOURNALIST'S STRUCTURE:
  Martins: "We're trying!"
  Journalist: "But here are all the terrible things 
              still happening..."
  
  THE STRUCTURE ITSELF is the journalist's comment.
  Placing alarming facts AFTER a defensive quote = 
  the journalist is QUESTIONING the defense without 
  explicitly saying so.

MISTAKE 2: IGNORING THE EXPERT'S INSTITUTIONAL IDENTITY

WRONG APPROACH:
  "Jean-Philippe Magnen says pollution is very concerning."
  → You treat this as an objective scientific statement
  → You give it the same weight as the journalist's data
  
RIGHT APPROACH:
  "Jean-Philippe Magnen, FORMER ELECTED OFFICIAL of the 
   GREEN PARTY, says pollution is very concerning."
  → He is a POLITICIAN, not a scientist
  → He represents an ENVIRONMENTAL political movement
  → His statement is a POLITICAL POSITION, not scientific data
  → It may be TRUE, but it is also MOTIVATED by his 
    political agenda
  
  THIS DOES NOT MEAN HE IS WRONG.
  It means you must RECOGNIZE his perspective:
  → He is MORE ALARMED than the journalist
  → He wants SYSTEMIC CHANGE (political goal)
  → He BROADENS the problem scope (strategic move)
  
  For exam purposes: when a question asks what THE TEXT 
  says, Magnen's quote is EVIDENCE OF A PERSPECTIVE,
  not necessarily the text's conclusion.

MISTAKE 3: CONFLATING THE TWO EXPERTS

THE CONFUSION TRAP:
                                                          
  MARTINS                    MAGNEN
  Industry insider           Political outsider
  Defends current practices  Attacks current model
  "We're making efforts"     "We must change the model"
  Talks about solutions      Talks about problems
  Represents Marine Harvest  Represents Green Party
  Uses "nous" (we = company) Uses "il faut" (one must)
  
  IF YOU CONFUSE THEM:
  → You might think the industry wants to change the model
    (wrong — that's MAGNEN's position)
  → You might think the Green Party says efforts are 
    being made (wrong — that's MARTINS' position)
  
  THE TWO EXPERTS ARE DELIBERATELY OPPOSED.
  The journalist chose them BECAUSE they disagree.
  Confusing them destroys the article's structure.
  
  FIX: When you see a quote, ALWAYS check the name 
  and title. Never assume you know who's speaking 
  without verifying.

MISTAKE 4: MISSING THE JOURNALIST'S VOICE BETWEEN QUOTES

THE INVISIBLE JOURNALIST TRAP:
                                                          
  After Martins' quote ends (»), students often think 
  the journalist "disappears" until the next quote.
  
  BUT: The journalist has 6+ sentences between Martins' 
  quote and Magnen's quote:
  
  → "Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés à des produits 
     toxiques." ← JOURNALIST
  → "Le saumon pourrait aussi contenir des produits 
     toxiques comme des dioxydes, des pesticides et même 
     des métaux lourds." ← JOURNALIST
  → "En 2013, la Norvège recommandait déjà une 
     consommation de saumon modérée pour les enfants

  → "Mais le risque, dû notamment à la pollution des 
     eaux, ne concerne plus uniquement le saumon 
     d'élevage." ← JOURNALIST
  
  THESE SENTENCES ARE THE JOURNALIST'S OWN REPORTING.
  They are NOT Martins' words.
  They are NOT Magnen's words (he hasn't spoken yet).
  
  They are the journalist's INDEPENDENT contribution 
  to the article — and they contain the MOST ALARMING 
  information in the entire text.
  
  IF YOU SKIP THEM (thinking they're "between quotes" 
  filler), you miss the article's CORE content.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "NOUS" AMBIGUITY TRAP

THE PROBLEM: "Nous" appears TWICE in Texte 102 
with DIFFERENT meanings:

  INSTANCE 1 (Paragraph 1):
  "nous consommons en moyenne 2,3 kilos"
  → "Nous" = WE (the French public, including the reader)
  → Speaker: JOURNALIST addressing French readers
  → VOICE 1

  INSTANCE 2 (Paragraph 3):
  «Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts»
  → "Nous" = WE (the company Marine Harvest)
  → Speaker: CATARINA MARTINS
  → VOICE 2

  SAME WORD, DIFFERENT SPEAKERS, DIFFERENT MEANINGS.
  
  If a question asks "Who consumes 2.3 kg per year?"
  → Answer: French consumers (journalist's "nous")
  → NOT: Marine Harvest (Martins' "nous")
  
  If a question asks "Who is making efforts?"
  → Answer: The industry/Marine Harvest (Martins' "nous")
  → NOT: French consumers (journalist's "nous")
  
  FIX: ALWAYS check whether "nous" is inside or 
  outside guillemets. Inside = the quoted person's "we."
  Outside = the journalist's "we" (= French readers).

PITFALL 2: THE INDIRECT SPEECH CONFUSION

THE PROBLEM: The journalist sometimes SUMMARIZES 
someone's position WITHOUT using guillemets.

  "la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation 
   de saumon modérée pour les enfants et les femmes 
   enceintes."
   
  → No guillemets → technically the JOURNALIST speaking
  → But the content is NORWAY'S recommendation
  → This is INDIRECT SPEECH (reported speech)
  → The journalist reports what Norway said WITHOUT 
    quoting Norway directly
  
  WHO IS THE SOURCE?
  → The WORDS are the journalist's
  → The INFORMATION is Norway's
  → This is Voice 1 REPORTING Voice 4
  
  FOR EXAM PURPOSES:
  → If asked "What does the article say?" → YES, 
    this counts as what the article says ✓
  → If asked "What does Norway recommend?" → YES, 
    this is Norway's recommendation ✓
  → If asked "What does the journalist think?" → NO,
    this is Norway's position, not the journalist's ✗
  
  INDIRECT SPEECH = journalist's words, someone else's content

PITFALL 3: THE QUOTE SANDWICH MISREAD

THE STRUCTURE: Quote sandwiches are common in journalism.

  JOURNALIST introduces topic
  → EXPERT speaks (quote)
  → JOURNALIST adds more information
  → DIFFERENT EXPERT speaks (quote)
  
  This creates a "sandwich":
  Journalist bread → Expert filling → Journalist bread → Expert filling
  
  THE TRAP: Reading the "bread" as part of the "filling."
  
  EXAMPLE:
  Martins: «...réduire les traitements chimiques...»  [FILLING 1]
  Journalist: "Des saumons... produits toxiques..."   [BREAD]
  Journalist: "Le saumon pourrait... métaux lourds."  [BREAD]
  Journalist: "En 2013, la Norvège recommandait..."   [BREAD]
  Journalist: "Mais le risque... ne concerne plus..."  [BREAD]
  Magnen: «...même le saumon bio est pollué...»       [FILLING 2]
  
  STUDENTS READ:
  "Martins says chemicals... toxiques... métaux lourds... 
   Magnen says bio is polluted..."
  
  → They skip the BREAD (journalist's own reporting)
  → They attribute the journalist's alarming data 
    to either Martins or Magnen
  
  CORRECT READING:
  → The BREAD (journalist's reporting) is the most 
    IMPORTANT part of the sandwich
  → It contains the article's OWN analysis
  → The quotes are ILLUSTRATIONS of perspectives
  → The journalist's reporting between quotes is the 
    article's BACKBONE

PITFALL 4: THE SPEED-READING VOICE BLUR

DIRECTLY FROM CHAPTER 20:

  When you speed-read Texte 102 under time pressure,
  the four voices BLUR together.
  
  Your brain processes:
  "Salmon... Norway... production... problems... chemicals...
   efforts... toxiques... mercure... pollution..."
  
  All the KEY WORDS are retained, but the ATTRIBUTION 
  is lost. You know WHAT was said but not WHO said it.
  
  THIS IS THE VOICE BLUR.
  
  CONSEQUENCE:
  → A question asks what the ARTICLE explains
  → Your blurred memory says "chemicals and efforts 
    and pollution and mercury"
  → You can't distinguish between what the JOURNALIST 
    reported and what the EXPERTS claimed
  → You pick the answer that matches your blurred memory
  → It might match MARTINS' claim, not the article's point
  → WRONG ANSWER
  
  FIX: The guillemet marking from DO 1 above.
  
  During speed-reading, the ONLY thing you need to 
  register about quotes is:
  1. WHERE they are (paragraph 3 and 4)
  2. WHO speaks (M and JM in margins)
  3. Their ONE-WORD stance (defense / attack)
  
  This takes 5 seconds and prevents the voice blur 
  that costs 30+ seconds of confusion during answering.
