Texte 66 : Baudelaire (texte à trous)

Baudelaire est né à Paris. ...(36)... Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans, était né bien avant la Révolution. Il avait été prêtre mais avait renoncé à cette fonction au moment de la Révolution. Il était très amateur de peinture et Baudelaire lui doit ce qu'il appelle le "culte des images (ma grande, mon unique, ma primitive passion)". ...(37)... La mère de Baudelaire se remarie avec un officier, le commandant Aupick, qui sera nommé général en 1839. ...(38)... C'est un bon élève, très brillant en vers latins. Il annonce à sa famille qu'il veut être écrivain. ...(39)... mais il mène une vie si désordonnée que le général Aupick lui conseille fermement de faire un voyage. Baudelaire part pour Calcutta, mais il ne va pas au terme de la route ; il s'arrête à l'île de la Réunion. ...(40)...

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH THIS TEXT

Step 1: Recognize What's Different From Chapter 6

In Chapter 6 (→ Project 6), you filled gaps in a factual text about recycling. The logic was systemic — mechanisms, methods, and solutions. Each gap was independent; solving Gap 16 didn't affect Gap 17.

This text is fundamentally different in three ways:

FeatureChapter 6 (Recycling)Chapter 7 (Baudelaire)
Text typeInformational system descriptionBiographical narrative
Logic typeSystemic (mechanisms)Chronological (life events)
Gap independenceEach gap stands aloneGaps form a CHAIN — each builds on the last
Coherence typeLogical + thematicChronological + relational + factual
Number of gaps35 (nearly double)

The chain effect is critical. If you fill Gap 37 incorrectly, it may make Gap 38 impossible to solve. In a biography, events follow a timeline. Each gap-filler must respect everything that came before AND set up everything that comes after.

Step 2: Map the Text Architecture — The Life Timeline

Before touching any gap, read the ENTIRE text including the gaps. Identify the life events you can already see:

BIRTH:        "Baudelaire est né à Paris"
              GAP 36 → [birth-related information]

FATHER:       Father was 62, born before the Revolution
              Was a priest, renounced during Revolution
              Loved painting → Baudelaire inherits "cult of images"
              GAP 37 → [something about the father's fate]

MOTHER:       "La mère de Baudelaire SE REMARIE avec un officier"
              Commandant Aupick, named general in 1839
              GAP 38 → [something about Baudelaire's education/youth]

SCHOOL:       "C'est un bon élève, très brillant en vers latins"
              Announces he wants to be a writer
              GAP 39 → [family's reaction to his choice]

CRISIS:       "mais il mène une vie si désordonnée"
              General Aupick advises a voyage
              Departs for Calcutta, stops at Réunion
              GAP 40 → [conclusion of the journey]

Step 3: Identify the NEW Challenge — Biographical Cross-Referencing

A biography creates factual constraints that standard texts don't have. Every gap-filler must be checked against these constraints:

CONSTRAINT 1: TIMELINE CONSISTENCY
  Events must follow chronological order.
  A person can't do something after dying.
  A mother can't remarry if she's alive and already married (unless widowed).

CONSTRAINT 2: RELATIONAL CONSISTENCY  
  If the father is mentioned as dead in Gap 37,
  he can't reappear alive in Gap 40.
  If the mother remarries, the father must be dead or absent.

CONSTRAINT 3: FACTUAL CONSISTENCY
  Known dates must not contradict.
  Baudelaire born in Paris → father aged 62 at birth.
  Revolution = 1789 → father born before 1789.
  General in 1839 → Baudelaire's school years around this date.

CONSTRAINT 4: NARRATIVE ARC
  A biography follows: birth → family → childhood → education → 
  early adulthood → crisis → journey. Each gap must fit its 
  position in this arc.

These constraints give you elimination power that doesn't exist in non-biographical texts. An option that contradicts a biographical fact established elsewhere in the text is automatically wrong — even if it sounds good in isolation.

Step 4: Apply the Sandwich Method With a Twist — The Chain Check

In Chapter 6 (→ Project 6), the Sandwich Method meant checking the sentence before and after each gap. For a biography with five chained gaps, add an extra step:

STANDARD SANDWICH (Chapter 6):
  Top bread → GAP → Bottom bread → Check fit

BIOGRAPHICAL CHAIN CHECK (Chapter 7):
  Top bread → GAP → Bottom bread → Check fit
                                  → ALSO check: does this gap-filler 
                                    contradict anything established in 
                                    a PREVIOUS gap?
                                  → ALSO check: does this gap-filler 
                                    make a FUTURE gap impossible?

This means you should solve the easier gaps first to establish facts, then use those facts to constrain the harder gaps.

GAP 36: After Baudelaire's birth, before his father's description

Step 1: Identify the sandwich

TOP BREAD:    "Baudelaire est né à Paris."

FILLING:       GAP 36 = ???

BOTTOM BREAD: "Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans, était né bien avant 
               la Révolution."

Step 2: Decode the bottom bread signal

The bottom bread starts with "Mais" (But). As you learned in Chapter 5 (→ Project 5: concessive structure), "mais" signals a contrast. Whatever fills the gap, the bottom bread must CONTRAST with it.

The bottom bread talks about the father being 62 years old. "Mais... âgé de 62 ans" — "But his father, aged 62..." The "mais" creates a contrast with something about AGE.

If the gap mentions a YOUNG age, then "But his father was 62" creates a perfect contrast: someone young vs. father being old.

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "Il est le seul garçon, après trois filles."
(He is the only boy, after three girls.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ WEAKBeing the only boy doesn't contrast with the father being 62. "He's the only boy. BUT his father was 62." — what's the "but" about? There's no logical opposition between birth order and father's age.
Biographical❌ FAILThe text never mentions siblings again. Three sisters play no role in the narrative. This introduces characters that disappear.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — no contrast for "mais," introduces orphan characters

Option B: "Sa mère, fragile, meurt à sa naissance."
(His mother, fragile, dies at his birth.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical≈ Partial"Mother dies" → "But father was 62" — weak contrast at best.
Biographical❌ FATAL FAILLater in the text: "La mère de Baudelaire se remarie avec un officier." A dead mother CANNOT remarry. This is a direct factual contradiction with a sentence that appears later in the text.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — mother can't remarry if dead. Biographical cross-reference kills this option.

This is the chain check in action. You didn't just check the sandwich — you checked the ENTIRE text. This is the new skill unique to biographical textes à trous.

Option C: "Dans la famille, sa naissance est très attendue."
(In the family, his birth is eagerly awaited.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ WEAK"His birth was awaited. BUT his father was 62." The "mais" doesn't create a meaningful contrast. Why would being awaited contrast with the father's age?
Thematic≈ PartialAbout birth, which fits the position.
Biographical✅ No contradictionNothing in this option contradicts later facts.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — no meaningful contrast for "mais"

Option D: "À sa naissance, sa mère a 28 ans."
(At his birth, his mother is 28 years old.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical✅ PERFECT"Mother is 28. BUT father is 62." Young mother contrasted with old father. The age gap (34 years!) is striking, and "mais" perfectly signals this surprising contrast.
Thematic✅ PASSAbout birth circumstances — fits the position perfectly.
Biographical✅ PASSA 28-year-old mother can later remarry (she'd be in her 30s). No contradiction.
Grammatical✅ PASS"À sa naissance" mirrors the birth context. "Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans" follows naturally as a contrasting detail.

Verdict: ✅ PASSES ALL TESTS

✅ ANSWER: D — À sa naissance, sa mère a 28 ans.

The logical flow with D inserted:

Baudelaire was born in Paris.
→ At his birth, his mother was 28.          [YOUNG mother]
→ BUT his father, aged 62, was born         [OLD father — contrast!]
   well before the Revolution.

The "mais" creates the contrast:  Mother 28 ←→ Father 62
                                  Young    ←→   Old

Key lesson: The word "mais" was the decoder ring. It demanded a contrast. Only Option D provided an element (age 28) that contrasts meaningfully with the bottom bread's element (age 62).

GAP 37: After the father's love of painting, before the mother's remarriage

Step 1: Identify the sandwich

TOP BREAD:    "...Baudelaire lui doit ce qu'il appelle le 'culte des images 
               (ma grande, mon unique, ma primitive passion)'."

FILLING:       GAP 37 = ???

BOTTOM BREAD: "La mère de Baudelaire se remarie avec un officier, 
               le commandant Aupick..."

Step 2: Decode the bottom bread signal

The bottom bread says the mother "se remarie" (remarries). This is an extremely powerful biographical constraint:

For a mother to REMARRY, she must:
  1. Have been married before (she was — to Baudelaire's father)
  2. No longer be married (either widowed or divorced)
  
In 19th-century France, divorce was essentially impossible.
Therefore: THE FATHER MUST HAVE DIED before the mother could remarry.

The gap must explain what happened to the father that freed the mother to remarry. The most logical event: the father's death.

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "Ce père meurt en 1827."
(This father dies in 1827.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical✅ PERFECTFather dies → mother is widowed → mother remarries. The chain is: death → widowhood → remarriage. This is the necessary event that makes the bottom bread possible.
Thematic✅ PASSContinues the paragraph about the father, then transitions to the mother.
Biographical✅ PASSBaudelaire was born in 1821. Father dies in 1827 → Baudelaire is 6. Mother remarries later. Chronologically sound.
Grammatical✅ PASS"Ce père" (this father) refers to the father just described. Natural reference.

Verdict: ✅ PASSES ALL TESTS

Option B: "La peinture ne l'intéresse donc pas autant que la poésie."
(Painting therefore doesn't interest him as much as poetry.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAILThe top bread just said Baudelaire owes his father the "cult of images" — his "great, unique, primitive passion." This means painting/images are EXTREMELY important to him. Saying painting doesn't interest him much directly contradicts the top bread.
Biographical❌ FAILDoesn't explain why the mother remarries. No bridge to the bottom bread.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — contradicts the top bread's "primitive passion"

Option C: "Le fils découvre ainsi la peinture en 1827."
(The son thus discovers painting in 1827.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAIL"Discovers painting in 1827" → then mother remarries. There's no causal or logical connection between discovering painting and the mother's remarriage. The gap doesn't bridge to the bottom bread.
Biographical❌ SUSPICIOUSBaudelaire was born in 1821. In 1827, he's 6 years old. "Discovering painting at 6" is plausible, but it doesn't explain the transition to the mother's remarriage.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — no bridge to the mother's remarriage

Option D: "Père et fils se souviennent de la révolution."
(Father and son remember the Revolution.)

TestResultReasoning
Biographical❌ FATAL FAILThe Revolution was in 1789. Baudelaire was born in 1821 — 32 years after the Revolution. He cannot possibly "remember" it. This is a factual impossibility.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — biographical impossibility

✅ ANSWER: A — Ce père meurt en 1827.

The logical flow with A inserted:

...Baudelaire owes his father the "cult of images."
→ This father dies in 1827.                     [FATHER'S DEATH]
→ Baudelaire's mother remarries with an officer. [CONSEQUENCE: remarriage]

CAUSE:  Father dies
EFFECT: Mother is free to remarry

Key lesson: The bottom bread's verb "se remarie" was the decoder. Remarriage requires prior widowhood. Only Option A provides the necessary prior event (death). This is biographical cause-effect reasoning — the new skill of this chapter.

Fact established for chain check: The father is now confirmed dead as of 1827. Any future option mentioning the father alive after 1827 is automatically wrong.

GAP 38: After the mother's remarriage, before the school description

Step 1: Identify the sandwich

TOP BREAD:    "...le commandant Aupick, qui sera nommé général en 1839."

FILLING:       GAP 38 = ???

BOTTOM BREAD: "C'est un bon élève, très brillant en vers latins."

Step 2: Analyze both breads

Top bread: Mentions 1839 — the year Aupick is named general. This gives us a time marker.

Bottom bread: Describes Baudelaire as a good student, brilliant in Latin verse. This places him in school.

The gap must bridge from the 1839 date to a description of school performance. What kind of event connects a year to being described as a good student?

A school milestone in 1839 would do it. Baudelaire was born in 1821 → in 1839 he is 18 years old → the age for the baccalauréat (French school-leaving exam).

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "Baudelaire assiste au décès de son père."
(Baudelaire witnesses his father's death.)

TestResultReasoning
Biographical❌ FATAL FAILChain check: In Gap 37, we established that the father died in 1827. Baudelaire was born in 1821 → he was 6 in 1827. Now Gap 38 is positioned around 1839. The father has been dead for 12 years. Baudelaire cannot witness his father's death in 1839 when the father died in 1827. Also, at age 6, "assiste au décès" is debatable, but the timing is completely wrong here.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — father already dead (chain check)

Option B: "La même année, Baudelaire est reçu à son baccalauréat."
(The same year, Baudelaire passes his baccalauréat.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical✅ PASS"The same year" = 1839. Baudelaire passes his baccalauréat. Then "He is a good student, brilliant in Latin verse" — this describes his academic abilities, perfectly consistent with passing an exam.
Biographical✅ PASSBorn 1821 + 1839 = 18 years old. Baccalauréat at 18 is the standard age. Historically accurate.
Grammatical✅ PASS"La même année" connects to 1839 just mentioned. "C'est un bon élève" then elaborates on his academic profile.

Verdict: ✅ PASSES ALL TESTS

Option C: "Alors, Baudelaire s'engage dans l'armée."
(Then, Baudelaire enlists in the army.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAIL"Enlists in the army" → "He is a good student." If he's in the army, why describe him as a student? Military service and school are different life phases. The bottom bread talks about school, not military life.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — military service contradicts student description

Option D: "Plus tard, Baudelaire s'enfuit et abandonne les études."
(Later, Baudelaire runs away and abandons his studies.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAIL"Abandons studies" → then "He is a good student." If he abandoned his studies, how can the next sentence describe him as a good student? This is a direct sequential contradiction. You can't abandon studies and then be described as studying well.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — abandoning studies contradicts being a good student

✅ ANSWER: B — La même année, Baudelaire est reçu à son baccalauréat.

The logical flow with B inserted:

...Aupick named general in 1839.
→ The same year, Baudelaire passes his baccalauréat. [MILESTONE: 1839]
→ He is a good student, brilliant in Latin verse.     [ELABORATION: abilities]
→ He announces he wants to be a writer.               [CONSEQUENCE: career choice]

TIMELINE: 1839 → baccalauréat → good student → wants to write

Key lesson: The time marker "la même année" was the anchor. It connected to 1839 and placed the event precisely. The bottom bread's description of academic ability confirmed the school context. Options C and D were eliminated by sequential logic — what comes next must be compatible with what follows.

GAP 39: After announcing he wants to be a writer, before the disorderly life

Step 1: Identify the sandwich

TOP BREAD:    "Il annonce à sa famille qu'il veut être écrivain."

FILLING:       GAP 39 = ???

BOTTOM BREAD: "mais il mène une vie si désordonnée que le général 
               Aupick lui conseille fermement de faire un voyage."

Step 2: Decode the bottom bread signal

The bottom bread starts with "mais" (but) — the same contrast signal you decoded in Gap 36. Whatever fills the gap, "mais" must create a meaningful opposition.

The bottom bread says: "BUT he leads such a disorderly life that..."

For "mais" to work, the gap must say something positive or permissive that the disorderly life then contradicts:

GAP 39:       [Something positive/accepting]
BOTTOM BREAD: "MAIS il mène une vie si désordonnée..."
               (BUT he leads such a disorderly life...)

CONTRAST:     Acceptance/positivity ←→ Disorder/negativity

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "On ne s'oppose pas à cette idée."
(No one opposes this idea.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical✅ PERFECTHis family doesn't oppose his wish to be a writer. BUT he leads a disorderly life. The contrast works: acceptance of his career choice → BUT his lifestyle is problematic. The family accepts the WHAT (being a writer) but then objects to the HOW (his disorderly behavior).
Thematic✅ PASSDirectly responds to the announcement of wanting to be a writer.
Grammatical✅ PASS"Cette idée" = the idea just mentioned (being a writer). "Mais" then introduces the problem.

Verdict: ✅ PASSES ALL TESTS

Option B: "Il renonce donc un moment à l'écriture."
(He therefore gives up writing for a moment.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAIL"He gives up writing" → "BUT he leads a disorderly life." The "mais" doesn't create a meaningful contrast. Giving up writing and living in disorder aren't opposites — they could be related consequences. Also, "donc" (therefore) implies his announcement led to giving up, which makes no sense: he announces he WANTS to write, therefore he STOPS writing?

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — illogical "donc" + weak contrast

Option C: "Sa vie est alors bien réglée."
(His life is then well-ordered.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAIL"His life is well-ordered" → "BUT he leads a disorderly life." This creates a temporal contradiction, not a contrast. You can't have a well-ordered life and a disorderly life at the same time. "Mais" signals contrast between two different aspects, not between two contradictory descriptions of the same thing. Also, "alors" (then) and the bottom bread's "mais" create an impossible sequence: his life IS ordered → BUT his life IS disordered. These can't coexist.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — self-contradictory within the same timeframe

Option D: "On ne fête pas ses 25 ans."
(His 25th birthday is not celebrated.)

TestResultReasoning
Logical❌ FAILNot celebrating a birthday has no logical connection to either announcing a writing career or leading a disorderly life. This is a random, disconnected fact.
Biographical❌ SUSPICIOUSWhy specifically 25? The text provides no context for this age. It feels arbitrary and unconnected to the narrative arc.

Verdict: ❌ ELIMINATED — disconnected from both breads

✅ ANSWER: A — On ne s'oppose pas à cette idée.

The logical flow with A inserted:

He announces to his family that he wants to be a writer.
→ No one opposes this idea.                    [ACCEPTANCE]
→ BUT he leads such a disorderly life that     [CONTRAST: disorder]
   General Aupick firmly advises a voyage.     [CONSEQUENCE: forced travel]

CONTRAST:     Family accepts his choice ←→ BUT his lifestyle is problematic
              (the WHAT is okay)          (the HOW is not)

Key lesson: This is the second time "mais" served as the decoder (after Gap 36). The pattern is now firmly established:

"MAIS" PATTERN IN BIOGRAPHICAL TEXTS:
  GAP must contain something that "mais" can contrast with.
  If the bottom bread is NEGATIVE (disorderly life),
  the gap must be POSITIVE or NEUTRAL (acceptance, order, success).
  
  Option C SEEMED to fit this pattern (well-ordered → but disordered),
  but failed because the contradiction was WITHIN the same domain
  (lifestyle vs. lifestyle), creating impossibility, not contrast.
  
  Option A succeeded because the contrast was BETWEEN domains:
  career acceptance vs. lifestyle problems.

✅ WHAT SHOULD BE DONE

1. Build the Biographical Timeline BEFORE Solving Any Gap

This is the chapter's defining strategy. Before attempting any gap, read the entire text and construct a life timeline with what you already know:

KNOWN FACTS (before solving gaps):

  - Father: 62 at birth, ex-priest, loved painting
  - Mother: remarries officer Aupick (general in 1839)
  - Good student, brilliant in Latin verse
  - Wants to be a writer
  - Disorderly life → Aupick advises voyage
  - Departs for Calcutta → stops at Réunion

TIMELINE:
  Birth → [GAP36] → Father described → [GAP37] → Mother remarries → 
  [GAP38] → Good student → Writer ambition → [GAP39] → Disorder → 
  Voyage → Réunion → [GAP40]

This timeline is your master reference. Every gap-filler must fit into it without creating contradictions.

2. Use the Chain Check for Every Option

In Chapter 6 (→ Project 6), each gap was independent. In a biography, every gap affects every other gap. Always check:

CHAIN CHECK QUESTIONS:
  □ Does this option contradict a fact from an EARLIER part of the text?
  □ Does this option contradict a fact I ESTABLISHED in a previous gap?
  □ Does this option make a LATER gap impossible to fill?
  □ Does this option introduce a character or event that never appears again?

In this chapter, the chain check eliminated:

  • Q38-A (father's death): Father already dead from Gap 37
  • Q40-D (finding father): Father dead since Gap 37
  • Q36-B (mother's death): Mother remarries later in the text

3. Decode "Mais" as a Contrast Demand

The word "mais" appeared as a bottom-bread signal in two gaps (36 and 39). Each time, it demanded a specific type of contrast:

"Mais" in a biography always signals that the gap must set up something the next sentence will complicate, oppose, or qualify.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Solve Gaps in Isolation

The biggest mistake in a biographical texte à trous is treating each gap as independent. If you solve Gap 36 without considering that the mother remarries later, you might choose Option B (mother dies) — which creates an impossible contradiction.

Rule: Always scan the ENTIRE text before solving ANY gap. Note which characters appear later and what they do. This prevents you from "killing" a character who reappears.

2. Do NOT Confuse "Mais" Contrast with "Mais" Contradiction

Gap 39 illustrates this perfectly:

CONTRAST (valid):      Family accepts → BUT lifestyle is disorderly
                       (Two different domains: career choice vs. daily behavior)

CONTRADICTION (invalid): Life is orderly → BUT life is disorderly  
                         (Same domain, opposite claims = impossibility)

Option C in Gap 39 ("Sa vie est alors bien réglée") creates a contradiction, not a contrast. "Mais" connects two DIFFERENT aspects, not two opposite claims about the SAME aspect.

Test: If you can't hold both statements as simultaneously true about different things, it's a contradiction, not a contrast.

"Mother is 28 BUT father is 62"     → Both true simultaneously ✓ = CONTRAST
"Family accepts BUT life is messy"  → Both true simultaneously ✓ = CONTRAST  
"Life is ordered BUT life is messy" → Cannot both be true      ✗ = CONTRADICTION

3. Do NOT Ignore Historical/Factual Impossibilities

Option D in Gap 37 says father and son "remember the Revolution." The Revolution was 1789. Baudelaire was born 1821. He cannot remember something that happened 32 years before his birth.

This seems obvious, but under exam pressure, candidates often skip the mental arithmetic. Always verify dates:

Date check for Gap 37-D:
  Revolution: 1789
  Baudelaire born: 1821
  1821 - 1789 = 32 years AFTER
  → Baudelaire cannot remember the Revolution
  → ELIMINATED

4. Do NOT Choose an Emotionally Compelling Option Without Fact-Checking

Option D in Gap 40 ("he finally finds his father again") is emotionally beautiful — a son reunited with his long-lost father on a distant island. It would make a great novel. But it's factually impossible because the father died in 1827.

The exam sometimes offers the most "touching" or "dramatic" option as a trap. Emotional appeal is not evidence. Facts are evidence.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Dead Character Reappears" Error

This is the signature pitfall of biographical textes à trous. A character dies or disappears early in the text, and a later gap option brings them back.

Established FactTrap OptionWhy It Fails
Father dies in 1827 (Gap 37)Gap 38-A: Baudelaire witnesses father's death (around 1839)12 years too late
Father dies in 1827 (Gap 37)Gap 40-D: Baudelaire finds his father on RéunionDead people don't travel
Mother remarries (text)Gap 36-B: Mother dies at birthDead people don't remarry

Defense: After establishing a death or departure, mentally mark that character as "unavailable." Any option bringing them back is automatically wrong.

Pitfall 2: The "Elegant but Disconnected" Sentence

Some options are beautifully written and thematically relevant but don't bridge to the bottom bread. Option C in Gap 37 ("The son discovers painting in 1827") is elegant and connects to the father's love of painting. But it doesn't explain why the mother remarries.

Defense: Beauty and relevance aren't enough. The gap-filler must BRIDGE both breads. Always check: "Does this sentence make the NEXT sentence logical?"

Pitfall 3: The "Geographical Confusion" Trap

Option A in Gap 40 says Baudelaire discovers life "in India." But the text says he stopped at Réunion, which is a French island in the Indian Ocean, NOT part of India. The Indian Ocean is not India.

CALCUTTA:  In India (his destination — never reached)
RÉUNION:   French island in the Indian Ocean (where he stopped)
INDIA ≠ RÉUNION

Candidates who confuse "Indian Ocean" with "India" will fall for this trap.

Pitfall 4: The "Five Gaps = Five Independent Problems" Mindset

The most dangerous mindset is treating this as five separate mini-exercises. In reality, it's ONE narrative with five missing pieces. Solving one gap gives you information that constrains other gaps:

SOLVING ORDER AND INFORMATION FLOW:
  Gap 37 (father dies) → constrains Gap 38 (can't witness death again)
                       → constrains Gap 40 (can't find father)
  Gap 36 (mother alive) → confirmed by text (mother remarries)
                        → constrains Gap 36 options (mother can't die)
