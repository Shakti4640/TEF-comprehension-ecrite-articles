CHAPTER 5: A STAR PARTY FOR 800 EUROS

📰 Texte 92 — Service Description | Phase 1: Foundation Thinking

📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

Ils vous organisent une soirée de star !

Vous rêvez de vous faire passer, l'espace de quelques heures, pour une star ? De débarquer en limousine dans les boites de nuits les plus sélectes de la ville ? Frappez à la porte de la société « Soirée de star ». Présente dans plusieurs villes européennes, elle se propose d'organiser des soirées pour le compte de particuliers et d'entreprises.

Le programme ? Du sur-mesure. Une formule de base à 300 euros propose un accompagnement d'une heure en berline de luxe et des entrées dans les restaurants ou les boites de nuits les plus huppés. À vous ensuite d'agrémenter votre soirée à votre guise : Accompagnement durant 1 à 8 heures, séance de relooking, commande de fans en furie ou de paparazzi à l'entrée des lieux où vous vous déplacez… Une large palette de possibilités permet de pimenter votre soirée.

Après seulement dix mois d'existence, « Soirée de star » réalise 80 % de ses ventes auprès de particuliers. « L'essentiel des demandes porte sur l'organisation d'anniversaires surprises ou de soirées de célibataires », note Frank Marie, l'un des deux fondateurs. Le prix moyen dépensé pour une soirée est d'environ 800 euros.

D'après le Parisien

🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's NEW in this chapter

Every chapter so far asked about WHAT the text says or WHAT someone thinks. This chapter introduces two brand-new question types:

NEW Question Type 1: "Ce document est..." — What TYPE of document is this?

This tests whether you can identify the nature of the text itself — not its content, but its format and source. Is it a news article? An advertisement? A letter? A promotional offer? This requires you to look at HOW the text is written, not just WHAT it says.

NEW Question Type 2: Distinguishing OBJECTIVE from RESULT

Question 18 asks about the company's objective (what they AIM to do). Question 19 asks about the company's actual performance (what they've ACHIEVED). These are different things:

OBJECTIVE  = What the company WANTS / TRIES to do
RESULT     = What the company HAS ACTUALLY accomplished

Mixing these up is one of the most common mistakes in commercial text comprehension.

The "Document Type" question — How to identify it

When asked "Ce document est...", you need to analyze TWO things:

1. WHO wrote it? (a journalist? the company itself? an institution?)
2. WHAT is the purpose? (to inform? to sell? to promote? to educate?)

These two answers together determine the document type:

Who wrote itPurposeDocument type
JournalistTo inform about a company/eventArticle de journal (newspaper article)
The company itselfTo sell its product/servicePublicité (advertisement)
The company itselfTo offer a temporary discountOffre promotionnelle (promotional offer)
A travel agencyTo describe a vacation packageProgramme de vacances (vacation program)

Applying the method (Rule #1)

STEP 1: Read all questions first

Q17: "Ce document est..." → WHAT TYPE of document is this?
Q18: "L'objectif de la société..." → WHAT is the company's GOAL?
Q19: "Soirée de stars..." → HOW is the company PERFORMING?

Three very different questions:

Q17 → META question (about the document ITSELF)
Q18 → CONTENT question (about the company's aim)
Q19 → ANALYSIS question (about actual results)

STEP 2: Read the title AND the source

Ils vous organisent une soirée de star !
D'après le Parisien

Two critical pieces of information here:

  1. The title sounds exciting and commercial ("une soirée de star!")
  2. The source says "D'après le Parisien" — this is FROM A NEWSPAPER

This source line is gold for Q17. If it comes from "le Parisien" (a well-known French newspaper), the document is a newspaper article — even if the content READS like an advertisement.

🚨 NEW PRINCIPLE: Always check the source line. "D'après [newspaper name]" = it's a newspaper article, regardless of how commercial the content sounds.

🔬 THE "ARTICLE vs. ADVERTISEMENT" DISTINCTION

This is a distinction you'll need throughout your exam. Here's how to tell them apart:

NEWSPAPER ARTICLE                    ADVERTISEMENT
├─ Written by a journalist           ├─ Written by the company
├─ Source: "D'après [journal]"       ├─ No journalistic source
├─ Quotes people as sources          ├─ Directly addresses "you"
├─ Third person: "la société..."     ├─ First person: "nous vous proposons"
├─ Balanced: facts + figures         ├─ Only positives, no data analysis
├─ Informs the reader                ├─ Persuades the reader to buy
└─ Verb: "note," "explique"          └─ Verb: "profitez," "découvrez"

The trap in this text: The opening paragraph ("Vous rêvez de...? Frappez à la porte de...") SOUNDS like advertising. It uses "vous" directly and is seductive. But this is just the journalist's engaging writing style. The SOURCE and STRUCTURE reveal it's an article.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #8: To identify document type, check the SOURCE LINE and the STRUCTURE — not just the tone. A journalist can write engagingly about a business without the text being an advertisement.

🔬 THE "OBJECTIVE vs. RESULT" DISTINCTION — Critical for Q18 and Q19

This is where many students get confused. Let's establish the difference clearly:

OBJECTIVE (Q18)                      RESULT (Q19)
├─ What the company AIMS to do       ├─ What the company HAS ACHIEVED
├─ Found in descriptive paragraphs   ├─ Found in data/statistics paragraphs
├─ Uses present tense: "se propose"  ├─ Uses past/present: "réalise 80%"
├─ Talks about PLANS and SCOPE       ├─ Talks about NUMBERS and OUTCOMES
└─ "Pour le compte de... en Europe"  └─ "80% de ses ventes... 10 mois"

Q18 asks about OBJECTIVE → Look at paragraph 1 (what they offer, to whom, where)
Q19 asks about RESULT → Look at paragraph 3 (actual sales data, client breakdown)

🔬 THE STATISTICS READING METHOD

This question tests your ability to interpret a percentage correctly:

TEXT: "80% de ses ventes auprès de particuliers"

What this MEANS:
→ 80% of revenue comes from individuals
→ Only 20% comes from businesses
→ Individuals are BY FAR the main client base
→ The company WORKS primarily thanks to individuals

What this does NOT mean:
→ The company has few individual clients (OPPOSITE)
→ The company needs to find more business clients (NOT stated)
→ The company is failing financially (NOT stated)

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #9: When the text contains a percentage or statistic, read it carefully and understand what it ACTUALLY says. Then check if the answer choice CORRECTLY interprets that statistic. Wrong answers often INVERT the meaning of data.

Here's a quick reference for common statistical misinterpretations:

Text saysWrong answer might sayThe error
"80% from individuals""Few individual clients"Inverts the proportion
"After only 10 months""After a very long time"Inverts the timeframe
"Average price 800€""Price too high for clients"Adds a judgment not in text
"Present in several cities""Present in one city"Reduces the scope

🎯 WHAT COULD BE DONE

The Commercial Text Reading Framework

When you encounter a text about a business or service, organize your reading into four layers:

LAYER 1: WHAT does the company do?
         → "organiser des soirées"
         
LAYER 2: WHO is it for?
         → "particuliers et entreprises"
         
LAYER 3: HOW does it work?
         → "formule de base à 300€" + customizable options
         
LAYER 4: HOW is it performing?
         → "80% particuliers" + "800€ moyen" + "10 mois d'existence"

Questions typically target one specific layer. Identify which layer the question asks about BEFORE looking at the answer choices.

The "Three Paragraphs = Three Layers" Pattern

This article has a very clean structure that appears often in commercial/service texts:

PARAGRAPH 1: Introduction + WHAT the company does + WHO it's for
             → Answers "Ce document est..." and "L'objectif est..."

PARAGRAPH 2: HOW the service works (details, options, prices)
             → Answers "Comment ça fonctionne?" questions

PARAGRAPH 3: RESULTS + DATA (statistics, quotes from founders)
             → Answers "Comment ça marche?" performance questions

Knowing this structure helps you go DIRECTLY to the right paragraph for each question.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #14: The "Tone vs. Type" Confusion 🎭

Text SOUNDS like: an advertisement (exciting, "vous rêvez de...", 
                  describes a glamorous service)
Text ACTUALLY IS: a newspaper article about a company
                  (source = Le Parisien, journalist quotes founder)

Many texts about exciting products or services are written in an engaging, almost promotional style. But the source and structure determine the type, not the tone.

How to avoid it: Develop the reflex of checking the source line BEFORE analyzing tone. If it says "D'après [newspaper]," the case is closed — it's an article.

PITFALL #15: The "Percentage Inversion" Trap 📊

Text: "80% de ses ventes auprès de particuliers"
      = HUGE success with individuals

Wrong answer B: "a peu de succès auprès des particuliers"
               = LITTLE success with individuals

The trap: The answer uses the SAME key words (succès, particuliers)
          but with "peu de" which REVERSES the meaning.

This is an evolution of the word-from-text trap (Rule #2 from Chapters 1-2), combined with a negation/minimizer:

Text words:  "ventes" + "particuliers" = HIGH sales to individuals
Trap answer: "peu de succès" + "particuliers" = LOW success with individuals
             ↑ INVERTER added

How to avoid it: When you see text-words in an answer (Rule #2 alert!), check for inverters — small words that flip the meaning:

InverterEffect
peu delittle / few → REDUCES
troptoo much → NEGATIVE judgment
à peinebarely → MINIMIZES
ne...queonly → RESTRICTS
faute defor lack of → IMPLIES ABSENCE

PITFALL #16: The "Invented Strategy" Trap 🔮

Answer C: "va se tourner vers les entreprises"
          = will shift toward business clients

The text describes CURRENT performance (80% individuals). It says NOTHING about future strategy. Answer C INVENTS a future plan that doesn't exist in the text.

This connects to Rule #3 (Chapter 1, expanded in Chapter 3): Don't extrapolate. Don't predict. Don't invent. Only answer from what the text STATES.

How to avoid it: When an answer uses future tense ("va se tourner") or implies a planned change, check: does the text actually ANNOUNCE this change? If not, the answer is fabricated.
