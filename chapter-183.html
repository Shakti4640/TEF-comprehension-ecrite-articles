CHAPTER 2: WHAT IS THE ARTICLE ACTUALLY ABOUT? — IDENTIFYING PURPOSE IN TEXTE 101

Foundation Thinking | Phase 1 of 5

📄 THE ARTICLE

We established the full text and its structural map in Chapter 1. Here, we focus exclusively on the relationship between that structure and this question:

Question 24 : cet article a pour but

 A. de dénoncer les thèses avant-gardistes publiées par un chercheur.
 B. de convaincre des avantages de la future révolution technologique.
 C. d'informer des évolutions technologiques avancées par un scientifique.
 D. de présenter des réalisations révolutionnaires d'une vedette de la science.

This is the single most important question type in Compréhension Écrite. It appears in nearly every exam. It asks: WHY does this text exist? What was the author trying to DO?

If you answer this question correctly, you understand the text. If you answer it wrong, every other answer becomes unreliable.

🧠 THE PROBLEM: PURPOSE IS INVISIBLE

Here is the difficulty most exam takers face with purpose questions:

WHAT THE TEXT SAYS:
  → Ray Kurzweil predicts things about technology
  → Robots, brain uploads, human-machine fusion
  → Debate will grow

WHAT THE QUESTION ASKS:
  → Why did the JOURNALIST write this?
  
THE GAP:
  The text talks about KURZWEIL'S ideas.
  The question asks about the JOURNALIST'S intention.
  
  These are TWO DIFFERENT THINGS.

Most students confuse WHAT the text talks about with WHY the text was written. They are not the same.

A journalist can WRITE ABOUT a revolutionary scientist without trying to CONVINCE you that the scientist is right. A journalist can DESCRIBE controversial predictions without DENOUNCING them.

📌 The subject of the text is Kurzweil. The purpose of the text belongs to the journalist. Question 24 asks about the journalist, NOT about Kurzweil.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE PURPOSE IDENTIFICATION FRAMEWORK

STEP 1: RECALL YOUR GENRE IDENTIFICATION (From Chapter 1)

In Chapter 1, you identified:

Source:  Courrier international (press)
Genre:  Informative journalistic article
Tone:   Neutral, reporting
Author: Journalist (not Kurzweil himself)

This identification already narrows your answer dramatically. Watch:

If the genre is...The purpose is likely to...The purpose is unlikely to...
Informative article (THIS TEXT)inform, present, report, describeconvince, denounce, sell, argue
Opinion editorialargue, convince, criticize, defendneutrally inform without position
Scientific paperdemonstrate, prove, analyzeemotionally persuade
Advertisementconvince, promote, sellobjectively inform of disadvantages

Since this is an informative article, the purpose is most likely to INFORM or PRESENT.

But wait — both "inform" (option C) and "present" (option D) seem possible. This is where most students get stuck. We need a deeper tool.

STEP 2: THE FOUR-VERB AUTOPSY

The entire question hinges on four verbs. Each verb carries a completely different meaning, and the exam designers chose them precisely to test whether you understand the difference.

Let us dissect each one with surgical precision:

╔═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                    THE FOUR VERBS OF PURPOSE                  ║
╠═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║                                                               ║
║  A. DÉNONCER (to denounce)                                    ║
║     = To publicly declare something as WRONG or HARMFUL       ║
║     = Requires: negative judgment, accusation, criticism      ║
║     = Tone: hostile, disapproving, alarmed                    ║
║     = Example: "Ces thèses dangereuses menacent l'humanité"   ║
║                                                               ║
║  B. CONVAINCRE (to convince)                                  ║
║     = To make the reader BELIEVE or AGREE with something      ║
║     = Requires: arguments, evidence FOR a position            ║
║     = Tone: persuasive, one-sided, enthusiastic               ║
║     = Example: "Grâce à ces avancées, nous vivrons mieux"     ║
║                                                               ║
║  C. INFORMER (to inform)                                      ║
║     = To give the reader KNOWLEDGE they didn't have           ║
║     = Requires: facts, descriptions, neutral reporting        ║
║     = Tone: neutral, balanced, distanced                      ║
║     = Example: "Selon X, il serait possible de..."            ║
║                                                               ║
║  D. PRÉSENTER (to present/showcase)                           ║
║     = To SHOW or DISPLAY something that EXISTS                ║
║     = Requires: real, accomplished things to display          ║
║     = Tone: descriptive, often admiring                       ║
║     = Example: "Voici les réalisations extraordinaires de..." ║
║                                                               ║
╚═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

Now, apply each verb to the text and see which one fits:

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: TESTING EACH OPTION AGAINST THE TEXT

OPTION A: "de dénoncer les thèses avant-gardistes publiées par un chercheur"

The test: Does the journalist DENOUNCE Kurzweil's ideas? Does he say they are wrong, dangerous, or harmful?

EVIDENCE SEARCH IN THE TEXT:
                                                    
  ✗ "vedette de la prospective scientifique"  → neutral/positive label
  ✗ "Ses prédictions ont le don de diviser"   → he notes division, 
                                                 doesn't take a side
  ✗ "Selon ses estimations"                   → attributes, doesn't attack
  ✗ "comme le prédit Kurzweil"                → reports, doesn't mock
  
  NOWHERE does the journalist say:
  - "these ideas are dangerous"
  - "this is irresponsible"
  - "we should reject this"
  - "this threatens humanity"
  
  VERDICT: ❌ NO DENUNCIATION FOUND

Additional trap in Option A: It says "publiées par un chercheur" — but Kurzweil is described as a "vedette de la prospective" (a futurist celebrity), not specifically as a "chercheur" (researcher). This is a subtle vocabulary mismatch.

Option A is ELIMINATED.

OPTION B: "de convaincre des avantages de la future révolution technologique"

The test: Does the journalist try to CONVINCE you that the technological revolution will be ADVANTAGEOUS?

EVIDENCE SEARCH IN THE TEXT:
                                                    
  ✗ "ont le don de diviser"                   → shows DISAGREEMENT exists
  ✗ "désaccord le plus véhément"              → mentions OPPOSITION
  ✗ "scepticisme le plus froid"               → mentions SKEPTICISM
  ✗ "nous serions capables" (conditional)     → NOT stated as certain
  ✗ "pourraient avoir fusionné" (conditional) → NOT stated as definite
  ✗ "thèmes extrêmement controversés"         → acknowledges CONTROVERSY
  
  A text trying to CONVINCE would:
  - Use definitive statements ("this WILL happen")
  - Hide opposition and skepticism
  - Present only advantages
  - Use enthusiastic, promotional language
  
  This text does THE OPPOSITE:
  - Uses conditional tense throughout
  - Presents the full range of reactions (for AND against)
  - Mentions controversy explicitly
  
  VERDICT: ❌ NO PERSUASION/CONVICTION FOUND

Option B is ELIMINATED.

OPTION C: "d'informer des évolutions technologiques avancées par un scientifique"

The test: Does the journalist INFORM the reader about technological developments PROPOSED BY a scientist?

EVIDENCE SEARCH IN THE TEXT:
                                                    
  ✓ "Ray Kurzweil est une vedette de la       → identifies a scientific
     prospective scientifique"                    figure
  ✓ "Selon ses estimations"                   → reports HIS estimates
                                                 (= "avancées par")
  ✓ "télécharger le cerveau humain"           → technological evolution
  ✓ "êtres humains et machines fusionné"      → technological evolution
  ✓ "l'intelligence humaine, améliorée,       → technological evolution
     pourrait s'étendre"                         
  ✓ Conditional tense throughout              → reporting, not endorsing
  ✓ Source: Courrier international            → informative press
  ✓ Presents both supporters AND skeptics     → balanced information
  
  EVERY element matches:
  - The journalist INFORMS (neutral reporting)
  - About TECHNOLOGICAL EVOLUTIONS (brain upload, fusion, etc.)
  - AVANCÉES PAR (proposed by / put forward by)
  - UN SCIENTIFIQUE (a scientific figure: Kurzweil)
  
  VERDICT: ✅ PERFECT MATCH

But wait — we must still check Option D before confirming. Never stop at the first "good" answer.

OPTION D: "de présenter des réalisations révolutionnaires d'une vedette de la science"

The test: Does the journalist PRESENT actual ACHIEVEMENTS (réalisations) by Kurzweil?

CRITICAL WORD ANALYSIS:
                                                    
  "réalisations" = things that have been ACCOMPLISHED, DONE, ACHIEVED
  
  But what does the text actually describe?
  
  → "Il IMAGINE un monde futur"           = imagines (NOT realized)
  → "nous SERIONS capables"               = we WOULD be able (NOT done)
  → "POURRAIENT avoir fusionné"           = COULD have fused (NOT done)
  → "POURRAIT commencer à s'étendre"      = COULD begin (NOT started)
  
  EVERYTHING in this text is about PREDICTIONS and HYPOTHESES.
  NOTHING has been accomplished yet.
  
  Kurzweil has not:
  - Built the tiny robots
  - Uploaded a brain
  - Fused humans with machines
  - Extended intelligence into the universe
  
  These are FUTURE PREDICTIONS, not PAST RÉALISATIONS.
  
  VERDICT: ❌ "RÉALISATIONS" IS FACTUALLY WRONG

This is the most dangerous distractor in Question 24. Here is why:

WHY OPTION D ALMOST WORKS:
                                                    
  ✓ "présenter"           → the article does present something
  ✓ "vedette de la science" → Kurzweil IS called a "vedette"
  ✗ "réalisations"         → NOTHING has been realized/achieved
  ✗ "révolutionnaires"     → predictions, not revolutions
  
  TWO words match. TWO words are wrong.
  Option D is a HALF-TRUTH — the most effective type of trap.

📌 THE ANSWER IS C.

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: ALWAYS IDENTIFY THE QUESTION TYPE FIRST

Before looking at answer choices, classify what the question is asking:

QUESTION TYPE TAXONOMY FOR COMPRÉHENSION ÉCRITE:
                                                    
  TYPE 1: PURPOSE    → "cet article a pour but..."
                       "l'auteur cherche à..."
                       "ce texte vise à..."
                       
  TYPE 2: MAIN IDEA  → "la principale théorie..."
                       "l'idée centrale est..."
                       
  TYPE 3: DETAIL     → "selon le texte, quels sont..."
                       "les changements mentionnés..."
                       
  TYPE 4: INFERENCE  → "on peut déduire que..."
                       "la polémique suscitée..."
                       
  TYPE 5: VOCABULARY → "le mot X signifie..."
                       "l'expression Y désigne..."
                       
  TYPE 6: TONE       → "le ton de l'auteur est..."
                       "l'attitude de l'auteur..."

  Q24 = TYPE 1 (PURPOSE) ← We solved this today

📌 Knowing the type tells you WHERE to look and HOW to evaluate answers.

For PURPOSE questions specifically, you always look at the whole text, not one paragraph. The purpose is the overall intent, not a local detail.

RULE 2: FOCUS ON THE VERB IN EACH ANSWER CHOICE

For purpose questions, the verb is everything. The rest of the sentence might match perfectly, but if the verb is wrong, the entire answer is wrong.

THE VERB TEST:
                                                    
  Read ONLY the verbs first:
  
  A. dénoncer    → Does the text ATTACK something?
  B. convaincre  → Does the text ARGUE FOR something?
  C. informer    → Does the text REPORT ON something?
  D. présenter   → Does the text SHOWCASE something achieved?
  
  Eliminate verbs that don't match the tone.
  THEN check the rest of the sentence.

This is a two-stage filter:

STAGE 1: Does the VERB match the text's tone?
         A. dénoncer   → ❌ No negative tone found
         B. convaincre → ❌ No persuasive tone found
         C. informer   → ✅ Neutral reporting tone matches
         D. présenter  → ✅ Possibly — presenting something?

STAGE 2: Does the REST of the sentence match the text's content?
         C. "évolutions technologiques avancées 
             par un scientifique"     → ✅ Matches perfectly
         D. "réalisations révolutionnaires 
             d'une vedette"           → ❌ "Réalisations" = wrong
             
RESULT:  C survives both stages. C is the answer.

RULE 3: USE THE "REVERSE AUTHOR" TEST

This is a powerful technique for purpose questions. Ask yourself:

"If the journalist had THIS purpose, what would the text look like?"

IF PURPOSE = DÉNONCER (Option A):
  The text would contain:
  → Negative adjectives: "dangereux", "irresponsable", "absurde"
  → Warnings: "il faut se méfier", "attention aux dangers"
  → Counter-arguments: "les experts rejettent cette théorie"
  → Emotional language: "c'est scandaleux", "inacceptable"
  
  Does Texte 101 contain ANY of this? → NO

IF PURPOSE = CONVAINCRE (Option B):
  The text would contain:
  → Only positive arguments for the technology
  → No mention of opposition or skepticism  
  → Definitive future tense: "nous VIVRONS éternellement"
  → Call to action: "nous devons soutenir ces recherches"
  
  Does Texte 101 contain ANY of this? → NO

IF PURPOSE = INFORMER (Option C):
  The text would contain:
  → Attribution: "selon lui", "ses estimations"
  → Balanced reporting: mentions supporters AND critics
  → Conditional/distanced language: "serions", "pourraient"
  → Factual source: press publication
  
  Does Texte 101 contain ALL of this? → YES ✅

IF PURPOSE = PRÉSENTER DES RÉALISATIONS (Option D):
  The text would contain:
  → Past tense: "il a réalisé", "il a accompli", "il a créé"
  → Concrete results: inventions, products, completed projects
  → Evidence of achievement: awards, recognitions, data
  
  Does Texte 101 contain ANY of this? → NO

The Reverse Author Test is one of the most reliable tools for purpose questions. It transforms a subjective judgment ("what feels right?") into an objective checklist ("what evidence exists?").

RULE 4: CHECK FOR LINGUISTIC MARKERS OF PURPOSE

French texts contain specific grammatical and lexical markers that reveal the author's purpose:

MARKERS OF INFORMING (neutral reporting):
  → "Selon X..."            (According to X)
  → "D'après..."            (According to)
  → Conditional tense        (serait, pourrait, aurait)
  → "Il affirme que..."     (He states that)
  → "On estime que..."      (It is estimated that)
  → Third-person narration   (il, ses, son)

MARKERS OF DENOUNCING (critical attack):
  → "Il est inadmissible..." (It is unacceptable)
  → "On ne peut tolérer..."  (We cannot tolerate)
  → Exclamation marks         (!)
  → Strong negative adjectives (dangereux, catastrophique)

MARKERS OF CONVINCING (persuasion):
  → "Il faut..."             (We must)
  → "Il est essentiel de..." (It is essential to)
  → Imperative mood           (Agissons! Soutenons!)
  → Rhetorical questions      (N'est-ce pas évident?)
  → Only one-sided arguments

MARKERS OF PRESENTING ACHIEVEMENTS:
  → Past tense of accomplishment (a réalisé, a construit)
  → "Grâce à ses travaux..."     (Thanks to his work)
  → Concrete results described

Texte 101 is FULL of informing markers:

TEXT EVIDENCE:
  "Considérés par certains comme"        → distanced attribution
  "Selon ses estimations"                → attribution marker
  "nous serions capables"                → conditional
  "pourraient avoir fusionné"            → conditional  
  "pourrait commencer"                   → conditional
  "comme le prédit Kurzweil"             → attribution
  "il est probable que"                  → hedged probability
  
  COUNT: 7 informing markers in 3 paragraphs
  COUNT: 0 denouncing markers
  COUNT: 0 convincing markers
  COUNT: 0 achievement-presenting markers
  
  The evidence is OVERWHELMING: purpose = INFORM

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: CHOOSING BASED ON TOPIC MATCH INSTEAD OF PURPOSE MATCH

DANGEROUS THINKING:

  "The text talks about revolutionary technology...
   Option D says 'réalisations révolutionnaires'...
   That sounds like the same thing!"
   
  NO. The TOPIC might overlap, but the PURPOSE is different.
  
  The text TALKS ABOUT revolutionary ideas ≠ 
  The text's PURPOSE IS TO present achievements.
  
  TOPIC ≠ PURPOSE

This is the #1 reason students choose Option D. They match words from the text to words in the answer without checking whether the PURPOSE VERB is correct.

MISTAKE 2: CONFUSING "MENTIONS" WITH "DOES"

DANGEROUS THINKING:

  "The text mentions that people disagree...
   and some admire Kurzweil...
   and some are skeptical...
   So the text is DENOUNCING his ideas!"
   
  NO. The text MENTIONS that others denounce.
  The text ITSELF does not denounce.
  
  MENTIONING a reaction ≠ HAVING that reaction

The journalist reports that people have different reactions. The journalist does NOT share those reactions. This distinction between mentioning and performing is critical for every purpose question.

MISTAKE 3: BEING SEDUCED BY THE STRONGEST-SOUNDING ANSWER

OPTION ATTRACTIVENESS RANKING (how "impressive" they sound):

  D. "réalisations révolutionnaires d'une vedette" → VERY impressive
  A. "dénoncer les thèses avant-gardistes"         → DRAMATIC
  B. "convaincre des avantages"                     → BOLD
  C. "informer des évolutions technologiques"       → ... plain?
  
  The correct answer (C) is the LEAST dramatic option.
  
  THIS IS BY DESIGN.
  
  Exam writers make wrong answers sound MORE appealing.
  The correct answer is often the most modest, precise,
  and boring-sounding option.

📌 In purpose questions, the correct answer almost always sounds less exciting than the distractors. Informing is less dramatic than denouncing, convincing, or showcasing achievements. But accuracy beats drama.

MISTAKE 4: READING "SCIENTIFIQUE" IN OPTION C AND DOUBTING IT

POSSIBLE DOUBT:

  "Option C says 'un scientifique' but Kurzweil is called 
   a 'vedette de la prospective scientifique', not just 
   'un scientifique'. Is C wrong because of this?"
   
  NO. "Vedette de la prospective SCIENTIFIQUE" confirms
  he IS in the scientific domain. Calling him "un scientifique"
  in the answer is a valid simplification/paraphrase.
  
  Compare with Option A which says "un chercheur" (researcher) —
  that's a DIFFERENT role that the text never attributes to him.
  
  "Scientifique" = acceptable paraphrase ✅
  "Chercheur"    = different, unsubstantiated label ❌

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "PARTIAL MATCH" TRAP

ANATOMY OF OPTION D (the most dangerous distractor):

  "de présenter    → ✅ text does present something (PARTIAL MATCH)
   des réalisations → ❌ nothing has been achieved (MISMATCH)
   révolutionnaires → ✅ ideas are revolutionary (PARTIAL MATCH)
   d'une vedette    → ✅ Kurzweil is called "vedette" (EXACT MATCH)
   de la science"   → ✅ "prospective scientifique" (PARTIAL MATCH)
   
  SCORE: 4 out of 5 elements match
  
  BUT: The ONE element that doesn't match ("réalisations")
       is the MOST IMPORTANT one because it changes the 
       entire meaning.
       
  LESSON: A chain is as strong as its weakest link.
          One wrong word can invalidate an entire answer,
          even if everything else matches.

PITFALL 2: OVERTHINKING THE WORD "DIVISER"

THE TRAP:

  "Ses prédictions ont le don de diviser"
  
  Some students think: "diviser = divide = create conflict
  = the article is about a CONTROVERSY = so maybe the purpose
  is to DENOUNCE (show the controversy is bad)"
  
  THE REALITY:
  
  The journalist OBSERVES that opinions are divided.
  He does not JUDGE that division as good or bad.
  He does not TAKE A SIDE in that division.
  
  Observing division ≠ Denouncing

PITFALL 3: CONFUSING "INFORMER" WITH "ENSEIGNER"

"Informer" = to provide information about something
             (journalistic function)
             
"Enseigner" = to teach (educational function)

The text INFORMS you about Kurzweil's predictions.
It does NOT teach you about technology.

This distinction matters because some students reject
"informer" thinking: "But I didn't LEARN anything new
about science from this text."

You don't need to learn science. You learned that 
KURZWEIL PREDICTS certain things. That is information.

PITFALL 4: FORGETTING TO CHECK ALL FOUR OPTIONS

FATAL SHORTCUT:

  Student reads Option A → "dénoncer? maybe..."
  Student reads Option B → "convaincre? no..."
  Student reads Option C → "informer? yes! that's it!"
  Student marks C WITHOUT READING Option D
  
  THIS TIME, they got lucky — C is correct.
  
  But what if C had a subtle flaw they missed?
  What if D was actually better?
  
  ALWAYS READ AND EVALUATE ALL FOUR OPTIONS.
  ALWAYS.
  
  The 30 seconds you "save" by skipping options
  can cost you the entire question.

🔗 BRIDGE TO CHAPTER 3

You now know HOW to identify purpose, and you've solved Q24 completely. But during our analysis, something kept happening: we encountered words and expressions we might not fully understand.

Words from the text we used but never fully decoded:

  "diseur de bonne aventure"    → What exactly does this mean?
  "frisant le culte du héros"   → What does "frisant" mean?
  "sans ciller"                 → What does "ciller" mean?
  "véhément"                    → How strong is this word?
  "éperdue"                     → What shade of meaning?
  "négligeables"                → Is this positive or negative?

In Chapter 2, we could afford to skip these because purpose questions look at the big picture. But the next questions (Q25, Q26) will ask about specific content and meaning. And for those, vocabulary matters.

Chapter 3 will teach you the systematic method for decoding unknown vocabulary using CONTEXT — without a dictionary, without panic, and without guessing blindly.

The technique we will learn is called Contextual Triangulation: using the words AROUND an unknown word to determine its meaning with enough precision to answer exam questions correctly.
