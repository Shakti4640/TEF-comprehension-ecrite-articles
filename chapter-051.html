CHAPTER 25: LE SPORT A-T-IL UN SEXE?

Ethical Debate and Complex Argumentation — Double Standards, Gender Deconstruction, and the Art of the Specific Proposal

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Texte 71 : Le sport a-t-il un sexe ?

Face aux prouesses du coureur jamaïcain Usain Bolt, les médias et les athlètes se félicitent d'avoir pu assister à l'envol d'un pareil phénomène qui a marqué à jamais l'athlétisme. Toute autre est la tonalité des réactions face aux exploits d'une athlète kenyane. Les commentaires passent très vite de l'admiration — elle a « assommé la concurrence » — à la suspicion du fait de son physique androgyne. À l'exception notable de la médaille de bronze britannique, les autres participantes n'hésitent pas à alimenter les rumeurs et s'emportent avec virulence jugeant que ce type de personnes ne devrait pas pouvoir courir à leurs côtés. Bref, l'invraisemblable gamine de 18 ans qui a plané sur la piste bleue sera donc soumise à des tests de féminité. Mais que peuvent-ils signifier puisqu'ils résultent d'un processus complexe mélangeant biologie, génétique, mais aussi psychologie et endocrinologie ? Quel est le bon équilibre qui définit immanquablement l'appartenance à un genre défini ? Avec cette affaire douloureuse, nous voilà revenus aux temps que l'on croyait oubliés où le sport faisait peur car il risquait de masculiniser le sexe faible. Pour séduire les foules et les sponsors, le sport doit-il accuser les stéréotypes ou les dépasser ? Le sport n'est-il pas devenu une machine à produire de la ségrégation ? Il est de notre devoir de protéger les droits de tous, et de veiller à l'éthique du sport.

Rappelons également que la pratique intensive du sport peut provoquer des modifications hormonales importantes. Le problème est complexe, même les scientifiques se contredisent. La jeune femme ne correspond pas aux canons habituels de l'athlétisme et ses performances sont impressionnantes. Mais celles d'Usain Bolt ne le sont-elles pas ? Remettrait-on en cause les performances d'un sauteur dont la légèreté et la grâce lui permettraient de repousser les limites ? Le soumettrait-on lui aussi à de tels tests ? Ces derniers n'ont pas fait leurs preuves dans la détection de fraudeurs, à la différence des tests antidopage ! Elle a même été stoppée lors de la conférence mondiale du CIO sur les femmes et ont fini par être stoppés en 1996, alors ne faisons pas marche arrière. Soumettons l'athlète kenyane à des analyses et des contrôles médicaux renforcés liés à la prise de produits dopants. Comme tous les autres athlètes.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH THIS TEXT

Why This Text Is the Final Challenge

Every chapter in this guide developed specific skills. Chapter 1 taught you to locate facts. Chapter 18 taught you to read political emotion. Chapter 23 taught you to follow sustained argumentation. Chapter 24 taught you to decode full ironic register.

This text requires ALL of those skills — and introduces the most demanding intellectual challenge of the entire guide: navigating an ethical argument where the "right" answer requires understanding NUANCE, not just identifying facts or detecting tone.

The complexity layers:

LAYER 1 — FACTUAL:       A Kenyan athlete is subjected to gender tests
LAYER 2 — COMPARATIVE:   Usain Bolt's similar achievements provoke admiration, not suspicion
LAYER 3 — ETHICAL:       The double standard reveals sexism and gender discrimination
LAYER 4 — SCIENTIFIC:    Gender is biologically complex — tests can't define it simply
LAYER 5 — HISTORICAL:    Gender tests were abandoned in 1996 — reintroducing them is regression
LAYER 6 — PROPOSITIONAL: The author proposes anti-doping tests instead of gender tests
LAYER 7 — PHILOSOPHICAL: Sport should protect rights and transcend stereotypes

Seven layers operating simultaneously. No previous text had more than four. And the questions test DIFFERENT layers — Q32 tests Layer 3, Q33 tests Layer 4, Q34 tests Layer 7, Q35 tests Layer 6. You must navigate between layers with surgical precision.

It combines factual reporting, comparative analysis, scientific complexity, historical context, and moral prescription. The journalist has a CLEAR position but expresses it through QUESTIONS and COMPARISONS rather than direct statements.

Step 3: Track the Journalist's Multi-Layered Argument

The text contains not one argument but SEVERAL interlocking arguments, each supporting the others:

ARGUMENT 1 — THE DOUBLE STANDARD (Comparative)
    Bolt: admired for extraordinary performance
    Kenyan: suspected for extraordinary performance
    CONCLUSION: Gender determines reaction, not performance

ARGUMENT 2 — THE SCIENTIFIC IMPOSSIBILITY (Technical)
    Gender = biology + genetics + psychology + endocrinology
    "Quel est le bon équilibre ?" → No clear answer exists
    Scientists contradict each other
    CONCLUSION: Gender tests are scientifically unreliable

ARGUMENT 3 — THE HISTORICAL REGRESSION (Temporal)
    Gender tests were stopped in 1996
    CIO conference on women also rejected them
    CONCLUSION: Reintroducing them is going backwards

ARGUMENT 4 — THE ETHICAL IMPERATIVE (Moral)
    Sport should protect rights, not produce segregation
    Sport should transcend stereotypes, not enforce them
    CONCLUSION: Gender testing is discriminatory

ARGUMENT 5 — THE PRACTICAL SOLUTION (Propositional)
    Gender tests don't detect fraud
    Anti-doping tests DO detect fraud
    THEREFORE: Use anti-doping tests on ALL athletes equally
    CONCLUSION: Treat the Kenyan athlete like everyone else

Five arguments, each from a different angle, all converging on the same conclusion: gender tests are wrong; anti-doping tests are right; treat all athletes equally.

Why this multi-argument structure matters for the exam: Each question targets a DIFFERENT argument. If you confuse the arguments — applying the scientific argument where the ethical argument is needed, or the practical solution where the moral imperative is needed — you'll pick the wrong answer.

Step 4: Decode the Rhetorical Questions as Self-Answering Arguments

This text contains FIVE rhetorical questions — more than any other text in the guide. Each one is a disguised argument:

Rhetorical Question 1:

"Mais que peuvent-ils signifier puisqu'ils résultent d'un processus complexe mélangeant biologie, génétique, mais aussi psychologie et endocrinologie ?"

Disguised argument: Gender tests are meaningless because gender is too complex to test.

Rhetorical Question 2:

"Quel est le bon équilibre qui définit immanquablement l'appartenance à un genre défini ?"

Disguised argument: There IS no clear boundary defining gender — the question is unanswerable.

Rhetorical Question 3:

"Pour séduire les foules et les sponsors, le sport doit-il accuser les stéréotypes ou les dépasser ?"

Disguised argument: Sport should TRANSCEND stereotypes, not reinforce them.

Rhetorical Question 4:

"Le sport n'est-il pas devenu une machine à produire de la ségrégation ?"

Disguised argument: Sport HAS become a segregation machine — this is wrong and must change.

Rhetorical Question 5:

"Mais celles d'Usain Bolt ne le sont-elles pas ?"

Disguised argument: Bolt's performances are EQUALLY impressive — but nobody questions HIS gender.

Rhetorical Question 6:

"Remettrait-on en cause les performances d'un sauteur dont la légèreté et la grâce lui permettraient de repousser les limites ? Le soumettrait-on lui aussi à de tels tests ?"

Disguised argument: A MALE athlete with "feminine" qualities (lightness, grace) would NEVER be gender-tested. The testing only goes one direction: questioning women, never men.

Connection to Chapter 22: In Allumer la Télé, the closing rhetorical question ("ça intéresse qui ?") carried the journalist's verdict. Here, SIX rhetorical questions carry SIX different arguments. The technique is the same; the density is unprecedented.

Step 5: Decode the Final Proposal — Its Precision and Its Equality Principle

The text's last three sentences contain the journalist's CONCRETE proposal:

"Soumettons l'athlète kenyane à des analyses et des contrôles médicaux renforcés liés à la prise de produits dopants. Comme tous les autres athlètes."

Decompose this with extreme precision:

"Soumettons"                          → Let us subject (imperative "nous")
"l'athlète kenyane"                   → the Kenyan athlete (specifically her)
"à des analyses et contrôles"         → to tests and checks
"médicaux renforcés"                  → reinforced medical (stronger than usual)
"liés à la prise de produits dopants" → related to doping substance use
"Comme tous les autres athlètes"      → Like ALL other athletes

The proposal has THREE precise components:

COMPONENT 1 — WHAT: Anti-doping tests (not gender tests)
COMPONENT 2 — WHO:  The Kenyan athlete specifically
COMPONENT 3 — HOW:  The same way as ALL other athletes (equality)

What the proposal IS NOT:

NOT: Gender tests on her
NOT: No tests at all
NOT: Tests only on "atypical" athletes
NOT: Random tests on some athletes
NOT: Gender tests when they become more reliable

The equality principle is the KEY: "Comme tous les autres athlètes" means the Kenyan athlete should receive EXACTLY the same treatment as everyone else — no more, no less. Not singled out for gender testing. Not exempted from anti-doping testing. Just treated EQUALLY.

Why this precision matters for Q35: The question asks what the author "propose." Every wrong option will distort ONE of the three components — changing the WHAT (gender tests instead of anti-doping), the WHO (atypical athletes instead of everyone equally), or the HOW (random instead of systematic).

Step 6: Identify the Text's Deepest Layer — Sport as Mirror of Society

Beneath all the specific arguments, the text makes a PHILOSOPHICAL claim about what sport SHOULD be:

WHAT SPORT IS (according to the text):
    "Une machine à produire de la ségrégation"
    → A system that enforces differences and excludes the "abnormal"

WHAT SPORT SHOULD BE (according to the journalist):
    A space that "dépasse les stéréotypes"
    → A system that transcends differences and celebrates diversity
    A space that "protège les droits de tous"
    → A system that guarantees equality regardless of appearance

This is the text's DEEPEST layer — the vision of sport as either a segregation machine or an equality space. The journalist argues for the second and condemns the first.

Connection to Chapter 21: In L'Appel des Sommets, the deepest layer was philosophical (beauty and inaccessibility). Here, the deepest layer is ETHICAL (equality and rights). Both require you to go BEYOND the surface facts to grasp the text's ultimate meaning.

The journalist is the athlete's DEFENDER, not her accuser.

The journalist wants gender tests PERMANENTLY abandoned, not paused until they improve. ❌N/AN/A❌ CONTRADICTS "NE FAISONS PAS MARCHE ARRIÈRE"
D — Soumettre les athlètes au physique atypique à des tests de genre"Tests de genre" = gender tests. The ENTIRE article argues AGAINST gender tests. ❌"Au physique atypique" = with atypical physique. This would mean TARGETING athletes who look different — exactly the discrimination the text denounces. ❌Targeting atypical athletes ≠ treating everyone equally. ❌❌ PROPOSES EXACTLY WHAT THE TEXT DENOUNCES

✅ ANSWER: B — De n'effectuer que des tests antidopage sur l'athlète kenyane

The mechanism: This question tests your ability to identify a PRECISE proposal with MULTIPLE components. The correct answer must match:

✅ WHAT:  Anti-doping tests (not gender tests)
✅ WHO:   The Kenyan athlete (specifically mentioned)
✅ HOW:   Only anti-doping = same as everyone else (equality)

Why Option D is the ULTIMATE trap — and why defeating it proves full mastery:

Option D proposes EXACTLY what the article's antagonists want — gender tests on athletes with atypical physiques. This is the position of the competitors who "s'emportent avec virulence." If you pick D, you have:

  1. Misidentified the journalist's position — siding with the accusers, not the defender
  2. Ignored the entire comparative argument — the double standard with Bolt
  3. Contradicted the historical argument — gender tests were abandoned in 1996
  4. Reversed the proposal — replacing anti-doping tests (the proposal) with gender tests (the problem)

Defeating Option D requires EVERY skill in the guide:

  • Fact extraction (Ch.1): The text says "stoppés en 1996"
  • Tone detection (Ch.5): The journalist DEFENDS the athlete
  • Purpose identification (Ch.18): The text DENOUNCES gender discrimination
  • Comparison reading (Ch.20): Bolt vs. Kenyan = proof of bias
  • Proposal precision (Ch.23): The solution is specific: anti-doping, not gender tests
  • Implicit accusation (Ch.24): Those who demand gender tests are perpetuating discrimination

If you can defeat Option D, you have mastered Compréhension Écrite.

✅ WHAT SHOULD BE DONE

1. Use Comparative Structures as Proof Mechanisms

This text's CORE technique — placing Bolt and the Kenyan athlete side by side — is the most powerful argumentative tool in journalism. Learn to recognize it:

COMPARISON STRUCTURE:
    Subject A + Achievement X → Positive Reaction
    Subject B + Achievement X → Negative Reaction
    ONLY DIFFERENCE: A characteristic unrelated to X
    
    CONCLUSION: The reaction is based on the characteristic, 
                not the achievement = DISCRIMINATION

When you detect this structure, the text is PROVING discrimination through juxtaposition. The correct answers will acknowledge the discrimination; wrong answers will ignore or invert it.

2. Distinguish the PRINCIPLE from the PROPOSAL

This text contains BOTH:

PRINCIPLE (Q34): Sport should demonstrate ethics 
                  by transcending stereotypes
                  → ABSTRACT, GENERAL, VALUE-BASED

PROPOSAL (Q35):  Use anti-doping tests instead of gender tests, 
                  treating the athlete like everyone else
                  → CONCRETE, SPECIFIC, ACTION-BASED

Q34 asks about the PRINCIPLE. Q35 asks about the PROPOSAL. These are DIFFERENT questions requiring DIFFERENT answers. If you give the proposal when asked for the principle (or vice versa), you'll pick the wrong answer.

This is the evolution of Chapter 20's "objective vs. justification" distinction:

Ch.20: Objective (WHAT) vs. Justification (WHY)
Ch.25: Principle (WHY) vs. Proposal (WHAT to do)

3. Verify ALL Components of a Multi-Part Proposal

Q35's correct answer must match THREE components: WHAT (anti-doping), WHO (Kenyan athlete), HOW (like everyone else). When a proposal has multiple components, test EACH one independently:

COMPONENT CHECK:
    Option A: WHAT wrong + WHO wrong + HOW wrong = ❌
    Option B: WHAT right + WHO right + HOW right = ✅
    Option C: WHAT wrong (gender tests, not anti-doping) = ❌
    Option D: WHAT wrong + WHO wrong + HOW wrong = ❌

Even ONE wrong component disqualifies an option.

4. Detect When an Option Represents the ANTAGONIST'S Position

Option D in Q32 accuses the Kenyan athlete of lacking integrity. Option D in Q35 proposes gender tests on atypical athletes. BOTH represent the position of the people the journalist is DENOUNCING — the competitors and institutions who discriminate.

The antagonist position test:

1. Identify WHO the journalist defends and WHO the journalist criticizes
2. For each option, ask: "Does this align with the DEFENDER or the CRITIC?"
3. If the option aligns with the CRITIC, it represents the ANTAGONIST'S position
4. The journalist would NEVER propose what the antagonist wants

This is the FINAL evolution of tone-matching (Ch.18): not just detecting the journalist's tone, but identifying which CHARACTER'S position each option represents.

5. Read "Comme tous les autres" as the Equality Principle

The phrase "Comme tous les autres athlètes" is the text's MORAL CONCLUSION — the journalist's ultimate demand. It means:

"Comme tous les autres athlètes"
= LIKE all other athletes
= No special treatment (neither discriminatory nor preferential)
= EQUALITY before the rules
= The Kenyan athlete is an athlete like any other
= Her appearance doesn't change her rights

This three-word phrase carries the weight of the entire article's ethical argument. When you see it in the text, recognize it as the KEY to the journalist's position: equality, not discrimination.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Side with the Antagonists

The text contains two opposing voices:

JOURNALIST'S VOICE:     Defends the athlete, demands equality
ANTAGONISTS' VOICE:     Suspects the athlete, demands gender tests

Options D (Q32) and D (Q35) represent the ANTAGONISTS' voice. Picking them means you've confused the journalist's position with the position the journalist is FIGHTING AGAINST.

How to avoid it: Before selecting any answer, ask: "Is this what the JOURNALIST believes, or what the JOURNALIST'S OPPONENTS believe?" The journalist's beliefs are marked by:

  • Empathetic language ("invraisemblable gamine," "affaire douloureuse")
  • First-person plural inclusion ("notre devoir," "soumettons")
  • Rhetorical questions that answer themselves ("Le soumettrait-on lui aussi ?")

2. Do NOT Confuse "More Controls" with "Better Ethics"

Option A in Q34 proposes "renforcer les contrôles." In many contexts, more controls = more fairness. But in THIS text, the controls (gender tests) ARE the discrimination. More controls would mean more discrimination.

GENERAL LOGIC:    More controls = more fairness (usually true)
THIS TEXT'S LOGIC: These specific controls = discrimination
                   → More of them = MORE discrimination
                   → REPLACE them with different controls (anti-doping)

How to avoid it: Check WHAT KIND of controls the text discusses. If the text argues that certain controls are discriminatory, "renforcer les contrôles" means reinforcing discrimination.

3. Do NOT Assume Science Can Solve Everything

Option C in Q34 proposes "se reposer sur l'expertise scientifique." The text EXPLICITLY undermines this: "même les scientifiques se contredisent."

ASSUMPTION: Science has the answers → rely on scientists
TEXT'S CLAIM: Scientists DISAGREE → science alone is insufficient
             → The problem is ETHICAL, not just scientific

How to avoid it: When a text says experts disagree, any option that proposes relying on expert consensus is contradicted.

4. Do NOT Read "Ne faisons pas marche arrière" as "Pause and Resume Later"

Option C in Q35 proposes resuming gender tests "when they become conclusive." The text says "ne faisons pas marche arrière" (let's not go backwards) — this is a call to MAINTAIN the 1996 decision to stop gender tests, not to pause them temporarily.

"Ne faisons pas marche arrière" = Don't go backwards = PERMANENT direction
≠ "Pause until conditions improve" = TEMPORARY halt

How to avoid it: "Ne faisons pas marche arrière" is a DIRECTIONAL statement — it says "keep moving forward." Any option that implies going back (even conditionally) contradicts this direction.

5. Do NOT Separate the Proposal from Its Equality Clause

The proposal is: "Soumettons l'athlète kenyane à des tests antidopage. Comme tous les autres athlètes."

The second sentence is NOT optional — it's the CORE of the proposal. Without "comme tous les autres," the proposal could be read as singling out the Kenyan athlete for special testing. WITH "comme tous les autres," the proposal demands EQUAL treatment.

Option A ("certains sportifs à des contrôles aléatoires") drops the equality clause — it proposes testing SOME athletes randomly, which is NOT equal treatment. The text demands the SAME tests for EVERYONE.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Antagonist Voice" Confusion (Most Dangerous in the Guide)

The text contains MULTIPLE voices:

VOICE 1 (Journalist):   Defends athlete, demands equality
VOICE 2 (Competitors):  "Ce type de personnes ne devrait pas courir"
VOICE 3 (Media):        Move from admiration to suspicion
VOICE 4 (Public):       "Beaucoup ne comprennent pas, réprouvent même"
         (from Ch.21 — a similar structure appears in sports discourse)

If you confuse Voice 2 (competitors) with Voice 1 (journalist), you'll pick Option D in both Q32 and Q35 — the antagonist's position presented as the journalist's proposal.

How to avoid it: The journalist's voice is identified by:

  • First person plural ("nous," "notre devoir," "soumettons")
  • Rhetorical questions (implying the answer is obvious)
  • Empathetic descriptions of the athlete
  • Historical references supporting the athlete's case

The antagonists' voice is identified by:

  • Reported speech ("jugeant que ce type de personnes...")
  • Virulence and rejection language
  • Demand for tests and exclusion

Pitfall 2: The "Science Will Save Us" Fallacy

Option C in Q34 seems reasonable — "let science decide." But the text SPECIFICALLY undermines scientific authority on this question:

"Même les scientifiques se contredisent"
= EVEN scientists disagree
→ If EXPERTS can't agree, science CANNOT be the sole arbiter
→ The problem requires ETHICAL judgment, not just scientific analysis

How to avoid it: When a text invokes scientific disagreement, it's arguing that the issue is TOO COMPLEX for science alone. Any option that proposes "relying on science" is contradicted.

Pitfall 3: The "Reinforced Controls" Reversal

Option A in Q34 proposes "renforcer les contrôles afin de surmonter les stéréotypes." This sounds progressive — controls to overcome stereotypes! But the text's logic runs in the OPPOSITE direction:

TEXT'S LOGIC:
    Gender tests = discriminatory controls based on stereotypes
    → To overcome stereotypes, REMOVE these controls
    → Replace with non-discriminatory controls (anti-doping)

OPTION A'S LOGIC:
    More controls = overcome stereotypes
    → Strengthening controls = progressive move

THESE ARE OPPOSITE DIRECTIONS

How to avoid it: Ask: "In THIS text, are controls presented as part of the PROBLEM or part of the SOLUTION?" If controls are the problem, "renforcing" them makes the problem worse.

Pitfall 4: The "Scope Mismatch" Between Principle and Proposal

Q34 asks about PRINCIPLES (what sport should be). Q35 asks about PROPOSALS (what to do specifically). Candidates sometimes answer Q34 with a concrete action (proposal) or Q35 with an abstract value (principle).

Q34 expects: Abstract ethical vision (déontologie, au-delà des clichés)
Q35 expects: Concrete specific action (tests antidopage, comme tous les autres)

How to avoid it: Check whether the question asks "devrait" (should — principle level) or "propose" (proposes — action level). Match your answer's abstraction level to the question's.

Pitfall 5: The "Partial Proposal" Acceptance

Option B in Q35 says "n'effectuer QUE des tests antidopage." "Que" (only) is crucial — it EXCLUDES gender tests. Some candidates might worry that "only" is too restrictive. But the text's entire argument leads to EXACTLY this: replace gender tests with anti-doping tests. "Only anti-doping" is not restrictive — it's LIBERATING the athlete from discriminatory gender testing.

How to avoid it: "Que" (only) in this context means "instead of gender tests, use anti-doping tests" — it's an EXCLUSION of the discriminatory test, not a limitation on necessary testing.

THE UNIVERSAL METHOD — What Never Changes

From Chapter 1 to Chapter 25, the fundamental method remained constant:

1. READ the text → Identify its TYPE
2. MAP the text → Structure (topics / stages / layers / arguments)
3. READ the question → Identify which ZONE or LAYER it targets
4. READ the options → Test each against the TEXT (not your knowledge)
5. ELIMINATE 3 → Using: contradiction / absence / trap detection / scope mismatch
6. CONFIRM 1 → Verify the remaining option matches the text's MEANING

What changed across phases was the DEPTH of each step:

StepPhase 1Phase 5
ReadIdentify factual textIdentify ethical editorial with comparative argument
MapTopics by paragraphMulti-layered arguments across paragraphs
ZoneOne paragraph per questionMultiple interacting zones per question
TestWord-level paraphrase matchingConceptual-philosophical meaning matching
EliminateSimple contradictionAntagonist voice detection, scope mismatch, tone incompatibility
ConfirmSurface meaning matchesDeep meaning, principle, and proposal all align

THE GOLDEN RULES — Carry These Forever

RULE 1:  The text is your ONLY authority. Never import outside knowledge.
RULE 2:  The correct answer is ALWAYS a paraphrase — never a copy, 
         never an invention.
RULE 3:  Wrong answers are wrong for SPECIFIC reasons: contradiction, 
         absence, trap word, scope error, tone mismatch, or 
         antagonist voice confusion.
RULE 4:  When a text uses "mais," "pourtant," or "cependant," 
         the real position is AFTER the connector.
RULE 5:  When a text uses "comme," "tel," or "une sorte de," 
         it's an ANALOGY — never take it literally.
RULE 6:  When a text uses guillemets around a single word, 
         the journalist DISTANCES from that word.
RULE 7:  When a question asks "propose" or "solution," 
         look for PRESCRIPTIONS, not descriptions.
RULE 8:  When multiple voices exist in a text, identify WHOSE 
         voice each option represents before choosing.
RULE 9:  "Enfin," "désormais," and "pourtant" are the three most 
         powerful single words in French argumentation — 
         each one carries an entire implicit argument.
RULE 10: The final sentence of a French editorial almost always 
         contains the journalist's STRONGEST position — 
         read it with maximum attention.
