Texte 55 : L'appel des sommets (L'alpinisme)

D'Hannibal à Bonaparte, les Alpes ont toujours fasciné les conquérants. C'est d'ailleurs à la suite de l'épopée napoléonienne que les écrivains romantiques allèrent y puiser leur inspiration. Les peintres eux aussi comme Turner et Caspar David Friedrich subirent cet attrait pour les précipices, les mers de glace et les sommets silencieux. Bientôt suivront les gens à la mode, qui se feront porter, quelquefois à dos d'homme, sur les pentes du Mont Blanc. Ce n'est qu'à la fin du XIXe siècle que se développera une véritable économie touristique poussant les agriculteurs des vallées à se transformer, l'été, en guides conduisant pour de l'argent les sportifs de l'époque vers les pics les plus impressionnants. Alors commença la longue histoire de l'alpinisme. Ce sport d'abord réservé aux seuls montagnards, sera l'occasion d'exploits légendaires et de tragédies non moins spectaculaires. À la frontière italo-suisse, le Mont Cervin, quelquefois appelé "la plus belle montagne du monde", résume à lui seul cet esprit d'aventure qui lança sur ses arêtes tant d'audacieux. Avec ses cinq cents victimes à ce jour, il fait à la fois figure de mythe et d'épouvantail. Mythe car dès 1865, son sommet fut atteint par un reporter anglais, dont l'exploit connut une extraordinaire publicité, mais comme lors de la descente, quatre hommes de la cordée avaient dévissé, les habitants de Zermatt préfèreront saluer la performance réalisée par Louis Carrel, homme du pays, lui, et sympathique contrebandier, qui, quelque temps plus tard, gravit les faces Sud et Est de la redoutable cime. En 1932, les frères Schmid s'attaquèrent avec succès à sa face nord et Walter Bonatti, en 1965, parvint à ce qui semblait irréalisable, à la même prouesse, mais cette fois-ci seul et en hiver. Pour expliquer ce choix, il dit : « Souvent on parle de solitude en termes négatifs en l'associant à l'angoisse. En fait, la solitude augmente la sensibilité et le degré d'émotion. » Beaucoup ne comprennent pas, réprouvent même, ce goût du péril extrême. Ceux-là n'entendront pas les mots de Gaston Rébuffat affirmant que « le rapport entre la beauté et l'inaccessibilité est indissoluble ».

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH THIS TEXT

Why This Text Demands a New Kind of Reading

In Chapter 20 (La Pianiste Mine Kawakami), you navigated cultural layers — different traditions (Japanese, Spanish, American) coexisting within a contemporary phenomenon. The temporal range was narrow: 1970s to present. The philosophical content was limited to one school's claims about brain function.

This text explodes those boundaries in every direction:

  • Temporal range: From Hannibal (218 BC) to 1965 — over two thousand years compressed into one article
  • Actor range: Military conquerors, Romantic writers, Impressionist painters, fashionable tourists, mountain farmers, reporters, contrabandists, brothers, solo climbers, philosopher-alpinists
  • Conceptual range: From factual history to philosophical meditation on beauty, solitude, and inaccessibility
  • The final challenge: The text ends with two philosophical quotations that are not decorative — they are the intellectual climax, and Q4 tests whether you truly understand them

The fundamental shift: In all previous chapters, correct answers were paraphrases of the text — you found the matching sentence and recognized the restatement. In Q4 of this chapter, the correct answer is a paraphrase of a philosophical idea — you must understand what Rébuffat MEANS, not just what he SAYS.

This is the threshold between comprehension and interpretation. Welcome to the deep end of Phase 4.

Step 4: Pre-Analyze the Two Philosophical Quotations

The exam WILL test these quotations. They are the intellectual heart of the text. Let's decode them now, before the questions force us to do it under pressure.

Quotation 1 — Walter Bonatti (on solitude):

« Souvent on parle de solitude en termes négatifs en l'associant à l'angoisse. En fait, la solitude augmente la sensibilité et le degré d'émotion. »

Decomposition:

COMMON VIEW:     Solitude = negative = associated with anguish
BONATTI'S VIEW:  Solitude = positive = increases sensitivity and emotion
STRUCTURE:       Reversal of conventional wisdom
PURPOSE:         Explains why he climbed ALONE — not out of 
                 desperation but for HEIGHTENED EXPERIENCE

Quotation 2 — Gaston Rébuffat (on beauty and inaccessibility):

« Le rapport entre la beauté et l'inaccessibilité est indissoluble. »

Decomposition:

KEY WORDS:
  "rapport"         = relationship, link, connection
  "beauté"          = beauty
  "inaccessibilité" = inaccessibility (what cannot be reached/attained)
  "indissoluble"    = inseparable, unbreakable, permanent

MEANING: Beauty and inaccessibility are PERMANENTLY LINKED.
         You cannot have one without the other.
         Something is beautiful BECAUSE it cannot be reached.
         If it were easy to reach, it would lose its beauty.

IMPLICATION: The mountain's beauty comes FROM its danger 
             and difficulty. The struggle IS the beauty.
             What you can't attain is, by nature, beautiful.

This is a profound philosophical claim. Rébuffat is saying that beauty requires inaccessibility — that the unattainable IS the beautiful. This is not just about mountains. It's about human aspiration itself.

Q4 will test whether you can match this abstract idea to its correct paraphrase. Let's hold this analysis and deploy it when we reach Q4.

Step 5: Detect the Journalist's Subtle Technique — "Mythe et Épouvantail"

The journalist introduces the Mont Cervin with a powerful duality:

"il fait à la fois figure de mythe et d'épouvantail"

MYTHE        = myth → something legendary, inspiring, aspirational
ÉPOUVANTAIL  = scarecrow → something frightening, repellent, warning

TOGETHER:    The mountain simultaneously ATTRACTS and REPELS
             It is both DREAM and NIGHTMARE
             It both INSPIRES and KILLS

This duality governs everything that follows about the Cervin. Every exploit is shadowed by death. The English reporter reaches the summit — but four men die on the descent. Five hundred victims total. The mountain's beauty is inseparable from its danger.

This is the same idea that Rébuffat articulates philosophically at the end. The journalist's "mythe et épouvantail" and Rébuffat's "beauté et inaccessibilité" are the same insight expressed at different registers — one journalistic, one philosophical.

QUESTION 2: Une seule de ces affirmations est vraie. Laquelle ?

Step 1: Understand the question's architecture

This is a fact-verification question — the most demanding type because you must test FOUR statements against the text, and only ONE is true. The other three contain subtle distortions.

Step 2: Test each option rigorously

Option A — "L'alpinisme a vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme"

The text describes the birth of tourism and the birth of alpinism as SEQUENTIAL but SEPARATE events:

TOURISM:   "Ce n'est qu'à la fin du XIXe siècle que se développera 
            une véritable économie touristique"
           → Farmers become paid guides

ALPINISM:  "Alors commença la longue histoire de l'alpinisme"
           → "Alors" = THEN (after tourism began)

"Alors" is crucial. It means alpinism came AFTER tourism, not WITH it. Tourism created the conditions for alpinism (guides, infrastructure), but they are not simultaneous births.

However — let's read more carefully. "Alors commença" = "Then began." This suggests alpinism emerged from the tourism economy. The guides who led tourists eventually enabled serious climbing. So alpinism DID begin alongside/from tourism.

But does "a vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme" accurately capture this? "A vu le jour" = was born. "Avec les débuts" = with the beginnings. The text says tourism came first, THEN alpinism followed. This is close but the relationship is SEQUENTIAL (tourism → then alpinism), not SIMULTANEOUS (tourism + alpinism at the same time).

Let me hold judgment and test the others.

Option B — "De tout temps, des hommes illustres ont péri dans les Alpes"

"De tout temps" = at all times / throughout history. "Hommes illustres" = famous/illustrious men. "Ont péri" = have perished/died.

The text mentions:

  • Hannibal → crossed the Alps (didn't die there)
  • Napoleon → crossed the Alps (didn't die there)
  • The English reporter in 1865 → reached the summit (survived; 4 others died but they're not described as "illustres")
  • 500 victims of the Cervin → not described as "illustres"

No famous person is described as dying in the Alps in this text. The text describes famous people being FASCINATED by the Alps, not dying in them. The 500 victims are anonymous. The four who died in 1865 are unnamed members of a cordée.

"A succédé" = was followed by / succeeded. This claims that fascination came FIRST, then was REPLACED by terror.

The text shows fascination and danger COEXISTING throughout, not succeeding each other:

RÉBUFFAT:           beauty and inaccessibility are "indissoluble"

"Mulets" = mules. The text says:

Alors commença la longue histoire de l'alpinisme."

The tourism economy PUSHED farmers to become guides, and THEN ("alors") alpinism began. Alpinism was born FROM tourism — they share the same origin point (late 19th century), with tourism being the immediate catalyst.

"A vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme" = was born with the beginnings of tourism. This is the closest accurate statement. Tourism created the conditions; alpinism emerged from those conditions. They share the same historical moment.

The mechanism: This question requires you to test FOUR claims against a dense historical text. Three claims contain specific factual errors:

  • B invents famous deaths
  • C claims sequential replacement when the text shows coexistence
  • D substitutes mules for human porters

Option A is imperfect (tourism slightly precedes alpinism), but it's the CLOSEST to what the text describes — and in a "which is TRUE" question, you're looking for the MOST accurate option, not a perfect one.

New skill introduced: In "one true statement" questions, ALL four options may be imperfect. Your job is to find the LEAST wrong one — the one with the smallest gap between claim and text. This is different from previous questions where the correct answer was a perfect paraphrase.

2. Distinguish "About the World" from "About Humans"

This is the critical philosophical skill introduced in Q4. Learn to classify statements:

ONTOLOGICAL (about what things ARE):
  "Beauty and inaccessibility are inseparable"
  "The link between X and Y is indissoluble"
  → Describes the NATURE of concepts

PSYCHOLOGICAL (about what humans FEEL/NEED):
  "Humans need to push limits"
  "Beauty makes people pursue it"
  → Describes HUMAN behavior or motivation

ETHICAL (about what humans SHOULD DO):
  "By surpassing ourselves, we can reach the ideal"
  → Describes HUMAN aspiration or duty

When a quote is ONTOLOGICAL, the correct paraphrase must also be ONTOLOGICAL. A psychological or ethical paraphrase, however close it sounds, operates at the wrong level.

3. Decode "Mythe et Épouvantail" Structures

The "mythe et épouvantail" (myth and scarecrow) structure — two contradictory qualities coexisting in one entity — appears throughout French literary writing:

STRUCTURE:   X is both [POSITIVE] and [NEGATIVE]
EXAMPLES:
  Mont Cervin = mythe (inspiring) + épouvantail (terrifying)
  Solitude    = angoisse (common view) + sensibilité (Bonatti's view)
  Beauty      = attracting + inaccessible (Rébuffat)

When you encounter this structure, the text is saying: both qualities are simultaneously true. Any answer option that claims only ONE quality (just myth, or just terror; just fascination, or just fear) is incomplete.

This is why Option C in Q2 ("fascination THEN terror") is wrong — it treats the two qualities as SEQUENTIAL when the text presents them as SIMULTANEOUS.

4. Read Quotation Questions at the Conceptual Level

When a question says "Selon [person]," it asks you to understand that person's idea, not just their words. This requires:

STEP 1: Identify the key terms in the quote
STEP 2: Determine the RELATIONSHIP between those terms
STEP 3: Classify the type of statement (ontological/psychological/ethical)
STEP 4: Find the option that preserves the RELATIONSHIP and the TYPE

For Rébuffat:

STEP 1: Key terms = beauté, inaccessibilité, indissoluble
STEP 2: Relationship = permanent link between beauty and inaccessibility
STEP 3: Type = ontological (about what beauty IS)
STEP 4: Option B preserves both the relationship and the type ✅

5. Handle "Which Is True" Questions Through Systematic Elimination

Q2 asks "une seule affirmation est vraie." This format is harder than "which answer matches" because you must verify FOUR claims, not just find one match.

Method:

1. Test each option for FACTUAL ACCURACY against the text
2. Mark clearly: ❌ or ✅ or ? (uncertain)
3. If you find a clear ✅, verify it's not contradicted elsewhere
4. If no clear ✅, choose the LEAST wrong option
5. Never assume — always verify against specific text evidence

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Read Philosophical Quotes as Literal Descriptions

Rébuffat does not literally mean that beautiful things must be physically unreachable. He means that the CONCEPT of beauty is linked to the CONCEPT of inaccessibility — that the difficulty of reaching something is part of what makes it beautiful.

If you read the quote literally, you might think it's about climbing technique (you can't reach certain peaks). But it's about the NATURE OF BEAUTY ITSELF.

Connection to Chapter 20: In La Pianiste Kawakami, you learned not to take "comme un médicament" literally. Here, the same principle applies at a higher level — philosophical language must be read philosophically, not physically.

2. Do NOT Confuse "Dévissé" with "Everyone Died"

The text says "quatre hommes de la cordée avaient dévissé" (four men had fallen off/come loose from the rock face). This means four men died. But the reporter who reached the summit SURVIVED — otherwise his exploit couldn't have "connu une extraordinaire publicité."

Option C in Q3 claims no one came back alive until 1932. This is false — the 1865 reporter survived. The four deaths don't mean ZERO survivors.

Rule: When a text says "X people died," don't assume EVERYONE died unless the text explicitly says so. Count the casualties against the total.

3. Do NOT Substitute Animals for Humans

Option D in Q2 replaces "à dos d'homme" (on men's backs) with "des mulets" (mules). This is a detail substitution trap — the exam takes a memorable image from the text and changes one element.

TEXT:    "se feront porter à dos D'HOMME"  (carried by HUMANS)
TRAP:    "des MULETS furent utilisés"       (carried by MULES)

This is the same mechanism as "amnistie/amnésie" from Chapter 1, but at a content level rather than a vocabulary level. The trap works because both involve being CARRIED up a mountain — the mode of transport sounds plausible either way.

Defense: When an option mentions a specific detail (mules, horses, donkeys), go back to the text and verify the EXACT detail. Don't trust your memory of the general scene.

4. Do NOT Confuse Sequential with Simultaneous

Option C in Q2 claims fascination was FOLLOWED BY terror ("a succédé"). The text presents fascination and danger as COEXISTING — "mythe ET épouvantail" (myth AND scarecrow), not "mythe PUIS épouvantail" (myth THEN scarecrow).

SEQUENTIAL (C claims):  First fascination → then terror
SIMULTANEOUS (text says): Fascination + danger = always together

This distinction matters enormously in French exam texts. Watch for temporal markers:

SEQUENTIAL markers:   "a succédé à," "puis," "ensuite," "après"
SIMULTANEOUS markers: "à la fois," "et," "en même temps," "tout autant"

The text uses "à la fois" (simultaneously) for "mythe et épouvantail." Option C uses "a succédé" (was followed by). These are incompatible.

5. Do NOT Pick the Most "Impressive-Sounding" Philosophical Option

Q4's options all sound philosophical and profound. Option A ("c'est en nous surpassant que nous pouvons prétendre à l'idéal") sounds VERY impressive — it evokes self-transcendence and idealism. Option D ("besoin profond de repousser les limites") sounds dramatic and deep.

But impressive-sounding ≠ correct. The correct option (B) is actually the SIMPLEST and most DIRECT restatement of the quote. It doesn't add drama or elevation — it just translates the quote's concepts into different words.

Rule: For quotation interpretation questions, the correct answer is usually the one that most faithfully restates the original idea — not the one that sounds the most philosophically exciting.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Romantic Reading" Error

This text is beautifully written. It uses words like "épopée," "audacieux," "redoutable cime," "mers de glace." The emotional pull is strong. Some candidates get so caught up in the beauty of the prose that they answer based on how the text makes them feel rather than what it actually says.

How to avoid it: After your first emotional reading, re-read with your ANALYTICAL brain. The text is literary; your answers must be precise.

Pitfall 2: The "Famous Person = Famous Death" Assumption

Q2 option B says "des hommes illustres ont péri." The text mentions famous people (Hannibal, Napoleon, Turner, Friedrich) and mentions deaths (500 victims, 4 men in 1865). But the famous people DIDN'T die, and the people who died WEREN'T famous.

How to avoid it: Keep two separate lists:

FAMOUS PEOPLE IN TEXT:  Hannibal, Napoleon, Turner, Friedrich, Bonatti, Rébuffat
PEOPLE WHO DIED:        4 unnamed cordée members, 500 anonymous victims

OVERLAP:                ZERO

Pitfall 3: The "Philosophical Level Mismatch"

This is the deepest pitfall in Q4. Options A, C, and D are all REASONABLE philosophical statements about mountain climbing. They just don't match RÉBUFFAT'S specific claim.

A says: Self-transcendence leads to the ideal     → about HUMAN EFFORT
B says: Beauty and inaccessibility are linked      → about BEAUTY'S NATURE
C says: Beauty drives human pursuit                → about BEAUTY'S EFFECT
D says: Humans need to push extreme limits         → about HUMAN PSYCHOLOGY

Rébuffat's quote is about what beauty IS (ontological), not about what humans do or need (psychological/ethical). Only B operates at the same level.

How to avoid it: Ask yourself: "Is this option about THINGS or about PEOPLE?" If the quote is about things (beauty, inaccessibility) and the option is about people (surpassing ourselves, pushing limits), there's a level mismatch.

Pitfall 4: The "Approximate Date" Trap

Q3 option C says "il a fallu attendre 1932." The date 1932 DOES appear in the text (the Schmid brothers' north face conquest). But the claim attached to this date (first person to return alive) is false — people returned alive from the Cervin in 1865.

This is a date displacement trap: taking a REAL date from the text and attaching it to a FALSE claim. You saw a similar trap in Chapter 19 (the number 4 displaced from auction frequency to owner reclamation frequency).

How to avoid it: When an option contains a specific date or number from the text, verify: what does this date ACTUALLY correspond to in the text?

1932 in text:  Schmid brothers conquer NORTH FACE
1932 in trap:  First person to come back ALIVE

These are completely different achievements.
