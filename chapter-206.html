🔍 PART 1: THE SELF-CORRECTION SYSTEM

THE ERROR TAXONOMY: CLASSIFYING EVERY POSSIBLE MISTAKE

After analyzing hundreds of comprehension errors across exam simulations, every mistake falls into one of exactly ten categories. These ten categories form your Personal Error Taxonomy:

THE 10 ERROR CATEGORIES:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  CATEGORY 1: READING ERRORS
  ──────────────────────────
  WHAT: You misread or misunderstood the text itself
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 1a: Missed a key word ("ne...que," "seulement," "même")
  → 1b: Misunderstood archaic/literary vocabulary
  → 1c: Confused conditional with factual statements
  → 1d: Lost track of who is speaking (voice confusion)
  → 1e: Misidentified the text's genre or tone
  
  DETECTION: During post-analysis, re-read the relevant 
  text passage. If the correct answer was CLEARLY stated 
  in the text and you missed it, this is a reading error.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Insufficient attention during reading, 
  or vocabulary/grammar gaps.
  
  FIX: Targeted re-reading practice with focus on the 
  specific subcategory (e.g., if 1c, practice identifying 
  conditional tense markers).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 2: QUESTION INTERPRETATION ERRORS
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  WHAT: You misunderstood what the question was asking
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 2a: Missed a scope-defining word ("principale," 
        "majeurs," "l'ensemble")
  → 2b: Answered at the wrong zoom level (macro vs specific)
  → 2c: Confused what the question asked about 
        (the article vs a person vs a theory)
  → 2d: Didn't distinguish between "what does X say" 
        and "what is true about X"
  
  DETECTION: Re-read the question stem. If the correct 
  answer DOES match the question but you chose something 
  that answers a DIFFERENT question, this is Category 2.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Rushing through the question stem, 
  or skipping the scope dissection step (Chapter 19).
  
  FIX: Practice the scope dissection protocol on 
  20+ questions WITHOUT looking at answer options. 
  Just dissect the question and build the template.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 3: ELIMINATION ERRORS
  ──────────────────────────────
  WHAT: You failed to eliminate a wrong answer, or 
  incorrectly eliminated the right answer
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 3a: Failed to detect meaning inversion
  → 3b: Failed to detect half-truth construction
  → 3c: Failed to detect scope shifting
  → 3d: Failed to detect temporal distortion
  → 3e: Incorrectly eliminated the correct answer 
        (over-elimination)
  
  DETECTION: Review which options you kept and which 
  you eliminated. If the correct answer was eliminated, 
  this is 3e. If a distractor survived, identify which 
  technique you missed (3a-3d).
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Incomplete application of the 4-check 
  protocol (Chapter 22), or applying checks too hastily.
  
  FIX: Practice the 4-check protocol on WRONG answers 
  specifically. Take 10 distractors and classify each 
  by technique. Build pattern recognition.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 4: SCORING/TIE-BREAKING ERRORS
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  WHAT: You correctly narrowed to 2 options but chose 
  the wrong one
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 4a: Chose the option with less textual proof
  → 4b: Chose the option with added information
  → 4c: Chose based on sophistication bias
  → 4d: Chose based on gut feeling over systematic scoring
  
  DETECTION: If elimination correctly removed 2 options 
  but you selected the wrong survivor, this is Category 4.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Not applying Chapter 18 scoring system, 
  or applying it with confirmation bias.
  
  FIX: Practice scoring on 10+ two-option scenarios. 
  Score the option you DON'T instinctively prefer FIRST.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 5: SCOPE TRAP ERRORS
  ────────────────────────────
  WHAT: You chose an answer that is TRUE but doesn't 
  answer THIS question
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 5a: Detail elevated to main topic
  → 5b: Context confused with topic
  → 5c: Activity confused with change
  → 5d: Outside knowledge imported
  → 5e: Cross-text contamination
  → 5f: Framework substitution
  → 5g: Consequence confused with cause
  
  DETECTION: If your chosen answer IS in the text and 
  IS true, but the correct answer BETTER matches the 
  specific question, this is Category 5.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Skipping or rushing the scope dissection 
  step (Chapter 19), or not using the Swap Test.
  
  FIX: Practice the Swap Test on 10+ answer options. 
  For each, ask: "Does this better answer a DIFFERENT 
  question?" Build scope sensitivity.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 6: VOICE ATTRIBUTION ERRORS
  ────────────────────────────────────
  WHAT: You attributed a claim to the wrong voice
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 6a: Expert's claim treated as article's position
  → 6b: Journalist's reporting treated as expert's opinion
  → 6c: One expert confused with another expert
  → 6d: Indirect speech misattributed
  
  DETECTION: If the correct answer depends on WHO said 
  something, and you attributed it to the wrong person, 
  this is Category 6.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Not marking guillemets during first scan, 
  or not reading attribution tags carefully (Chapter 21).
  
  FIX: Practice voice mapping on 5+ journalistic texts. 
  Color-code each voice with a different color.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 7: TIME MANAGEMENT ERRORS
  ──────────────────────────────────
  WHAT: You ran out of time or made rushed decisions
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 7a: Spent too long on one question (derailment)
  → 7b: Spent too long reading (insufficient answering time)
  → 7c: Didn't answer in efficiency order
  → 7d: Skipped review phase due to time pressure
  
  DETECTION: If you got a question wrong that you 
  COULD have gotten right with more time, or if you 
  ran out of time entirely, this is Category 7.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Not following the time allocation protocol 
  (Chapter 20), or not setting the 2.5-minute maximum 
  per question.
  
  FIX: Practice timed simulations with a visible clock. 
  Set alarms at transition points.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 8: FATIGUE-INDUCED ERRORS
  ──────────────────────────────────
  WHAT: Your performance dropped on later texts due 
  to mental exhaustion
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 8a: Tool deployment degraded (skipped checks)
  → 8b: Reading comprehension degraded (missed content)
  → 8c: Decision quality degraded (chose gut over system)
  → 8d: Review quality degraded (superficial or skipped)
  
  DETECTION: If your accuracy on the LAST text is 
  significantly lower than on the FIRST text, and the 
  errors are not explained by text difficulty alone, 
  this is Category 8.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Not using the transition protocol, 
  not adapting strategy to energy level, or insufficient 
  exam stamina.
  
  FIX: Practice full-length simulations (3 texts, 
  24 minutes) to build stamina. Use the energy monitoring 
  protocol and adaptive strategy levels.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 9: BIAS-INDUCED ERRORS
  ──────────────────────────────
  WHAT: A cognitive bias overrode your systematic analysis
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 9a: First-match satisfaction (grabbed first matching element)
  → 9b: Sophistication bias (chose impressive-sounding answer)
  → 9c: Confirmation bias (chose answer confirming prior belief)
  → 9d: Outside knowledge bias (answered from general knowledge)
  → 9e: Second-guess spiral (changed correct answer to wrong one)
  
  DETECTION: If your systematic analysis pointed to 
  the correct answer but you OVERRODE it with a feeling, 
  this is Category 9.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Not trusting the system, or not completing 
  the full protocol before making a selection.
  
  FIX: After each simulation, identify which biases 
  were ACTIVE. Practice the specific counter-protocol 
  for your most common bias.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CATEGORY 10: CROSS-TEXT ERRORS
  ────────────────────────────
  WHAT: Information from one text infected your answers 
  about another text
  SUBCATEGORIES:
  → 10a: Vocabulary bleed (term from Text A used in Text B)
  → 10b: Theme bleed (topic from Text A imported to Text B)
  → 10c: Confidence bleed (uncertainty from one text 
         affecting confidence on another)
  
  DETECTION: If your wrong answer contains elements 
  that are true about a DIFFERENT text on the same exam, 
  this is Category 10.
  
  ROOT CAUSE: Not using the transition protocol, or 
  not asking "Which text is this from?"
  
  FIX: Practice the transition protocol until it becomes 
  automatic. Add the "which text?" check to your 
  answer verification routine.

THE WEAKNESS-TARGETED PRACTICE SYSTEM

Once you know your top weaknesses, design focused practice that targets ONLY those weaknesses:

TARGETED PRACTICE DESIGN:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  FOR EACH WEAKNESS, CREATE A MICRO-DRILL:
  
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WEAKNESS: Half-Truth Detection (Category 3b)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  DRILL: "The Both-Halves Drill"
  
  MATERIALS: 20 compound answer options from practice exams
  (Mix: 10 with both halves correct, 10 with one half wrong)
  
  PROCEDURE:
  1. Read the answer option
  2. Split it into Part 1 and Part 2
  3. For EACH part separately, ask:
     "Can I find this EXACT claim in the text?"
  4. Mark: ✓✓ (both verified) or ✓✗ (half-truth) or ✗✗ (both wrong)
  5. Check against answer key
  
  TIME: 2 minutes per option × 20 options = 40 minutes
  
  SUCCESS METRIC: Correctly classify 18/20 (90%)
  
  FREQUENCY: Once per week until the weakness is eliminated
  
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WEAKNESS: Scope Trap Detection (Category 5c/5d)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  DRILL: "The Swap Test Drill"
  
  MATERIALS: 15 answer options that are TRUE about the text 
  but DON'T answer the specific question
  
  PROCEDURE:
  1. Read the question stem
  2. Read the answer option
  3. Ask: "What question would this BETTER answer?"
  4. If you can find a better question → SCOPE TRAP
  5. If this is the BEST question for this answer → CORRECT
  6. Check against answer key
  
  TIME: 3 minutes per option × 15 options = 45 minutes
  
  SUCCESS METRIC: Correctly identify 13/15 (87%)
  
  FREQUENCY: Once per week until the weakness is eliminated

📌 CRITICAL INSIGHT: 80% of your Compréhension Écrite skills transfer directly to Compréhension Orale. The QUESTION-ANSWERING skills are IDENTICAL. Only the INPUT changes: eyes → ears.

WHAT IS DIFFERENT IN LISTENING

While 80% transfers, 20% is genuinely different:

THE 20% THAT IS DIFFERENT:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  DIFFERENCE 1: YOU CANNOT RE-READ
  ────────────────────────────────
  In reading: "I missed something → re-read the paragraph"
  In listening: "I missed something → IT'S GONE"
  
  IMPACT: No re-reading windows (Chapter 20) are possible
  ADAPTATION: You must capture information on FIRST listen
  → Pre-read questions BEFORE listening (unlike reading 
    where we read text first)
  → Know what to listen FOR before the audio starts
  → Take notes during listening (external memory)
  
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  DIFFERENCE 2: SPEED IS NOT YOUR CHOICE
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  In reading: You control reading speed
  In listening: The SPEAKER controls speed
  
  IMPACT: No slowing down for difficult passages
  ADAPTATION: 
  → Accept that you will miss some words
  → Focus on KEYWORDS, not every word
  → Use the same "don't understand every word" 
    principle from Chapter 1, but more aggressively
  → Capture the GIST of each segment
  
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  DIFFERENCE 3: PHONETIC CHALLENGES
  ──────────────────────────────────
  In reading: Words are spelled out → visual recognition
  In listening: Words are SOUNDS → auditory recognition
  
  IMPACT: 
  → Homophones confuse: "vers/vert/verre/ver"
  → Liaisons blur word boundaries: "les‿amis" sounds 
    like one word
  → Fast speech swallows syllables
  → Accents vary between speakers
  
  ADAPTATION:
  → Practice listening to varied French accents
  → Focus on MEANING, not individual word identification
  → Use context to resolve ambiguous sounds
    (same principle as Chapter 3's context clues, 
    but applied to SOUNDS instead of SPELLINGS)
  
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  DIFFERENCE 4: NOTE-TAKING BECOMES CRITICAL
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  In reading: The text is your reference → always available
  In listening: The audio plays once (or twice) → then gone
  
  IMPACT: Your notes ARE your text
  ADAPTATION:
  → Develop a shorthand notation system
  → Note: WHO said WHAT + KEY NUMBERS + KEY WORDS
  → Don't try to transcribe → capture STRUCTURE
  → Your notes should look like Chapter 20's margin notes:
    quick, structural, keyword-based

THE LISTENING COMPREHENSION PROTOCOL (Built From Reading Skills)

THE COMPRÉHENSION ORALE PROTOCOL:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  BEFORE LISTENING (equivalent to Chapter 1's pre-reading):
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  1. READ ALL QUESTIONS AND OPTIONS FIRST
     → Unlike reading, where you read the text first
     → In listening, you MUST know what to listen for
     → Apply Chapter 19's scope dissection to each question
     → Build answer templates BEFORE the audio starts
     → Know your "listening targets" (what keywords/facts 
       you need to capture)
  
  2. PREDICT THE CONTENT
     → From the questions, predict what the audio is about
     → "The questions mention a scientist and predictions → 
       probably a report about technology"
     → This prediction PRIMES your brain for relevant 
       vocabulary (same as Chapter 16's domain recognition)

  DURING FIRST LISTEN (equivalent to Chapter 1's structural read):
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  3. LISTEN FOR STRUCTURE, NOT DETAILS
     → How many speakers?
     → What is the context? (interview, news report, 
       conversation, lecture)
     → What is the overall topic?
     → What is the tone? (serious, casual, alarmed, neutral)
     → DON'T try to understand every word
     → Build a MENTAL MAP of the audio
  
  4. TAKE STRUCTURAL NOTES
     → Speaker 1: [topic/position]
     → Speaker 2: [topic/position]
     → Key numbers: [dates, quantities]
     → Key words: [repeated or emphasized terms]

  DURING SECOND LISTEN (equivalent to Chapter 20's targeted re-read):
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  5. LISTEN FOR SPECIFIC ANSWERS
     → You know the questions
     → You know the structure from first listen
     → Now listen for the SPECIFIC information each 
       question asks about
     → Fill in your notes with targeted details
  
  6. MARK CONFIDENCE LEVELS
     → "I clearly heard this" → high confidence
     → "I think I heard this" → medium confidence
     → "I'm not sure" → low confidence → flag for 
       careful answer selection

  AFTER LISTENING (equivalent to Chapters 6, 18, 19, 22):
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  7. ANSWER QUESTIONS USING ALL READING TOOLS
     → Scope dissection (Chapter 19) → IDENTICAL
     → Elimination method (Chapter 6) → IDENTICAL
     → 4-check protocol (Chapter 22) → IDENTICAL
     → Scoring system (Chapter 18) → IDENTICAL
     → The answer options are WRITTEN, not spoken
     → You use READING skills to evaluate them
     → Your NOTES replace the text as your reference
  
  8. REVIEW AND VERIFY
     → Check answers against your notes
     → If notes are insufficient, use logical inference
     → Apply the "most supported" principle from Chapter 13

THE SKILL TRANSFER PRIORITY LIST

Not all reading skills transfer equally. Here is the priority order for applying reading skills to listening:

SKILL TRANSFER PRIORITY (Highest to Lowest):
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PRIORITY 1: TRANSFERS PERFECTLY (use immediately)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Chapter 6:  Elimination method
  → Chapter 18: Tie-breaking scoring
  → Chapter 19: Scope analysis + question dissection
  → Chapter 22: Distractor detection (all 8 techniques)
  
  WHY: These skills operate on the ANSWER OPTIONS, 
  which are WRITTEN in both reading and listening exams.
  The input (text vs audio) doesn't affect these skills.

  PRIORITY 2: TRANSFERS WITH ADAPTATION
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Chapter 1:  First scan → becomes pre-listening prep
  → Chapter 8:  Paraphrase recognition → same skill, 
                audio source instead of text source
  → Chapter 14: Tone detection → enhanced by voice/intonation
  → Chapter 20: Speed reading → becomes active listening
  → Chapter 21: Voice tracking → becomes speaker tracking
  
  WHY: The core PRINCIPLE transfers, but the APPLICATION 
  method changes from visual to auditory.

  PRIORITY 3: TRANSFERS CONCEPTUALLY
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Chapter 3:  Context clues → phonetic context clues
  → Chapter 11: Inference → same skill, less evidence
  → Chapter 12: Cause-effect → must capture from audio
  → Chapter 16: Technical vocabulary → must recognize 
                aurally, not visually
  → Chapter 17: Historical comprehension → note-dependent
  
  WHY: The THINKING SKILL transfers, but the 
  INFORMATION CAPTURE method is fundamentally different.

  PRIORITY 4: DOES NOT TRANSFER
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  → Chapter 20: Re-reading window technique
    (cannot re-listen to specific sections)
  → Chapter 7: Keyword scanning in text
    (cannot visually scan audio)
  → Physical text marking (no text to mark)
  
  WHY: These skills depend on the TEXT being available 
  as a persistent, scannable reference.
  → REPLACEMENT: Note-taking fills this role in listening

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: SKIPPING THE SELF-CORRECTION LOOP

"I got 8/10, that's good enough. I don't need to 
 analyze the 2 I got wrong."

REALITY: Those 2 errors contain the MOST valuable 
information in the entire simulation. They reveal 
your blind spots — the patterns you CAN'T see 
without analysis.

If you skip analysis:
→ Same 2 error types will repeat on the real exam
→ You'll get 8/10 again (or worse under pressure)
→ You'll think "I'm just not good at this"

If you analyze:
→ You discover: "Both errors were half-truth traps"
→ You practice compound verification for 1 week
→ Next simulation: 10/10
→ The weakness is GONE permanently

MISTAKE 2: PRACTICING WITHOUT A SYSTEM

"I'll just read lots of French texts and do lots 
 of practice questions. Quantity will make me better."

REALITY: Practice without diagnosis = repeating mistakes.

100 practice questions with no error analysis = 
  SAME error rate, just more familiarity with the format

20 practice questions WITH full error analysis = 
  TARGETED improvement on specific weaknesses

QUALITY × ANALYSIS > QUANTITY × REPETITION

Every practice session should end with:
→ What did I get wrong?
→ WHY did I get it wrong?
→ Which category?
→ What will I do differently?

MISTAKE 3: TRYING TO MASTER LISTENING BEFORE MASTERING READING

"I should practice both reading and listening at 
 the same time to be efficient."

REALITY: Reading comprehension is the FOUNDATION.
Listening comprehension is the EXTENSION.

If your reading skills are weak:
→ Your question-answering skills are weak
→ Your scope analysis is weak
→ Your distractor detection is weak
→ These weaknesses TRANSFER to listening
→ You'll be bad at BOTH

If your reading skills are strong:
→ 80% of the skills transfer to listening
→ You only need to add note-taking + phonetic recognition
→ Listening improvement is FAST because the foundation exists

SEQUENCE: Master reading FIRST (Chapters 1-25)
          THEN bridge to listening (this chapter's protocol)
          
The bridge only works if the foundation is solid.

MISTAKE 4: ABANDONING THE SYSTEM ON EXAM DAY

"I know all the protocols, but on the actual exam 
 I'll just... read and answer. The system is too rigid."

REALITY: Exam pressure activates your EMOTIONAL brain.
The emotional brain makes decisions based on:
→ Gut feeling (unreliable under stress)
→ First impressions (susceptible to traps)
→ Sophistication bias (amplified by anxiety)
→ Speed impulse (rushing to finish)

The SYSTEM activates your ANALYTICAL brain.
The analytical brain makes decisions based on:
→ Textual evidence (reliable regardless of stress)
→ Systematic checks (immune to traps)
→ Protocol steps (override biases)
→ Time management (prevent rushing)

ON EXAM DAY:
→ Your emotional brain will say: "Just pick the one 
   that sounds right!"
→ Your analytical brain will say: "Run the 4-check 
   protocol."
→ LISTEN TO THE ANALYTICAL BRAIN.
→ That is why you practiced the system.
→ That is why you built the protocols.
→ They exist to PROTECT you from your emotional brain 
   under pressure.
