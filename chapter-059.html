Chapter 7: BAUDELAIRE — CHRONOLOGICAL GAP-FILL

✒️ Texte 66 | Phase 2: Easy | Core Patterns

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Baudelaire est né à Paris. ...(36)... Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans, était né bien avant la Révolution. Il avait été prêtre mais avait renoncé à cette fonction au moment de la Révolution. Il était très amateur de peinture et Baudelaire lui doit ce qu'il appelle le "culte des images (ma grande, mon unique, ma primitive passion)", ...(37)... La mère de Baudelaire se remarie avec un officier, le commandant Aupick, qui sera nommé général en 1839.

...(38)... C'est un bon élève, très brillant en vers latins. Il annonce à sa famille qu'il veut être écrivain. ...(39)... mais il mène une vie si désordonnée que le général Aupick lui conseille fermement de faire un voyage. Baudelaire part pour Calcutta, mais il ne va pas au terme de la route ; il s'arrête à l'île de la Réunion. ...(40)...

THE FIVE QUESTIONS

Question 36 :

  • A — Il est le seul garçon, après trois filles.
  • B — Sa mère, fragile, meurt à sa naissance.
  • C — Dans la famille, sa naissance est très attendue.
  • D — À sa naissance, sa mère a 28 ans. ✅

Question 37 :

  • A — Ce père meurt en 1827. ✅
  • B — La peinture ne l'intéresse donc pas autant que la poésie.
  • C — Le fils découvre ainsi la peinture en 1827.
  • D — Père et fils se souviennent de la révolution.

Question 38 :

  • A — Baudelaire assiste au décès de son père.
  • B — La même année, Baudelaire est reçu à son baccalauréat. ✅
  • C — Alors, Baudelaire s'engage dans l'armée.
  • D — Plus tard, Baudelaire s'enfuit et abandonne les études.

Question 39 :

  • A — On ne s'oppose pas à cette idée. ✅
  • B — Il renonce donc un moment à l'écriture.
  • C — Sa vie est alors bien réglée.
  • D — On ne fête pas ses 25 ans.

Question 40 :

  • A — Il découvre alors la vie en Inde.
  • B — Il écrit donc ses plus beaux poèmes.
  • C — Et là, il prend le premier bateau qui revient en France. ✅
  • D — Là, il retrouve enfin son père.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's different from Project 6?

Project 6 introduced gap-fill with a factual/informational text. The connectors were clear ("C'est ainsi," "eux"), and each gap was independent — solving Gap 16 didn't affect Gap 17.

This text introduces THREE new challenges:

ChallengeWhat It Means
Biographical narrativeEvents must follow a LIFE CHRONOLOGY (birth → childhood → school → adulthood)
Five interconnected gapsInformation from one gap affects what can go in another gap
Pronoun chains"Il," "son," "ce père," "lui" create reference chains that constrain options

The critical difference: INTERCONNECTED gaps

In Project 6, each gap lived in its own paragraph. Here, Gaps 36 and 37 are in the SAME paragraph. Gaps 38 and 39 are in the SAME paragraph. This means:

Solving one gap correctly may ELIMINATE options for another gap. Solving one gap INCORRECTLY may make another gap unsolvable.

This creates a new strategic requirement:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #23: In multi-gap texts, solve the EASIEST gap first (the one with the clearest constraints), then use that answer to constrain the remaining gaps. Gaps are not independent — they form an interconnected system.

Step 1: Identify the text type

Quick scan reveals:

  • A famous person's name (Baudelaire)
  • Birth, family, education, travel events
  • Chronological progression
  • Past tenses throughout

Verdict: BIOGRAPHICAL NARRATIVE GAP-FILL (texte biographique à trous)

Biographical Text FeatureWhat It Means for Gap-Fill
Events follow chronological orderEach gap must respect the timeline
Family relationships are centralOptions mentioning dead people can't coexist with later events involving them
Dates and ages provide anchorsUse them to verify chronological plausibility
Life transitions mark paragraph shiftsSchool → career → travel follows a predictable life sequence

Step 2: Map the timeline BEFORE solving any gap

Before touching the options, read the COMPLETE text (with gaps) and extract every fixed date/event:

BAUDELAIRE'S TIMELINE (from fixed text only):

[BIRTH]      → "est né à Paris"
[GAP 36]     → ???
[FATHER]     → père âgé de 62 ans, born before Revolution (1789)
              → was a priest, renounced during Revolution
              → lover of painting → Baudelaire inherits "culte des images"
[GAP 37]     → ???

Now I have the SKELETON. Each gap must fit into this skeleton without breaking the chronology or contradicting fixed facts.

Step 3: Identify the easiest gap to solve first

Looking at the constraints:

  • Gap 36: Between birth and father description. Moderate constraints.
  • Gap 37: Between father description and mother's remarriage. STRONG constraint — mother remarries, so something must happen to father.
  • Gap 38: Between 1839 (Aupick named general) and school description. Moderate constraints.
  • Gap 39: Between "wants to be a writer" and "mais" (disordered life). STRONG constraint — "mais" demands contrast.
  • Gap 40: After stopping at Réunion. Moderate constraints.

Easiest gaps: 37 and 39 — they have the strongest contextual constraints. Let me solve these first.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT — Strategic Solving Order

I'll solve in this order: 37 → 39 → 36 → 40 → 38

This is NOT the text order — it's the ORDER OF CONSTRAINT STRENGTH. Starting with the most constrained gaps reduces uncertainty for the rest.

GAP 37 (Solved FIRST — strongest constraint)

The Sandwich:

BEFORE Gap 37:

"Il était très amateur de peinture et Baudelaire lui doit ce qu'il appelle le 'culte des images (ma grande, mon unique, ma primitive passion)'"

The BEFORE talks about the FATHER's love of painting and its influence on Baudelaire.

AFTER Gap 37:

"La mère de Baudelaire se remarie avec un officier, le commandant Aupick"

The AFTER says the MOTHER REMARRIES.

The critical deduction:

LOGICAL CHAIN:
Mother "se remarie" (remarries)
         ↓
"Re-marier" = marry AGAIN
         ↓
She must have been married before (to Baudelaire's father)
         ↓
For her to remarry, the first marriage must have ENDED
         ↓
In early 19th century context: the husband must have DIED
         ↓
Therefore: THE FATHER MUST DIE before the gap ends

The gap MUST mention the father's DEATH. This is the only logical bridge between "father's influence on Baudelaire" and "mother remarries."

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #24: In biographical gap-fill texts, LIFE EVENTS create mandatory logical chains. If the text says someone remarries, the previous spouse must have died or divorced. If the text says someone graduated, they must have been enrolled. These IMPLICIT PREREQUISITES must be satisfied by the gap.

Now let's test:

Option A: "Ce père meurt en 1827." (This father dies in 1827.)

Test BACKWARD:
BEFORE discusses the father's qualities. "Ce père" (this father) refers directly back to the father just described. The pronoun reference is clean. ✓

Test FORWARD:
Father dies → mother can remarry. The logical chain is complete. ✓

Chronological check:
Father dies 1827. Aupick named general 1839. Remarriage happens between these dates. Plausible. ✓

Pronoun check:
"Ce père" — the demonstrative "ce" (this) refers to the father just described in detail. This is a closing reference — it wraps up the father section before transitioning to the mother.

✅ CORRECT — Satisfies the death-before-remarriage prerequisite

Option B: "La peinture ne l'intéresse donc pas autant que la poésie."
(Painting doesn't interest him as much as poetry.)

Test BACKWARD:
BEFORE says Baudelaire inherited the "culte des images" from his father. This is presented as a POSITIVE influence. Option B says painting doesn't interest him much — this CONTRADICTS the "culte des images" passage.

"Ma grande, mon unique, ma primitive passion" — Baudelaire calls images his GREATEST, UNIQUE, PRIMITIVE passion. Then Option B says painting doesn't interest him? Direct contradiction.

❌ ELIMINATED — Contradicts the established passion for images

Option C: "Le fils découvre ainsi la peinture en 1827."
(The son thus discovers painting in 1827.)

Test FORWARD:
After the gap, the mother remarries. Option C mentions painting discovery — but there's NO logical bridge from "discovering painting" to "mother remarries." The remarriage still lacks its prerequisite (father's death).

The gap MUST enable the remarriage. Discovering painting does not.

❌ ELIMINATED — Fails to provide the death-before-remarriage prerequisite

Option D: "Père et fils se souviennent de la révolution."
(Father and son remember the Revolution.)

Factual check:
The text says the father was "né bien avant la Révolution" (born well before the Revolution of 1789). Baudelaire was born around 1821. He was born 32 years AFTER the Revolution. He CANNOT remember it.

This is a factual impossibility trap — the option contains a claim that is historically impossible given the dates in the text.

❌ ELIMINATED — Baudelaire was born decades after the Revolution

This introduces an important verification tool:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #25: In biographical/historical texts, CROSS-CHECK dates and ages. If the text provides birth dates, ages, or historical anchors, use arithmetic to verify whether an option is chronologically possible.

🎯 Gap 37 Answer: A — Ce père meurt en 1827.

CRITICAL CASCADING EFFECT: Now that we know the father dies in 1827, this fact becomes a CONSTRAINT for all other gaps. Any option mentioning the father as alive after 1827 is automatically eliminated.

CASCADE RULE:
Father dies 1827 → 
  ✗ Any gap after 37 cannot show father alive
  ✗ Gap 40 Option D ("retrouve enfin son père") → IMPOSSIBLE

I've already eliminated an option for Gap 40 without even solving it yet. This is the power of Golden Rule #23 — solve the most constrained gap first, then cascade.

GAP 39 (Solved SECOND — strong "mais" constraint)

The Sandwich:

BEFORE Gap 39:

"Il annonce à sa famille qu'il veut être écrivain."

Baudelaire announces his ambition to be a writer.

AFTER Gap 39:

"mais il mène une vie si désordonnée que le général Aupick lui conseille fermement de faire un voyage."

"Mais" (but) introduces a CONTRAST. Something positive or neutral → BUT → disordered life.

The pattern:

BEFORE: Announces writing ambition
GAP: [reaction to the announcement]
AFTER: "mais" (BUT) → disordered life → forced travel

The "mais" tells us: whatever the gap says will be CONTRASTED by the disordered life. The gap should be something POSITIVE or PERMISSIVE that is then undermined.

Predict: The family accepts his writing ambition (positive) → BUT he leads a disordered life (negative contrast).

Now let's test:

Option A: "On ne s'oppose pas à cette idée."
(No one opposes this idea.)

Test BACKWARD:
"Cette idée" (this idea) refers to "il veut être écrivain" (he wants to be a writer). The pronoun reference is clean — "this idea" = the writing ambition. ✓

Test FORWARD:
Family doesn't oppose → "mais" → disordered life.

The contrast works perfectly:

POSITIVE: Family accepts his writing ambition
    ↕ CONTRAST ("mais")
NEGATIVE: But he leads a disordered life

The "mais" creates a logical pivot: "They let him pursue writing, BUT he abused this freedom by living recklessly."

✅ CORRECT — Creates the contrast that "mais" demands

Option B: "Il renonce donc un moment à l'écriture."
(He therefore gives up writing for a while.)

Test BACKWARD:
He announces he wants to be a writer → then immediately gives up writing? This is a bizarre sequence. Why announce an ambition only to immediately abandon it?

Test FORWARD:
He gives up writing → "mais" → disordered life. The "mais" contrast would be: "He gives up writing, BUT he leads a disordered life." This contrast is weak — giving up writing and living recklessly are BOTH negative. "Mais" needs positive vs. negative, not negative vs. negative.

❌ ELIMINATED — Contradicts BEFORE (announces ambition then abandons it) + weak contrast with AFTER

Option C: "Sa vie est alors bien réglée."
(His life is then well-ordered.)

Test FORWARD:
"His life is well-ordered" → "mais il mène une vie si désordonnée"

"Bien réglée" (well-ordered) → "mais" → "désordonnée" (disordered).

At first glance, this seems like a PERFECT contrast. Ordered → but → disordered.

BUT WAIT. Read the temporal logic:

The BEFORE says he announces his writing ambition. Option C says "sa vie est alors bien réglée" (his life is then well-ordered). Then "mais il mène une vie si désordonnée."

"Alors" = at that time / then. "Mais" = but. The sequence becomes:

"He announces he wants to be a writer. His life is then well-ordered. But he leads such a disordered life..."

This creates a temporal paradox. At the SAME moment in his life, his life is both "bien réglée" AND "désordonnée"? These are simultaneous contradictions about the same period, not a progression from one state to another.

For this to work, there would need to be a TIME GAP between "bien réglée" and "désordonnée" — but the text presents them as consecutive without any time marker between them.

Compare with Option A:
"He announces writing. No one opposes this. But he leads a disordered life."

Here, the family's acceptance (a DECISION) leads to freedom, which leads to disorder. The causal chain is logical. Option C has no causal chain — just a flat contradiction.

❌ ELIMINATED — Temporal paradox (simultaneously ordered and disordered) + no causal logic

This is subtle but critical. The lesson:

In gap-fill, a contrast created by "mais" must be CAUSALLY connected, not just semantically opposite. "They accepted → but he abused the freedom" is causal. "Life was ordered → but life was disordered" is a bare contradiction without cause.

Option D: "On ne fête pas ses 25 ans."
(His 25th birthday is not celebrated.)

Test BACKWARD:
He announces writing ambition → his birthday isn't celebrated? No logical connection. Why would his birthday be relevant here?

Test FORWARD:
Birthday not celebrated → "mais" → disordered life? No meaningful contrast. These are unrelated facts.

Additionally: There's no chronological anchor that makes "25 ans" relevant at this point in the narrative. The text has not established Baudelaire's age at this stage.

❌ ELIMINATED — No connection to BEFORE or meaningful contrast with AFTER

🎯 Gap 39 Answer: A — On ne s'oppose pas à cette idée.

GAP 36 (Solved THIRD)

The Sandwich:

BEFORE Gap 36:

"Baudelaire est né à Paris."

Simple birth statement. The gap comes immediately after.

AFTER Gap 36:

"Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans, était né bien avant la Révolution."

"Mais" = But. This introduces a CONTRAST with the gap.
"Son père, âgé de 62 ans" = his father, aged 62.

The critical clue: "Mais...âgé de 62 ans"

The "Mais" (But) signals contrast. The AFTER sentence emphasizes the father's OLD AGE (62). For "But his father was 62" to be a meaningful contrast, the gap must mention something about YOUTH or a YOUNGER person.

Predict: The gap should mention something about a YOUNG person (probably the mother) so that "But his father was 62" creates an age contrast.

Now let's test:

Option A: "Il est le seul garçon, après trois filles."
(He is the only boy, after three girls.)

Test FORWARD:
"He's the only boy after three girls" → "Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans..."

What contrast does "mais" create? Being the only boy vs. father being 62? These are unrelated facts. "Mais" needs a logical opposition, not just two different facts.

❌ ELIMINATED — No meaningful contrast with "Mais...62 ans"

Option B: "Sa mère, fragile, meurt à sa naissance."
(His mother, fragile, dies at his birth.)

Test against LATER text (cascading check):
The text says AFTER Gap 37: "La mère de Baudelaire se remarie"

If the mother dies at birth (Option B), she CANNOT remarry later. This is a FATAL CONTRADICTION with a later fixed element.

CASCADING CONTRADICTION:
Gap 36 Option B: Mother DIES at birth
         ↓ CONTRADICTS
Fixed text after Gap 37: Mother REMARRIES
         ↓
IMPOSSIBLE — dead people don't remarry

This is the most powerful demonstration of Golden Rule #23 (interconnected gaps). Even without checking the Sandwich, we can eliminate this option by checking it against the REST of the text.

❌ ELIMINATED — Mother can't remarry if she died at birth (fatal cascade contradiction)

This teaches a crucial new technique:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #26: In multi-gap texts, always check each option against the ENTIRE text, not just the immediate Sandwich. An option that fits the local context perfectly may contradict a fact mentioned paragraphs later. Scan forward AND backward across all fixed text.

Option C: "Dans la famille, sa naissance est très attendue."
(In the family, his birth is eagerly awaited.)

Test FORWARD:
"His birth is eagerly awaited" → "Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans..."

"Mais" contrast: eagerly awaited birth → but father is 62? The contrast is weak. Being awaited and having an old father aren't naturally opposing ideas. There's no logical "but" between excitement and age.

❌ ELIMINATED — Weak/illogical contrast with "Mais...62 ans"

Option D: "À sa naissance, sa mère a 28 ans."
(At his birth, his mother is 28.)

Test FORWARD:
"His mother is 28" → "Mais son père, âgé de 62 ans..."

CONTRAST:
Mother = 28 years old (YOUNG)
    ↕ "Mais" (BUT)
Father = 62 years old (OLD)

PERFECT AGE CONTRAST. The "Mais" creates a striking opposition between a young mother and an old father. The numbers make the contrast vivid: 28 vs. 62 — a 34-year age difference.

This is exactly what "Mais" demands — a meaningful, sharp contrast.

Test BACKWARD:
"Born in Paris" → "Mother is 28 at his birth" → natural continuation of birth circumstances. ✓

Cascade check:
Mother is alive and 28 at birth → she can remarry later. ✓ (No contradiction with rest of text.)

✅ CORRECT — Creates perfect age contrast demanded by "Mais"

🎯 Gap 36 Answer: D — À sa naissance, sa mère a 28 ans.

THE MASTER LESSON FROM GAP 36:

The entire solution depended on understanding WHY "Mais" was there. "Mais" doesn't just mean "but" — it creates a LOGICAL OPPOSITION that the gap must provide one side of.

Here's a framework for "Mais" in gap-fill:

WHEN "MAIS" APPEARS IN THE AFTER SENTENCE:

Case 1: "Mais" starts the AFTER sentence
→ The gap provides SIDE A
→ AFTER provides SIDE B (the contrasting side)
→ The gap must be the POSITIVE/FIRST element of the contrast

Case 2: "Mais" appears WITHIN the gap sentence
→ The gap itself contains both sides of the contrast
→ Less common in gap-fill

Gap 36 is Case 1: the gap provides the mother's youth (Side A), and "Mais" introduces the father's age (Side B).

GAP 40 (Solved FOURTH)

The Sandwich:

BEFORE Gap 40:

"Baudelaire part pour Calcutta, mais il ne va pas au terme de la route ; il s'arrête à l'île de la Réunion."

He departs for Calcutta but stops at Réunion. He doesn't complete the journey.

AFTER Gap 40:
This is the LAST sentence of the text. There is NO after sentence. The gap IS the ending.

Special case: No AFTER constraint.

When a gap is at the END of the text, you lose the forward constraint. You can only check:

  1. Backward connection (does it follow from BEFORE?)
  2. Cascade coherence (does it contradict anything established in the text?)
  3. Narrative closure (does it provide a satisfying ending to the story?)

Predict: He stops at Réunion and... either stays there, goes back to France, or does something on the island. Given the narrative arc (sent away because of bad behavior), returning home would be the most narratively logical conclusion.

Now let's test:

Option A: "Il découvre alors la vie en Inde."
(He then discovers life in India.)

Test BACKWARD:
The text EXPLICITLY says: "il ne va pas au terme de la route" (he does NOT reach his destination) and "il s'arrête à l'île de la Réunion" (he stops at Réunion).

Réunion is in the Indian Ocean, but it is NOT India. And the text says he did NOT reach Calcutta (India). Option A says he discovers life in India — but he NEVER got to India.

DIRECT FACTUAL CONTRADICTION. The text says he stopped short of India; the option says he experienced India.

❌ ELIMINATED — Contradicts explicit statement that he didn't reach India

Option B: "Il écrit donc ses plus beaux poèmes."
(He therefore writes his most beautiful poems.)

Test BACKWARD:
He stops at Réunion → therefore writes his best poems? The "donc" (therefore) implies a causal link between stopping at Réunion and writing poetry. This causal link is not established.

Narrative coherence:
The text has been building a story of a young man whose disordered life forced him to travel. Writing his "most beautiful poems" at Réunion would be a major claim requiring more context. The text provides none.

More importantly, this would be a POSITIVE ending to what has been presented as a PROBLEM narrative (forced travel due to bad behavior). The tonal shift is too abrupt.

❌ ELIMINATED — Unsubstantiated claim + tonal mismatch with problem narrative

Option C: "Et là, il prend le premier bateau qui revient en France."
(And there, he takes the first boat back to France.)

Test BACKWARD:
He was sent to Calcutta → stopped at Réunion → takes the first boat back to France.

This is narratively perfect. The story has established:

  1. He was FORCED to travel (didn't choose to go)
  2. He didn't complete the journey (stopped short)
  3. Taking the first boat home = he wanted to return as quickly as possible

The pattern: reluctant traveler → incomplete journey → immediate return. Each step logically follows the previous one.

"Et là" (and there) connects smoothly to "il s'arrête à l'île de la Réunion" — "there" = at Réunion. ✓

Narrative closure:
The story ends with Baudelaire cutting his forced trip short and rushing home. This is a satisfying biographical ending — the young rebel who refused to be sent away.

✅ CORRECT — Logical narrative conclusion to the reluctant journey

Option D: "Là, il retrouve enfin son père."
(There, he finally finds his father again.)

CASCADE CHECK (from Gap 37):
We established in Gap 37 that the father DIED in 1827. Baudelaire cannot "find his father again" because his father is dead.

CASCADING ELIMINATION:
Gap 37 answer: Father dies in 1827
         ↓
Gap 40 Option D: He finds his father again
         ↓
IMPOSSIBLE — the father is dead

This is exactly the cascade we predicted when solving Gap 37. The power of solving interconnected gaps in the right order.

❌ ELIMINATED — Father is dead (cascade contradiction from Gap 37)

🎯 Gap 40 Answer: C — Et là, il prend le premier bateau qui revient en France.

GAP 38 (Solved LAST)

The Sandwich:

BEFORE Gap 38:

"La mère de Baudelaire se remarie avec un officier, le commandant Aupick, qui sera nommé général en 1839."

Last fixed date: 1839 (Aupick becomes general).

AFTER Gap 38:

"C'est un bon élève, très brillant en vers latins."

Describes Baudelaire as a good student. This is about EDUCATION.

The bridge needed:

BEFORE: Mother remarries, stepfather becomes general in 1839
GAP: [must transition to education context]
AFTER: "Good student, brilliant in Latin verse"

The gap must introduce Baudelaire's SCHOOL LIFE so that "C'est un bon élève" has a context.

Now let's test:

Option A: "Baudelaire assiste au décès de son père."
(Baudelaire witnesses his father's death.)

CASCADE CHECK:
The father died in 1827 (established in Gap 37). Baudelaire was born around 1821, making him about 6 years old in 1827.

But the main problem: this gap comes AFTER the mother's remarriage, which itself comes AFTER the father's death. Witnessing the father's death should appear BEFORE Gap 37, not after the remarriage.

CHRONOLOGICAL ERROR. The father's death is already past at this point in the narrative. Mentioning it here would be going BACKWARD in time.

❌ ELIMINATED — Chronological regression (father's death already occurred earlier)

Option B: "La même année, Baudelaire est reçu à son baccalauréat."
(The same year, Baudelaire passes his baccalauréat.)

"La même année" (the same year) refers to 1839 — the last date mentioned (Aupick becomes general).

Test FORWARD:
"Passes his baccalauréat in 1839" → "C'est un bon élève, très brillant en vers latins."

Baudelaire passes his baccalauréat (final secondary school exam). Then the text describes him as a "good student" — this is a RETROSPECTIVE summary of his school career, triggered by the mention of his exam success.

FLOW:
1839: Passes baccalauréat
         ↓
Retrospective: "He was a good student" (summary of school years)
         ↓
Announces writing ambition (post-school decision)

This chronological sequence makes sense: finish school → described as good student → decide on career.

Chronological check:
Born ~1821. Baccalauréat in 1839. Age: ~18. This is the CORRECT age for the baccalauréat in 19th century France. ✓

Cascade check:
Father died 1827. Mother remarried. Stepfather becomes general 1839. Same year, Baudelaire finishes school. All consistent. ✓

✅ CORRECT — "La même année" links to 1839, introduces education context

Option C: "Alors, Baudelaire s'engage dans l'armée."
(Then, Baudelaire enlists in the army.)

Test FORWARD:
Enlists in army → "C'est un bon élève" (he's a good student)?

If he enlisted in the army, why would the next sentence describe him as a student? Soldiers and students are different roles. The AFTER sentence specifically mentions school-related qualities (Latin verse).

❌ ELIMINATED — Army context contradicts student description in AFTER

Option D: "Plus tard, Baudelaire s'enfuit et abandonne les études."
(Later, Baudelaire runs away and abandons his studies.)

Test FORWARD:
Abandons studies → "C'est un bon élève"?

If he abandoned his studies, calling him a "good student" immediately after creates a jarring contradiction. The text would read: "He runs away and abandons his studies. He's a good student." — this makes no sense.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Abandons studies" directly contradicts "good student"

🎯 Gap 38 Answer: B — La même année, Baudelaire est reçu à son baccalauréat.

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For Biographical/Chronological Gap-Fill Texts Specifically:

1. Build the timeline FIRST — before touching any option.
Extract every date, age, and event anchor. This is your chronological skeleton (as demonstrated above).

2. Solve the most CONSTRAINED gap first.
Don't solve in text order. Solve in CONSTRAINT order. The gap with the clearest logical requirement (death before remarriage, contrast demanded by "mais") should be solved first.

3. CASCADE every answer.
After solving each gap, ask: "Does this answer eliminate any options in other gaps?"

Example from this text:

Gap 37: Father dies 1827
  → CASCADE: Gap 40 Option D eliminated (father can't be found)
  → CASCADE: Gap 38 Option A eliminated (father's death already past)

4. Check "La même année" / "Alors" / "Plus tard" against the timeline.
These temporal expressions must match the chronological position of the gap.

ExpressionMeaningWhat It Anchors To
"La même année"The same yearThe LAST date mentioned before the gap
"Alors"Then / At that timeThe general period of the narrative
"Plus tard"LaterAn unspecified future point
"Depuis"Since thenFrom a past event to the present

5. Verify AGES arithmetically.
If you know a birth date and a gap mentions an age (or vice versa), calculate: "Is this age plausible at this point in the timeline?"

6. Check pronoun references ("ce père," "cette idée," "y," "en").
These pronouns MUST refer to something specific. Identify what they refer to and verify the reference is valid.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 20: The Life Event Prerequisite Miss (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 19, 20.

What it is: Failing to recognize that a life event mentioned in the fixed text REQUIRES a prior event that the gap must provide.

How to detect: When you see remarriage, graduation, retirement, or any major life event in the AFTER text, ask: "What MUST have happened before this event could occur?"

In this text: Mother remarries → father must have died first.

Defense: Build the prerequisite chain for every major life event.

PITFALL 21: The Chronological Regression (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Project 12.

What it is: An option that mentions an event that should have occurred EARLIER in the timeline, creating a backward jump.

How to detect: Check if the event in the option has ALREADY been covered by a previous gap or fixed text section.

In this text: Gap 38, Option A mentions the father's death — but the father already died in the Gap 37 section. Mentioning it again after the remarriage goes backward in time.

PITFALL 22: The Cascade Blindness (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 19, 20.

What it is: Solving each gap independently without checking how answers interact across gaps.

How to detect: After solving any gap, immediately ask: "What does this answer tell me about the OTHER gaps?"

In this text: Solving Gap 37 (father dies) immediately eliminates Gap 40 Option D (finds father) and Gap 38 Option A (witnesses death).
