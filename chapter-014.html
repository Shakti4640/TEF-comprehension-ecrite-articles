📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le saumon d'élevage : mine d'or de la Norvège

La consommation de saumon augmente chaque année, et la Norvège, premier producteur mondial, doit s'adapter pour répondre à la demande quitte à industrialiser toujours plus sa production. Multiplier par cinq la production d'ici 2050, voilà l'objectif ambitieux de la Norvège. Les exportations norvégiennes ont atteint un record en 2016 avec un chiffre d'affaire de 7,5 milliards d'euros, rien que pour le saumon. C'est la France qui se trouve en tête du classement des consommateurs de produits marins norvégiens : nous consommons en moyenne 2,3 kilos de saumon norvégien par an.

Cette augmentation constante de la demande pousse le pays scandinave à mettre en place des projets faramineux, comme par exemple une ferme offshore en haute mer capable d'accueillir plus d'un million de saumons. Mais l'industrialisation massive de l'élevage pose des problèmes pour les saumons sauvages. Le saumon sauvage mis en péril par le saumon industriel.

L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes en ce qui concerne le saumon : la prolifération des poux de mer et l'évasion des saumons d'élevage. Ces deux conséquences de l'élevage à très grande échelle mettent en péril l'écosystème et le patrimoine génétique des saumons sauvages. « Nous faisons beaucoup d'efforts d'un point de vue technique pour trouver des solutions pour réduire les traitements chimiques et les remplacer par des techniques naturelles, » indique Catarina Martins, directrice environnement de la compagnie Marine Harvest. Des saumons qui peuvent être exposés à des produits toxiques. Le saumon pourrait aussi contenir des produits toxiques comme des dioxines, des pesticides et même des métaux lourds. En 2013, la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation de saumon modérée pour les enfants et les femmes enceintes. Mais le risque, dû notamment à la pollution des eaux, ne concerne plus uniquement le saumon d'élevage.

« Aujourd'hui même le saumon bio est pollué par les métaux lourds et notamment le mercure », affirme Jean-Philippe Magnen, ancien élu Europe Écologie Les Verts pour qui « Il faut changer le modèle industriel d'élevage, aujourd'hui il y a une pollution qui est très inquiétante, qui n'est pas seulement sur le saumon d'élevage puisque les eaux sont polluées avec des métaux lourds. »

Why this is correct:

             = INDUSTRIAL PRACTICES (production targets, exports, consumption)

PARAGRAPH 2: Industrial expansion — offshore farms, massive scale
             = INDUSTRIAL PRACTICES (new farming methods, industrialization)

             = INDUSTRIAL PRACTICES (how they handle parasites)
             + CONSEQUENCES (toxic substances, health warnings)

             = CONSEQUENCES (systemic pollution)

The dominant theme across ALL paragraphs: How the salmon farming industry OPERATES — its scale, methods, problems, and consequences. In other words: the practices in effect in salmon farming.

What the article is NOT primarily about:

❌ It's not primarily about cooking salmon
❌ It's not primarily about Norwegian history
❌ It's not primarily about consumer preferences
❌ It's not primarily about fishing regulations

The article is an INVESTIGATION into an INDUSTRY — its practices, its scale, its problems, and its impact.

🔬 HOW TO IDENTIFY THE GLOBAL THEME OF A MULTI-SOURCE TEXT

When a text contains multiple voices, the GLOBAL theme is what the JOURNALIST frames — not what any single quoted source says:

JOURNALIST'S FRAMING (= the article's theme):
  "La Norvège doit s'adapter... quitte à industrialiser toujours plus"
  "l'industrialisation massive de l'élevage pose des problèmes"
  "L'élevage industriel pose deux problèmes"
  → Theme: industrial salmon farming practices and their consequences

INDUSTRY'S POSITION (= one perspective within the article):
  "Nous faisons des efforts pour réduire les traitements chimiques"
  → This is ONE voice, not the article's overall theme

ECOLOGIST'S POSITION (= another perspective within the article):
  "Il faut changer le modèle industriel"
  → This is ONE voice, not the article's overall theme

The article's theme = the JOURNALIST's organizing framework, which encompasses all the voices.

  2. Build offshore farms for millions of salmon
  3. Use chemical treatments (against sea lice)

The broader context of salmon farming:

In industrial salmon farming, breeders routinely:

  • Feed salmon with specific diets that include colorants (astaxanthin)
  • This gives the flesh its characteristic pink/orange color
  • Wild salmon gets this color naturally from eating krill/shrimp
  • Farmed salmon would be grey without additives
  • The color makes the product more appealing to consumers

This practice falls under the broader theme of industrial practices that modify the natural product for commercial purposes — which is exactly what the article investigates.

The "methods" in question are the treatments — both chemical and natural — used to fight the sea lice. Their purpose is to ELIMINATE these PARASITES.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #49: The "Voice Confusion" Trap 🗣️

INDUSTRY says: "We're making efforts to reduce chemicals"
  → Defensive, optimistic tone

ECOLOGIST says: "Even bio salmon is polluted, the model must change"
  → Critical, alarming tone

JOURNALIST says: "Industrial farming poses two problems"
  → Neutral, informative tone

If a question asks "What does the article explain?" and you answer with the ecologist's quote alone, you're confusing one voice with the article's overall framework. The article EXPLAINS the industry and its problems — it doesn't just repeat what one critic says.

How to avoid it: For "the article explains" questions, look at what the JOURNALIST describes throughout the text, not what any single quoted source claims.

PITFALL #50: The "Purpose vs. Side Effect" Inversion 🔄

PURPOSE of chemical treatments: to fight sea lice (parasites)
SIDE EFFECT of chemical treatments: toxic residues in salmon

Wrong answer: "Les méthodes servent à contaminer le saumon"
             = Methods serve to contaminate salmon
             → Treats a side effect as an intention

Nobody INTENDS to contaminate salmon. Contamination is an unintended CONSEQUENCE of treatments whose PURPOSE is parasite elimination.

How to avoid it: Ask: "Did anyone INTEND this outcome?" If yes → it's a purpose. If no → it's a side effect. Methods serve their INTENDED purpose, not their unintended consequences.

PITFALL #51: The "Industrial Goal vs. Method Purpose" Confusion 🏭

INDUSTRIAL GOAL: Multiply production by 5 by 2050
METHOD PURPOSE:  Eliminate parasites (sea lice)

These are DIFFERENT things at DIFFERENT levels:
  GOAL = what the industry WANTS to achieve (commercial)
  METHOD PURPOSE = what specific techniques DO (operational)

If a question asks about method purpose, don't answer with the industry's commercial goal. These are different levels of "why."

How to avoid it: Match the LEVEL of the question:

  • "What is the industry's objective?" → commercial goal (multiply production)
  • "What do the methods serve?" → operational purpose (fight parasites)

PITFALL #52: The "Escalation Blindness" Trap 📈

The text presents a PROGRESSION of contamination:

LEVEL 1: Farmed salmon has parasites (specific)
LEVEL 2: Farmed salmon contains toxic substances (broader)
LEVEL 3: Even bio salmon is polluted (broadest)
LEVEL 4: Water itself is polluted (systemic)

A student who reads only Level 1 might miss the article's broader point: the problem isn't just about farmed salmon — it's about systemic water pollution affecting ALL fish.

How to avoid it: Read the ENTIRE text, especially the last paragraph, which often contains the broadest/most alarming statement (Rule #7 from Chapter 4 — last paragraph reveals direction).
