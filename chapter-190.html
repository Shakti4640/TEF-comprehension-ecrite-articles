CHAPTER 9: READING A LONG NARRATIVE — FIRST CONTACT WITH L'ALPINISME

Core Patterns | Phase 2 of 5

📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

L'alpinisme — Un peu d'histoire !

En 1760, Horace-Bénédict de Saussure savant Genevois désireux de faire
des observations scientifiques sur la cime la plus haute des Alpes,
promet une forte récompense au montagnard qui trouvera une route
praticable pour y parvenir. Sur le moment la chose parut sans doute
extravagante, car la première tentative n'eut lieu qu'au bout de 15 ans.
Elle fut arrêtée, comme les suivantes, plutôt par la longueur de la
course, l'étonnement, le dépaysement des hautes neiges que par de
véritables difficultés. Le glacier des Bossons, la Mer de Glace,
l'Arête du Goûter sont successivement explorés, sans succès. Il faut,
pour que le pas décisif soit franchi, l'entrée en scène de Jacques
Balmat. Originaire du village des Pèlerin, déjà père de famille,
Jacques Balmat avait 24 ans en 1786. C'était un grand coureur de
glaciers ; il conduisait des voyageurs, mais n'était pas guide en
titre. Le 30 Juin de cette année-là, descendant d'une course, il
rencontre une caravane qui part conquérir la récompense promise par
Saussure. Il se joint à eux, fait une tentative par l'Arête des
Bosses, est abandonné par ses compagnons et redescend sur le Grand
Plateau. Il raconte ensuite avoir ensuite gravi la pente à droite des
Rochers Rouges, atteint l'épaule du Mont Blanc et vu à ses pieds le
versant italien. Mais pris par le brouillard, il ne peut continuer ce
soir-là. Après un bivouac improvisé, il redescend dans la vallée, sûr
d'avoir trouvé l'accès à la cime. Un mois après, le 7 Août, Balmat
repart avec Michel Gabriel Paccard. C'est le docteur de Chamonix,
neveu du doyen des guides de la vallée, homme instruit, qui a déjà
fait trois tentatives au Mont Blanc. Après une nuit passée au sommet
de la montagne de la Côte, reprenant l'itinéraire des Rochers rouges,
ils arrivent tous deux au sommet à 18h30. Le Mont Blanc était vaincu,
la plus grande « première » des Alpes était accomplie. Saussure
lui-même se fit conduire au Mont Blanc en 1787. Il fit un long séjour
en 1788 au col du Géant, multipliant les observations scientifiques.
Si la science lui avait fait faire la conquête du Mont Blanc, on peut
dire qu'en retour les Alpes l'avaient conquis, comme beaucoup d'entre
nous. C'est seulement en 1808, qu'une servante de Chamonix, Maria
Paradis, fit la première ascension féminine. Citons aussi la tragique
expédition du docteur Hamel en 1820, l'ascension de Mlle d'Angeville
(1838) qui, exténuée, disait à ses guides: «Si je meurs avant
d'avoir atteint la cime, promettez-moi d'y porter mon corps et de
l'ensevelir là-haut.» Mais aucun des amateurs qui suivent les traces
de Saussure, ne laisse une trace durable dans l'histoire alpine. Ils
sont venus là par curiosité, peut-être pour la gloire. La preuve en
est que malgré le nombre de ces ascensions, l'exploration du Mont
Blanc n'avance pas. La première voie, avec toutefois le détour par le
mur de la Côte, est la seule connue jusqu'en 1855. Cette année-là
seulement est faite l'ascension par l'Arête du Goûter, bien que la
voie ait été reconnue dès les premières tentatives. L'Arête des
Bosses ne sera vaincue qu'en 1859. Quant à la connaissance du massif,
il semble qu'elle se borne à la voie du col du Géant. Dans le reste
des Alpes, c'est le silence.

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: VOCABULARY DECODING FOR THE ALPINE TEXT

Before tackling the questions, let us apply Chapter 3's tools to the most challenging vocabulary in this text:

THE CRITICAL VOCABULARY

WORD: "cime"
  TOOL 1 (Cognate): No obvious English cognate
  TOOL 3 (Context): "la cime la plus haute des Alpes"
                     = the highest [something] of the Alps
                     Must mean SUMMIT/PEAK ✅
  DECODED: summit, peak, top of a mountain

WORD: "praticable"
  TOOL 1 (Cognate): English "practicable" = feasible, usable
  TOOL 3 (Context): "une route praticable pour y parvenir"
                     = a [feasible] route to get there
  DECODED: passable, usable, navigable ✅

WORD: "extravagante"
  TOOL 1 (Cognate): English "extravagant" = excessive, outrageous
  TOOL 3 (Context): "la chose parut sans doute extravagante"
                     = the thing seemed certainly [outrageous]
                     → because no one tried for 15 years
  DECODED: crazy, outrageous, absurd ✅

WORD: "dépaysement"
  TOOL 2 (Word Family): "dé-pays-ement"
                         "dé" = removal/separation
                         "pays" = country/land
                         "-ement" = noun suffix
                         = the feeling of being OUT of one's land
  DECODED: disorientation, feeling of strangeness ✅

EXPRESSION: "coureur de glaciers"
  TOOL 2 (Word Family): "coureur" = runner (from courir)
                         "de glaciers" = of glaciers
  TOOL 3 (Context): describes Balmat, who guides travelers 
                     on glaciers → experienced mountaineer
  DECODED: glacier runner = experienced mountain climber ✅

WORD: "bivouac"
  TOOL 1 (Cognate): English "bivouac" = temporary camp
  DECODED: improvised overnight camp ✅

WORD: "versant"
  TOOL 2 (Word Family): from "verser" = to pour/tilt
                         "versant" = the side that tilts
  TOOL 3 (Context): "vu à ses pieds le versant italien"
                     = saw at his feet the Italian [side]
  DECODED: mountainside, slope (the Italian side) ✅

WORD: "vaincu"
  TOOL 1 (Cognate): from "vaincre" = to vanquish/defeat
                     English "vanquish"
  TOOL 3 (Context): "Le Mont Blanc était vaincu"
                     = Mont Blanc was [conquered/defeated]
  DECODED: conquered, vanquished, defeated ✅

EXPRESSION: "première" (in quotes)
  TOOL 3 (Context): "la plus grande «première» des Alpes"
                     In mountaineering, a "première" = first ascent
  DECODED: first ascent / first successful climb ✅

WORD: "exténuée"
  TOOL 1 (Cognate): close to "extenuated" but means EXHAUSTED
  TOOL 3 (Context): Mlle d'Angeville, climbing, saying 
                     "if I die before reaching the summit"
                     → she was extremely tired
  DECODED: exhausted, spent, drained ✅

WORD: "ensevelir"
  TOOL 2 (Word Family): "en-sevel-ir" → to bury
  TOOL 3 (Context): "porter mon corps et de l'ensevelir là-haut"
                     = carry my body and [bury] it up there
  DECODED: to bury, to entomb ✅

EXPRESSION: "se borne à"
  TOOL 3 (Context): "la connaissance du massif se borne à 
                      la voie du col du Géant"
                     = knowledge of the massif [limits itself to]
                     one route
  DECODED: is limited to, is restricted to ✅

📌 Each text type requires ADAPTED reading strategies. The foundation skills (Chapters 1-5) remain the same, but the MAPPING TOOLS change based on the text's structure. This is why Phase 1 taught you PRINCIPLES, not rigid procedures.

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: DATES ARE YOUR PARAGRAPH BREAKS

THE DATE-AS-PARAGRAPH TECHNIQUE:

  In narrative texts without paragraph breaks,
  treat each DATE as the start of a new "virtual paragraph."
  
  "Virtual Paragraph 1": 1760 — Saussure offers reward
  "Virtual Paragraph 2": ~1775 — First failed attempts  
  "Virtual Paragraph 3": 1786 — Balmat finds the route
  "Virtual Paragraph 4": 7 Aug 1786 — Summit reached
  "Virtual Paragraph 5": 1787-1788 — Saussure's own visits
  "Virtual Paragraph 6": 1808-1838 — Followers era
  "Virtual Paragraph 7": 1855-1859 — Route stagnation
  
  NOW you have 7 "paragraphs" to navigate.
  Your Chapter 1 skills work again.

RULE 2: IDENTIFY THE NARRATIVE ARC BEFORE ANSWERING

Every historical narrative has an ARC — a shape to the story:

COMMON NARRATIVE ARCS:

  ARC 1: RISE (things get better)
  challenge → effort → success → triumph
  
  ARC 2: FALL (things get worse)
  prosperity → problems → decline → disaster
  
  ARC 3: RISE-THEN-FALL (triumph then decline)
  challenge → success → complacency → stagnation ← THIS TEXT
  
  ARC 4: FALL-THEN-RISE (disaster then recovery)
  disaster → struggle → adaptation → success

L'ALPINISME follows ARC 3:
  1760: Challenge (mountain unconquered)
  1786: Success (Balmat & Paccard summit)
  1808-1838: Followers (but no real progress)
  1855-1859: Stagnation (same routes, silence elsewhere)
  
  THE TEXT'S ARGUMENT:
  "The conquest was glorious, but what followed was 
   disappointing — people came for curiosity and glory,
   not for true exploration."

Identifying the arc helps you understand the text's MAIN IDEA and TONE — critical for title questions (Q20) and true/false questions (Q21).

RULE 3: TRACK THE AUTHOR'S JUDGMENTS

Unlike purely informative texts, narratives often contain the author's opinions embedded in the storytelling. Find them:

AUTHOR'S JUDGMENTS IN L'ALPINISME:

  POSITIVE JUDGMENTS (about pioneers):
  → "le pas décisif" (the decisive step) — about Balmat
  → "Le Mont Blanc était vaincu" (was conquered) — triumphant tone
  → "la plus grande «première» des Alpes" — superlative praise
  → "les Alpes l'avaient conquis" — poetic admiration of Saussure
  
  NEGATIVE JUDGMENTS (about followers):
  → "AUCUN des amateurs... ne laisse une trace DURABLE"
    (NONE of the amateurs leaves a LASTING mark)
  → "Ils sont venus là par CURIOSITÉ, peut-être pour la GLOIRE"
    (They came for curiosity, maybe for glory)
    → NOT for science, NOT for exploration
  → "MALGRÉ le nombre de ces ascensions, l'exploration 
     n'AVANCE PAS" (DESPITE the number, exploration 
     DOESN'T PROGRESS)
  → "Dans le reste des Alpes, c'est le SILENCE"
    (In the rest of the Alps, SILENCE)
    → Devastating final judgment
    
  THE AUTHOR'S THESIS:
  Pioneers (Saussure, Balmat) = heroes driven by science/passion
  Followers = tourists driven by curiosity/glory
  Result: real exploration stagnated

RULE 4: NOTE WHAT THE TEXT DOES NOT SAY

For the true/false question (Q21), you need to distinguish what the text SAYS from what it DOESN'T SAY. Build a "NOT MENTIONED" list:

THINGS THE TEXT DOES NOT MENTION:

  ❌ Tourism (never mentioned)
  ❌ Mulets/mules (never mentioned)
  ❌ Terror/fear of mountains (early attempts failed due to 
     "longueur" and "dépaysement," not terror)
  ❌ Illustrious men DYING in the Alps (Hamel's expedition 
     is "tragique" but the text doesn't say HE died, 
     and it doesn't say "de tout temps" — always)
  ❌ Mont Cervin (never mentioned in this excerpt)
  ❌ Ropes/cordées (never mentioned)
  ❌ 1932 (never mentioned)
  ❌ "Montagnards de naissance" (native mountaineers — 
     Balmat was local but Paccard was a doctor, 
     Saussure was from Geneva)

RULE 5: THE PROPER NOUN SCAN TECHNIQUE

When a text has 15+ proper nouns, you need a quick-scan technique to avoid confusion:

THE PROPER NOUN QUICK-SCAN:

  On your first read, UNDERLINE every proper noun 
  and immediately classify it:
  
  PERSON ── Saussure, Balmat, Paccard, Paradis, 
             Hamel, d'Angeville
  PLACE ─── Mont Blanc, Chamonix, Alpes, Genève,
             Col du Géant
  ROUTE ─── Glacier des Bossons, Mer de Glace,
             Arête du Goûter, Arête des Bosses,
             Rochers Rouges, Grand Plateau
  
  This prevents you from confusing a PERSON with a PLACE 
  or a ROUTE with a PERSON during the exam.
  
  EXAMPLE CONFUSION TO AVOID:
  "Arête du Goûter" — Is this a person or a place?
  → It's a ROUTE (arête = ridge)
  
  "la Côte" — Is this a place?
  → It's a MOUNTAIN ("montagne de la Côte")

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: READING A LONG NARRATIVE THE SAME WAY AS A SHORT ARTICLE

WRONG:
  Read the 500-word block straight through, 
  try to remember everything, then answer questions.
  
  RESULT: Information overload. You remember the beginning 
  and end but forget the middle (where most details are).
  
RIGHT:
  First pass: Scan for DATES and PROPER NOUNS only (1 min)
  → Build chronological skeleton
  
  Second pass: Read for EVENTS and JUDGMENTS (2-3 min)
  → Fill in what happened at each date
  
  Third pass: Targeted re-reading for specific questions (1-2 min per Q)
  → Go directly to the relevant "virtual paragraph"
  
  THREE PASSES, each with a different focus.
  Total time: ~8 minutes for a 500-word narrative + 3 questions.

MISTAKE 2: TRYING TO REMEMBER EVERY DETAIL

WRONG:
  "Balmat was from Pèlerin, he was 24, he was a father,
   he wasn't an official guide, he joined on June 30,
   he went via Arête des Bosses, then Rochers Rouges,
   he reached the shoulder, saw Italy, fog stopped him,
   he bivouacked, came back, then on August 7..."
   
  You're trying to memorize a STORY, not understand a TEXT.
  
RIGHT:
  "Balmat = the hero who found the route and made 
   the first ascent with Paccard in 1786."
   
  That's ALL you need for the big picture.
  Specific details (which route, which date, what weather)
  can be LOOKED UP in the text when a question asks for them.
  
  REMEMBER THE STORY. LOOK UP THE DETAILS.

MISTAKE 3: IGNORING THE TEXT'S FINAL SECTION

WRONG:
  The dramatic story of Balmat is so compelling that 
  students focus entirely on the conquest and ignore 
  everything after 1788.
  
  But the text's ARGUMENT lives in the FINAL section:
  → "aucun ne laisse une trace durable"
  → "par curiosité, peut-être pour la gloire"
  → "l'exploration n'avance pas"
  → "c'est le silence"
  
  This is where the author reveals his THESIS:
  exploration stagnated after the initial triumph.
  
  THE LAST PARAGRAPH RULE (from Chapter 4) applies here too.
  Even in narratives, the ending carries the author's 
  most important message.

MISTAKE 4: CONFUSING PEOPLE AND THEIR ACTIONS

DANGER IN THIS TEXT:

  Balmat and Paccard both summited Mont Blanc.
  But the text gives them DIFFERENT roles:
  
  BALMAT: Found the route, explored alone, took the initiative
  PACCARD: Experienced climber, educated, joined Balmat
  
  If a question asks "Who found the route?" → BALMAT
  If a question asks "Who was the doctor?" → PACCARD
  If a question asks "Who first reached the summit?" → BOTH
  
  SIMILARLY:
  Maria Paradis: first FEMALE ascent (1808)
  Mlle d'Angeville: dramatic ascent with famous quote (1838)
  
  Students mix these two women up.
  Paradis = first woman, servant
  Angeville = dramatic quote, "if I die..."
  
  The CHARACTER MAP prevents these confusions.

MISTAKE 5: ASSUMING CHRONOLOGICAL = SIMPLE

WRONG ASSUMPTION:
  "The text goes in order from 1760 to 1859,
   so it's just a timeline — easy."
   
REALITY:
  The text SOMETIMES breaks chronological order:
  
  → "Le glacier des Bossons, la Mer de Glace, 
     l'Arête du Goûter sont successivement explorés"
  → These happened over 10+ years, compressed into one sentence
  
  → Balmat's June 30 exploration is told in DETAIL
  → Then "Un mois après" jumps to August 7
  
  → After the 1786 summit, text goes to 1787, 1788 (Saussure)
  → Then JUMPS to 1808 (Paradis) — 20-year gap
  
  → Then 1820 (Hamel), 1838 (Angeville) — compressed
  → Then the author COMMENTS on all followers as a group
  → Then returns to ROUTES: 1855, 1859
  
  The chronology is not perfectly linear.
  It COMPRESSES some periods and EXPANDS others.
  Balmat's story gets the most space (= most important).

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "WALL OF TEXT" PANIC

SYMPTOM: You see the massive unbroken paragraph 
         and your brain shuts down.
         "This is too long. I can't process this."
         
CURE: The Date-Anchoring technique.
  → Scan for dates FIRST (10 seconds)
  → You find 10 dates
  → Suddenly: 10 "virtual paragraphs" instead of 1 wall
  → Each virtual paragraph is 2-3 sentences
  → NOW it's manageable
  
  THE WALL IS AN ILLUSION.
  Dates break it into pieces.

PITFALL 2: THE PROPER NOUN OVERLOAD

SYMPTOM: You encounter Saussure, Balmat, Paccard, 
         Paradis, Hamel, d'Angeville, plus 10 place names,
         and you can't keep track of who is who.
         
CURE: The Character Map + Place Map.
  → On first read, just note: PERSON or PLACE?
  → Don't try to remember details yet
  → When a question mentions a specific person,
    go back to your map and look them up
    
  REMEMBER: You're not memorizing.
  You're MAPPING for later NAVIGATION.

PITFALL 3: THE "TRAGIQUE" MISINTERPRETATION

DANGER: The text says "la tragique expédition du docteur Hamel"
        
  Student thinks: "Tragic = he died"
  
  ACTUALLY: The text says the EXPEDITION was tragic.
  It doesn't say Hamel DIED.
  "Tragique" could mean: people died, there was an accident,
  someone was injured, they had to abandon.
  
  We DON'T KNOW exactly what happened from this text alone.
  
  The text says "tragique expédition" — that's ALL.
  Don't ADD information the text doesn't provide.
  
  (In real history, three guides died in an avalanche 
   on Hamel's expedition — but the TEXT doesn't say this.)
  
  RULE: "Tragique" ≠ "everyone died"
        It means something very sad happened.
        Only state what the text EXPLICITLY says.

PITFALL 4: THE TEMPORAL WORD CONFUSION

NARRATIVE TEXTS USE MANY TEMPORAL EXPRESSIONS:

  "au bout de 15 ans"         = after 15 years
  "de cette année-là"         = of that year
  "un mois après"             = one month later
  "d'ici là"                  = by then
  "cette année-là seulement"  = only that year
  "dès les premières tentatives" = from the first attempts
  "seulement en 1808"         = only in 1808
  "qu'en 1859"                = not until 1859
  
  DANGER: Misreading these expressions shifts 
  events to the WRONG date.
  
  "La première tentative n'eut lieu qu'au bout de 15 ans"
  = The first attempt didn't happen until 15 years later
  → 1760 + 15 = 1775 (approximately)
  
  "ne...que" = only / not until
  This is RESTRICTIVE, not negative.
  "n'eut lieu QU'au bout de 15 ans" 
  ≠ "didn't take place in 15 years"
  = "didn't take place UNTIL 15 years later"
  
  HUGE difference in meaning from one small word.

Chapter 10 tackles Question 20: "Quel titre convient le mieux à cet article?" — the BEST TITLE question.

This is different from the PURPOSE question (Chapter 2). A title question doesn't ask WHY the text was written — it asks what label best captures the text's ENTIRE content and spirit.

Title questions are uniquely challenging because:

WHY TITLE QUESTIONS ARE HARD:

  → You must evaluate the title against the WHOLE text
    (not just one paragraph)
  → The title must be neither too broad nor too narrow
  → The title must match the text's TONE (not just its topic)
  → Wrong titles often capture ONE part of the text perfectly
    but miss the rest
  → For narrative texts, the title must capture the ARC,
    not just one event

Chapter 10 will use your narrative map from Chapter 9 to systematically evaluate all four title options and introduce the Coverage Test — a technique for measuring how well a title captures the FULL scope of a text.
