🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's different from all previous projects?

Every text so far has been written by ONE voice — an author presenting information, reviewing a film, or exposing practices. Even the formal letter in Project 4 had one sender.

This text has TWO voices — an interviewer asking questions and a respondent giving answers. This creates a completely new reading dynamic:

Single-Voice Text (Projects 1-8)Interview Text (Project 9)
One perspectiveTwo perspectives (questioner + respondent)
Author = narratorSpeaker = advocate for a position
Information presented as factInformation presented as OPINION of the speaker
Neutral or investigative toneBIASED tone (speaker has a stake)
"What does the text say?""What does the SPEAKER believe/claim?"

Why interviews are tricky

The speaker in an interview is NOT a neutral information source. They have a POSITION they're defending, a PRODUCT they're promoting, or a BELIEF they're advocating. Everything they say must be understood through this lens.

In this text, the speaker is clearly a SUPPORTER of the electronic book. Every answer they give is designed to:

  1. PRAISE the e-book's qualities
  2. DISMISS concerns about it replacing traditional books
  3. REASSURE those who feel threatened by it

Understanding this SPEAKER STANCE is the key to answering all four questions.

Step 1: Identify the text type

Quick scan reveals:

  • Question-answer format (dashes before each line)
  • Technical product being discussed
  • Three distinct questions asked
  • Speaker gives detailed, positive responses

Verdict: PRODUCT-FOCUSED INTERVIEW (entretien / interview)

Interview FeatureWhat It Means for Questions
Question-answer structureEach Q&A block addresses a DIFFERENT concern
Speaker has a positionQuestions will ask WHAT position and WHO the speaker is
Questions reveal FEARSThe interviewer voices public concerns; the speaker addresses them
Answers are strategicThe speaker chooses what to emphasize and what to downplay

Step 2: Read questions first — spot the SPEAKER ANALYSIS requirement

Notice Q34 — it doesn't ask what the speaker SAYS. It asks what the speaker's answers are "de nature à" (likely to / capable of) doing. This requires you to EVALUATE the impact of the speaker's words on different audiences.

Step 3: The "Speaker Stance Mapping" technique (NEW)

Before answering, identify the speaker's OVERALL POSITION and their STRATEGY:

SPEAKER STANCE MAP:

WHO IS SPEAKING?
→ Someone knowledgeable about the e-book
→ Someone who views it POSITIVELY ("extraordinaire," "idéal")
→ NOT a neutral observer — a SUPPORTER/ADVOCATE

WHAT IS THEIR STRATEGY?
→ Q1 (characteristics): PRAISE the product's qualities
→ Q2 (replace paper books?): DENY replacement, emphasize COMPLEMENT
→ Q3 (end of publishers?): DENY threat, REASSURE traditional actors

OVERALL STANCE: 
"The e-book is great AND it won't destroy what already exists"
= POSITIVE + REASSURING

This map will guide every answer.

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #31: In interview texts, identify the speaker's STANCE before answering any question. Ask: "Is this person FOR or AGAINST the subject? What are they trying to ACHIEVE with their answers?" The speaker's stance is the lens through which ALL their statements must be interpreted.

THE MASTER LESSON FROM Q34: The "Reassure vs. Threaten" Pattern

This question introduces a critical exam pattern that will reappear in many future projects:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #32: When a question asks about the EFFECT of a speaker's words, identify the speaker's COMMUNICATION STRATEGY. Is the speaker trying to REASSURE (calm fears, promise continuity) or THREATEN (warn of danger, predict disruption)? The correct answer will match the strategy, and traps will offer the opposite.

The Reassure vs. Threaten framework:

REASSURANCE SIGNALS:
→ "Non" (direct denial of fears)
→ "Complément" / "en plus de" (addition, not replacement)
→ "Toute leur place" / "ne changera rien" (continuity promised)
→ "Davantage" / "en plus" (more of the same, not less)

THREAT SIGNALS:
→ "Oui" (confirmation of fears)
→ "Remplacement" / "au lieu de" (substitution)
→ "N'auront plus" / "disparaîtront" (loss predicted)
→ "Moins" / "de moins en moins" (decline)

This speaker uses EXCLUSIVELY reassurance signals. Therefore, the effect is reassurance.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 26: The Fact-to-Wish Conversion (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 11, 16.

What it is: A neutral factual description is transformed into a desire for change or improvement.

How to detect: Ask: "Does the speaker express SATISFACTION or DISSATISFACTION with this feature?" If neutral or positive → not a wish. If negative → could be a wish.

In this text: "900 grams" is neutral. "Lisibilité extraordinaire" is positive. Neither is a complaint.

Defense: Check for evaluative language. "Extraordinaire" = praise, not complaint. Neutral specifications are descriptions, not wishes.

PITFALL 27: The Strategy Inversion (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 14, 17, 23.

What it is: The speaker's communication strategy (reassure/threaten) is INVERTED in an answer option.

How to detect: Map the speaker's strategy FIRST (Rule #31). Then check: does the answer match the strategy or invert it?

In this text: Speaker's strategy = REASSURE. Option D (Q34) says "inquiéter" (worry) = INVERSION of the strategy.

Defense: Always identify the strategy before evaluating impact options.

PITFALL 28: The Comparison-to-Prediction Leap (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 11, 20.

What it is: A current COMPARISON between two things is extrapolated into a future PREDICTION about one of them.

How to detect: Ask: "Is the text comparing NOW or predicting LATER?"

In this text: "Without paying laptop prices" = current comparison. Option A (Q35) extrapolates to "will cost as much as a laptop" = future prediction.

Defense: Check verb tenses. Present comparison ≠ future prediction.
