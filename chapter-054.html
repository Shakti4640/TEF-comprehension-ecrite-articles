Chapter 2: LES TROUBLES DU SOMMEIL — READING A MEDICAL INFORMATION TEXT

💤 Texte 51 | Phase 1: Very Easy | Foundation Thinking

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Le somnambulisme est un trouble très fréquent chez les enfants. Il se représente comme un passage difficile du sommeil profond à l'éveil. C'est pourquoi, le matin, le somnambule a tout oublié. Si on le réveille, il ne risque pas sa vie mais sera très mal à l'aise et désorienté.

Il faut donc éviter de le réveiller, mais plutôt l'accompagner doucement jusqu'à son lit pour qu'il finisse sa nuit. Si ce trouble est sans danger, il faut cependant faire en sorte de protéger de tout risque de chute ou de choc la personne, qui souffre de ce dysfonctionnement.

La somniloquie est un autre trouble du sommeil. On parle parfois au moment de s'endormir, dans un demi-sommeil et il arrive même qu'on réponde à des questions simples. Parfois aussi, on marmonne lorsque l'on rêve mais à ce stade, ce que l'on raconte est bien souvent incompréhensible.

Enfin, de nombreux enfants ronflent la nuit : leur sommeil est agité, ils transpirent à grosses gouttes et se réveillent fréquemment, ce qui a pour conséquence une fatigue anormale durant la journée. La responsabilité en revient le plus souvent à des obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air, par exemple les amygdales qu'on peut alors faire enlever.

  • D — Le manque d'aération de la pièce

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's different from Project 1?

In Project 1 (Rapid'Rail), each answer could be verified by pointing to ONE exact sentence. The text said "succès grandissant" and the answer said "constamment augmenté" — a clean, direct paraphrase.

This text introduces a new challenge: the answers are CLOSE to each other. Multiple options seem partially right. The differences are subtle — sometimes a single word separates correct from wrong.

Step 1: Identify the text type

A quick glance reveals:

  • A scientific/medical topic (sleep disorders)
  • Structured in clear sections (one disorder per paragraph)
  • Factual, neutral tone — no opinion, no sales pitch
  • Technical vocabulary (somnambulisme, somniloquie)

Verdict: INFORMATIONAL/MEDICAL TEXT (texte informatif)

Informational Text FeatureWhat It Means for Questions
Organized by subtopic (one per section)Each question targets ONE specific section
Uses precise vocabularyTraps will use similar-sounding words
States facts, not opinionsAnswers must match stated facts exactly
Contains cause-effect relationshipsQuestions will test if you understand WHY

Step 2: Read questions first — notice the STRUCTURE

Look at the three questions:

  • Q14 → about somnambulisme → targets Paragraphs 1-2
  • Q15 → about somniloquie → targets Paragraph 3
  • Q16 → about ronfleurs → targets Paragraph 4

This is a ONE-QUESTION-PER-SECTION structure. Each question maps perfectly to one part of the text. This means:

If you're answering Q14, ONLY Paragraphs 1-2 matter. Ignore everything else.

This is Golden Rule #3 from Project 1 in action — but now it's even more important because the text has parallel sections that could confuse you.

Step 3: Read with the "medical text mindset"

Medical/informational texts reward precision reading. Every word matters. Unlike a promotional text where the overall message is obvious, here the difference between right and wrong hides in one specific phrase.

Your mental approach should be:

"What EXACTLY does the text say about this disorder? Not approximately. EXACTLY."

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT — Question by Question

QUESTION 14: Le somnambule est à surveiller car les crises peuvent...

First: locate the answer zone.
Q14 is about somnambulisme → Paragraphs 1-2 ONLY.

Second: find every relevant statement in those paragraphs.

Let me extract ALL the facts the text gives about somnambulism:

Fact #What the text says (exact quote)What it means
F1"trouble très fréquent chez les enfants"Common in children
F2"passage difficile du sommeil profond à l'éveil"Transition problem between deep sleep and waking
F3"le matin, le somnambule a tout oublié"No memory the next morning
F4"il ne risque pas sa vie"NOT life-threatening
F5"sera très mal à l'aise et désorienté"Uncomfortable and confused if woken
F6"ce trouble est sans danger"The disorder itself is harmless
F7"protéger de tout risque de chute ou de choc"Must protect from falls and impacts

Now, with ALL facts mapped, let's test each answer:

Option A: "Mettre sa vie en jeu" (Put his life at risk)

Check against facts:

  • F4 says: "il ne risque pas sa vie" → The text EXPLICITLY says the opposite
  • F6 says: "ce trouble est sans danger" → Confirmed: not dangerous

This is a DIRECT CONTRADICTION trap. The text uses the exact words "sa vie" and "risque" — but in a NEGATIVE construction ("ne risque PAS sa vie"). If you read too fast and catch "risque" + "sa vie" without the "ne...pas," you fall into the trap.

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct negation in the text

Option B: "Causer des accidents" (Cause accidents)

Check against facts:

  • F7 says: "protéger de tout risque de chute ou de choc"
  • Chute = fall. Choc = impact/collision.
  • Falls and impacts ARE accidents.

But notice: the text never uses the word "accident." It says "chute" and "choc." The answer REPHRASES these as "accidents."

Text saysAnswer saysRelationship
"risque de chute ou de choc""causer des accidents"PARAPHRASE ✅

This is Golden Rule #2 from Project 1: correct answers rephrase.

✅ STRONG CANDIDATE

Option C: "Provoquer de l'amnistie" (Provoke amnesty)

This is the most important trap in this entire project. Let me show you why.

The text says: "le somnambule a tout oublié" (the sleepwalker has forgotten everything)

Forgetting everything = amnésie (amnesia)

The answer says: amnistie (amnesty)

WordMeaningDomain
AmnésieLoss of memoryMedical
AmnistieLegal pardon / forgiveness of crimesLegal/Political

These words:

  • Sound almost identical when spoken
  • Look almost identical when written
  • Have COMPLETELY different meanings

This is a PHONETIC TRAP — a new trap type that Project 1 didn't introduce.

Even if we replaced "amnistie" with "amnésie," the answer would STILL be problematic because:

  • The question asks why the sleepwalker must be watched/monitored ("à surveiller")
  • Forgetting is a SYMPTOM, not a DANGER
  • The text asks us to protect against physical risks (falls, impacts), not memory loss

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong word (phonetic trap) AND wrong logic

Option D: "Perturber durablement le sommeil" (Permanently disturb sleep)

Check against facts:

  • F6 says: "ce trouble est sans danger" → harmless
  • The text says to "accompagner doucement jusqu'à son lit pour qu'il finisse sa nuit" → he can finish his night normally
  • Nothing in the text suggests LASTING ("durablement") disruption

The key word here is "durablement" (lastingly/permanently). The text describes a temporary episode, not a chronic disruption. This word ADDS information not in the text.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Durablement" is an addition not supported by the text

🎯 Answer: B — Causer des accidents

THE NEW LESSON FROM Q14:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #4: Watch for PHONETIC TRAPS — words that SOUND alike but mean completely different things. When you see a technical or semi-technical word in an answer, verify its MEANING, not just its sound.

Common phonetic trap pairs in TCF/TEF exams:

Trap WordReal WordDifference

Émigrer (leave country)Immigrer (enter country)Opposite directions
Infecté (infected)Infesté (infested)Disease vs. Pests

QUESTION 15: Pendant son sommeil, une personne atteinte de somniloquie...

Answer zone: Paragraph 3 ONLY.

Let me extract the facts:

Fact #What the text saysWhat it means
F1"On parle parfois au moment de s'endormir"Sometimes talks when falling asleep
F2"dans un demi-sommeil"In a half-sleep state
F3"il arrive même qu'on réponde à des questions simples"Can answer simple questions
F4"on marmonne lorsque l'on rêve"Mumbles while dreaming
F5"ce que l'on raconte est bien souvent incompréhensible"What is said is mostly incomprehensible

Now let's test:

Option A: "A une conversation soutenue" (Has a sustained conversation)

  • F3 says one can answer "des questions simples" → simple questions, not complex dialogue
  • F4 says "on marmonne" → mumbling, not conversing
  • "Conversation soutenue" = elaborate, articulate conversation

The text describes fragments (mumbling, simple responses). Option A describes fluent exchange. This is an exaggeration trap — it takes a real element (responding to questions) and inflates it beyond what the text says.

❌ ELIMINATED — Exaggeration of a partial truth

Option B: "Raconte ses rêves tout hauts" (Tells dreams out loud)

  • F4 says: "on marmonne lorsque l'on rêve" → mumbles while dreaming
  • F5 says: "ce que l'on raconte est incompréhensible"

The person mumbles DURING dreams, but what they say is incomprehensible. They are NOT narrating their dreams clearly. Option B implies a coherent retelling of dreams. The text says the opposite.

This is a logical leap trap: the text says "talks while dreaming" and the answer transforms it into "tells their dreams" — a subtle but critical difference.

What the text saysWhat Option B claimsThe difference
Mumbles during dreamsNarrates dreams out loudIncomprehensible noise vs. coherent storytelling

❌ ELIMINATED — Logical leap from "talks during dreams" to "tells dreams"

Option C: "Parle de façon confuse" (Speaks in a confused manner)

  • F4: "on marmonne" → mumbling = confused speech ✓
  • F5: "incompréhensible" → incomprehensible = confused ✓
Text saysAnswer saysRelationship
"marmonne" + "incompréhensible""parle de façon confuse"PARAPHRASE ✅

Two text elements are synthesized into one answer. "Marmonne" (mumbles) + "incompréhensible" (incomprehensible) = "parle de façon confuse" (speaks confusedly).

✅ CORRECT — Double paraphrase synthesis

Option D: "Chante à voix basse" (Sings quietly)

  • The text mentions "parle," "marmonne," "raconte" — all forms of SPEAKING
  • Nowhere does it mention singing
  • This is a pure invention — information that simply doesn't exist in the text

❌ ELIMINATED — Fabricated information

🎯 Answer: C — Parle de façon confuse

THE NEW LESSON FROM Q15:

This question introduces a crucial new skill: SYNTHESIS PARAPHRASING.

In Project 1, paraphrasing was simple: one text phrase → one answer phrase.

Here, TWO text phrases combine into ONE answer:

"marmonne"          ─┐
                      ├──→  "parle de façon confuse"
"incompréhensible"  ─┘

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #5: The correct answer can SYNTHESIZE multiple pieces of information from the text into a single reformulation. Don't expect a one-to-one match — sometimes two or three text elements merge into one answer.

QUESTION 16: La fatigue des ronfleurs s'explique par...

Answer zone: Paragraph 4 ONLY.

Let me extract the facts with a cause-effect chain — because this question asks "s'explique par" (is explained by), which means it's testing your understanding of CAUSALITY:

CAUSE CHAIN IN THE TEXT:

"obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air"
(obstacles blocking air circulation)
         ↓
"par exemple les amygdales"
(e.g., enlarged tonsils)
         ↓
sommeil agité, transpiration, réveils fréquents
(restless sleep, sweating, frequent waking)
         ↓
"fatigue anormale durant la journée"
(abnormal daytime fatigue)

The question asks: Why are snorers tired?

The DIRECT cause = frequent waking
The ROOT cause = obstacles blocking air circulation (breathing difficulty)

Now let's test:

Option A: "Des nuits trop longues" (Nights that are too long)

The text says nothing about the LENGTH of nights. It discusses the QUALITY of sleep (agitated, interrupted). Long nights would typically mean MORE rest, not less.

❌ ELIMINATED — Not in the text, logically contradictory

Option B: "Leur difficulté à respirer" (Their difficulty breathing)

The text says: "des obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air"

Text saysAnswer saysRelationship
"obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air""difficulté à respirer"PARAPHRASE ✅

"Circulation de l'air" being blocked = difficulty breathing. The air in question is the air in the respiratory tract (nose, throat), not the air in a room.

✅ CORRECT — Root cause paraphrase

Option C: "Des accès fréquents de fièvre" (Frequent bouts of fever)

The text says: "ils transpirent à grosses gouttes" (they sweat profusely)

Sweating CAN be associated with fever — but the text never mentions fever. Sweating here is a SYMPTOM of agitated sleep, not of illness. This trap exploits the association between sweating and fever that exists in everyday thinking but NOT in this text.

This is a new trap type:

COMMON ASSOCIATION TRAP: The answer exploits a real-world association (sweating → fever) that the text does NOT make. Just because two things are related in general knowledge doesn't mean the text connects them.

❌ ELIMINATED — Association trap (sweating ≠ fever in this context)

Option D: "Le manque d'aération de la pièce" (Lack of room ventilation)

The text says: "obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air"

Here is a brilliant trap. The text mentions "circulation de l'air" (air circulation). Option D talks about "aération de la pièce" (room ventilation). Both involve AIR.

BUT:

  • The text's "circulation de l'air" refers to air flowing through the respiratory tract (nose, throat) — blocked by tonsils
  • Option D's "aération de la pièce" refers to air flowing through a room

Same word ("air"), completely different context. This is Golden Rule #1 from Project 1 (vocabulary recycling) combined with a context shift — the word "air" is moved from BODY to ROOM.

Text meansOption D claimsThe shift
Air circulation inside the body (breathing)Air circulation inside a room (ventilation)BODY → ROOM

❌ ELIMINATED — Context shift trap (internal air → external air)

🎯 Answer: B — Leur difficulté à respirer

THE NEW LESSON FROM Q16:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #6: When the text uses a word with multiple possible meanings, the CONTEXT determines which meaning applies. The exam will offer an answer that uses the WRONG context for a word that appears in the text.

This also introduces:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #7: Don't import outside knowledge. The answer must come from the TEXT, not from what you know about the real world. (Sweating → fever is real-world logic, not text logic.)

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

The "Cause-Effect Chain" Technique (NEW — introduced here)

For medical and scientific texts, the most powerful approach is to map the cause-effect chain before looking at answers:

Step 1: Find the PHENOMENON the question asks about
        → "fatigue des ronfleurs" (snorers' fatigue)

Step 2: Trace BACKWARD in the text to find the cause
        → frequent waking → agitated sleep → blocked air → tonsils

Step 3: Identify the ROOT CAUSE
        → blocked air circulation (breathing difficulty)

Step 4: Match root cause to answer choices
        → "difficulté à respirer" = root cause paraphrase ✅

This technique will be essential in Projects 8, 13, and 18 where cause-effect chains become longer and more complex.

For Medical/Scientific Texts Specifically:

1. Map the structure before answering.
This text has 3 sections (somnambulisme / somniloquie / ronflement). Draw mental borders. Each question lives inside ONE border.

2. Distinguish SYMPTOMS from CAUSES.
The text describes both. Questions may ask about either. Don't confuse them.

Element in TextTypeExample

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 5: The Phonetic Twin (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 14, 23, 24.

What it is: Two words that sound nearly identical but have unrelated meanings.

How to detect it: When you see a "technical" word in an answer choice, pause and ask: "Do I actually know what this word means, or does it just SOUND like a word I know?"

In this text: Amnistie ≠ Amnésie

Defense strategy: If unsure about a word, ELIMINATE based on other evidence. Even if "amnésie" were correct, the question asks about DANGER (why to watch/monitor), not about memory loss.

PITFALL 6: The Common Association Trap (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 8, 13, 21.

What it is: An answer that connects two concepts using real-world logic that the TEXT does not establish.

How to detect it: Ask: "Does the TEXT make this connection, or am I making it myself?"

In this text: Sweating → fever (real-world association, but the text connects sweating to agitated sleep, NOT fever)

PITFALL 7: The Context Shift (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 11, 16, 20.

What it is: A word from the text is reused in an answer but placed in a completely different context, changing its meaning.

How to detect it: When you see a text word in an answer, ask: "Is this word being used in the SAME context as in the text?"

In this text: "Circulation de l'air" — body context (breathing) vs. room context (ventilation)
