🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's New in This Chapter

Chapter 6 introduced inference from behavior in a lifestyle feature. The stakes were low — commuters choosing where to live. Chapter 7 introduces a completely different text type: the hard news report about a catastrophe. The stakes are now life and death, environmental disaster, and political accusations.

News reports have their own DNA, fundamentally different from any text type you've seen:

NEWS REPORT ANATOMY:

1. HEADLINE       → Summarizes the event (often with wordplay)
2. LEAD (lede)    → Who, What, When, Where (core facts in one sentence)
3. DETAILS        → How, consequences, casualties
4. CAUSE          → Why it happened (confirmed or speculated)
5. EXPERT OPINION → What authorities/specialists say
6. OUTLOOK        → What happens next, risks remaining

The New Core Concept: The Fact-Hypothesis Spectrum

In a news report, NOT all information has the same reliability level. Some things are CONFIRMED FACTS. Others are UNCONFIRMED HYPOTHESES. The grammar tells you which is which:

THE FACT-HYPOTHESIS SPECTRUM:

CONFIRMED FACTS (indicative mood):
  "a coulé"           → sank (passé composé = it happened)
  "repose désormais"   → now rests (present = current state)
  "demeurent inconnues" → remain unknown (present = current reality)
  "penchent pour"      → lean toward (present = current position)
  "sont unanimes"      → are unanimous (present = agreed position)

UNCONFIRMED CLAIMS (conditionnel mood):
  "aurait évoqué"      → supposedly mentioned
  "aurait permis"      → would have allowed

HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIOS (conditionnel + si):
  "s'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés" → if they escaped, would be pumped

The conditionnel journalistique is the single most important grammar feature in news comprehension:

CONDITIONNEL JOURNALISTIQUE:

WHAT IT IS:
  A verb in the conditional tense used in journalism
  to report information that is NOT YET CONFIRMED.

HOW TO RECOGNIZE IT:
  Endings: -rait, -raient, -rait
  "aurait + past participle" (conditionnel passé)

WHAT IT MEANS:
  "The source CLAIMS this, but we (the journalist) haven't verified it."
  "This is alleged, not proven."
  "Take this with a grain of salt."

EXAMPLES:
  "Le président a annoncé" = The president announced (FACT)
  "Le président aurait annoncé" = The president supposedly announced (CLAIM)

  "La plateforme a coulé" = The platform sank (FACT)
  "Un député aurait évoqué un sabotage" = A politician supposedly mentioned sabotage (CLAIM)

Why This Matters for the Questions

Questions about news reports test whether you can:

SkillTest
Identify the main eventWhat actually HAPPENED? (not what's speculated)
Distinguish fact from hypothesisIs this confirmed or alleged?
Read expert opinions preciselyWhat do experts say, exactly? (not more, not less)
Avoid confusing related entitiesCompany ≠ platform, workers ≠ suspects

Sentence 1: The Main Event

"Le mardi 20 mars au matin, dans une mer démontée, à 150 km des côtes brésiliennes, la plus grande plateforme pétrolière du monde, la P 36 (120 mètres de haut et 100 de large), exploitée par la compagnie Petropal, a coulé en 10 minutes au fond de l'océan Atlantique."

🏷️ FACT (passé composé: "a coulé")

Let me decompose this information-dense sentence:

WHEN:     Tuesday, March 20, morning
WHERE:    150 km from Brazilian coast, Atlantic Ocean
WHAT:     The P36, world's largest oil platform
SIZE:     120m high, 100m wide
OPERATOR: Petropal company
EVENT:    Sank ("a coulé") in 10 minutes
DEPTH:    (mentioned next sentence: 1360m)
WEATHER:  "mer démontée" = rough/stormy sea

🚨 KEY DETAIL: "a coulé" = SANK. The platform SANK. It didn't explode, didn't get hit by a storm (the storm is context, not cause), didn't get inaugurated.

🚨 KEY DETAIL: "exploitée par la compagnie Petropal" = OPERATED BY Petropal. The platform belongs to Petropal. Petropal is the COMPANY; the P36 is the PLATFORM. These are different entities.

Sentence 2: Current State + Casualties

"Elle repose désormais par 1360 mètres de fond et renferme les corps de huit ouvriers disparus lors de l'explosion de gaz qui a causé la catastrophe dans la nuit du 15 mars."

🏷️ FACT (present tense: "repose," "renferme")

CURRENT STATE:  Rests at 1360m depth
CASUALTIES:     8 workers' bodies inside
                "disparus" = disappeared/lost (euphemism for dead)
CAUSE OF SINKING: Gas explosion
EXPLOSION DATE:   Night of March 15
                  (5 days before sinking on March 20)

🚨 CRITICAL VOCABULARY: "renferme les corps de huit ouvriers disparus"

"Disparus" literally means "disappeared" but in this context — their bodies are INSIDE the sunken platform — it's a euphemism for DEAD. They didn't "disappear" (escape); they died and their bodies are trapped inside.

🚨 TIMELINE:

March 15 (night): Gas explosion → 8 workers killed
March 20 (morning): Platform sinks to ocean floor

The explosion and the sinking are SEPARATE events, 5 days apart.

Sentence 3: Unknown Cause

"Les causes de l'explosion demeurent inconnues."

🏷️ FACT (present: "demeurent")

The causes are STILL UNKNOWN. This is an important confirmed fact — nobody knows why the explosion happened.

Sentence 4: Political Speculation

"Un député brésilien aurait évoqué un sabotage : cette plateforme aurait permis au Brésil de produire le pétrole dont il avait besoin et, ainsi, d'éviter les importations."

🏷️ CLAIM (conditionnel: "aurait évoqué," "aurait permis")

WHO CLAIMS:     A Brazilian politician (député)
CLAIM:          Sabotage
REASONING:      The platform would have allowed Brazil
                to produce its own oil and avoid imports
RELIABILITY:    UNCONFIRMED — both verbs are conditional

"aurait évoqué" = supposedly mentioned
"aurait permis" = would have allowed

🚨 DOUBLE CONDITIONAL: Both the claim AND its justification are in the conditional. The journalist is doubly distancing themselves from this information.

🚨 KEY INSIGHT: The platform "aurait permis" (would have allowed) Brazil to produce its own oil. Even this supposed benefit is in the conditional — suggesting the platform HADN'T YET achieved this, or the claim itself is unverified.

Sentence 5: Expert Opinion

"Les experts penchent pour un accident."

🏷️ FACT about expert opinion (present indicative: "penchent")

EXPERTS' POSITION: They lean toward ("penchent pour") an accident
                   (NOT sabotage)
NOTE: "penchent pour" = lean toward, favor
      This is a TENDENCY, not absolute certainty
      But it's reported as a FACT about what experts think

Sentence 6: Oil Spill Risk

"Ils sont unanimes pour écarter tout risque de marée noire."

🏷️ FACT about expert opinion (present: "sont unanimes")

EXPERTS' POSITION: UNANIMOUS in RULING OUT oil spill risk
"écarter" = to dismiss, rule out, set aside
"tout risque" = ALL risk
"unanimes" = unanimous (all experts agree)

THIS IS STRONG LANGUAGE:
  - Not "unlikely" but "ruled out"
  - Not "most experts" but "unanimous"
  → Experts are CERTAIN there's no oil spill risk

Sentence 7: Safety Measures

"Les puits du forage sont complètement fermés, et les 1,5 millions de litres de brut retenus dans les oléoducs, s'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés par huit bateaux dépêchés sur place."

🏷️ FACT + HYPOTHETICAL

FACT:         Wells are completely closed ("sont fermés" — present)
FACT:         1.5 million liters of crude oil in pipelines
HYPOTHETICAL: IF they escaped ("s'ils s'échappaient" — imperfect)
              THEN they would be pumped ("seraient pompés" — conditional)
              BY eight boats dispatched on site

THE OIL HAS NOT ESCAPED. This is a "what if" scenario.
The boats are there AS A PRECAUTION, not because there's an active spill.

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "Entity Confusion" Trap (new and critical for news):

TRAP TYPE: "ENTITY CONFUSION"

The text mentions MULTIPLE entities:
  Entity 1: Petropal (the company)
  Entity 2: P36 (the platform)
  Entity 3: The workers
  Entity 4: The politician
  Entity 5: The experts

Options can SWAP entities:
  Option A: says the COMPANY sank → actually the PLATFORM sank
  Option C: says the HEADQUARTERS was destroyed → actually the PLATFORM was

A company cannot sink. Headquarters are on land, not at sea.
The action (sinking) happened to the PLATFORM, not the company.

This is a more sophisticated version of the Subject-Object confusion from Chapter 5. In Chapter 5, you confused the remote control with what it controls. Here, you confuse the company with what it operates.

ENTITY CONFUSION EVOLUTION:
Ch.5: Product (TX8) vs. devices it controls (TV, hi-fi)
Ch.7: Company (Petropal) vs. equipment it operates (P36 platform)

Skill 2 — Distinguishing CONTEXT from CAUSE:

TEXT mentions TWO circumstances:
  "mer démontée"      → rough sea (CONTEXT — weather conditions)
  "explosion de gaz"  → gas explosion (CAUSE — what made it sink)

Option D says: "a essuyé une grosse tempête" (weathered a storm)
→ This presents the CONTEXT (rough sea) as the CAUSE (why it sank)

But the text clearly states: "l'explosion de gaz qui A CAUSÉ la catastrophe"
→ The explosion CAUSED it. The sea conditions are just the setting.

CONTEXT ≠ CAUSE
Weather described around the event ≠ Weather caused the event

This is related to the DATE ≠ CAUSE trap from Chapter 4, but now applied to CIRCUMSTANCES:

CONFUSION EVOLUTION:
Ch.4: Date ≠ Cause (when ≠ why)
Ch.7: Context ≠ Cause (setting ≠ reason)

Skill 3 — "Disparu" as a valid synonym for "coulé":

TEXT:     "a coulé au fond de l'océan" (sank to the ocean floor)
OPTION B: "a disparu en mer"           (disappeared at sea)

Is "disparu" a valid synonym for "coulé"?

When a massive structure sinks to 1360m below the surface:
  → It is no longer visible
  → It has effectively "disappeared" from the surface
  → "Disparu en mer" accurately describes the outcome

"Coulé" describes the PROCESS (sinking)
"Disparu" describes the RESULT (gone from sight)

Both are valid descriptions of the same event.
Process synonym ≈ result synonym → ACCEPTABLE MATCH ✅

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — Understanding euphemisms for death (new):

EUPHEMISMS FOR DEATH IN FRENCH NEWS:

"disparus"          = disappeared (euphemism for dead)
"les corps"         = the bodies (implies death — living people aren't "corps")
"perdu la vie"      = lost their life
"décédé"            = deceased
"victime"           = victim (often implies death in disaster context)

THE TEXT'S EVIDENCE:
  "renferme les CORPS" → bodies are inside → they're dead
  "ouvriers DISPARUS" → disappeared (during explosion) → died

COMBINING BOTH: bodies trapped + disappeared during explosion
  = they died in the explosion
  = "perdu la vie dans la catastrophe" ✅

The word "disparus" is a classic French news euphemism. It literally means "disappeared," but when combined with "corps" (bodies), it unambiguously means "dead." This is not inference — it's register-specific vocabulary that you need to recognize.

Skill 2 — Preventing victim/suspect role reversal:

Option C is a particularly insidious trap:

TRAP TYPE: "ROLE REVERSAL"

The text mentions:
  VICTIMS:  8 workers (died in explosion)
  CLAIM:    sabotage (by unknown external actors)

Option C says:
  Workers are SUSPECTED OF sabotage

This reverses their ROLE:
  TEXT: Workers = VICTIMS of the disaster
  OPTION: Workers = SUSPECTS who caused the disaster

The sabotage theory (from the politician) implies:
  Someone OUTSIDE destroyed the platform
  (to prevent Brazil from producing its own oil)
  → The workers are victims, not perpetrators

ROLE REVERSAL: Victim → Suspect = WRONG

This is a new trap type specific to news reports:

ROLE REVERSAL IN NEWS:
  Victim ↔ Suspect
  Witness ↔ Accused
  Expert ↔ Politician
  Rescuer ↔ Responsible party

Always check: WHO does WHAT in the text?
Don't let an option SWAP roles between actors.

Skill 3 — Eliminating impossible physical actions:

Option D says dead workers sued the company. This is physically impossible — dead people can't file lawsuits. While this seems obvious, under exam pressure, students sometimes pick options without checking for logical impossibility.

LOGIC CHECK: Is this option PHYSICALLY POSSIBLE?

"Workers sued the company" → requires living workers
Text: workers are dead (bodies inside sunken platform)
→ Dead people cannot sue → PHYSICALLY IMPOSSIBLE → eliminate

Always ask: "Given what the text says, is this option even possible?"

Skill 1 — Using conditionnel information as valid evidence (nuanced):

Wait — the information about the platform's economic value comes from the politician's claim, which is in the CONDITIONAL ("aurait permis"). Can we use conditional information to support an answer?

NUANCED RULE ABOUT CONDITIONNEL:

The conditionnel makes the CLAIM uncertain, but:
  → The claim IS reported in the text
  → The question asks about the platform's significance
  → Even as an uncertain claim, it represents what was BELIEVED about the platform
  → No other option has ANY text support

Options A, C, D: ← supported by NOTHING in the text

When the ONLY textual evidence is a conditional claim,
and all other options are fabricated or contradicted,

This is an important nuance: the conditionnel makes information LESS certain, but it doesn't make it NONEXISTENT. When the only evidence points to one answer, even conditional evidence is sufficient.

Position ≠ Movement

The critical reading skills here:

Skill 1 — The "Hypothetical → Completed" Trap (the chapter's most critical trap):

Option C is the most sophisticated trap in this text. Let me dissect it completely:

TRAP TYPE: "HYPOTHETICAL → COMPLETED CONVERSION"

TEXT SAYS (hypothetical):
  "s'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés"
  = IF they escaped, [they] WOULD BE pumped
  = si + imperfect → conditional = HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIO
  = This HAS NOT HAPPENED

OPTION C SAYS (completed):
  "la marée noire a été vite maîtrisée par pompage"
  = the oil spill WAS quickly controlled by pumping
  = passé composé = COMPLETED ACTION
  = This DID HAPPEN (according to option)

THE CONVERSION:
  Hypothetical scenario → Completed event
  "Would be pumped" → "Was pumped"
  Not yet happened → Already happened

This is EXTREMELY dangerous because:
  - The VOCABULARY matches (pompage/pompés, marée noire)
  - The MECHANISM matches (pumping by boats)
  - ONLY the TENSE differs (hypothetical vs. completed)
  - Under time pressure, students miss the tense difference

This trap exploits the gap between what WOULD happen and what DID happen:

TENSE-REALITY ALIGNMENT:

HYPOTHETICAL (conditionnel / si + imparfait):
  "s'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés"
  → Reality: oil has NOT escaped, pumping has NOT occurred

COMPLETED (passé composé):
  "a été maîtrisée par pompage"
  → Reality: oil DID escape, pumping DID occur

These are MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE realities.
If the text uses hypothetical tense, the event DIDN'T happen.
If an option uses completed tense, it claims the event DID happen.
→ CONTRADICTION

Skill 2 — Precision of negation: "very low" vs. "ruled out":

TEXT:     "unanimes pour écarter TOUT risque"  (rule out ALL risk)
OPTION B: "probabilité TRÈS FAIBLE"            (very low probability)

Are these the same?

"Rule out all risk" = probability ≈ 0%
"Very low probability" = probability ≈ 5-10%

Strictly speaking, "very low" is WEAKER than "ruled out."
But in exam context, Option B is the CLOSEST match.

No option says "zero probability" or "no risk at all."
Option B is the nearest approximation available.

EXAM STRATEGY:
When no option is a PERFECT match, pick the CLOSEST one.
"Very low" is closer to "ruled out" than "expected" (A),
"already controlled" (C), or "would be catastrophic" (D).

This introduces an important exam principle:

THE "CLOSEST MATCH" PRINCIPLE:

Sometimes no option perfectly mirrors the text.
In that case, pick the option that:
  1. Has the same DIRECTION (positive/negative, likely/unlikely)
  2. Has the closest INTENSITY (even if not exact)
  3. Does NOT contradict the text

Option B: same direction (unlikely) + close intensity (very low ≈ ruled out)
  → CLOSEST MATCH → CORRECT

Skill 3 — Attribution precision: "selon les experts" (new):

The question says: "Selon les experts" (according to experts)

This means I can ONLY use information attributed to EXPERTS.
I cannot use:
  - The journalist's descriptions (facts about the sinking)
  - The politician's claims (sabotage theory)
  - General context (rough sea, location)

EXPERT-ATTRIBUTED information in the text:
  ✅ "Les experts penchent pour un accident"
  ✅ "Ils sont unanimes pour écarter tout risque de marée noire"
  ✅ "Les puits sont complètement fermés" (stated in expert context)
  ✅ "S'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés" (expert contingency)

Only these can support the answer.

This is a new skill: attribution-restricted reading. When a question says "according to X," you can ONLY use statements attributed to X.

ATTRIBUTION-RESTRICTED READING:

"Selon les experts" → only expert statements count
"Selon l'auteur" → only the author's opinions count
"D'après le texte" → any information in the text counts
"Selon le député" → only the politician's claims count

Match the attribution in the QUESTION to the attribution in the TEXT.

Strategy: The "Tense-Reality Check"

Before accepting any option about an event:

STEP 1: What tense does the OPTION use?
  "a été maîtrisée" → passé composé → claims event HAPPENED

STEP 2: What tense does the TEXT use for this event?
  "s'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés" → hypothetical → event NOT happened

STEP 3: Do they match?
  Option: happened ≠ Text: hypothetical → MISMATCH → ELIMINATE

Strategy: The "Entity Card" for News

For any news report with multiple actors, create mental cards:

CARD 1: PETROPAL (company)
  Role: operator of the platform
  Status: still exists (a company doesn't "sink")

CARD 2: P36 (platform)
  Role: the object that sank
  Status: at 1360m depth

CARD 3: 8 WORKERS
  Role: victims
  Status: dead (bodies inside)

CARD 4: POLITICIAN
  Role: speculator
  Claim: sabotage (unconfirmed)

CARD 5: EXPERTS
  Role: analysts
  Position: accident (not sabotage), no oil spill risk

When an option mentions an entity, check its CARD. Does the option match the entity's role and status?

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall #1: The "Hypothetical → Completed" Conversion (Most Important Pitfall of This Chapter)

What happens: The text describes a HYPOTHETICAL scenario (what would happen IF). An option presents this scenario as a COMPLETED event (what DID happen).

THE MECHANISM:

TEXT (hypothetical):
  "S'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés par huit bateaux"
  Grammar: si + imparfait → conditionnel
  Reality: has NOT happened

OPTION (completed):
  "La marée noire a été vite maîtrisée par pompage"
  Grammar: passé composé
  Reality: DID happen (according to option)

WHY IT'S DANGEROUS:
  The vocabulary MATCHES (pompage, bateaux, marée noire)
  The mechanism MATCHES (pumping oil from sea)
  ONLY the tense differs
  Under pressure, students see matching vocabulary and pick it

Defense: For every option that describes a completed event, find the matching sentence in the text and CHECK THE TENSE. If the text uses conditional/hypothetical, the event didn't happen.

Pitfall #2: The "Entity Swap" Trap

What happens: The text mentions multiple entities (company, platform, workers, politician). An option assigns an ACTION to the wrong entity.

CORRECT:  Platform sank (P36 → a coulé)
WRONG:    Company sank (Petropal → a coulé)

CORRECT:  Workers died (ouvriers → disparus)
WRONG:    Workers committed sabotage (ouvriers → suspects)

Defense: For every option, identify WHO does WHAT. Then verify against your entity cards.

Pitfall #3: The "Euphemism Blindness" Trap

What happens: The text uses euphemisms (polite/indirect language for unpleasant realities). If you read literally, you miss the meaning.

"disparus" literally = disappeared
"disparus" in disaster context = dead

"les corps de huit ouvriers disparus"
  Literal: the bodies of eight workers who disappeared
  Real: the bodies of eight dead workers

If you read "disparus" as "escaped" or "went missing,"
you might think they're alive somewhere.
But "corps" (bodies) removes all ambiguity: they're dead.

Defense: When reading disaster/catastrophe news, know that "disparus" almost always means "dead" — especially when combined with "corps" (bodies).

Pitfall #4: The "Attribution Leak" Trap

What happens: A question asks "selon les experts" but you accidentally use information from a different source (the politician, the journalist).

QUESTION: "Selon les experts..."
EXPERT says: accident, no oil spill risk
POLITICIAN says: sabotage

If you mix up the sources:
  "Experts mentioned sabotage" → WRONG (politician said this)
  "Experts rule out oil spill" → CORRECT (experts said this)

Defense: When you see "selon X" in a question, mentally HIGHLIGHT only X's statements in the text. Ignore everything else.

Bridge to Chapter 8:

Chapter 7 dealt with a PAST event — something that already happened. The questions tested whether you could accurately report what occurred. Chapter 8 shifts to the FUTURE.

"Tourisme: On visitera avant de payer" (Texte 44) describes predictions about how tourism will change in 10-20 years. This text is full of future tense and conditional language — but here, the conditional doesn't mean "unconfirmed claim" (as in news). It means "prediction" or "projection."

You'll need to master:

  1. Future projections vs. current facts — What is TRUE NOW vs. what WILL BE true later?
  2. Comparative reasoning — How does the future differ from the present?
  3. The conditional as prediction (different from the journalistic conditional in Ch.7)

The grammar is the same (conditional tense), but the MEANING is completely different:

Ch.7 conditionnel: "aurait évoqué" = supposedly mentioned (unconfirmed past)
Ch.8 conditionnel: "celui-ci visitera" = will visit (predicted future)

Same grammar, different function.
Context determines meaning.
