The Three New Skills of Chapter 7

Skill 1: SEPARATING CONFIRMED FACTS FROM HYPOTHESES

This text presents information at THREE levels of certainty:

Certainty levelGrammar markerExample from text
CONFIRMED FACTPassé composé (past tense), present tense"la P36 a coulé" — it DID sink
UNCONFIRMED CLAIMConditionnel passé (conditional past)"Un député aurait évoqué un sabotage" — allegedly mentioned
HYPOTHETICAL SCENARIOConditionnel + "si" (if)"s'ils s'échappaient, seraient pompés" — IF they escaped, they WOULD be pumped

The exam LOVES to test whether you can tell these apart. A confirmed fact is TRUE in the text. An unconfirmed claim is ALLEGED. A hypothetical scenario is a POSSIBILITY.

Skill 2: THE CONDITIONAL TENSE AS UNCERTAINTY MARKER

In French journalism, the conditionnel (would/could/allegedly) is the grammatical fingerprint of UNVERIFIED information:

INDICATIVE (confirmed):     "Un député a évoqué un sabotage"
                            = A deputy DID mention sabotage (confirmed)

CONDITIONAL (unconfirmed):  "Un député AURAIT évoqué un sabotage"
                            = A deputy ALLEGEDLY mentioned sabotage (unconfirmed)

The single word "aurait" instead of "a" changes the ENTIRE reliability of the statement. In French journalism, this is the equivalent of writing "reportedly" or "allegedly" in English.

🔑 This is one of the most tested grammatical features in TCF reading comprehension. Master it here and it will serve you in every news-based text from now on.

Skill 3: EXPERT vs. POLITICAL OPINION

This text presents two competing interpretations of the same event:

POLITICAL CLAIM (député):    Sabotage → to prevent Brazil from being oil-independent
EXPERT ASSESSMENT:           Accident → no sabotage, no oil spill risk

WHO is more reliable in the text's framing?
→ The experts: "Ils sont UNANIMES" (they are unanimous)
→ The deputy: "AURAIT évoqué" (allegedly mentioned) — conditional = uncertain

The text FRAMES the expert opinion as more credible through:

  • Unanimity ("unanimes")
  • Concrete evidence ("puits fermés," "bateaux dépêchés")
  • Indicative tense ("penchent pour," "sont unanimes," "sont fermés")

And FRAMES the political claim as less credible through:

  • Conditional tense ("aurait évoqué," "aurait permis")
  • Single source (one deputy vs. multiple experts)

The "Context vs. Cause" Trap (Option D)

Option D introduces a crucial new trap type for news articles:

THE TEXT:
  CONTEXT:  "dans une mer démontée" (rough sea)    → the SETTING
  CAUSE:    "l'explosion de gaz" (gas explosion)    → the REASON it sank
  EVENT:    "a coulé" (sank)                        → WHAT happened

OPTION D CLAIMS:
  The platform "essuyé une grosse tempête" (endured a big storm)
  → This elevates the CONTEXT (rough sea) into the CAUSE (storm)

In news articles, the SETTING and the CAUSE are different things:

SETTING = WHERE/WHEN it happened    → "dans une mer démontée, à 150 km des côtes"
CAUSE   = WHY it happened           → "l'explosion de gaz qui a causé la catastrophe"

The text explicitly uses the word "causé" (caused) to identify the gas explosion as the cause. The rough sea is merely descriptive context.

🔑 Rule for news articles: Distinguish SETTING (background conditions described as context) from CAUSE (explicitly linked to the event by words like "causé," "provoqué," "dû à," "en raison de"). The exam will offer the setting as a false cause.

The "Entity Confusion" Trap (Option A)

Option A exploits a confusion between two ENTITIES mentioned in the same sentence:

TEXT: "la P36...exploitée par la compagnie Petropal, a coulé"

ENTITIES:
  P36      = the platform (physical structure)    → SUBJECT of "a coulé"
  Petropal = the company (business entity)        → OPERATOR of the platform

OPTION A: "la compagnie Petropal a coulé"
  → Makes PETROPAL the subject of "a coulé"
  → A company cannot physically sink
  → Confuses the OPERATOR with the OBJECT

🔑 Rule: When a sentence mentions multiple entities (a company, a platform, a person), verify WHICH entity is the grammatical SUBJECT of the main verb. The exam will swap entities to create confusion.

The "Victim vs. Suspect" Confusion (Option C)

Option C is a dangerous trap that conflates two completely different roles:

IN THE TEXT:
  THE WORKERS  = victims (they died in the explosion)
  THE SABOTEUR = unknown (the deputy SPECULATES about sabotage)

OPTION C CLAIMS:
  THE WORKERS  = suspects (accused of sabotage by the deputy)

This SWAPS the workers from VICTIMS to SUSPECTS.

The deputy's sabotage theory implies that SOMEONE (unknown) deliberately caused the explosion to prevent Brazil from becoming oil-independent. The workers were KILLED by this explosion — they are its VICTIMS, not its perpetrators.

🔑 Rule: In disaster reports, keep the ROLES clear: who are the VICTIMS? who are the WITNESSES? who are the SUSPECTS? who are the EXPERTS? The exam will swap these roles between options.

The "Static vs. Dynamic" Trap (Option C)

Option C exploits a subtle but important distinction:

STATIC DESCRIPTION:   "à 150 km des côtes" = AT 150 km from the coast (fixed position)
DYNAMIC DESCRIPTION:  "s'éloignait des côtes" = was moving AWAY from the coast (changing position)

"À" + distance = fixed location ≠ "S'éloigner de" = progressive movement

The platform was STATIONED at a fixed point, 150 km offshore. It wasn't moving. Option C invents a movement that doesn't exist.

🔑 Rule: "À [distance] de [location]" describes a FIXED position. Don't confuse it with verbs of movement. "À 150 km des côtes" = positioned at 150 km. "S'éloignait des côtes" = moving away from the coast. These are fundamentally different.

Same date. Completely different event.
SINKING ≠ INAUGURATION

🔑 Rule: When a date appears in a news article, note EXACTLY what event is associated with it. The exam will offer the same date connected to a DIFFERENT event.

The "Conditional-to-Indicative" Trap (Option C) — CRITICAL LESSON

This is the MOST IMPORTANT trap of Chapter 7. It transforms a HYPOTHETICAL scenario into a PAST EVENT:

TEXT (HYPOTHETICAL):
  "s'ils S'ÉCHAPPAIENT, SERAIENT pompés par huit bateaux"
  = IF they escaped (conditional), they WOULD BE pumped (conditional)
  → This is a CONTINGENCY PLAN for something that HASN'T happened

OPTION C (PAST EVENT):
  "la marée noire A ÉTÉ vite maîtrisée par pompage"
  = The oil spill WAS quickly controlled by pumping (past tense)
  → This describes something that DID happen

TRANSFORMATION:
  Conditional ("seraient") → Past tense ("a été")
  Hypothetical scenario → Actual past event
  Contingency plan → Accomplished fact

The exam takes a conditional/hypothetical statement and presents it in the indicative/past tense — making something that MIGHT happen sound like something that DID happen.

🔑 Rule: When the text uses conditional tense ("seraient," "pourraient," "s'échappaient"), the event is HYPOTHETICAL — it hasn't happened. If an answer option describes the same event in past tense ("a été," "ont été," "a eu lieu"), it's falsely converting a possibility into a reality. REJECT it.

This is the mirror of the conditional-as-uncertainty-marker: just as "aurait évoqué" signals an unconfirmed claim, "seraient pompés" signals an unrealized contingency.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Conditional-to-Indicative" Conversion

What happens: The text describes a HYPOTHETICAL scenario using conditional tense. An answer option presents the same scenario using past tense, as if it actually occurred.

Full mechanism:

TEXT:    "les 1,5 millions de litres...s'ils S'ÉCHAPPAIENT, SERAIENT pompés"
         → HYPOTHETICAL: IF they escaped, they WOULD be pumped
         → STATUS: The oil has NOT escaped. The pumping has NOT happened.

OPTION C: "la marée noire A ÉTÉ vite maîtrisée par pompage"
         → PAST EVENT: The oil spill WAS controlled by pumping
         → STATUS: This claims the spill DID happen and WAS handled.

THE CONVERSION:
  "seraient pompés" (conditional) → "a été maîtrisée" (passé composé)
  Possibility → Reality
  Future contingency → Past accomplishment

How to detect it: When an answer option uses past tense for something the text describes in conditional tense, it's a conversion trap.

The defense test:

Ask: "Did this event ACTUALLY HAPPEN according to the text?"

"Did an oil spill occur?"       → NO (experts dismiss ALL risk)
"Was pumping carried out?"      → NO (ships are ready, but not pumping)
"Were 1.5 million liters lost?" → NO (they are "retenus" = retained/held)

Since none of this happened, any option describing it as a past event is FALSE.

Pitfall 2: The "Two Dates, Two Events" Confusion

What happens: The text mentions TWO dates associated with TWO different events. You mix them up.

DATE 1: "la nuit du 15 mars"  → the GAS EXPLOSION (cause)
DATE 2: "le mardi 20 mars"   → the SINKING of the platform (result)

TIMELINE:
  March 15 (night): Gas explosion → kills 8 workers, damages platform
  March 20 (morning): Damaged platform finally sinks in rough seas
  
  5 DAYS between explosion and sinking

The exam could exploit this by:

  • Saying the explosion happened on March 20 (wrong — it was March 15)
  • Saying the platform sank on March 15 (wrong — it sank on March 20)
  • Saying the explosion and sinking were simultaneous (wrong — 5 days apart)

The defense: When a news article mentions multiple dates, create a mini-timeline:

March 15 (night) → Explosion → 8 workers die
[5 days pass — platform gradually deteriorates]
March 20 (morning) → Platform sinks → rests at 1360m

🔑 Rule: In disaster reports, the CAUSE and the FINAL RESULT often happen on DIFFERENT dates. Don't assume they're simultaneous.

Pitfall 3: The "Euphemism Blindness" Trap

What happens: The text uses a euphemism (a gentle word for something harsh), and you take it literally.

Example: "disparus" in "huit ouvriers disparus lors de l'explosion"

LITERAL meaning:    "disappeared" → they went missing
EUPHEMISTIC meaning: "deceased" → they died

CONTEXT PROOF:      "renferme les CORPS de huit ouvriers disparus"
                    → The platform contains their BODIES
                    → If their bodies are inside a platform at 1360m depth
                    → They are DEAD, not merely "missing"

French journalism frequently uses euphemisms for death:

  • "disparu" = disappeared → dead
  • "décédé" = deceased → dead (formal)
  • "a perdu la vie" = lost their life → died
  • "n'a pas survécu" = didn't survive → died

🔑 Rule: When you encounter "disparu" in a disaster context where bodies are mentioned, it means DEAD, not MISSING. Read euphemisms through their context, not their literal dictionary definition.

Pitfall 4: The "Unanimous = Absolute" Precision

What happens: You struggle between "the risk is ZERO" and "the risk is VERY LOW" because the experts say they "écartent tout risque" (dismiss all risk).

"Écarter tout risque"        = dismiss ALL risk → sounds like ZERO risk
"Probabilité très faible"    = very low probability → sounds like NEAR-ZERO risk

Are these the same?

In practical terms, YES — dismissing "all risk" and saying "very low probability" express the same expert confidence. The experts EXPLAIN why the risk is negligible (wells sealed, pumping ships ready), which supports "very low" rather than literally "impossible."

The exam chose "très faible" (B) over "aucun risque" because it's more scientifically precise. Experts rarely say risk is ZERO — they say it's NEGLIGIBLE.

Why Option A is clearly wrong despite being the "stronger" version:

Option A: "le risque est À PRÉVOIR" = the risk is to be EXPECTED (foreseeable)
→ This means the risk is LIKELY — the OPPOSITE of what experts say

Option B: "la probabilité est TRÈS FAIBLE" = the probability is VERY LOW
→ This means the risk is NEGLIGIBLE — consistent with "écarter tout risque"

Between "foreseeable" and "very low," the answer is clear: very low matches "dismiss all risk."
