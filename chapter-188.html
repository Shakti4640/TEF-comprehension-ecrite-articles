Note: These questions appear with only one answer option visible in our source material. This actually creates a PERFECT teaching opportunity — because we can analyze whether each given answer is SUPPORTED or UNSUPPORTED by the text, which is the CORE skill of detail questions.

🧠 THE PROBLEM: THE DETAIL QUESTION IS A NEEDLE-IN-A-HAYSTACK CHALLENGE

Purpose questions (Chapter 2) look at the WHOLE text. Main idea questions (Chapter 4) synthesize ALL paragraphs. But detail questions are fundamentally different:

PURPOSE QUESTION:     "What is the text's overall goal?"
                      → Look EVERYWHERE → SYNTHESIZE

MAIN IDEA QUESTION:   "What is the central message?"
                      → Look EVERYWHERE → COMBINE

DETAIL QUESTION:      "What specific thing does the text say about X?"
                      → Look in ONE PLACE → EXTRACT
                      
  The challenge is not understanding.
  The challenge is FINDING.

Here is what happens to most students on detail questions:

THE DETAIL QUESTION FAILURE PATTERN:

  Read question: "Les éleveurs..."
  → Start scanning text from the beginning
  → Paragraph 1: economic data... no mention of éleveurs specifically
  → Paragraph 2: offshore farms... maybe?
  → Paragraph 3: lots of information, quotes, problems...
  → Getting lost in the density
  → Time running out
  → Pick the answer that "sounds like" something from the text
  → WRONG — picked a distractor with borrowed words

The problem is undirected scanning. The student reads the entire text looking for "something about farmers" without a systematic method. This is like searching for a specific book in a library by walking through every aisle randomly.

📌 Detail questions require TARGETED reading, not general reading. You need to know WHERE to look BEFORE you start looking.

📊 THE PRECISION READING TECHNIQUE — DEMONSTRATED

The core skill this chapter teaches is precision reading — reading with surgical accuracy to determine EXACTLY what the text says, no more, no less. Let us formalize this as a technique:

THE PRECISION READING GRID:

  For any statement in the text, extract:
  
  ┌────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ WHO        │ Who performs the action or makes the claim│
  │ DOES WHAT  │ What EXACTLY is the action/state         │
  │ TO WHAT    │ What is the object/target                │
  │ HOW        │ By what means/method                     │
  │ WHY        │ For what stated purpose                  │
  │ WHEN       │ What time frame                          │
  │ HOW SURE   │ Fact, possibility, or claim?             │
  └────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┘

Applied to the Martins quote:

PRECISION GRID — MARTINS STATEMENT:

  WHO:       "Nous" (we = Marine Harvest / the industry)
  DOES WHAT: "faisons beaucoup d'efforts" (make many efforts)
  TO WHAT:   [implied: the treatment methods used]
  HOW:       "d'un point de vue technique" (technically)
  WHY:       "pour trouver des solutions pour RÉDUIRE 
              les traitements chimiques et les REMPLACER 
              par des techniques naturelles"
             (to find solutions to REDUCE chemicals 
              and REPLACE them with natural techniques)
  WHEN:      Present tense (ongoing effort)
  HOW SURE:  Attributed quote (her claim, not verified)
  
  PRECISE MEANING:
  The industry claims to be working on reducing 
  chemical treatments and replacing them with 
  natural alternatives.
  
  NOT: "Methods eliminate parasites inside salmon"
  NOT: "Methods improve salmon color"
  NOT: "Methods have solved the problems"

Applied to the problems statement:

PRECISION GRID — PROBLEMS STATEMENT:

  WHO:       "L'élevage industriel" (industrial farming)
  DOES WHAT: "pose deux problèmes" (creates two problems)
  TO WHAT:   "en ce qui concerne le saumon" (regarding salmon)
  PROBLEM 1: "la prolifération des poux de mer"
             (proliferation of sea lice)
  PROBLEM 2: "l'évasion des saumons d'élevage"
             (escape of farmed salmon)
  WHEN:      Present tense (ongoing reality)
  HOW SURE:  Stated as fact by journalist
  
  PRECISE MEANING:
  Industrial farming causes two problems:
  sea lice multiplying, and farmed salmon escaping.
  
  NOT: "Parasites inside salmon"
  NOT: "Methods used to eliminate parasites"
  (The text describes the PROBLEMS, not the SOLUTIONS)

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: BUILD A KEYWORD SEARCH LIST BEFORE SCANNING

THE KEYWORD LIST TECHNIQUE:

  Before scanning the text, write (mentally or on paper):
  
  PRIMARY KEYWORDS: exact words from the question/answer
  SECONDARY KEYWORDS: synonyms and related terms
  
  EXAMPLE FOR Q2:
  PRIMARY:   couleur, appétissant, améliorer, éleveurs
  SECONDARY: apparence, aspect, teinte, rose, rouge, 
             fermiers, producteurs, additifs
             
  EXAMPLE FOR Q3:
  PRIMARY:   méthodes, parasites, éliminer
  SECONDARY: traitements, techniques, solutions, 
             poux, lice, détruire, supprimer
             
  Then scan ONLY for these words.
  This is TARGETED scanning, not random reading.

RULE 2: USE THE PARAGRAPH FUNCTION MAP FROM CHAPTER 1

Your structural map (Chapter 1) tells you WHERE to look:

TEXTE 102 — PARAGRAPH FUNCTION MAP (from Chapter 4):

  PARA 1: Economic context (data, numbers)
          → Questions about ECONOMICS → look here
          
  PARA 2: Scale + transition to problems
          → Questions about SCALE/SIZE → look here
          
  PARA 3: Specific problems + methods + health risks
          → Questions about METHODS → look HERE ✅
          → Questions about PARASITES → look HERE ✅
          → Questions about TOXINS → look HERE ✅
          
  PARA 4: Expert opinion + call for change
          → Questions about OPINIONS → look here
          
  SHORTCUT: For Q2 and Q3 (about farming practices 
  and methods), go DIRECTLY to Paragraph 3.
  Don't waste time scanning Paragraphs 1, 2, or 4.

📌 Your Chapter 1 structural map is NOT just for understanding. It is a NAVIGATION TOOL that saves time on detail questions. If you mapped the text correctly during first reading, you already know where to look.

RULE 3: APPLY THE PRECISION READING GRID TO EVERY KEY SENTENCE

When you find the relevant sentence, do NOT read it casually. Apply the grid:

PRECISION GRID — APPLIED EVERY TIME:

  WHO does what?
  → Subject of the action
  
  WHAT exactly happens?
  → Verb (precise meaning, not approximate)
  → "réduire" ≠ "éliminer"
  → "fusionner" ≠ "dominer"
  
  TO/ABOUT what?
  → Object of the action
  → "les traitements chimiques" (the methods)
  → NOT "les parasites" (the parasites)
  
  WHY?
  → Stated purpose
  → "pour réduire" (to reduce)
  → NOT "pour éliminer" (to eliminate)
  
  HOW SURE?
  → Fact, claim, or possibility?
  → This is a QUOTE (attributed to Martins)
  → NOT confirmed by the journalist as fact

RULE 4: THE WORD-BY-WORD COMPARISON

When comparing an answer option with the text, check EVERY word:

THE WORD-BY-WORD PROTOCOL:

  Answer option: "Éliminer parasites présentes dans les saumons"
  
  Word 1: "Éliminer"
  → Text says: "réduire" → MISMATCH ❌
  
  Word 2: "parasites"
  → Text says: "poux de mer" → APPROXIMATE MATCH ≈
  
  Word 3: "présentes"
  → Text says: "prolifération" → DIFFERENT CONCEPT ❌
  
  Word 4: "dans"
  → Text implies: "on/around" (external parasites) → WRONG PREPOSITION ❌
  
  Word 5: "les saumons"
  → Text says: "le saumon" / "saumons" → MATCH ✅
  
  SCORE: 1 match, 1 approximate, 3 mismatches
  VERDICT: NOT SUPPORTED ❌

📌 This word-by-word protocol is the MOST RELIABLE technique for detail questions. It transforms subjective "this sounds right" into objective "3 out of 5 words don't match."

RULE 5: ABSENCE IS EVIDENCE

This is the most counterintuitive but most powerful rule for detail questions:

╔════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                                                            ║
║  IF THE TEXT DOESN'T MENTION SOMETHING,                    ║
║  ANY ANSWER ABOUT THAT SOMETHING IS WRONG.                 ║
║                                                            ║
║  The text never mentions:                                  ║
║  → Salmon color → answers about color are WRONG            ║
║  → Making salmon appetizing → answers about this are WRONG ║
║  → Antibiotics → answers about antibiotics are WRONG       ║
║  → Consumer preferences → answers about this are WRONG     ║
║                                                            ║
║  ABSENCE IN THE TEXT = ELIMINATION OF THE ANSWER           ║
║                                                            ║
║  You don't need to prove it's FALSE.                       ║
║  You only need to prove it's NOT IN THE TEXT.              ║
║                                                            ║
╚════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

This is different from Chapter 6's approach (finding specific FLAWS in each answer). For detail questions, the simplest elimination is often: "The text simply doesn't say this."

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: SCANNING TOO BROADLY

WRONG:
  Question asks about "methods used" in salmon farming.
  Student reads the ENTIRE text from beginning to end, 
  hoping to stumble upon the answer.
  Time: 4-5 minutes scanning all 4 paragraphs.
  
RIGHT:
  Question asks about "methods used" in salmon farming.
  Student uses paragraph function map → Paragraph 3.
  Student goes DIRECTLY to Paragraph 3.
  Student reads ONLY the relevant sentences.
  Time: 1-2 minutes, targeted and precise.
  
  SAVINGS: 2-3 minutes per question.
  Over 4 questions: 8-12 minutes saved.

MISTAKE 2: MATCHING VIBES INSTEAD OF WORDS

WRONG:
  "The text talks about problems with salmon farming,
   and the answer mentions problems with parasites...
   they're both about problems, so the answer must be right!"
   
  This is VIBE MATCHING — connecting general themes 
  without checking specific details.
  
RIGHT:
  "The text says 'prolifération des poux de mer' 
   and the answer says 'éliminer parasites dans les saumons.'
   
   Let me check:
   - 'poux de mer' ≈ parasites? YES (approximately)
   - 'prolifération' = 'présentes dans'? NO (different concept)
   - 'éliminer' = anything in the text? NO (text says 'réduire')
   
   Two mismatches → answer is distorted."
   
  This is WORD MATCHING — precise and reliable.

MISTAKE 3: ASSUMING SYNONYMS ARE EXACT MATCHES

WRONG:
  "Éliminer and réduire mean basically the same thing —
   both mean getting rid of something."
   
RIGHT:
  "Éliminer = COMPLETELY remove/destroy (100% gone)
   Réduire = DECREASE/lessen (still present, but less)
   
   These are DIFFERENT degrees of action.
   If the text says 'reduce' and the answer says 'eliminate,'
   the answer OVERSTATES the text's claim."
   
  IN EXAM CONTEXT:
  This distinction is PRECISELY what examiners test.
  They KNOW students will treat synonyms as interchangeable.
  The difference between "reduce" and "eliminate" 
  is often the difference between right and wrong.

Common false synonyms in French exams:

FALSE SYNONYM PAIRS (DIFFERENT MEANINGS):

  réduire ≠ éliminer       (reduce ≠ eliminate)
  certains ≠ tous          (some ≠ all)
  pourrait ≠ va            (could ≠ will)
  contribuer ≠ causer      (contribute ≠ cause)
  suggérer ≠ affirmer      (suggest ≠ affirm)
  influencer ≠ déterminer  (influence ≠ determine)
  améliorer ≠ transformer  (improve ≠ transform)
  partiellement ≠ entièrement (partially ≠ entirely)
  
  EXAM DESIGNERS LOVE THESE SWAPS.
  Always check the DEGREE and SCOPE of verbs.

MISTAKE 4: FORGETTING THE ATTRIBUTION CHECK (Chapter 5)

WRONG:
  "The text says methods are being developed to replace 
   chemical treatments with natural ones."
   
RIGHT:
  "CATARINA MARTINS says methods are being developed.
   She is an INDUSTRY EXECUTIVE with potential BIAS.
   The JOURNALIST reports her claim but doesn't confirm it.
   This is an ATTRIBUTED CLAIM, not a verified fact."
   
  WHY THIS MATTERS FOR DETAIL QUESTIONS:
  
  If the question asks "Selon le texte..." (According to the text),
  Martins's quote IS part of the text → acceptable source
  
  If the question asks "Le texte confirme que..." (The text confirms),
  Martins's quote is NOT confirmation → it's just a claim
  
  The QUESTION'S WORDING determines whether quotes count.

MISTAKE 5: IGNORING SMALL WORDS THAT CHANGE EVERYTHING

THE POWER OF SMALL WORDS:

  "dans les saumons" (INSIDE the salmon)
  vs.
  "sur les saumons" (ON the salmon)
  vs.
  "autour des saumons" (AROUND the salmon)
  
  One preposition changes the entire meaning.
  Sea lice are ON fish (external parasites).
  The answer says "DANS" (inside).
  This single small word makes the answer wrong.
  
  SIMILARLY:
  "ne...plus UNIQUEMENT" = no longer ONLY
  vs.
  "ne...plus" = no longer (at all)
  
  "UNIQUEMENT" changes the meaning completely:
  "no longer only farmed salmon" ≠ "no longer farmed salmon"
  
  RULE: In detail questions, EVERY word matters.
  Small words (prepositions, adverbs, quantifiers) 
  often carry the most critical meaning.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE PROXIMITY TRAP

DANGER: Two concepts appear NEAR each other in the text,
        so the student assumes they're CONNECTED.
        
  Text says (same paragraph):
  → "poux de mer" (sea lice)
  → "traitements chimiques" (chemical treatments)
  
  Student thinks: "Chemical treatments are for sea lice!"
  
  BUT the text never EXPLICITLY connects them:
  → "Poux de mer" is listed as a PROBLEM
  → "Traitements chimiques" is mentioned by Martins 
    in the context of REPLACING them with natural methods
  → The text never says "we use chemicals to treat sea lice"
  
  PROXIMITY ≠ CONNECTION
  
  Just because two things appear in the same paragraph 
  doesn't mean one CAUSES or RELATES TO the other.
  
  The text might IMPLY a connection, but if the question 
  asks about what the text SAYS (not implies), 
  the connection must be EXPLICIT.

PITFALL 2: THE LOGICAL INFERENCE TRAP

DANGER: The student makes a LOGICAL chain that the text 
        never makes.
        
  Student's reasoning:
  "Sea lice are a problem → 
   Chemical treatments exist → 
   Therefore chemicals are used to eliminate sea lice → 
   Therefore methods serve to eliminate parasites"
  
  This chain is LOGICALLY PLAUSIBLE.
  But the TEXT never makes this chain.
  
  The text says:
  1. Sea lice are a problem (stated separately)
  2. Chemical treatments exist (stated in Martins's quote)
  3. Industry wants to REDUCE chemicals (Martins's stated goal)
  
  The text does NOT say:
  1. Chemicals are used AGAINST sea lice (not stated)
  2. The goal is to ELIMINATE parasites (not stated)
  
  RULE FOR DETAIL QUESTIONS:
  Only EXPLICIT connections count.
  Logical inferences are for INFERENCE questions (Chapter 11),
  not for DETAIL questions.

PITFALL 3: THE QUANTIFIER TRAP

DANGER: The answer changes a QUANTIFIER from the text.
        
  Text: "L'élevage industriel pose DEUX problèmes"
        (creates TWO problems)
  
  Possible wrong answer: "L'élevage pose DE NOMBREUX problèmes"
                         (creates MANY/NUMEROUS problems)
  
  "Deux" (two) ≠ "nombreux" (many/numerous)
  The text is SPECIFIC: exactly two.
  The answer is VAGUE: many.
  
  COMMON QUANTIFIER SWAPS IN EXAMS:
  
  Text says:          Answer says:         Match?
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────
  "deux" (two)        "plusieurs" (several) ❌
  "certains" (some)   "tous" (all)          ❌
  "la plupart" (most) "tous" (all)          ❌
  "parfois" (sometimes)"toujours" (always)  ❌
  "un peu" (a little) "beaucoup" (a lot)    ❌
  "peut" (can)        "doit" (must)         ❌
  
  ALWAYS check quantifiers word-by-word.

PITFALL 4: THE "SOUNDS SCIENTIFIC" TRAP

DANGER: An answer uses scientific-sounding language 
        that FEELS authoritative.
        
  "Eliminer parasites présentes dans les saumons"
  
  This SOUNDS like a scientific/technical statement.
  It uses precise-seeming vocabulary.
  Students think: "This sounds like what a farming 
  article would say."
  
  But SOUNDING scientific ≠ BEING in the text.
  
  The text's actual language is:
  → "prolifération des poux de mer" (less clinical)
  → "réduire les traitements chimiques" (different focus)
  
  RULE: Judge answers by TEXT MATCH, not by how 
  professional or scientific they sound.

🔗 BRIDGE TO CHAPTER 8

Chapter 7 revealed a critical challenge: the correct answer to a question is almost NEVER a word-for-word copy from the text. It is always a paraphrase — a restatement using different words.

In Q25 (Chapter 6), the correct answer was:

TEXT:   "êtres humains et machines non biologiques pourraient 
        avoir si parfaitement fusionné que les différences 
        entre eux seraient devenues négligeables"

ANSWER: "les hommes et les outils technologiques ne feront 
         plus qu'un"

These say the SAME THING in COMPLETELY DIFFERENT WORDS.

How do you recognize that two completely different sentences mean the same thing? This is paraphrase recognition — the skill of detecting when an answer RESTATES the text without copying it.

Chapter 8 returns to Texte 101 and Q25 to build a systematic method for recognizing paraphrases, including:

  • Synonym substitution patterns
  • Structural rephrasing (passive → active, complex → simple)
  • Abstraction levels (specific text → general answer, or vice versa)
  • The difference between legitimate paraphrase and meaning distortion

This is arguably the most important skill for Compréhension Écrite, because every correct answer is a paraphrase. If you can't recognize paraphrases, you will always struggle between the correct answer and a distractor that uses the text's own words.

CHAPTER 8: Teaches you to recognize the answer 
           EVEN WHEN it looks nothing like the text

📌 Chapter 7 taught you precision reading and word-by-word matching. Chapter 8 teaches the OPPOSITE skill: recognizing that two different-looking sentences can carry the SAME meaning. Together, these two skills make you unstoppable on any question type.
