CHAPTER 11: WHAT THE TEXT IMPLIES BUT NEVER SAYS — INFERENCE IN TEXTE 101, Q26

Professional Techniques | Phase 3 of 5

📄 THE ARTICLE

We return to Texte 101 (Paroles de techno prophète). Refer back to Chapter 1 for full text. This chapter focuses on the invisible layer of meaning that lies BENEATH the surface of every text — and on the exam questions that test whether you can see it.

Our primary laboratory is Q26, which we partially solved in Chapter 6 but which contains a deeper lesson about inference:

Question 26 : les changements majeurs prédits par Ray Kurzweil sont
 A. la vie éternelle et le bonheur de tous.
 B. l'intelligence artificielle et la dépollution.
 C. la technologie humanisée et l'anticipation de l'avenir.
 D. La robotisation du quotidien et la dictature des machines.

🧠 THE PROBLEM: THE GAP BETWEEN WHAT IS SAID AND WHAT IS MEANT

Until now, every skill you have learned dealt with explicit information — things the text DIRECTLY states:

EXPLICIT COMPREHENSION (Phases 1-2):

  TEXT SAYS:  "Ray Kurzweil est une vedette de la 
               prospective scientifique"
  YOU KNOW:   Kurzweil is a celebrity of scientific futurism
  
  The meaning IS the words. No gap. No interpretation needed.

But many exam questions — and Q26 is a perfect example — require you to work with implicit information:

IMPLICIT COMPREHENSION (Phase 3):

  TEXT SAYS:  "de minuscules robots annuleront les effets 
               de la pollution"
  TEXT MEANS: Kurzweil predicts DEPOLLUTION through technology
  
  BUT: The word "dépollution" NEVER appears in the text.
  The text describes tiny robots canceling pollution effects.
  The CONCEPT of "dépollution" is the INFERENCE you must make.
  
  TEXT SAYS:  → DESCRIBES a specific prediction (tiny robots)
  ANSWER SAYS:→ NAMES the concept (dépollution)
  
  THE GAP between description and concept = INFERENCE

This gap is where most students fail. They can find what the text SAYS (Chapter 7). They can recognize when an answer PARAPHRASES the text (Chapter 8). But they cannot bridge the gap between a specific description and its general concept name.

📌 Inference is the skill of seeing the CONCEPT behind the DESCRIPTION — the general idea behind the specific example — the conclusion behind the evidence. It is reading what the text MEANS, not just what it SAYS.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE THREE TYPES OF INFERENCE

Not all inferences are created equal. There are exactly three types, each with different reliability and different exam applications:

╔═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║              THE THREE TYPES OF INFERENCE                         ║
╠═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║                                                                   ║
║  TYPE 1: LOGICAL INFERENCE (Deduction)                            ║
║  → The conclusion NECESSARILY follows from the text               ║
║  → If A is true, then B MUST be true                              ║
║  → Reliability: VERY HIGH                                         ║
║  → Example:                                                       ║
║    Text: "Balmat avait 24 ans en 1786"                            ║
║    Inference: He was born around 1762                              ║
║    → This MUST be true if the text is accurate                    ║
║                                                                   ║
║  TYPE 2: REASONABLE INFERENCE (Induction)                         ║
║  → The conclusion PROBABLY follows from the text                  ║
║  → If A is true, then B is LIKELY true                            ║
║  → Reliability: HIGH (but not certain)                            ║
║  → Example:                                                       ║
║    Text: "minuscules robots annuleront les effets                 ║
║           de la pollution"                                        ║
║    Inference: Kurzweil predicts depollution                       ║
║    → VERY LIKELY true — robots canceling pollution                ║
║      IS depollution by definition                                 ║
║                                                                   ║
║  TYPE 3: SPECULATIVE INFERENCE (Extrapolation)                    ║
║  → The conclusion MIGHT follow but isn't supported                ║
║  → If A is true, then B is POSSIBLE but not demonstrated          ║
║  → Reliability: LOW                                               ║
║  → Example:                                                       ║
║    Text: "l'humanité pourra vivre éternellement"                  ║
║    Inference: This will bring happiness to everyone               ║
║    → POSSIBLE but NOT supported by the text                       ║
║    → Eternal life ≠ guaranteed happiness                          ║
║                                                                   ║
╚═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

The critical distinction for exams:

THE EXAM INFERENCE RULE:

  ╔════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
  ║                                                        ║
  ║  CORRECT ANSWERS require Type 1 or Type 2 inferences   ║
  ║  (logical or reasonable — SUPPORTED by text)            ║
  ║                                                        ║
  ║  WRONG ANSWERS often require Type 3 inferences          ║
  ║  (speculative — NOT supported by text)                  ║
  ║                                                        ║
  ║  THE EXAM TESTS WHETHER YOU CAN DISTINGUISH             ║
  ║  BETWEEN SUPPORTED AND UNSUPPORTED INFERENCES           ║
  ║                                                        ║
  ╚════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

VERIFYING: WHY THE OTHER OPTIONS REQUIRE TYPE 3 (SPECULATIVE) INFERENCES

OPTION A: "la vie éternelle et le bonheur de tous"

  "vie éternelle" ← INFERENCE from "vivre éternellement"
  → Type 1 inference (direct equivalence) ✅ VALID
  
  "bonheur de tous" ← INFERENCE from... what?
  → Text says: "vivre éternellement sans vieillir"
  → Inference chain: eternal life → happiness for all
  → STEP 1: Eternal life = good thing (assumption)
  → STEP 2: Good thing = happiness (assumption)
  → STEP 3: Happiness for EVERYONE (generalization)
  → THREE STEPS, each adding UNPROVEN assumptions
  → INFERENCE TYPE: Type 3 (Speculative) ❌
  
  The text NEVER connects eternal life to happiness.
  Eternal life could bring: existential crisis, boredom,
  overpopulation, social inequality...
  "Happiness for all" is a HOPE, not a supported inference.
  
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ INFERENCE CHAIN LENGTH:                          │
  │                                                  │
  │ SHORT CHAIN (1-2 steps): Usually VALID           │
  │ → "cancel pollution" → "dépollution" (2 steps)  │
  │                                                  │
  │ LONG CHAIN (3+ steps): Usually SPECULATIVE       │
  │ → "eternal life" → "good" → "happiness"         │
  │   → "for ALL" (4 steps of assumption)            │
  │                                                  │
  │ RULE: The LONGER the inference chain,            │
  │ the MORE LIKELY it is speculative (Type 3),      │
  │ and the MORE LIKELY it is WRONG on the exam.     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
OPTION C: "la technologie humanisée et l'anticipation de l'avenir"

  "technologie humanisée" ← INFERENCE from "fusionné"
  → Text says: humans and machines MERGE
  → Inference: this makes technology more HUMAN? 
  → OR: this makes humans more TECHNOLOGICAL?
  → The text says MERGER (equal), not HUMANIZATION (one-directional)
  → "Humanisée" implies technology becoming human-like
  → But the text describes humans becoming machine-like TOO
  → INFERENCE TYPE: Type 3 (Speculative — imposes a direction
    that the text doesn't specify) ❌
  
  "anticipation de l'avenir" ← INFERENCE from Kurzweil's predictions
  → Kurzweil DOES anticipate the future (he predicts things)
  → But "anticipation de l'avenir" as a PREDICTED CHANGE?
  → Kurzweil doesn't predict that humanity will GAIN 
    the ability to anticipate the future
  → He himself anticipates, but that's HIS ACTIVITY,
    not a PREDICTED CHANGE for humanity
  → INFERENCE TYPE: Type 3 (Confuses the predictor's activity
    with his predictions) ❌
OPTION D: "La robotisation du quotidien et la dictature des machines"

  "robotisation du quotidien" ← INFERENCE from "minuscules robots"
  → Text says: tiny robots cancel pollution
  → Inference: robots in daily life = robotization of daily life
  → STEP 1: Pollution robots exist → VALID
  → STEP 2: Pollution robots = daily life robots → SPECULATIVE
  → The text mentions robots for ONE purpose (depollution)
  → "Robotisation du quotidien" implies robots EVERYWHERE
  → Jumping from one specific use to universal presence
  → INFERENCE TYPE: Type 3 (Over-generalization) ❌
  
  "dictature des machines" ← INFERENCE from "fusionné"
  → Text says: humans and machines merge with negligible differences
  → Inference: machines will DICTATE to humans
  → STEP 1: Merger → VALID
  → STEP 2: Merger = one side dominates → SPECULATIVE
  → STEP 3: The dominating side is machines → SPECULATIVE
  → STEP 4: Domination = dictatorship → EXTREME
  → FOUR STEPS, each unproven
  → INFERENCE TYPE: Type 3 (Extreme extrapolation) ❌
  → The text says MERGER (equality), not DOMINATION (hierarchy)

📊 THE INFERENCE FRAMEWORK — GENERALIZED

Now let us formalize the inference method so you can apply it to ANY text and ANY question:

THE 3-STEP INFERENCE PROTOCOL

╔═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║              THE 3-STEP INFERENCE PROTOCOL                    ║
╠═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║                                                               ║
║  STEP 1: IDENTIFY WHAT THE TEXT EXPLICITLY SAYS               ║
║  → Quote the exact words                                      ║
║  → This is your STARTING POINT                                ║
║  → "minuscules robots annuleront les effets de la pollution"  ║
║                                                               ║
║  STEP 2: IDENTIFY WHAT THE ANSWER CLAIMS                      ║
║  → Note the exact concept or statement                        ║
║  → This is your DESTINATION                                   ║
║  → "dépollution"                                              ║
║                                                               ║
║  STEP 3: MAP THE INFERENCE CHAIN BETWEEN THEM                 ║
║  → How many STEPS does it take to get from                    ║
║    STARTING POINT to DESTINATION?                             ║
║  → Is each step SUPPORTED or ASSUMED?                         ║
║  → "cancel pollution effects" → "depollution" = 1 step,      ║
║    definitionally equivalent → SUPPORTED ✅                    ║
║                                                               ║
║  EVALUATION:                                                  ║
║  → 1-2 supported steps = VALID inference ✅                   ║
║  → 3+ steps or unsupported steps = SPECULATIVE inference ❌   ║
║                                                               ║

APPLYING THE PROTOCOL TO TEXTE 102 (SALMON)

Let us test the inference framework on a different text to prove it works universally.

Potential inference question about Texte 102:

"The article suggests that Norwegian salmon farming..."

POTENTIAL INFERENCE: "threatens human health"

  STEP 1 — TEXT SAYS:
  → "Le saumon pourrait contenir des produits toxiques 
     comme des dioxydes, des pesticides et même des 
     métaux lourds" (Para 3)
  → "la Norvège recommandait déjà une consommation modérée 
     pour les enfants et les femmes enceintes" (Para 3)
  
  STEP 2 — INFERENCE CLAIMS:
  → Salmon farming threatens human health
  
  STEP 3 — CHAIN:
  → Salmon could contain toxins (TEXT) 
  → Toxins in food = health risk (1 step, definitional)
  → Health risk = threatens human health (1 step, definitional)
  → CHAIN LENGTH: 2 steps, both supported ✅
  
  ADDITIONAL SUPPORT:
  → Norway warned about children/pregnant women = 
    official recognition of health risk ✅
  
  VERDICT: VALID Type 2 inference ✅
POTENTIAL INFERENCE: "will cause a global health crisis"

  STEP 1 — TEXT SAYS:
  → "pourrait contenir des produits toxiques" (COULD)
  → Norway warned specific groups (children, pregnant women)
  
  STEP 2 — INFERENCE CLAIMS:
  → Global health crisis
  
  STEP 3 — CHAIN:
  → Salmon could contain toxins (TEXT)
  → Toxins = health risk (1 step) ✅
  → Health risk in salmon = health risk for consumers (1 step) ✅
  → Health risk for consumers = GLOBAL crisis (SPECULATIVE) ❌
  → "Pourrait" (could) → "will cause" (certain) ❌
  
  TWO PROBLEMS:
  → "Global" is not supported (text discusses one product)
  → "Will cause" contradicts "pourrait" (could)
  
  VERDICT: Type 3 speculative inference ❌
  Chain breaks at step 3 (local → global = unsupported)
  and modality shift (could → will = unsupported)

APPLYING THE PROTOCOL TO L'ALPINISME

POTENTIAL INFERENCE: "The author admires Balmat more than Saussure"

  STEP 1 — TEXT SAYS:
  → Saussure: "savant Genevois", offered reward, 
    went to Mont Blanc, did science
  → Balmat: "grand coureur de glaciers", found the route,
    "le pas décisif", described in extensive detail
  
  STEP 2 — INFERENCE CLAIMS:
  → Author admires Balmat MORE
  
  STEP 3 — CHAIN:
  → Balmat gets MORE text space than Saussure (observable) ✅
  → "Le pas décisif" = "the decisive step" = highest praise ✅
  → But: "les Alpes l'avaient conquis" about Saussure = 
    also deep admiration ⚠️
  → Both are praised, but in DIFFERENT ways
  → Balmat = action hero; Saussure = intellectual inspiration
  → "More" admiration? Text doesn't explicitly rank them
  
  VERDICT: Type 3 speculative inference ❌
  The text admires BOTH for different reasons.
  Claiming "more" for one requires assuming the author's 
  internal ranking, which is not stated.
POTENTIAL INFERENCE: "After the initial conquest, exploration 
                      of Mont Blanc stagnated"

  STEP 1 — TEXT SAYS:
  → "aucun des amateurs... ne laisse une trace durable"
  → "malgré le nombre de ces ascensions, l'exploration 
     n'avance pas"
  → "La première voie... est la seule connue jusqu'en 1855"
  
  STEP 2 — INFERENCE CLAIMS:
  → Exploration stagnated after the initial conquest
  
  STEP 3 — CHAIN:
  → "exploration n'avance pas" = exploration doesn't advance

  → "après" the initial conquest? Text shows:
    1786 = conquest; 1808-1855 = no new routes
    = decades of stagnation AFTER conquest (1 step, 
    temporal reading) ✅

  The text EXPLICITLY says "exploration n'avance pas"
  which IS stagnation. Almost no inference needed.

📐 THE INFERENCE DISTANCE SCALE

Let us formalize the relationship between inference distance and reliability:

THE INFERENCE DISTANCE SCALE:

  DISTANCE 0: EXPLICIT
  → Text says X, answer says X (same words)
  → No inference needed
  → Example: Text: "vedette" → Answer: "vedette"
  → RELIABILITY: 100%
  
  DISTANCE 1: PARAPHRASE
  → Text says X, answer says Y (Y = synonym of X)
  → Recognized through Chapter 8 skills
  → Example: Text: "fusionné" → Answer: "ne feront qu'un"
  → RELIABILITY: 95%
  
  DISTANCE 2: SHORT INFERENCE
  → Text describes X, answer names the CONCEPT of X
  → 1-2 definitional steps
  → Example: Text: "robots cancel pollution" → 
    Answer: "dépollution"
  → RELIABILITY: 85%
  
  DISTANCE 3: CROSS-PARAGRAPH INFERENCE
  → Text says X in one place and Y in another,
    answer COMBINES X and Y into a new statement
  → Example: Predictions (Para 1-2) + debate topics (Para 3)
    → "intelligence artificielle" connects both
  → RELIABILITY: 70%
  
  DISTANCE 4: THEMATIC INFERENCE
  → Text's overall pattern/arc implies a conclusion
  → Not stated anywhere, but the PATTERN suggests it
  → Example: L'Alpinisme's arc of triumph → stagnation
    → "exploration stagnated" (supported by pattern)
  → RELIABILITY: 60%
  
  DISTANCE 5: SPECULATIVE INFERENCE
  → Requires assumptions NOT in the text
  → Could be true but is NOT supported
  → Example: "eternal life" → "happiness for all"
  → RELIABILITY: 20% — ALMOST ALWAYS WRONG ON EXAMS

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                      │
  │  CORRECT ANSWERS: Distance 0-3 (supported)           │
  │  WRONG ANSWERS:   Distance 4-5 (speculative)         │
  │                                                      │
  │  THE EXAM BOUNDARY IS AT DISTANCE 3.                 │
  │  Anything beyond requires too much assumption.       │
  │                                                      │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: ALWAYS START FROM THE TEXT, NEVER FROM THE ANSWER

THE DIRECTION OF INFERENCE:

  WRONG DIRECTION:
  Answer says "dépollution" 
  → "Where does the text say dépollution?"
  → "It doesn't! So this answer must be wrong."
  → ❌ WRONG — you missed the inference
  
  RIGHT DIRECTION:
  Text says "robots cancel pollution effects"
  → "What CONCEPT does this describe?"
  → "Canceling pollution = depollution"
  → "Does any answer match 'dépollution'?"
  → "Yes! Option B!" ✅
  
  START: Text description → ARRIVE: Concept name
  NOT: Concept name → Search for exact match in text

📌 Inference works FROM text TO answer, not from answer to text. If you search the text for the answer's exact words, you will miss every inference-based correct answer.

RULE 2: COUNT THE INFERENCE STEPS

Before accepting or rejecting an inference, count the steps:

THE STEP-COUNTING METHOD:

  TEXT: "minuscules robots annuleront les effets de la pollution"
  ANSWER: "dépollution"
  
  STEP 1: "cancel pollution effects" = "remove pollution"
           → DEFINITIONAL (removing pollution effects = depolluting)
           → SUPPORTED ✅
  
  STEP 2: "remove pollution" = "dépollution"
           → NAMING (giving the concept its French name)
           → SUPPORTED ✅
  
  TOTAL STEPS: 2 → WITHIN ACCEPTABLE RANGE ✅
  
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  TEXT: "l'humanité pourra vivre éternellement sans vieillir"
  ANSWER: "bonheur de tous"
  
  STEP 1: "live eternally" = "eternal life"
           → NAMING → SUPPORTED ✅
  
  STEP 2: "eternal life" = "good thing"
           → ASSUMPTION (is it always good?) → UNCERTAIN ⚠️
  
  STEP 3: "good thing" = "happiness"
           → ASSUMPTION (good ≠ necessarily happy) → UNSUPPORTED ❌
  
  STEP 4: "happiness" = "happiness for ALL"
           → GENERALIZATION (why everyone?) → UNSUPPORTED ❌
  
  TOTAL STEPS: 4, with 2 unsupported → BEYOND ACCEPTABLE RANGE ❌

RULE 3: USE THE "WOULD EVERYONE AGREE?" TEST

THE UNIVERSAL AGREEMENT TEST:

  Ask: "Would EVERY reasonable reader make this inference?"
  
  TEXT: "robots cancel pollution"
  INFERENCE: "depollution"
  → Would every reader agree this describes depollution?
  → YES — it's definitionally depollution ✅
  
  TEXT: "live eternally without aging"
  INFERENCE: "happiness for all"
  → Would every reader agree eternal life = happiness for ALL?
  → NO — many people would question this:
    "What about overpopulation?"
    "What about boredom?"
    "What about inequality?"
    "What about those who don't WANT to live forever?"
  → NOT universal agreement → SPECULATIVE ❌
  
  RULE: If reasonable people could DISAGREE with the inference,
  it is speculative (Type 3) and likely WRONG on the exam.

RULE 4: CROSS-PARAGRAPH INFERENCE REQUIRES TEXTUAL BRIDGES

When an inference requires combining information from different parts of the text, look for textual bridges — moments where the text ITSELF makes the connection:

THE TEXTUAL BRIDGE IN TEXTE 101:

  PARAGRAPH 1-2: Predictions (robots, brain upload, fusion, 
                  intelligence expansion)
  PARAGRAPH 3:   "des concepts de science-fiction 
                  (l'intelligence artificielle...)"
  
  THE BRIDGE:
  Paragraph 3 says the debate links to "intelligence artificielle"
  → The TEXT ITSELF connects the predictions to the AI concept
  → You don't need to make this connection yourself
  → The text builds the bridge FOR you
  
  RULE: Cross-paragraph inferences are VALID when the text 
  itself provides a connecting sentence or phrase.
  
  If the text provides NO bridge between two sections,
  combining them is YOUR inference, not the text's — 
  and likely speculative.

Example of a MISSING bridge (invalid inference):

NO TEXTUAL BRIDGE:

  PARAGRAPH 1: "vivre éternellement sans vieillir"
  PARAGRAPH 3: mentions "Dieu" as a controversial theme
  
  POSSIBLE INFERENCE: "Kurzweil's predictions challenge God"
  
  BUT: The text never connects "eternal life" to "God" directly.
  "Dieu" appears in the DEBATE TOPICS list,
  not as a consequence of the predictions.
  
  There is NO bridge sentence like:
  "These predictions about eternal life raise questions about God"
  
  Without a textual bridge, this inference is SPECULATIVE.
  The text lists them NEAR each other but doesn't CONNECT them.
  
  PROXIMITY ≠ CONNECTION (Chapter 7 rule still applies)

RULE 5: MODALITY MUST BE PRESERVED IN INFERENCE

This is the most commonly violated rule:

THE MODALITY PRESERVATION RULE:

  If the text uses CONDITIONAL/POSSIBLE language,
  your inference must MAINTAIN that uncertainty.
  
  TEXT: "le saumon POURRAIT contenir des métaux lourds"
  VALID INFERENCE: "Salmon may pose a health risk" 
  → Preserves "could/may" → VALID ✅
  
  INVALID INFERENCE: "Salmon IS dangerous to health"
  → Converts "could" to "is" → INVALID ❌
  → Modality shifted from possible to certain
  
  TEXT: "nous SERIONS capables de télécharger le cerveau"
  VALID INFERENCE: "Brain uploading might become possible"
  → Preserves "would/might" → VALID ✅
  
  INVALID INFERENCE: "Brain uploading WILL happen"
  → Converts conditional to definite → INVALID ❌
  
  RULE: Your inference can NEVER be more certain 
  than the text's own language.
  
  "Pourrait" → inference must include "might/could/possibly"
  "Selon lui" → inference must include "according to him"
  "Il est probable" → inference must include "probably"

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: CONFUSING YOUR KNOWLEDGE WITH THE TEXT'S IMPLICATIONS

WRONG:
  "I know that AI is about machines being smarter than humans.
   The text talks about AI-related concepts.
   Therefore, the text implies machines will be smarter."
   
  This uses YOUR knowledge about AI, not the TEXT's content.
  The TEXT says humans and machines MERGE (equal fusion),
  not that machines become smarter (hierarchy).
  
RIGHT:
  "The text says humans and machines merge with negligible 
   differences. This implies they become EQUAL, not that 
   one surpasses the other."
   
  RULE: Inferences must come FROM the text, 
  not from your background knowledge ABOUT the topic.

MISTAKE 2: INFERRING EMOTIONS NOT DESCRIBED

WRONG:
  Text: "l'humanité pourra vivre éternellement sans vieillir"
  Inference: "People will be HAPPY about this"
  
  The text describes a PREDICTION about biology.
  It says NOTHING about emotional reactions.
  
  You cannot infer:
  → happiness (not mentioned)
  → fear (not mentioned)
  → excitement (not mentioned)
  → resistance (not mentioned)
  
  UNLESS the text explicitly describes emotions,
  you cannot infer them from predictions alone.
  
  THE TEXT DOES describe REACTIONS to Kurzweil:
  → "désaccord véhément" (fierce disagreement)
  → "admiration éperdue" (wild admiration)
  → "scepticisme froid" (cold skepticism)
  
  THESE emotions are explicitly stated → you CAN use them.
  But you cannot ADD emotions the text doesn't mention.

MISTAKE 3: THE "LOGICAL THEREFORE" TRAP

WRONG:
  "If humans and machines merge, THEREFORE machines will 
   eventually control humans, because machines are more 
   powerful."
   
  This follows a LOGICAL pattern (if-then-therefore)
  that SOUNDS rigorous but contains hidden assumptions:
  
  HIDDEN ASSUMPTION 1: "Machines are more powerful"
  → Text doesn't say this
  
  HIDDEN ASSUMPTION 2: "The more powerful entity controls"
  → Text doesn't say this
  
  HIDDEN ASSUMPTION 3: "Merger leads to domination"
  → Text says merger leads to NEGLIGIBLE DIFFERENCES
    (= equality, not domination)
  
  The "logical therefore" structure DISGUISES speculation 
  as deduction. Just because a chain SOUNDS logical 
  doesn't mean each link is SUPPORTED by the text.
  
  RULE: Check EVERY step in a logical chain.
  If ANY step relies on an assumption not in the text,
  the entire chain is speculative.

MISTAKE 4: MAKING INFERENCES FROM TITLES OR HEADERS

WRONG:
  "The title says 'Paroles de techno prophète.'
   'Prophète' implies religious or spiritual predictions.
   Therefore, the text is about spiritual matters."
   
  The title uses "prophète" METAPHORICALLY.
  It means "someone who predicts" — not literally religious.
  
  You cannot build inferences on METAPHORICAL language 
  in titles, which is often playful or ironic.
  
  RULE: Base inferences on the TEXT BODY, 
  not on the title (which may be figurative).

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "OBVIOUSLY TRUE" TRAP

DANGER: An inference seems SO obviously true that 
        you don't check if the text supports it.
        
  "Obviously, eternal life would make people happy."
  "Obviously, machines will become smarter than humans."
  "Obviously, robots in daily life means robotization."
  
  Each of these feels OBVIOUS — but none is supported 
  by the text.
  
  "OBVIOUSLY" IS A WARNING SIGNAL.
  
  When something feels obvious, it means you're using 
  COMMON SENSE, not TEXT EVIDENCE.
  
  Common sense ≠ textual support.
  
  The exam tests whether you can distinguish between 
  what SEEMS logical and what the text ACTUALLY supports.

PITFALL 2: THE DIRECTION CONFUSION

DANGER: Inferring in the WRONG DIRECTION.
        
  TEXT: "êtres humains et machines fusionné"
  
  DIRECTION A: Humans become more machine-like
  DIRECTION B: Machines become more human-like
  DIRECTION C: Both merge into something new (neither human nor machine)
  
  THE TEXT says: "fusionné" with "différences négligeables"
  → This is DIRECTION C (mutual merger)
  → NOT Direction A alone, NOT Direction B alone
  
  OPTION C in Q26: "technologie humanisée" = Direction B only
  → WRONG because it picks ONE direction of a TWO-WAY process
  
  RULE: When the text describes a MUTUAL process,
  don't infer it as a ONE-DIRECTIONAL process.
  Merger ≠ humanization. Merger ≠ mechanization.
  Merger = BOTH simultaneously.

PITFALL 3: THE INFERENCE SNOWBALL

DANGER: Building inference upon inference upon inference.
        
  SNOWBALL EXAMPLE:
  
  Text: "robots cancel pollution"
  Step 1: Therefore depollution is predicted ✅
  Step 2: Therefore the environment will improve ≈
  Step 3: Therefore climate change will be solved ❌
  Step 4: Therefore global warming will reverse ❌
  Step 5: Therefore coastal cities are safe ❌
  
  Each step SEEMS to follow from the previous one.
  But by step 3, you're MILES from the text.
  
  THE SNOWBALL RULE:
  Each inference step MULTIPLIES uncertainty.
  
  Step 1: 90% reliable
  Step 2: 90% × 80% = 72% reliable
  Step 3: 72% × 60% = 43% reliable
  Step 4: 43% × 50% = 21% reliable
  Step 5: 21% × 40% = 8% reliable
  
  By step 5, your "inference" is 92% likely to be WRONG.
  
  RULE: Stop at step 2. NEVER go beyond step 3.

PITFALL 4: THE "TEXT MENTIONS IT" CONFUSION

DANGER: Thinking that because the text MENTIONS a concept,
        any inference involving that concept is valid.
        
  Text mentions: "intelligence artificielle" (Para 3)
  Text mentions: "Dieu" (Para 3)
  Text mentions: "génie génétique" (Para 3)
  
  VALID INFERENCE:
  "The debate involves questions about AI" ✅
  (Text says AI is part of the debate)
  
  INVALID INFERENCE:
  "Kurzweil predicts that AI will replace God" ❌
  (Text mentions both but never connects them this way)
  
  INVALID INFERENCE:
  "Kurzweil's theory is about genetic engineering" ❌
  (Text mentions genetics as a DEBATE TOPIC, 
   not as Kurzweil's prediction)
  
  RULE: MENTIONING a concept ≠ CONNECTING it to everything else.
  Check the SPECIFIC CONTEXT in which each concept appears.
  Paragraph 3 lists DEBATE TOPICS — not PREDICTIONS.
  Paragraph 1-2 lists PREDICTIONS — not debate topics.
  Don't cross these boundaries without a textual bridge.

🔗 BRIDGE TO CHAPTER 12

Chapter 11 taught you to make valid inferences — seeing concepts behind descriptions. But inference has a specific and powerful application: cause-and-effect reasoning.

Chapter 12 returns to Texte 102 (Le Saumon) to analyze the text's complex cause-and-effect chains:

THE CAUSE-EFFECT CHAINS IN TEXTE 102:

  CAUSE: Rising global demand for salmon
  → EFFECT: Norway industrializes production
  → EFFECT: Massive offshore farms built
  → EFFECT: Sea lice proliferate
  → EFFECT: Farmed salmon escape
  → EFFECT: Wild salmon ecosystems threatened
  → EFFECT: Genetic heritage of wild salmon at risk
  → EFFECT: Toxic chemicals used in treatments
  → EFFECT: Salmon contain dioxides, pesticides, metals
  → EFFECT: Human health at risk
  → EFFECT: Even organic salmon contaminated
  → EFFECT: Entire aquatic environment polluted
  
  This is a 12-STEP cause-effect chain.
  The text presents it across paragraphs 1-4.
  
  Exam questions can ask about ANY link in this chain.
  To answer correctly, you must trace the chain 
  and identify which links the text EXPLICITLY makes 
  vs. which links require INFERENCE.

Chapter 12 will teach you the Cause-Effect Mapping technique — a systematic method for tracing how one event leads to another across multiple paragraphs, and for distinguishing between cause-effect relationships the text STATES versus those it merely IMPLIES.

CHAPTER 11: General inference (concept behind description)
CHAPTER 12: Specific inference (cause behind effect, effect of cause)

📌 Chapter 11 taught you to infer WHAT things mean. Chapter 12 teaches you to infer WHY things happen and WHAT results from them — the most practical form of inference for real-world texts about problems and consequences.
