QUESTION 28 — What is this program?

The question: dans cet article, on décrit...

This question asks you to identify the OVERALL NATURE of the program — what it IS and what it's FOR.

Step 1: Identify the program's core elements.

WHAT:    A point-based reward system in school canteens
WHERE:   Glasgow, Scotland (30 schools, 30,000 students)
WHO:     School students (adolescents)
HOW:     Healthy food choices → points → prizes
WHY:     To fight childhood obesity ("lutter contre l'obésité des jeunes")

Step 2: Identify the key distinctions.

The program is:

  • A METHOD (not a diet, not a ban, not a competition)
  • To PREVENT overweight (not to cure it, not to cause it)
  • Through INCENTIVES (not through force or prohibition)
  • In SCHOOLS (the institutional setting)

Step 3: Test each option.

The "Incentive vs. Imposition" Distinction (Option A)

This distinction is crucial for understanding institutional programs:

IMPOSITION (imposer):
  → Students MUST eat healthy food
  → Unhealthy food is BANNED or UNAVAILABLE
  → No choice involved — compliance is mandatory
  → Signal words: "imposé," "obligatoire," "interdit," "supprimé"

INCENTIVE (inciter):
  → Students are ENCOURAGED to eat healthy food
  → Unhealthy food is still AVAILABLE but unrewarded
  → Choice exists — but healthy choices are rewarded
  → Signal words: "encourager," "récompenser," "points," "gagner"

The text shows that unhealthy food is still available:

  • Hamburger: 4 points (low reward, but still available)
  • Fries: 0 points (no reward, but NOT banned)

The system STEERS behavior through rewards, not through prohibition.

🔑 Rule: When a text describes a system that REWARDS desired behavior (rather than BANNING undesired behavior), it's an INCENTIVE system, not an IMPOSITION. The key test: is the undesired option still available? If yes → incentive. If no → imposition.

The "Goal Reversal" Trap (Option D)

Option D commits the most extreme error possible — it REVERSES the program's goal:

TEXT:     "lutter CONTRE l'obésité"  = fight AGAINST obesity
OPTION D: "favorisant l'obésité"    = PROMOTING obesity

This isn't a subtle reversal — it's a 180° flip.

This trap catches speed-readers who see "obésité" in both the text and the option and assume they match, without reading the VERB that connects to it.

🔑 Rule: When a key noun (like "obésité") appears in both the text and an answer option, read the VERB attached to it. "Lutter contre l'obésité" (fight against) and "favoriser l'obésité" (promote) contain the same noun but express OPPOSITE actions.

The "Ambiguous Negation" Problem (Options C vs. D)

This question presents a fascinating linguistic challenge that deserves careful analysis:

OPTION C: "Tous les aliments ne rapportent pas de points"
          
READING 1 (total negation):
  "ALL foods DON'T earn points" = NO food earns points
  → FALSE (salads earn 15 points)
  
READING 2 (partial negation):  
  "NOT ALL foods earn points" = some foods don't earn points
  → TRUE (fries earn 0 points)

OPTION D: "Certains aliments ne rapportent pas de points"
  "CERTAIN foods don't earn points" = some foods earn no points
  → TRUE (fries earn 0 points) — UNAMBIGUOUS

Both C (under reading 2) and D express the same idea. But D is CLEARER and UNAMBIGUOUS. In a multiple-choice exam, when two options express similar ideas but one is ambiguous and the other is precise, the PRECISE one is correct.

🔑 Rule: When two options seem to express similar ideas, choose the UNAMBIGUOUS one. "Certains" (some/certain) is clearer than "tous...ne...pas" (which can mean "not all" or "none"). Clarity wins in multiple choice.

The "Sufficient vs. Necessary" Distinction — CRITICAL LOGIC LESSON

This is one of the most important logical distinctions in the entire exam:

SUFFICIENT CONDITION ("il suffit de"):
  "Eating 100 healthy menus is ENOUGH to win a player"
  = If you do THIS, you WILL get the prize
  = But there may be OTHER ways to get the prize too
  = This is ONE path, not the ONLY path

NECESSARY CONDITION ("il faut obligatoirement"):
  "You MUST eat 100 healthy menus to win a player"
  = This is the ONLY way to get the prize
  = No other method works
  = This is the REQUIRED path

THE TEXT SAYS: "il SUFFIT" → SUFFICIENT
OPTION C SAYS: "OBLIGATOIREMENT" → NECESSARY

SUFFICIENT ≠ NECESSARY

Why is Option A correct instead of C? Because A identifies the TRUE NECESSARY condition:

TRUE NECESSARY CONDITION: Accumulate 4,000 points (no matter how)
SUFFICIENT CONDITION:     Eat 100 healthy menus (one way to get 4,000 points)

A captures the NECESSARY condition → CORRECT
C presents the SUFFICIENT condition as NECESSARY → WRONG

🔑 Rule: "Il suffit de X" = X is ENOUGH (but not the only way). "Il faut obligatoirement X" = X is REQUIRED (the only way). When the text says "il suffit," reject options that say "obligatoirement." The real requirement is the UNDERLYING condition (points), not one specific method of achieving it.

The "Price Misinterpretation" Trap (Option D)

Option D misreads the function of a number:

TEXT:     "Le menu sain...pour 1.80 euros"
MEANING:  The healthy menu COSTS 1.80€ (its price)

OPTION D: "ajouter 1.80 euros chaque fois qu'on achète un plat"
MEANING:  ADD 1.80€ each time you buy a dish (a surcharge)

PRICE ≠ SURCHARGE
The 1.80€ is what you PAY for the meal.
It is NOT an extra fee added to another cost.

🔑 Rule: When numbers appear in a text, verify what each number REPRESENTS. A price (what something costs) is different from a surcharge (an extra fee), a reward (what you earn), or a threshold (what you need). Don't let numbers float free of their context.

The "Budget vs. Prize" Confusion (Option B)

Option B commits a numerical misinterpretation:

TEXT:     "dépenser plus de 60 000 euros pour ACHETER LES PRIX"
          = spend 60,000€ to BUY THE PRIZES (plural — many prizes)
          = 60,000€ = total BUDGET for all prizes combined

          = offer ONE PRIZE worth 60,000€
          = 60,000€ = value of a SINGLE reward

BUDGET for many prizes ≠ Value of one prize
"Les prix" (plural) ≠ "un prix" (singular)

Also note: "prix" in French has two meanings:

  • Prix = price (cost of something)
  • Prix = prize (reward, award)

In the text, "acheter les prix" = buy the prizes (rewards for students).
In Option B, "un prix de 60 000 euros" = a prize worth 60,000€ (one single reward).

3. Apply the "Sufficient vs. Necessary" test

Whenever you see "il suffit de" in the text:

"Il suffit de X pour obtenir Y"
= X is ENOUGH for Y (sufficient)
≠ X is the ONLY WAY to get Y (necessary)

TEST: Is there ANOTHER way to achieve Y?
  If YES → X is sufficient but not necessary
  If NO → X is both sufficient and necessary

In this text:
  "Il suffit d'ingurgiter 100 menus sains pour gagner un baladeur"
  Can you win a baladeur another way? YES (accumulate 4000 points through other foods)
  → 100 menus is SUFFICIENT but not NECESSARY
  → The NECESSARY condition is 4000 points (the threshold)

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Incentive ≠ Imposition" Confusion

What happens: You misidentify the program's METHOD — confusing voluntary incentives with mandatory requirements.

The key test:

Can students still choose unhealthy food?
  → YES: hamburger (4 points) and fries (0 points) are available
  → The system doesn't REMOVE these options
  → It simply makes them UNREWARDING

THEREFORE: This is an INCENTIVE system (rewards for good choices)
           NOT an IMPOSITION system (bans on bad choices)

How to detect incentive vs. imposition in texts:

FeatureIncentive systemImposition system
Bad option available?YES (but unrewarded)NO (banned/removed)
Good option rewarded?YES (points, prizes)Maybe (or just required)
Student's choice?FREE (choose any food)RESTRICTED (must eat X)
Signal wordsgagner, points, récompenserimposer, interdire, obliger

Pitfall 2: The "Sufficient-to-Necessary" Upgrade

What happens: You read "il suffit de" (it's enough to) and interpret it as "il faut" (you must).

Full mechanism:

TEXT:     "Il SUFFIT d'en ingurgiter 100"
          = It is SUFFICIENT to eat 100
          = 100 is ENOUGH (but not the only way)

OPTION C: "OBLIGATOIREMENT commander 100 menus sains"
          = You MUST order 100 healthy menus
          = 100 is REQUIRED (the only way)

UPGRADE: sufficient → necessary
         "enough" → "must"
         "one path" → "the only path"

The defense: When you see "il suffit de" in the text, immediately ask:

"Is there another way to achieve the same result?"

For this text:
  Goal: 4,000 points
  Path 1: 100 healthy menus × 40 = 4,000 ✅
  Path 2: 267 salads × 15 = 4,005 ✅
  Path 3: Mix of various healthy items ✅

  Multiple paths exist → "il suffit" is TRULY sufficient, not necessary
  → The ONLY necessary condition is 4,000 POINTS (the threshold)

Pitfall 3: The "Number Context Swap"

What happens: A number from the text appears in an answer option, but its CONTEXT has been changed.

Three examples in this text:

SWAP 1: Budget → Prize
  TEXT:    60,000€ = budget for ALL prizes over 12 months
  TRAP:   60,000€ = ONE prize for one student
  
SWAP 2: Price → Surcharge  
  TEXT:    1.80€ = cost of the healthy menu
  TRAP:   1.80€ = additional fee per purchase

SWAP 3: Future → Past
  TEXT:    "promet de dépenser" = will spend (future)
  TRAP:   "a déjà dépensé" = has spent (past)

The defense: For every number in an answer option, verify THREE things:

1. Is the NUMBER correct?        (Does the text mention this amount?)
2. Is the CONTEXT correct?       (Does it refer to the same thing?)
3. Is the TENSE correct?         (Same timeframe as the text?)

All three must match. If any one is wrong, the option is wrong.

Pitfall 4: The "Already Done vs. Will Do" Confusion

What happens: The text mentions something that EXISTS (website) and something PLANNED (budget). You mix up which is accomplished and which is planned.

ALREADY EXISTS:
  "Un site internet A MÊME ÉTÉ CRÉÉ" = a website HAS been created
  → Past tense → accomplished fact

PLANNED FOR FUTURE:
  "PROMET DE dépenser...pour les 12 PROCHAINS mois" = promises to spend for next 12 months
  → Future orientation → commitment, not yet done

The municipality's ASSESSMENT is present:
  "CONSIDÉRANT que c'est la meilleure solution" = considering it the best solution
  → Present participle → current judgment

Option D confuses the website (already created) with a future action. Option C confuses future spending with past spending. Only Option A correctly identifies the municipality's FUTURE commitment based on its CURRENT positive assessment.

🔑 Rule: In texts that describe ongoing programs, separate THREE temporal categories: (1) what has ALREADY BEEN DONE (past tense), (2) what IS CURRENTLY happening (present tense), (3) what is PLANNED (future/commitment language). Match each question to the correct temporal category.
