Texte 70 : Le cauchemar de Darwin

Une fois passé le cap de la première semaine d'exploitation, le bouche-à-oreille a fonctionné à plein. Déjà couvert de récompenses (quinze prix dans quinze festivals du monde entier), Le Cauchemar de Darwin a dépassé les deux cent mille entrées. Destin de rêve pour un documentaire au sujet peu engageant, réalisé par un cinéaste inconnu. (..) Un destin que ne suffisent pas à expliquer le titre accrocheur, l'affiche efficace ou la bonne couverture médiatique. Pour le distributeur, un autre élément explique ce succès : « Le film est arrivé à un moment où, référendum oblige, on s'interroge beaucoup sur la politique, sur les conséquences de la mondialisation et les rapports Nord-Sud ». Ainsi, pour soulager leur mauvaise conscience, certains croient avoir trouvé la solution : le boycott. Objet d'un commerce que le film assimile à du pillage, la perche du Nil ne passera plus par leur assiette.

Step 4: Decode the "Insufficient vs. Sufficient Explanation" Structure

This is the chapter's defining analytical pattern. The text explicitly rejects certain explanations before offering the real one:

REJECTED EXPLANATIONS (insufficient):
  ✗ Catchy title
  ✗ Effective poster
  ✗ Good media coverage
  → "ne suffisent PAS à expliquer"

ACCEPTED EXPLANATION (sufficient):
  ✓ Political context (referendum, globalization debate)
  ✓ Personal relevance (links Tanzania to European consumption)
  → "un autre élément explique ce succès"

The structure is:

STEP 1: List obvious explanations
STEP 2: Dismiss them as INSUFFICIENT ("ne suffisent pas")
STEP 3: Introduce the REAL explanation ("un autre élément")

This is a new pattern — the "insufficient vs. sufficient explanation" structure. It's different from all previous patterns because it explicitly RANKS explanations:

Previous PatternsThis Pattern

Before/After pivotInsufficient/Sufficient ranking

The text doesn't say word of mouth, awards, and media coverage played NO role. It says they're not enough to explain the success. The REAL explanation is the political context. This nuance will be critical for Q4.

Step 5: Understand the Causal Chain — From Film to Boycott

The text describes a cause-effect chain that connects a film to consumer behavior:

CAUSE CHAIN: Film → Context → Emotion → Action

STEP 1: Film depicts exploitation in Tanzania (Nile perch trade = "pillage")
STEP 2: Film arrives during political questioning (referendum, globalization)
STEP 3: Audience connects Tanzanian situation to their OWN consumption
STEP 4: Connection produces GUILT ("mauvaise conscience")
STEP 5: Guilt motivates ACTION (boycott of Nile perch)

Each step causes the next:
  Film content → resonance with context → personal connection →
  guilty conscience → boycott

The exam will ask about DIFFERENT LINKS in this chain. You must identify which link each question targets.

✅ WHAT SHOULD BE DONE

1. Detect the "Insufficient vs. Sufficient Explanation" Hierarchy

This is the chapter's defining analytical tool. When a text lists explanations, check whether they're presented as SUFFICIENT or INSUFFICIENT:

INSUFFICIENCY MARKERS:
  "ne suffisent PAS à expliquer"     = don't sufficiently explain
  "ce n'est pas seulement"           = it's not only
  "ne s'explique pas uniquement par" = isn't explained solely by
  
SUFFICIENCY MARKERS:
  "un AUTRE élément explique"        = another element explains
  "c'est SURTOUT parce que"          = it's mainly because
  "la VRAIE raison est"              = the real reason is
  "s'explique AVANT TOUT par"        = is explained above all by

When the question uses "surtout" (mainly), you need the SUFFICIENT explanation, not the insufficient ones.

HIERARCHY IN THIS TEXT:
  INSUFFICIENT: Title + poster + media coverage (marketing)
  SUFFICIENT:   Political context (referendum, globalization)
  
  Q4 asks for "surtout" → SUFFICIENT explanation → political context

2. Distinguish Three Levels of Analysis for Cultural Products

When a text discusses a film, book, or cultural product, track THREE distinct levels:

LEVEL 1 — THE PRODUCT:
  What is it? What does it show/say?
  (Documentary about Nile perch trade in Tanzania)

LEVEL 2 — THE CONTEXT:
  When/where does it appear? What political/social conditions exist?
  (Referendum period, globalization debate)

LEVEL 3 — THE IMPACT:
  How does the audience react? What behavior changes?
  (Guilty conscience → boycott of Nile perch)

Questions may target ANY level.
Don't answer a Level 2 question with Level 1 information.

3. Separate Mechanism from Cause in Success Stories

This new distinction applies to any text analyzing why something succeeded:

MECHANISM (HOW success spreads):
  Word of mouth, media coverage, awards, social media
  → CHANNELS through which information travels
  → Answer to: "How did people LEARN about it?"

CAUSE (WHY something resonates):
  Political context, personal relevance, timing, cultural moment
  → REASONS the product strikes a chord with audiences
  → Answer to: "Why did people CARE about it?"

Both contribute to success.
But they answer DIFFERENT questions.
"S'explique surtout par" asks for the CAUSE, not the MECHANISM.

4. Track Scope Precisely in Audience Reactions

When a text describes how audiences respond, note the EXACT scope of their action:

NARROW SCOPE (text):   Boycott ONE product (Nile perch)
MEDIUM SCOPE:          Boycott products from one country
WIDE SCOPE:            Join anti-globalization movements
WIDEST SCOPE:          Change entire consumption philosophy

The text describes NARROW scope.
Options B and C inflate the scope to MEDIUM and WIDE.
Always match the option's scope to the text's scope.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Confuse the Film's Subject with the Article's Subject

This was the core trap in Q1 (Option C):

THE FILM'S SUBJECT:    Globalization, exploitation, Nile perch trade
THE ARTICLE'S SUBJECT: Why the film succeeded commercially

Option C describes the FILM'S subject.
The question asks about the ARTICLE'S subject.

This "layer confusion" recurs whenever a text discusses a cultural product. The text and the product it discusses are TWO DIFFERENT things with TWO DIFFERENT subjects.

Rule: When a question asks about "cet article" (this article), answer about the ARTICLE's purpose, not about the FILM's content.

2. Do NOT Select Explanations the Text Explicitly Classifies as Insufficient

Option B in Q4 describes "bonne campagne publicitaire" (good advertising campaign). The text mentions effective marketing elements BUT classifies them as "ne suffisent pas à expliquer." When the text explicitly RANKS an explanation as insufficient, selecting it for a question about the MAIN explanation is wrong.

TEXT SAYS:  "ne suffisent PAS à expliquer"
QUESTION:  "s'explique SURTOUT par"
OPTION B:  Describes exactly what the text says ISN'T enough

The text told you this isn't the answer.
Trust the text's own hierarchy.

3. Do NOT Inflate the Scope of Audience Reactions

Trap B in Q3 expands "boycott Nile perch" to "boycott ALL Tanzanian products." This is scope inflation:

TEXT:     Boycott ONE specific product (perche du Nil)
TRAP B:   Boycott ALL products from one country (ensemble des produits de Tanzanie)

ONE product ≠ ALL products
The boycott is targeted, not general.

Scope inflation is a common trap in texts about consumer behavior, activism, or social movements. Always check whether the option's scope matches the text's scope.

4. Do NOT Confuse Reporting with Advocating

The article REPORTS that some viewers boycott Nile perch. The article does NOT ADVOCATE for boycott. This distinction appeared in Chapter 10 (→ Project 10: describing rescue ≠ manifesto for rescue) and is now reinforced:

REPORTING:    "certains croient avoir trouvé la solution : le boycott"
              = The article DESCRIBES what some viewers do
              
ADVOCATING:   "Boycottez la perche du Nil !"
              = The article URGES readers to act
              
The text REPORTS. It doesn't ADVOCATE.
Option D in Q1 ("dénonce les risques") implies advocacy.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Word of Mouth = Main Reason" Confusion

Word of mouth is mentioned prominently at the text's opening. Candidates remember it vividly because it appears first and describes the success dramatically ("a fonctionné à plein"). But the text then EXPLICITLY says marketing factors (including word of mouth) are INSUFFICIENT:

CANDIDATE'S MEMORY:    "Word of mouth worked brilliantly!" → Must be the reason!
TEXT'S HIERARCHY:       "Title, poster, media DON'T sufficiently explain"
                       → Word of mouth is a MECHANISM, not the CAUSE

Defense: Don't let FIRST IMPRESSIONS override the text's explicit hierarchy. The text introduces success THEN dismisses obvious explanations THEN provides the real one. Follow the STRUCTURE, not your memory of the opening.

Pitfall 2: The "Cauchemar → Sommeil" Title Wordplay

Option D in Q2 exploits the association between "cauchemar" (nightmare) and "sommeil" (sleep). Nightmares happen during sleep, so "sommeil de plomb" (deep sleep) seems connected to the title.

But "Le Cauchemar de Darwin" uses "cauchemar" METAPHORICALLY — it describes a nightmarish SITUATION (exploitation in Tanzania), not literal sleep. The film doesn't cause sleep; it causes awareness and guilt.

METAPHORICAL: "Cauchemar" = nightmarish situation → awareness, guilt
LITERAL:      "Cauchemar" = bad dream during sleep → "sommeil de plomb"

The title is metaphorical. The trap takes it literally.

This is the same pattern as Chapter 2's "croisière" trap, Chapter 12's "bijoux" trap, and now "cauchemar."

Pitfall 3: The "European Daily Life" Direction Reversal

Option D in Q4 says "prise de conscience du quotidien des Européens" (awareness of Europeans' daily life). The text says the film connects "la situation tanzanienne et le quotidien des Européens." But the DIRECTION of awareness matters:

TEXT'S DIRECTION:     Europeans become aware of their IMPACT ON TANZANIA
                      (their consumption contributes to exploitation)

OPTION D'S DIRECTION: Europeans become aware of their OWN DAILY LIFE
                      (they learn about themselves)

The film doesn't make Europeans discover their own daily life
(they already know it).
It makes them see their daily life's CONSEQUENCES for others.

This is a subtle direction reversal — the option describes awareness flowing the wrong way.

Pitfall 4: The "Content Feature as Success Factor" Swap

The film's ability to connect Tanzania to European consumption is a CONTENT FEATURE — something the film DOES. The political context is a SUCCESS FACTOR — something EXTERNAL that made audiences receptive.

CONTENT FEATURE:  The film connects Tanzania to Europe (what the film does)
SUCCESS FACTOR:   Political questioning period (what the audience brings)

The film's connection feature would exist regardless of timing.
The political context made audiences RECEPTIVE to that connection.

Q4 asks for the success FACTOR (external condition),
not the content FEATURE (internal quality).
