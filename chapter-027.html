CHAPTER 1: LES TROUBLES DU SOMMEIL

Reading a Factual Medical Text — Learning to Extract Direct Facts

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Texte 51 : Les troubles du sommeil

Le somnambulisme est un trouble très fréquent chez les enfants. Il se représente comme un passage difficile du sommeil profond à l'éveil. C'est pourquoi, le matin, le somnambule a tout oublié. Si on le réveille, il ne risque pas sa vie mais sera très mal à l'aise et désorienté. Il faut donc éviter de le réveiller, mais plutôt l'accompagner doucement jusqu'à son lit pour qu'il finisse sa nuit. Si ce trouble est sans danger, il faut cependant faire en sorte de protéger de tout risque de chute ou de choc la personne, qui souffre de ce dysfonctionnement.

La somniloquie est un autre trouble du sommeil. On parle parfois au moment de s'endormir, dans un demi-sommeil et il arrive même qu'on réponde à des questions simples. Parfois aussi, on marmonne lorsque l'on rêve mais à ce stade, ce que l'on raconte est bien souvent incompréhensible.

Enfin, de nombreux enfants ronflent la nuit : leur sommeil est agité, ils transpirent à grosses gouttes et se réveillent fréquemment, ce qui a pour conséquence une fatigue anormale durant la journée. La responsabilité en revient le plus souvent à des obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air, par exemple les amygdales qu'on peut alors faire enlever.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH THIS TEXT

Step 1: Recognize the Text Type

Before reading a single question, take 30 seconds to understand what kind of text this is. This matters enormously because the text type tells you where the answers will hide.

This text is a factual/informational medical article. Here's how you recognize it:

SignalWhat You See
StructureThree clearly separated paragraphs, each covering one disorder
VocabularyScientific terms: somnambulisme, somniloquie, dysfonctionnement
ToneNeutral, explanatory, no personal opinion
Source"D'après Genius" — a knowledge/science publication
PurposeTo inform and educate, not to persuade or entertain

Why this matters: In a factual text, answers are almost always restated versions of sentences in the text. The exam doesn't ask you to imagine or invent — it asks you to find and match.

Step 2: Map the Text Architecture

This is the single most important habit you will build across all 25 chapters. Before touching any question, draw a mental map of the text:

PARAGRAPH 1 → Somnambulisme (sleepwalking)
    - Common in children
    - Forgets everything by morning
    - NOT life-threatening
    - BUT risk of falls/injuries
    - Action: guide back to bed, don't wake up

PARAGRAPH 2 → Somniloquie (sleep-talking)
    - Talking while falling asleep
    - Can answer simple questions
    - Mumbling during dreams = incomprehensible

PARAGRAPH 3 → Ronflement (snoring)
    - Agitated sleep, sweating, frequent waking
    - Consequence: abnormal daytime fatigue
    - Cause: obstacles blocking air circulation (e.g., tonsils)
    - Solution: remove tonsils

This map takes 60 seconds to build and saves you minutes of re-reading later. Each question will point you to one specific zone of this map.

Step 3: Understand the One-Paragraph-Per-Question Pattern

Notice something critical: there are 3 questions and 3 paragraphs. This is not a coincidence. In the vast majority of TCF/TEF-style factual texts:

  • Question 1 → targets Paragraph 1
  • Question 2 → targets Paragraph 2
  • Question 3 → targets Paragraph 3

This means you don't need to search the entire text for each answer. You need to search the correct zone.

🧩 HOW TO SOLVE EACH QUESTION

QUESTION 14: Le somnambule est à surveiller car les crises peuvent...

Step 1: Locate the zone

The question is about "somnambule" → Paragraph 1. Go there directly.

Step 2: Find the relevant sentence

The question asks WHY the sleepwalker must be watched ("est à surveiller"). Look for the sentence about danger or risk:

"il faut cependant faire en sorte de protéger de tout risque de chute ou de choc la personne"

The key words are chute (fall) and choc (impact/bump). These are physical dangers — they are accidents.

Step 3: Test each option against the text

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — Mettre sa vie en jeuThe text explicitly says "il ne risque pas sa vie". This is a direct contradiction.❌ ELIMINATED
B — Causer des accidentsFalls and bumps ARE accidents. "Chute" and "choc" are forms of accidents.✅ MATCH
C — Provoquer de l'amnistieTRAP WORD. "Amnistie" means amnesty (legal pardon). The text mentions forgetting ("a tout oublié"), which would be "amnésie." But even "amnésie" wouldn't be the answer because forgetting is a symptom, not a danger to surveil.❌ WRONG WORD + WRONG LOGIC
D — Perturber durablement le sommeilThe text never says the disorder permanently disrupts sleep.❌ NOT IN TEXT

✅ ANSWER: B — Causer des accidents

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Located the right paragraph
  2. Found the sentence about risk
  3. Translated "chute/choc" → "accidents" (paraphrase)
  4. Eliminated A by direct contradiction
  5. Eliminated C by vocabulary trap detection
  6. Eliminated D by absence from text

QUESTION 15: Pendant son sommeil, une personne atteinte de somniloquie...

Step 1: Locate the zone

"Somniloquie" → Paragraph 2.

Step 2: Find the relevant sentence

The question asks what happens during sleep. The paragraph describes two phases:

Phase 1 (falling asleep): "On parle parfois au moment de s'endormir... il arrive même qu'on réponde à des questions simples"

Phase 2 (dreaming): "on marmonne lorsque l'on rêve mais à ce stade, ce que l'on raconte est bien souvent incompréhensible"

Now, the question specifies "pendant son sommeil" — this points more to Phase 2 (actually asleep and dreaming), not Phase 1 (falling asleep). During actual sleep, the person mumbles incomprehensibly.

Step 3: Test each option

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — A une conversation soutenue"Conversation soutenue" means a sustained, coherent conversation. The text says the opposite: speech is incompréhensible. Also, only simple questions get answered, and only while falling asleep, not during deep sleep.❌ EXAGGERATION
B — Raconte ses rêves tout hautsThe text says people mumble while dreaming, but it never says they narrate their dreams. This is an interpretation trap — adding meaning that isn't there.❌ OVER-INTERPRETATION
C — Parle de façon confuse"Incompréhensible" mumbling = confused speech. "Confuse" is a perfect paraphrase of "incompréhensible" + "marmonne."✅ MATCH
D — Chante à voix basseSinging is never mentioned anywhere in the text.❌ INVENTED

✅ ANSWER: C — Parle de façon confuse

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Located Paragraph 2
  2. Distinguished between the two phases described
  3. Matched "pendant son sommeil" to the dreaming phase
  4. Recognized "parle de façon confuse" as a paraphrase of "marmonne... incompréhensible"
  5. Rejected B for adding information not in the text

QUESTION 16: La fatigue des ronfleurs s'explique par...

Step 1: Locate the zone

"Ronfleurs" (snorers) + "fatigue" → Paragraph 3.

Step 2: Find the relevant sentence

The question asks for the explanation of fatigue. Follow the causal chain in the paragraph:

CAUSE:    obstacles gênent la circulation de l'air (e.g., amygdales)
    ↓
EFFECT 1: sommeil agité, transpirent, se réveillent fréquemment
    ↓
EFFECT 2: fatigue anormale durant la journée

The ROOT CAUSE is obstacles blocking air circulation = difficulty breathing.

Step 3: Test each option

OptionTest Against TextVerdict
A — Des nuits trop longuesNever mentioned. The text talks about disrupted sleep, not long sleep.❌ NOT IN TEXT
B — Leur difficulté à respirer"Obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air" = difficulty breathing. This is the root cause of everything described.✅ MATCH
C — Des accès fréquents de fièvre"Transpirent à grosses gouttes" (sweating heavily) is NOT fever. TRAP: The test wants you to confuse sweating with fever. Sweating is a symptom of agitated sleep, not illness.❌ MISINTERPRETATION TRAP
D — Le manque d'aération de la pièceMOST DANGEROUS TRAP IN THIS TEXT. "Circulation de l'air" in the text refers to air flowing through the body's airways (nose, throat), NOT air in the room. "Aération de la pièce" means room ventilation. The text is talking about anatomy, not architecture.❌ CONTEXT CONFUSION TRAP

✅ ANSWER: B — Leur difficulté à respirer

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Located Paragraph 3
  2. Traced the causal chain from root cause to final effect
  3. Recognized "difficulté à respirer" as a paraphrase of "obstacles qui gênent la circulation de l'air"
  4. Defeated Trap C (sweating ≠ fever)
  5. Defeated Trap D (air in airways ≠ air in the room)

✅ WHAT SHOULD BE DONE (Best Practices)

1. Always Map Before You Answer

Never jump to the questions first. Spend 60–90 seconds building a mental (or physical) map of the text. For this text, the map was:

P1 = Somnambulisme → risk of falls → guide to bed
P2 = Somniloquie → mumbles → incomprehensible
P3 = Ronflement → air blocked → fatigue

This map is your GPS. Every question becomes a simple lookup.

2. Identify the Question's "Target Word"

Every question contains a target word that points you to the right zone:

QuestionTarget WordPoints To
Q14"somnambule"Paragraph 1
Q15"somniloquie"Paragraph 2
Q16"ronfleurs" + "fatigue"Paragraph 3

Train yourself to circle or underline the target word instantly.

3. Read the Answer Choices BEFORE Re-reading the Text

Once you know which paragraph to look at, read all four options first. This gives your brain a filter: you now know exactly what to look for when you re-read the paragraph. You're not reading blindly — you're reading with a mission.

4. Use the Text's Own Words as Your Anchor

The correct answer is almost always a paraphrase of the text's exact words:

Text SaysCorrect Answer Says
risque de chute ou de choccauser des accidents
marmonne... incompréhensibleparle de façon confuse
obstacles gênent la circulation de l'airdifficulté à respirer

Train your brain to recognize that the exam never copies the text exactly. It always rephrases. Your job is to match the paraphrase to the original.

5. Eliminate Before You Choose

Never choose an answer because it "sounds right." Instead, eliminate the three wrong answers because they are provably wrong. This is safer and more reliable:

  • Contradicted by text → Eliminate (like option A in Q14: "ne risque pas sa vie")
  • Not mentioned in text → Eliminate (like option D in Q15: singing)
  • Trap word/misinterpretation → Eliminate (like option C in Q14: amnistie ≠ amnésie)
  • Context confusion → Eliminate (like option D in Q16: room air ≠ body air)

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Answer from General Knowledge

You might know that sleepwalking can be dangerous. You might think "mettre sa vie en jeu" is correct because you've heard of sleepwalkers falling from balconies. IGNORE your general knowledge. The exam tests whether you understood this specific text, not whether you know about sleep disorders. This text explicitly says "il ne risque pas sa vie." The text is your only authority.

2. Do NOT Confuse Similar-Sounding Words

This is one of the exam's favorite weapons:

Trap Word in OptionsLooks LikeActually Means
AmnistieAmnésie (memory loss)Amnesty (legal pardon)
Aération de la pièceCirculation de l'airRoom ventilation

These are designed to catch candidates who read quickly and match sounds instead of meanings. Read every word in every option carefully.

3. Do NOT Pick the First Option That Seems Plausible

Option A in Q14 ("mettre sa vie en jeu") sounds dramatic and plausible for sleepwalking. Many candidates would pick it immediately without checking the text. But the text directly contradicts it. Always verify against the text before committing.

4. Do NOT Add Information That Isn't There

Option B in Q15 says the person "raconte ses rêves." The text says people mumble while dreaming, but it never says they describe their dreams. There's a difference between:

  • Mumbling during a dream (what the text says)
  • Narrating a dream out loud (what option B claims)

If the text doesn't say it, the answer is wrong — even if it seems like a reasonable assumption.

5. Do NOT Spend Equal Time on Every Question

In a factual text like this one, most questions are locatable in under 30 seconds once you have your mental map. If you find yourself spending more than 90 seconds on a single question, you've probably lost your way. Move on and come back.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Sounds Medical, Must Be Right" Trap

When reading a medical/scientific text, candidates tend to gravitate toward the most "scientific-sounding" answer. In Q14, "mettre sa vie en jeu" sounds more dramatic and medical than "causer des accidents." But drama ≠ accuracy. The correct answer is often the simpler, less dramatic paraphrase.

How to avoid it: Always return to the text. The text is not dramatic here — it explicitly downplays the danger ("sans danger," "ne risque pas sa vie") while noting a specific, practical risk (falls and bumps).

Pitfall 2: The "Partial Match" Trap

Option D in Q16 ("manque d'aération de la pièce") contains the word "air," which also appears in the text ("circulation de l'air"). Candidates who scan quickly will see "air" in both places and think they've found a match.

How to avoid it: Match meanings, not individual words. The text's "circulation de l'air" refers to airways in the body. The option's "aération de la pièce" refers to room ventilation. Same word, completely different context.

Pitfall 3: The "Symptom vs. Cause" Confusion

In Q16, option C mentions "accès de fièvre" (fever). The text mentions "transpirent à grosses gouttes" (sweating). Sweating can be associated with fever, so candidates might link them.

How to avoid it: The text provides its own causal chain. Sweating is listed as a symptom of agitated sleep, not as a cause. And the text never mentions fever. Don't create medical connections that the text doesn't make.

Pitfall 4: The "I Already Know This" Override

Many people have personal experience with sleep disorders. They might think: "My cousin sleepwalks and it's really dangerous — he almost fell down the stairs." This personal knowledge can override what the text actually says.

How to avoid it: Treat every text as if you're encountering the topic for the first time. Your only source of truth is the text in front of you. Period.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

What You Learned in This Chapter

You learned the four foundational skills of Compréhension Écrite:

SKILL 1: MAP the text before answering (structure → zones → lookup)
SKILL 2: LOCATE the answer zone using the question's target word
SKILL 3: MATCH the text's words to the answer's paraphrase
SKILL 4: ELIMINATE wrong answers using four tests:
         → Contradicted by text?
         → Not mentioned in text?
         → Trap word (similar sound, different meaning)?
         → Context confusion (same word, different meaning)?

These four skills are the bedrock of everything that follows. Every single chapter from here forward will use them. The difference is that texts will get more complex, answers will get more subtle, and traps will get more sophisticated. But the process never changes.

The Formula You Will Use Forever

READ TEXT → MAP STRUCTURE → READ QUESTION → IDENTIFY TARGET WORD 
→ GO TO ZONE → READ OPTIONS → ELIMINATE 3 → CONFIRM 1
