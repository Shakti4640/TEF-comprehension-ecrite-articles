This text is about MANIPULATION — how cigarette companies deliberately target young people. It's built around three DIFFERENT STRATEGIES, each described in a different paragraph, and each attributed to a different SOURCE.

The Core Challenge: MULTIPLE SOURCES, ONE ARTICLE

For the first time, you're reading a text that quotes multiple external sources. The journalist doesn't just give their opinion — they bring in voices from different institutions:

SOURCE 1: A cigarette company executive (Paragraph 1)
  → WHAT she reveals: "When you gain a very young adult smoker, 
     their consumption increases with age"
  → WHY this matters: Explains the MOTIVATION for targeting youth

SOURCE 2: INPES — National health prevention institute (Paragraph 2)
  → WHAT they note: Too many French films show cigarette brands
  → WHAT they suspect: Either ignorance of the law OR deliberate product placement

SOURCE 3: Health Affairs / Harvard University (Paragraph 3)
  → WHAT they report: Latest strategy = flavored cigarettes
  → EXAMPLES: Cherry, chocolate, vanilla

Each question may target a SPECIFIC source's statement. If Q15 asks "L'INPES constate..." — you must ONLY use what INPES says, not what Harvard says or what the journalist adds.

This is your Multi-Stakeholder reading from Chapter 5, now applied to institutional sources instead of groups of people.

The New Skill: STRATEGY-METHOD-TARGET Analysis

Unlike Chapter 6's natural market dynamics, this text describes DELIBERATE strategies. Each strategy has three components:

STRATEGY = The overall approach (what they're trying to achieve)
METHOD = The specific technique used (how they do it)
TARGET = Who they're aiming at (whom they want to reach)

Let's map ALL strategies in this text:

STRATEGY 1 (Paragraph 1): Package design
├── METHOD: "travail sur la présentation des paquets"
├── TARGET: 15-25 year olds
└── SOURCE: Journalist's description

STRATEGY 2 (Paragraph 1-2): Cinema product placement
├── METHOD: "images glissées dans les salles obscures"
│           = images slipped into movie theaters
├── TARGET: Young moviegoers
└── SOURCE: Journalist + INPES analysis

STRATEGY 3 (Paragraph 3): Flavored cigarettes
├── METHOD: "offrant des saveurs plaisantes" (pleasant flavors)
│           = cherry, chocolate, vanilla
├── TARGET: "les plus jeunes" (the youngest)
└── SOURCE: Health Affairs / Harvard

All three strategies share ONE OVERARCHING GOAL: attract young people to smoking.

The New Skill: "SUBJECT OF THIS ARTICLE" Questions

You encountered "Best Title" in Chapter 6. "Subject of this article" is its cousin:

"BEST TITLE" (Ch.6):    Choose a title → must capture main argument + tone + perspective
"SUBJECT OF" (Ch.7):    Identify the subject → must capture what the WHOLE article is ABOUT

The difference is subtle but real:

  • A TITLE can be catchy and slightly metaphorical
  • A SUBJECT statement must be PRECISE and COMPREHENSIVE

The strategy is similar: summarize the WHOLE article first, then find the option that matches.

QUESTION 15: L'INPES constate...

Step 1: Identify the question type — SOURCE-SPECIFIC.

"L'INPES constate..." = "INPES observes that..." → This asks ONLY about what INPES says. Not what the journalist says, not what Harvard reports, not what the industry executive reveals.

This is your first SOURCE-SPECIFIC question. You must isolate ONE source's contribution and ignore everything else.

Step 2: Locate EXACTLY what INPES says.

Paragraph 2:

"L'institut national de prévention et d'éducation pour la santé (INPES) note que « le nombre de films français laissant apparaître une marque de tabac semble indiquer au mieux une méconnaissance de la loi, et au pire une action voulue de placement du produit »."

Let's parse this sentence with surgical precision:

INPES elementMeaning
"note que"observes/notes that (neutral, factual verb)
"le nombre de films français"the number of French films
"laissant apparaître une marque de tabac"that show/display a tobacco brand
"semble indiquer"seems to indicate (cautious language)
"au mieux"at best (least serious interpretation)
"une méconnaissance de la loi"ignorance/unawareness of the law
"au pire"at worst (most serious interpretation)
"une action voulue de placement du produit"a deliberate act of product placement

INPES's statement has THREE parts:

OBSERVATION:     Many French films show tobacco brands
INTERPRETATION 1 (best case): Filmmakers don't know the law
INTERPRETATION 2 (worst case): It's deliberate product placement

INPES is saying: too many films show cigarette brands, and this is either ignorance or intentional.

Step 3: Check each option.

Option A — "qu'il devrait y avoir plus de films sur les méfaits du tabac"

"Plus de films sur les méfaits du tabac" = more films about the harmful effects of tobacco.

INPES says NOTHING about wanting more anti-tobacco films. INPES talks about films that SHOW tobacco brands (pro-tobacco presence), not about films that WARN AGAINST tobacco. This completely inverts the direction.

❌ ELIMINATED — INPES discusses tobacco PRESENCE in films, not anti-tobacco AWARENESS films

This is a TOPIC INVERSION TRAP — the answer takes the correct domain (films + tobacco) but REVERSES the direction:

TEXT:   Films SHOW tobacco brands (problem: pro-tobacco presence)
TRAP:   Films ABOUT tobacco's harms (solution: anti-tobacco education)
→ Same domain (films + tobacco), opposite direction (showing brands vs. warning about harms)

Option B — "qu'il y a trop de films où l'on peut apercevoir des marques de cigarettes"

Let's verify each element:

  • "trop de films" ≈ "le nombre de films" — INPES mentions the NUMBER of films, and their tone ("semble indiquer au mieux une méconnaissance de la loi") implies this number is PROBLEMATIC. "Trop" (too many) captures INPES's critical stance.

  • "où l'on peut apercevoir" ≈ "laissant apparaître" — both mean "where one can see/notice." Perfect paraphrase.

  • "des marques de cigarettes" ≈ "une marque de tabac" — "cigarettes" and "tabac" are interchangeable in this context. "Marques" (plural) vs. "marque" (singular) is a minor difference — INPES uses the singular generically.

✅ CORRECT — Accurate paraphrase of INPES's core observation

But wait — does this capture EVERYTHING INPES says? INPES also interprets the observation ("au mieux méconnaissance... au pire action voulue"). Option B only captures the OBSERVATION, not the interpretation.

Is this a problem? No — because the question asks "L'INPES constate" (INPES observes/notes). "Constater" means to observe, to note a fact. The OBSERVATION is that too many films show cigarette brands. The interpretation ("au mieux... au pire") is INPES's ANALYSIS of the observation, which goes beyond what "constate" asks for.

So Option B correctly captures what INPES OBSERVES (the fact), even though it doesn't include what INPES INTERPRETS (the analysis). The verb "constate" limits the scope to observation.

Let me still check Options C and D to be thorough.

Option C — "qu'il y a trop d'acteurs connus qui font de la publicité pour les cigarettes"

"Trop d'acteurs connus qui font de la publicité" = too many famous actors who advertise cigarettes.

INPES says NOTHING about actors doing advertising. INPES talks about FILMS showing tobacco BRANDS — not actors personally promoting cigarettes. There's a crucial difference:

INPES says:     Films SHOW brands (passive: brands appear in films)
Option C says:  Actors DO advertising (active: actors deliberately promote)

INPES doesn't blame actors — they blame the SYSTEM (either ignorance of law or deliberate product placement). The subject of INPES's critique is the NUMBER OF FILMS, not individual actors.

❌ ELIMINATED — INPES discusses brand presence in films, not actors doing advertising

Option D — "qu'il devrait y avoir davantage d'information au sujet de la loi anti-tabac"

"Davantage d'information sur la loi anti-tabac" = more information about the anti-tobacco law.

INPES mentions "méconnaissance de la loi" (ignorance of the law) as the BEST-CASE interpretation. Does this mean INPES wants more information about the law?

This is TEMPTING because there's a logical connection: if filmmakers don't know the law, maybe they need more information. But INPES doesn't SAY this. INPES OBSERVES the problem and INTERPRETS it — they don't PRESCRIBE a solution.

INPES SAYS:         "Seems to indicate ignorance of the law" (observation/interpretation)
INPES DOES NOT SAY: "There should be more information about the law" (recommendation)

The question asks what INPES "constate" (observes), not what INPES "recommande" (recommends). Drawing a recommendation from an observation is going BEYOND what the text states.

❌ ELIMINATED — Extrapolates a RECOMMENDATION from an OBSERVATION; INPES observes, doesn't prescribe

This is an OBSERVATION-TO-RECOMMENDATION TRAP — a new and subtle trap. The text states a FACT or OBSERVATION, and the answer transforms it into a RECOMMENDATION or WISH. The jump from "they seem to not know the law" to "there should be more information about the law" is a logical inference, but it's NOT what INPES stated.

WHAT INPES STATES:  "The number of films showing brands indicates 
                     ignorance of the law at best"
                                ↓

                                ↓
BUT THE QUESTION ASKS: What does INPES OBSERVE ("constate")?
                                ↓
NOT what should be done, but what IS observed

New Strategy: Verb-Level Matching in Source Questions

The verb in the question tells you WHAT LEVEL of information to look for:

"L'INPES CONSTATE..."    → observation level (what they SEE/NOTE)
"L'INPES RECOMMANDE..."  → prescription level (what they ADVISE)
"L'INPES DÉNONCE..."     → criticism level (what they CONDEMN)
"L'INPES EXPLIQUE..."    → analysis level (how they INTERPRET)

If the question says "constate" (observes), give the OBSERVATION, not the recommendation. If it says "recommande," give the recommendation, not the observation.

This is a refinement of Chapter 5's P.A.R. Framework — now applied to SOURCE statements rather than project descriptions.

Count how many voices speak in the text. For each, note:

  • WHO they are (institution, person, role)
  • WHAT they say (direct quote or paraphrased statement)
  • WHERE in the text (which paragraph)

DO #2: When a question names a source, ISOLATE that source

"L'INPES constate..." → Go ONLY to Paragraph 2. Ignore Paragraphs 1 and 3 entirely. What Harvard says is irrelevant. What the journalist adds is irrelevant. Only INPES's words matter.

DO #3: Match the question's VERB to the right level of response

"constate" → give what they OBSERVED (fact)
"recommande" → give what they SUGGESTED (solution)
"critique" → give what they OPPOSED (objection)

Don't give a recommendation when asked for an observation.
Don't give an observation when asked for a criticism.

DO #4: For "subject of this article" questions, check ALL paragraphs

The subject must encompass the WHOLE article. If an option only covers one paragraph, it's too narrow. Your summary should account for every paragraph's contribution.

DO #5: Learn common French idiomatic expressions

This text contains two expressions that are ESSENTIAL to understand:

"salles obscures"    = movie theaters (NOT dark rooms)
"font un malheur"    = are wildly popular (NOT cause a disaster)

Misunderstanding either of these changes the meaning of the text completely. Build an idiom bank alongside your synonym bank.

❌ WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

DON'T #1: Confuse the SUBJECT of an article with a SOURCE cited in it

WRONG: "The article is about a Harvard study on flavored cigarettes"
RIGHT: "The article is about strategies used to target young smokers; 
        Harvard is ONE of several sources cited"

A cited source ≠ the article's subject.
The article USES the study; it's not ABOUT the study.

DON'T #2: Extrapolate RECOMMENDATIONS from OBSERVATIONS

INPES OBSERVES:     "Filmmakers seem to not know the law"
YOU MIGHT INFER:    "So INPES wants more information about the law"
BUT INPES DIDN'T SAY THIS.

The question asks what INPES "constate" (observes), not what 
INPES would recommend. Stay at the observation level.

DON'T #3: Merge different strategies into one

TEXT has THREE separate strategies:
  1. Package design (P1)
  2. Cinema placement (P1-2)
  3. Flavored cigarettes (P3)

WRONG: Mixing elements from Strategy 1 (packaging) into a question 
       about Strategy 3 (flavors)
RIGHT: Each strategy has its own paragraph and its own characteristics. 
       Keep them separate.

DON'T #4: Misread idiomatic expressions

"Salles obscures" ≠ dark rooms → = movie theaters
"Font un malheur" ≠ cause disaster → = are wildly popular
"Au mieux... au pire" ≠ good and bad → = best case... worst case

If you don't know an expression, use CONTEXT to guess:

"images glissées dans les salles obscures"
→ "images" suggests visual content
→ "glissées" (slipped in) suggests subtly inserted
→ Where do you subtly insert images? In films/movies
→ "salles obscures" must mean movie theaters

DON'T #5: Assume a word means the same thing in different contexts

From Chapter 1's Word-Match Trap, now at a higher level:

"magazine" in the text:   Health Affairs (a scientific journal)
"magazine" in Option B:   A consumer magazine given with cigarettes
→ Same word, completely different referents

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: The Problem-Cause Inversion Trap (NEW — Core concept of Chapter 7)

MECHANISM:  The answer describes the EFFECT/PROBLEM when the article 
            is about the CAUSE/STRATEGY
EXAMPLE:    Article: How companies TARGET youth (cause/strategy)
            Trap: "The drama of youth tobacco consumption" (effect/problem)
DEFENSE:    Ask: "Is the article about WHAT happens or WHY it happens?"
            If WHY → the subject is the cause/strategy, not the effect/problem

PITFALL 2: The Source-as-Subject Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  A source CITED in the article is presented as the article's SUBJECT
EXAMPLE:    Harvard's study is CITED as evidence → 
            Trap: "The article is about Harvard's study"
            Reality: The article is about industry strategies; Harvard is one source
DEFENSE:    Sources are EVIDENCE, not subjects. 
            The subject is the ARGUMENT the sources support.

PITFALL 3: The Observation-to-Recommendation Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  An observed fact is transformed into a prescription/wish
EXAMPLE:    INPES observes: "filmmakers seem unaware of the law"
            Trap: "INPES says there should be more information about the law"
            Reality: INPES OBSERVED a problem; it didn't PRESCRIBE a solution
DEFENSE:    Match the question's verb level (constate = observation only)

PITFALL 4: The Strategy Conflation Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  Elements from different strategies are mixed into one answer
EXAMPLE:    Q asks about latest strategy (flavors, P3)
            Trap mentions "new shapes and colors" (packaging, P1)
DEFENSE:    Keep strategies SEPARATE. Use temporal/ordinal markers 
            ("dernière stratégie en date") to identify which one is targeted.

PITFALL 5: The Component-as-Accessory Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  An ingredient INSIDE a product is presented as a separate item 
            given WITH the product
EXAMPLE:    Text: Flavors IN cigarettes (cherry, chocolate = part of cigarette)
            Trap: Perfumes given WITH cigarettes (separate gift items)
DEFENSE:    Check whether the element is PART OF the product or ALONGSIDE it

PITFALL 6: The Topic Inversion Trap (NEW)

MECHANISM:  The correct domain is identified (films + tobacco) but the 
            DIRECTION is reversed
EXAMPLE:    Text: Films SHOW tobacco brands (pro-tobacco presence)
            Trap: Films about tobacco HARMS (anti-tobacco education)
DEFENSE:    Verify not just the TOPIC but the DIRECTION/ANGLE
