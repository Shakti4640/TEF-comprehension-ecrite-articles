📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Texte 64 : Allumer la télé pour connaître ses origines

Le nouveau sport à la mode en Europe ? Grimper aux arbres... généalogiques. Anglais et Français y sont particulièrement accros. En témoignent la multiplication de sites Web dédiés à cette quête identitaire ou encore l'affluence record, 15 000 visiteurs, de la 4e Biennale de généalogie, en décembre à Paris. Des deux côtés de la Manche, ce boom n'a pas échappé aux producteurs, prompts à avaler les tendances pour les régurgiter sous forme de concepts télé. Comme souvent, la BBC a ouvert le feu, aidée par des people. L'émission « Who Do You Think You Are ? » (« Qui pensez-vous être ? »), l'automne dernier, mettait en scène des personnalités lancées sur les traces de leurs ancêtres. Au final, de bonnes audiences, qui ont donné des idées aux chaînes françaises. Gérard Louvin reprend donc le format, rebaptisé « Tous cousins » et attendu fin juin sur France 2. « C'est l'occasion de redécouvrir les habitués des plateaux, qui ont déjà tout raconté chez Drucker », commente le producteur. De son côté, Jean-Pierre Pernaut cogiterait sur un pilote d'émission spéciale titrée « Comme un air de famille ». Il devrait en assurer la présentation avant l'été. La généalogie, alibi pour caser des célébrités en promo ? Certes, mais pas seulement. Les chaînes ont aussi senti venir le vent de la « psychogénéalogie », nouvelle thérapie fondée sur l'exploration de l'histoire familiale. De quoi faire frétiller le petit écran, friand de récits à valeur émotionnelle ajoutée. Le 13 mars, « Zone interdite » a déjà décrypté cet épiphénomène. Sur la BBC4, la série documentaire « Family Ties » (« Les liens familiaux »), en octobre, a suivi des anonymes dans leur enquête transgénérationnelle. Et dans leur découverte de secrets enfouis : frères et sœurs cachés, oncle sanguinaire, bébé assassiné. En Allemagne, on va même jusqu'à placer devant les caméras des hommes et des femmes qui recourent au test ADN pour s'assurer des origines de leurs enfants. Mais le pire est à voir avec une émission américaine « Qui est ton papa ? ». Une jeune femme adoptée à la naissance doit reconnaître son géniteur parmi huit candidats. Si elle désigne le bon, elle repart avec son paternel et gagne 100 000 dollars. On finit par se demander si la bonne question n'est pas « qui est ton papa ? » mais « ça intéresse qui ? »

Step 3: Decode the Ironic Register — The Journalist's Hidden Voice

This text is a masterclass in disguised opinion. The journalist never says "I think this is terrible." Instead, the opinion is embedded in:

Technique 1: The Digestive Metaphor

"prompts à avaler les tendances pour les régurgiter sous forme de concepts télé"

"Avaler" (swallow) + "régurgiter" (regurgitate/vomit back) = producers are compared to animals that mindlessly consume and expel. This is not a neutral description of creative adaptation — it's a portrait of intellectual vomiting.

Technique 2: Commercial Language Applied to Emotions

"récits à valeur émotionnelle ajoutée"

"Valeur ajoutée" is an ECONOMIC term (value added — as in VAT/TVA). Applying it to EMOTIONS reveals the journalist's view: television treats human feelings as commercial commodities. Emotions are "added" to stories like tax is added to products — mechanically, for profit.

Technique 3: The "Frétiller" Verb

"De quoi faire frétiller le petit écran"

"Frétiller" = to wriggle, to quiver with excitement (usually said of fish or excited animals). The TV screen "wriggles" with excitement at the prospect of emotional stories. This is MOCKERY — television is portrayed as an excited animal, not a thoughtful medium.

Technique 4: The Escalation Itself

The journalist doesn't need to say "each program is worse." By ARRANGING the examples from mild (celebrity genealogy) to extreme (identity game show), the journalist lets the STRUCTURE speak. The reader feels the descent without being told about it.

Technique 5: The Closing Kill

"On finit par se demander si la bonne question n'est pas « qui est ton papa ? » mais « ça intéresse qui ? »"

This final sentence is the journalist's VERDICT — the mask drops completely. "Ça intéresse qui ?" (Who cares? / Who is this even for?) is not a genuine question. It's a rhetorical demolition. The journalist is saying: this has gone so far that we should question whether ANYONE should be watching this.

Connection to Chapter 21: In L'Appel des Sommets, the text ended with a philosopher's PROFOUND quotation (Rébuffat on beauty). Here, the text ends with a journalist's SARCASTIC question. Same structural position (ending = climax), opposite register (reverence vs. contempt).

Step 5: Detect the "Certes, mais pas seulement" Pivot

The text contains a crucial structural pivot:

"La généalogie, alibi pour caser des célébrités en promo ? Certes, mais pas seulement."

Decompose this:

"alibi"              = cover story, excuse (negative connotation)
"caser"              = to stick/shove (dismissive verb)
"célébrités en promo" = celebrities doing promotion (cynical view)
"Certes"             = certainly, yes (concession)
"mais pas seulement"  = but not only (expansion)

The journalist asks a LOADED question ("Is genealogy just an excuse to put celebrities on TV?"), CONCEDES the answer is partly yes ("certes"), then EXPANDS to show it's WORSE than that ("but not only").

This is the Concession-Expansion structure — different from Chapter 18's Concession-Demolition:

Chapter 18 (Bulgaria):  "Si [small positive]... mais [massive negative]"
                        → Concession softens the blow

                        → Concession ADMITS the bad thing, then shows 
                          it's EVEN WORSE than you thought

After "mais pas seulement," the text introduces psychogenealogy, dark family secrets, DNA tests, and the American game show — each progressively worse than "just putting celebrities on TV."

1. Map Escalation Patterns as a Deliberate Authorial Strategy

This is the new structural skill of this chapter. Unlike chronological timelines (Chapter 21) or process stages (Chapter 19), escalation patterns are ARGUMENTATIVE structures — the journalist arranges examples in order of increasing extremity to BUILD A CASE:

CHAPTER 19 MAP:  Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3 (procedural)
CHAPTER 21 MAP:  Era 1 → Era 2 → Era 3 (chronological)
CHAPTER 22 MAP:  Level 1 → Level 2 → Level 7 (escalation = argument)

Why escalation matters for the exam: The final question (Q21) often targets the CULMINATION of the escalation — the point where the journalist's argument reaches its conclusion. If you mapped the escalation, you know the conclusion: the identity quest has become reality TV.

2. Track Country-Program Attribution with Precision

This text mentions 5 countries and 7+ programs. The exam tests whether you can attribute the right program to the right country. Build your matrix (as shown in Step 4 of the approach) and consult it for every question.

The specific danger: Options that take a REAL attribute and attach it to the WRONG country:

  • Option A (Q19): Copying attributed to UK instead of France
  • Option C (Q21): "Le pire" attributed to Germany instead of USA

Defense: For every option that mentions a country, verify: does the text actually say THIS about THIS country?

3. Check Verb Tenses in Options Against Text Tenses

Option B in Q20 uses "sera proposée" (future) when the text uses "a suivi" (past). This tense mismatch is a DISQUALIFYING error — if the text describes something that already happened, an option presenting it as future is automatically wrong.

Build a tense-checking reflex:

This skill transfers directly to Phase 5, where tense manipulation becomes even more subtle.

4. Recognize Rhetorical Questions as Authorial Verdicts

The text's closing question — "ça intéresse qui ?" — is NOT a genuine question seeking an answer. It's a rhetorical device expressing the journalist's conclusion: this has gone too far, and nobody should care about such degraded programming.

Rhetorical questions in French journalism almost always appear at the END of a text and carry the author's STRONGEST opinion:

"ça intéresse qui ?"     = Nobody should care about this
"Jusqu'où ira-t-on ?"    = This has gone too far
"Faut-il s'en étonner ?" = This was predictable and depressing

When you see a rhetorical question at the end, ask yourself: what opinion does this question IMPLY? That implied opinion is often the key to the final question.

5. Detect When Commercial Language Invades Emotional Territory

The phrase "récits à valeur émotionnelle ajoutée" is a masterpiece of ironic writing. "Valeur ajoutée" (added value) is a COMMERCIAL/ECONOMIC term. Applying it to "émotionnelle" (emotional) reveals the journalist's view: television treats emotions as products to be manufactured and sold.

This technique — using vocabulary from one domain (commerce) in another domain (human emotions) — is a hallmark of French ironic journalism. Learn to detect it:

COMMERCIAL term + HUMAN subject = IRONIC CRITIQUE
"valeur émotionnelle ajoutée"   = emotions as commodity
"régurgiter sous forme de concepts" = ideas as vomit
"avaler les tendances"          = trends as food to consume

Connection to Chapter 18: In Bulgarie, loaded vocabulary revealed political judgment ("sacrifiée," "institutionnalisée"). Here, loaded vocabulary reveals CULTURAL judgment — the journalist condemns TV's commodification of human identity through deliberately inappropriate commercial metaphors.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Read This Text as Neutral Reporting

The text LOOKS like a media survey — "here's what's happening in UK TV, French TV, German TV, American TV." But it's not neutral. Every sentence carries ironic judgment:

APPEARS TO SAY:  "Producers are quick to pick up trends"
ACTUALLY SAYS:   "Producers SWALLOW and VOMIT trends" (avaler/régurgiter)

APPEARS TO SAY:  "TV channels are excited about emotional stories"
ACTUALLY SAYS:   "The screen WRIGGLES like an animal" (frétiller)

APPEARS TO SAY:  "Here's an American show about finding your father"
ACTUALLY SAYS:   "Identity has been turned into a $100K game show"

If you read neutrally, you'll miss the irony — and the irony IS the text's real message.

2. Do NOT Confuse "Direction of Influence" Between Countries

This is the deadliest factual trap in the text. The flow of influence is:

UK (BBC) ──creates──→ "Who Do You Think You Are?"
                          │
                          ▼ good audiences
                          │
FRANCE ──copies──→ "Tous cousins" (Louvin)
                   "Comme un air de famille" (Pernaut)

The BBC goes FIRST. France FOLLOWS. Option A in Q19 reverses this. Always verify: who created the original, and who copied it?

3. Do NOT Displace Judgments Across Countries

The text's "le pire" (the worst) is specifically attached to the AMERICAN show:

"Mais le pire est à voir avec une émission américaine"

Option C in Q21 attaches "le pire" to GERMANY. This is factually wrong — the text explicitly labels the American show as "le pire."

Rule: When an option contains a superlative (le pire, le meilleur, le premier), verify which SPECIFIC entity the text attaches that superlative to. Superlatives are precise — they cannot be transferred to different subjects.

4. Do NOT Confuse Verb Tenses Between Text and Options

As analyzed in Q20, the text says "Family Ties a suivi" (past tense — already aired). Option B says "sera proposée" (future — will be offered). This tense mismatch makes the option automatically wrong.

PAST in text + FUTURE in option = INCOMPATIBLE

Never accept an option whose tense contradicts the text's tense for the same event. The event either happened or it didn't — it can't be both past and future.

5. Do NOT Confuse "Content About X" with "Effect on X"

Option D in Q21 says the identity quest "peut favoriser l'adoption." The American show features an ADOPTED woman — but featuring adoption as CONTENT does not mean the show PROMOTES adoption.

CONTENT:  The show is ABOUT an adopted person (descriptive)
EFFECT:   The show PROMOTES adoption (causal claim)

The text never claims any program has real-world effects on adoption practices. It describes TV CONTENT, not social IMPACT. Never infer causal effects that the text doesn't claim.

This is an evolution of Chapter 20's "comparison firewall" principle: just as "comme un médicament" doesn't make music actual medicine, a show ABOUT adoption doesn't make it an adoption program.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Irony Blindness" Problem

The most fundamental failure mode with this text is reading it straight. If you don't detect the irony, you'll think the journalist is enthusiastically reporting on an exciting TV trend. You'll miss the mockery in "régurgiter," the commodification critique in "valeur émotionnelle ajoutée," and the devastating contempt in "ça intéresse qui?"

How to avoid it: Look for THREE signals of irony:

  1. Inappropriate metaphors (digestive imagery for creative processes)
  2. Domain-crossing vocabulary (commercial terms for emotional experiences)
  3. Rhetorical questions at the end (disguised verdicts)

If you find all three, the text is ironic. Read it as critique, not as celebration.

Pitfall 2: The "Matrix Confusion" Error

With 5 countries and 7+ programs, it's easy to mix up which country does what. The exam exploits this by:

  • Attributing British innovation to France (Q19 option A)
  • Attributing America's "pire" to Germany (Q21 option C)
  • Attributing past events to the future (Q20 option B)

How to avoid it: Build the country-program matrix during your first reading. Consult it for EVERY question. Never trust your memory of "which country had the DNA show" — verify against the text.

Pitfall 3: The "Emotional Response" Override

The American game show is genuinely shocking. Some candidates react emotionally — "That's terrible! The worst!" — and then see Option C mentioning "le pire" and pick it without checking the COUNTRY attribution.

How to avoid it: Your emotional reaction is valid but irrelevant to the exam. The question asks what the TEXT says, not how you feel. Verify EVERY element in the option against the text — including the country name.

Pitfall 4: The "Conditional Mood" Trap

Option D in Q20 uses "aurait pu" (could have) — the conditional past. This mood implies something DIDN'T happen:

"Aurait pu faire l'objet d'un programme" 
= Could have been the subject of a program (but wasn't)

But the text shows it IS the subject of multiple French programs. The conditional mood contradicts the text's reality.

How to avoid it: When you see "aurait," "pourrait," "serait" in an option, ask: does this imply the thing didn't happen? If the text shows it DID happen, the conditional option is wrong.

Pitfall 5: The "Shared Word, Different Scope" Error

Option C in Q18 uses "racines" (roots), which connects to genealogy ("arbres généalogiques" = family trees with roots). But "racines communes" (common/shared roots) adds a meaning the text doesn't contain. The French and English share an INTEREST in genealogy, not genealogical ROOTS.

SHARED INTEREST:  Both populations are "accros" to genealogy ✅
SHARED ROOTS:     They descend from common ancestors ❌ (not in text)

How to avoid it: When an option uses a word that RELATES to the text's theme but adds a new qualifier (here: "communes"), check whether that qualifier is supported. "Racines" fits the genealogy theme, but "communes" introduces a claim about Franco-British shared ancestry that the text never makes.
