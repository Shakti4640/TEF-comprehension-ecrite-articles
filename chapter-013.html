📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE

L'eau sera un enjeu majeur

L'eau sera un enjeu majeur du XXIe siècle, tant sur le plan écologique qu'économique. L'augmentation de la population mondiale — on comptera 2 milliards d'habitants supplémentaires en 2025 — devrait conduire, selon l'UNESCO, à une pénurie de cette ressource vitale. Les régions où le manque d'eau devient un problème récurrent sont de plus en plus nombreuses, un phénomène en partie dû aux changements climatiques observés actuellement. Dans les vingt ans à venir, on s'attend à une diminution d'un tiers de l'eau disponible pour chaque habitant de la planète. Pour lutter contre ce problème, les experts réunis en mars 2003 au Forum mondial de l'eau de Kyoto ont invité les pays participants à lutter contre le gaspillage, à améliorer l'irrigation et à investir dans des équipements. L'inquiétude vaut tant pour la quantité que pour la qualité de l'eau potable.

Dans de nombreux pays, les eaux usées sont rejetées sans traitement dans les rivières. Avec des conséquences sur la santé que l'on peut imaginer, car de nombreuses maladies sont transmises par les eaux polluées. La production agricole et l'équilibre écologique sont aussi en danger. Certains pays envisagent la construction de barrages, comme vient de le faire la Chine sur le Yangtsé, l'aménagement de fleuves comme le Gange en Inde ou le dessalement des eaux saumâtres ou salées.

Courrier International, avril 2005

🧠 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's NEW in this chapter

Chapter 12 navigated emotional complexity. This chapter enters analytical territory — a global issue article with statistics, expert opinions, causes, consequences, and solutions all woven together.

NEW CONCEPT 1: The "Find the FALSE Statement" Question Type

Question 3 introduces a completely new challenge:

"Parmi les affirmations suivantes, une seule est fausse, laquelle ?"
(Among the following statements, only ONE is false, which one?)

This INVERTS your normal approach. Usually, you look for the ONE correct answer among three wrong ones. Here, you look for the ONE wrong answer among three CORRECT ones.

NORMAL QUESTION:     3 wrong + 1 right → FIND the right one
FALSE STATEMENT Q:   3 right + 1 wrong → FIND the wrong one

This requires a fundamentally different mental process:

NORMAL: "Which answer matches the text?" → Find the match
FALSE:  "Which answer CONTRADICTS or DISTORTS the text?" → Find the mismatch

NEW CONCEPT 2: Statistic Distortion

This text is PACKED with numbers and projections:

  • "2 milliards d'habitants supplémentaires en 2025"
  • "diminution d'un tiers de l'eau disponible"
  • "mars 2003"

The exam will take these statistics and DISTORT them in wrong answers — changing what the numbers refer to, exaggerating their scope, or shifting their meaning.

NEW CONCEPT 3: Three-Category Organization

Global issue articles typically present information in three categories:

CAUSES:       WHY the problem exists
CONSEQUENCES: WHAT happens because of the problem
SOLUTIONS:    WHAT can be done about the problem

Questions target specific categories. Mixing them up — answering a "solution" question with a "cause" — leads to wrong answers.

Applying the method (Rule #1)

STEP 1: Read all questions first

Q1: "Pourquoi l'eau sera-t-elle un enjeu majeur?" → CAUSE of the crisis
Q2: "Quelles solutions proposent les experts?" → SOLUTIONS recommended
Q3: "Une seule est fausse, laquelle?" → Find the FALSE statement

Three questions, three categories:

Q1 → CAUSES
Q2 → SOLUTIONS
Q3 → VERIFICATION (true/false across the text)

STEP 2: Read the title

L'eau sera un enjeu majeur

"Water will be a major challenge" — future tense, global scope, serious topic. The article will explain WHY water is a crisis and WHAT can be done.

🔬 THE STATISTICAL DISTORTION IN ANSWER C — Critical Analysis

Answer C is the most dangerous trap in this question. Let's dissect it:

What the text ACTUALLY says:

"une diminution d'un tiers de l'eau disponible pour chaque habitant de la planète"

Translation: "a decrease of one third of the available water per inhabitant of the planet"

What Answer C claims:

"un tiers des habitants de la planète n'aura plus d'eau"

Translation: "one third of the inhabitants of the planet will no longer have water"

Let's compare side by side:

TEXT:     "un tiers de L'EAU disponible pour chaque habitant"
          = 1/3 less WATER per person
          = EVERYONE gets 33% less water
          = Water QUANTITY decreases per person

ANSWER C: "un tiers DES HABITANTS n'aura plus d'eau"
          = 1/3 of PEOPLE will have NO water
          = Some people get ZERO water
          = PEOPLE are divided into have/have-not

The distortion:

TEXT:                          ANSWER C:
─────────                     ─────────
Subject: WATER                Subject: PEOPLE
Quantity: decreases by 1/3    Quantity: completely gone (ZERO)
Scope: per person (everyone)  Scope: 1/3 of population (some)
Severity: REDUCTION           Severity: TOTAL LOSS

"Un tiers de l'eau" (one third OF THE WATER) became "un tiers des habitants" (one third OF THE PEOPLE). The fraction "un tiers" is preserved, but what it MODIFIES completely changes. This is statistical sleight of hand.

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #23: When a text contains fractions, percentages, or numbers, check EXACTLY what the number MODIFIES. "1/3 of the water per person" ≠ "1/3 of people without water." The same number can be attached to completely different subjects, creating a false equivalence.

This expands Rule #9 (Chapter 5: statistics must be read precisely) into a more specific principle about WHAT the number modifies.

EXPERT SOLUTION 2: "améliorer l'irrigation" = improve irrigation

🔬 SYNTHESIS PARAPHRASE — Three Solutions Become Two Concepts

This is a Type 3 paraphrase (synthesis, from Chapter 2), at its most sophisticated:

TEXT (three specific solutions):
  1. "lutter contre le gaspillage"    ─┐
                                       ├→ "Changer les comportements"
  2. "améliorer l'irrigation"         ─┤
                                       ├→ "Développer des infrastructures"
  3. "investir dans des équipements"  ─┘

ANSWER (two general concepts):
  "Changer les comportements et développer des infrastructures"

Three specific actions → two general categories. This is the highest level of paraphrase: abstraction — going from specific actions to general concepts.

🔬 THE "CAUSE ≠ SOLUTION" TRAP — Answer C

Answer C is a particularly clever trap:

TEXT mentions climate change as: a CAUSE of water shortage
                                "en partie dû aux changements climatiques"

ANSWER C claims experts propose: fighting climate change as a SOLUTION
                                "Lutter contre le réchauffement climatique"

The text identifies climate change as part of the PROBLEM. The wrong answer turns it into the SOLUTION. But the experts' actual solutions are WATER-SPECIFIC (waste, irrigation, equipment), not climate-specific.

CAUSES (paragraph 1):     population growth + climate change
SOLUTIONS (paragraph 1):  fight waste + improve irrigation + invest in equipment
COUNTRY ACTIONS (para 2): dams + river management + desalination

The question asks about SOLUTIONS → look in the SOLUTIONS section
Answer C takes from CAUSES section → wrong category

🚨 NEW CONCEPT: Category Contamination. In global issue articles, CAUSES, CONSEQUENCES, and SOLUTIONS are separate categories. A wrong answer may take an element from one category and present it as belonging to another. Climate change is a CAUSE, not a proposed SOLUTION.

QUESTION 3: Parmi les affirmations suivantes, une seule est fausse, laquelle ?

This is the FALSE STATEMENT question — the new question type introduced in this chapter.

Strategy for "Find the False" questions:

STEP 1: Test EACH statement against the text
STEP 2: Mark each as TRUE (✓) or FALSE (✗)
STEP 3: The one marked FALSE is your answer

This requires PATIENCE. You must check ALL FOUR statements, not stop at the first one that seems wrong.

Let's test each statement:

Statement A: "La qualité de l'eau inquiète tout autant les experts"
(Water quality concerns experts just as much)

Text evidence:

"L'inquiétude vaut tant pour la quantité que pour la qualité de l'eau potable"

"Tant pour... que pour..." = both for... and for... = EQUALLY for quantity AND quality.

"Inquiète tout autant" (concerns just as much) ≈ "tant pour... que pour..." (both for... and for...).

Verdict: ✅ TRUE — The text explicitly states quality is AS concerning as quantity.

Statement B: "Les eaux polluées sont néfastes pour la santé"
(Polluted water is harmful for health)

Text evidence:

"de nombreuses maladies sont transmises par les eaux polluées"

Polluted water transmits diseases → polluted water is harmful for health.

"Néfastes pour la santé" (harmful for health) ≈ "maladies transmises" (diseases transmitted).

Verdict: ✅ TRUE — The text explicitly states polluted water causes diseases.

Statement C: "La mauvaise qualité de l'eau perturbe l'écosystème"
(Poor water quality disturbs the ecosystem)

Text evidence:

"La production agricole et l'équilibre écologique sont aussi en danger"

"L'équilibre écologique en danger" (ecological balance in danger) ≈ "perturbe l'écosystème" (disturbs the ecosystem).

Verdict: ✅ TRUE — The text explicitly states ecological balance is threatened.

Statement D: "Des barrages sont construits pour traiter les eaux usées"
(Dams are built to treat wastewater)

Text evidence:

"Certains pays envisagent la construction de barrages, comme vient de le faire la Chine sur le Yangtsé"

And separately:

"les eaux usées sont rejetées sans traitement dans les rivières"

Now let's analyze Statement D carefully:

The text mentions TWO separate things:

  1. Dams — being built by countries (China on the Yangtze)
  2. Wastewater — being discharged WITHOUT treatment into rivers

But the text NEVER connects these two. Dams are NOT built to treat wastewater. Dams serve to:

  • Store water
  • Control flooding
  • Generate hydroelectric power
  • Manage water supply

Wastewater treatment requires treatment plants, filtration systems, purification facilities — NOT dams.

Statement D falsely connects two separate elements from the text:

TEXT element 1: "construction de barrages" (building dams) — for water management
TEXT element 2: "eaux usées sans traitement" (untreated wastewater) — a PROBLEM

STATEMENT D: "barrages construits POUR traiter les eaux usées"
             = connects dams TO wastewater treatment
             = FALSE connection — dams don't treat wastewater

Verdict: ❌ FALSE — Dams are mentioned for water management, NOT for wastewater treatment. The text never makes this connection.

✅ Answer: D (the FALSE statement)

🔬 THE "FALSE CONNECTION" MECHANISM

Statement D is false because it creates a causal link between two SEPARATE elements that the text mentions INDEPENDENTLY:

ELEMENT A: Dams are being built (for water management)
ELEMENT B: Wastewater goes untreated (a problem)

FALSE STATEMENT: Dams are built TO treat wastewater
                 = A is done FOR the purpose of solving B
                 = FALSE — A and B are unrelated in the text

This is a sophisticated trap type: taking two TRUE elements from the text and connecting them with a FALSE purpose ("pour" = in order to).

🚨 GOLDEN RULE #24: In "Find the FALSE statement" questions, the false statement often connects two REAL elements from the text with a FALSE relationship. Both elements exist in the text, but the link between them is INVENTED. Check not just whether the ELEMENTS are in the text, but whether the RELATIONSHIP between them is.

🎯 WHAT COULD BE DONE

The "Find the FALSE Statement" Method

This question type requires a DIFFERENT approach from normal questions:

NORMAL QUESTION:
  Read text → Find matching answer → Confirm → Done
  Speed: Find ONE match among FOUR

FALSE STATEMENT QUESTION:
  Read text → Check EACH statement → Find the ONE mismatch → Confirm
  Speed: Must check ALL FOUR (slower, more methodical)

Step-by-step process:

STEP 1: Read each statement independently
STEP 2: For each, find the corresponding text passage
STEP 3: Compare PRECISELY — check for:
         □ Does the text SAY this? (content match)
         □ Does the text make this CONNECTION? (relationship match)
         □ Is the PURPOSE stated correctly? (purpose match)
STEP 4: Mark: TRUE (✓) or FALSE (✗) or UNCERTAIN (?)
STEP 5: The FALSE one is your answer
STEP 6: Double-check the FALSE one to be sure

The "Expert vs. Country" Distinction

The text presents TWO LEVELS of action:

LEVEL 1: EXPERT RECOMMENDATIONS (what experts SUGGEST)
         "les experts... ont invité les pays à..."
         → General strategies: fight waste, improve irrigation, invest

LEVEL 2: COUNTRY ACTIONS (what specific nations DO)
         "Certains pays envisagent..."
         → Specific projects: dams (China), river management (India), desalination

When a question asks about EXPERT solutions → look at Level 1 only.
When a question asks about what countries DO → look at Level 2.

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For "Why" questions about global issues:

  1. Apply Rule #4 (causal connectors) — find the stated causes
  2. Check statistical claims in answers against the text — verify WHAT the number modifies
  3. Reject absolute claims ("rien n'est fait," "ne sera plus potable dans la majorité") unless the text uses equally absolute language
  4. Look for the CORE logic — usually a supply-demand imbalance or cause-consequence chain

For "What solutions" questions:

  1. Identify WHO proposes the solutions — experts? governments? the author?
  2. Stay in the SOLUTIONS category — don't bring in causes or consequences
  3. Expect synthesis paraphrase — three specific actions may become two general concepts
  4. Distinguish between behavior change and infrastructure — these are the two main types of solutions

  1. Check ALL FOUR statements — don't stop at the first suspicious one
  2. Verify both ELEMENTS and RELATIONSHIPS — are the facts correct AND is the connection correct?
  3. Be especially suspicious of "pour" (in order to) — false purpose connections are common
  4. Mark each statement TRUE or FALSE as you go

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL #45: The "Statistical Subject Swap" Trap 📊

TEXT:     "diminution d'un tiers de L'EAU DISPONIBLE pour chaque habitant"
          = 1/3 reduction of AVAILABLE WATER per person

ANSWER C: "un tiers DES HABITANTS de la planète n'aura plus d'eau"
          = 1/3 of the planet's INHABITANTS will have no water

The fraction "un tiers" is CORRECT. But what it modifies is SWAPPED:

TEXT:     un tiers de [L'EAU]      → water decreases
ANSWER:   un tiers des [HABITANTS]  → people lose water entirely

Additionally, the severity is exaggerated:

TEXT:     "diminution" (reduction)     → less water, but some remains
ANSWER:   "n'aura plus" (will have no) → ZERO water

How to avoid it: Apply Rule #23 — when you see a number/fraction in an answer, go back to the text and check: "What does this number MODIFY in the text? Is it the same thing in the answer?"

PITFALL #46: The "Absolute Word" Rejection Test 🚫

ANSWER A: "RIEN n'est fait pour l'améliorer"
          = NOTHING is being done to improve it

"Rien" (nothing) is an ABSOLUTE word (Rule #6 from Chapter 3). The text describes:

  • Expert recommendations at Kyoto
  • Countries building dams
  • Desalination projects

These are NOT "nothing." The text shows action IS being taken. "Rien" is too absolute.

How to avoid it: When you see absolute words in an answer (rien, jamais, toujours, tous, aucun), ask: "Does the text support this ABSOLUTE claim?" Usually, if the text provides even ONE counter-example, the absolute claim is false.

ABSOLUTE CLAIM: "Rien n'est fait" (nothing is done)
COUNTER-EVIDENCE: Experts proposed solutions + countries are building dams
→ ABSOLUTE CLAIM FAILS

PITFALL #47: The "Category Contamination" Trap 🔀

CAUSES column:    Climate change
SOLUTIONS column: Fight waste, improve irrigation, invest in equipment

Q2 asks about SOLUTIONS.
ANSWER C offers: "Lutter contre le réchauffement climatique" (fight climate change)
→ This takes from the CAUSES column and puts it in the SOLUTIONS answer

Climate change is mentioned as a CAUSE. The experts' solutions are water-specific. They don't propose fighting climate change as their solution — they propose fighting waste, improving irrigation, and investing in equipment.

How to avoid it: When answering a question, stay within the category the question asks about. If the question asks about SOLUTIONS, look only in the solutions section of the text. Don't bring in elements from the causes section.

PITFALL #48: The "False Connection" Trap 🔗

This is the KEY pitfall of Q3 and the most important new concept in this chapter:

TEXT mentions: "construction de barrages" (TRUE — dams are being built)
TEXT mentions: "eaux usées sans traitement" (TRUE — wastewater is untreated)

FALSE STATEMENT D: "barrages construits POUR traiter les eaux usées"
                   = dams built TO treat wastewater
                   = FALSE connection between two true elements

Both elements exist independently in the text. But the PURPOSE connection ("pour") is INVENTED. Dams are for water management, not wastewater treatment.

How to detect false connections:

STEP 1: Is Element A in the text? → YES (barrages are mentioned)
STEP 2: Is Element B in the text? → YES (eaux usées are mentioned)
STEP 3: Does the text CONNECT A to B? → NO (they're in separate sentences,
         separate paragraphs, separate contexts)
STEP 4: Does the answer CONNECT A to B? → YES ("pour" links them)
→ FALSE CONNECTION DETECTED
