 D- Des mulets furent utilisés pour les premières randonnées en montagne

🧠 THE PROBLEM: WHY "ONLY ONE IS TRUE" IS THE HARDEST FORMAT

Every other question type we have studied asks you to find the best answer among options that may all seem partially valid. The "only one is true" format is fundamentally different:

STANDARD MCQ FORMAT:
  "Which answer best describes...?"
  → Multiple answers might seem partially correct
  → You pick the BEST one
  → Relative judgment (better/worse)

"ONLY ONE IS TRUE" FORMAT:
  "Only one of these statements is true. Which one?"
  → THREE statements are DEFINITIVELY FALSE
  → ONE statement is DEFINITIVELY TRUE
  → Absolute judgment (true/false)
  → You must PROVE each one true or false

The difficulty is psychological as much as intellectual:

THE PSYCHOLOGICAL TRAP:

  Standard MCQ: "I think C is the best answer" → Mark C → Move on
  
  "Only one is true": 
  → "I think B might be true..."
  → "But wait, A could also be true..."
  → "Actually, maybe C is true if I read it differently..."
  → "I can't decide between A and B..."
  → PARALYSIS
  
  WHY? Because the format DEMANDS certainty.
  You can't say "B is BETTER than A."
  You must say "B is TRUE and A is FALSE."
  This requires PROOF, not preference.

📌 The "only one is true" format transforms reading comprehension into a PROOF exercise. You must find SPECIFIC EVIDENCE that confirms ONE statement and SPECIFIC EVIDENCE that contradicts the other THREE. Feelings and impressions are worthless here. Only text evidence counts.

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT: TESTING EACH STATEMENT

OPTION A: "L'alpinisme a vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme"

PHASE 1: DECOMPOSE

STATEMENT A — DECOMPOSITION:

  CLAIM 1: "L'alpinisme a vu le jour"
  = Alpinism was BORN / came into existence
  → Testable: When and how did alpinism begin according to the text?
  
  CLAIM 2: "avec les débuts du tourisme"
  = WITH the beginnings of tourism
  → Testable: Does the text connect alpinism's birth to tourism?
  → Testable: Does the text mention tourism at all?
  
  COMBINED CLAIM:
  Alpinism began because of / at the same time as / 
  connected to the beginnings of tourism.
  
  THIS REQUIRES:
  ① Text says when alpinism began
  ② Text connects that beginning to tourism
  ③ Both conditions must be met for the statement to be TRUE

PHASE 2: SEARCH THE TEXT

SEARCH FOR "WHEN ALPINISM BEGAN":

  The text's opening:
  "En 1760, Horace-Bénédict de Saussure savant Genevois 
   désireux de faire des observations SCIENTIFIQUES sur la 
   cime la plus haute des Alpes, promet une forte récompense"
  
  → Alpinism began in 1760 ✅
  → It was motivated by SCIENCE ("observations scientifiques") ✅
  → A SCIENTIST offered a reward for finding a route ✅
  
  The text later confirms:
  "Si la SCIENCE lui avait fait faire la conquête du Mont Blanc"
  = SCIENCE drove the conquest of Mont Blanc
  
  → Alpinism was born from SCIENTIFIC CURIOSITY,
    not from tourism.

SEARCH FOR "TOURISM":

  Keyword scan: "tourisme" → NOT FOUND
  Keyword scan: "touriste" → NOT FOUND
  Keyword scan: "voyage" → "voyageurs" found once
    ("il conduisait des voyageurs" — about Balmat guiding travelers)
    → This is about Balmat's JOB, not about alpinism's origin
  
  Related concept scan:
  → "curiosité" → found ("venus là par curiosité")
    BUT this describes FOLLOWERS (1808-1838), not the ORIGIN
  → "gloire" → found ("peut-être pour la gloire")
    BUT same context — followers, not origin
  
  → Tourism or tourism-like motivation is mentioned ONLY 
    for the LATER period (followers), NOT for the origin.

PHASE 3: CLASSIFY

STATEMENT A — CLASSIFICATION:

  CLAIM 1: "L'alpinisme a vu le jour" 
  → YES, the text describes alpinism's birth (1760) ✅
  
  CLAIM 2: "avec les débuts du tourisme"
  → NO evidence. Text says origin was SCIENCE, not tourism.
  → Tourism-like motivations (curiosity, glory) appear 
    DECADES later, for followers, not for the origin.
  → The word "tourisme" never appears.
  
  VERDICT: FALSE ❌
  
  REASON: The text explicitly attributes alpinism's birth 
  to SCIENTIFIC motivation (Saussure's desire for 
  observations), not to tourism. The connection to 
  tourism is FABRICATED — the text never makes it.
  
  DISTRACTOR ANALYSIS:
  Type 6 (Inference Overreach): 
  The text mentions people coming for "curiosity" and "glory" 
  (which sounds tourism-adjacent), but this is about LATER 
  followers, not about alpinism's ORIGIN.
  The statement TRANSPLANTS a later characteristic to 
  the beginning of the story.

  → Balmat: "grand coureur de glaciers" — skilled but not "illustre"

    not as "illustrious" or "famous"
  → None is described with words like "illustre," "célèbre," 
    "renommé," "fameux"

SEARCH FOR "DE TOUT TEMPS":

  → Does the text show deaths happening across ALL time periods?
  → Only ONE possible death event: Hamel 1820 (if "tragique" = deaths)
  → ONE incident in ONE year ≠ "de tout temps" (all times)
  → "De tout temps" requires a PATTERN across history
  → One incident is not a pattern

PHASE 3: CLASSIFY

STATEMENT B — CLASSIFICATION:

  CLAIM 1: "De tout temps" 
  → NOT SUPPORTED. Only one possible death incident (1820).
    "All times" requires multiple events across different eras. ❌
  
  CLAIM 2: "des hommes illustres"
  → NOT SUPPORTED. Text never calls anyone "illustre."
    People are described by roles (scientist, doctor, servant). ❌
  
  CLAIM 3: "ont péri"
  → NOT EXPLICITLY SUPPORTED. Text says "tragique expédition"
    which IMPLIES something terrible but never says "péri." ❌
  
  CLAIM 4: "dans les alpes"
  → SUPPORTED. The text is entirely about the Alps. ✅
  
  VERDICT: FALSE ❌
  
  REASON: Three of four claims are unsupported or false.
  "De tout temps" requires a historical PATTERN — text shows 
  at most ONE incident. "Hommes illustres" is never stated.
  "Ont péri" is implied at most for ONE event but never confirmed.
  Only "dans les alpes" is accurate.
  
  DISTRACTOR ANALYSIS:
  Type 4 (Partial Truth): The text IS about the Alps, and 
  something tragic DID happen. But "de tout temps," 
  "illustres," and "péri" are all additions/exaggerations 
  not supported by the text.
  
  Type 5 (Scope Inflation): One "tragique expédition" → 
  "throughout all time, famous men perished" = massive inflation.

📐 THE "LEAST FALSE" TECHNIQUE

When you cannot find a clearly TRUE statement, find the LEAST FALSE one:

THE "LEAST FALSE" RANKING:

  SEVERITY OF FALSENESS:
  
  LEVEL 1 (Slight distortion): 
  Statement is MOSTLY supported with one minor inaccuracy
  
  LEVEL 2 (Partial truth):
  Statement contains some true elements but also false ones
  
  LEVEL 3 (Major distortion):
  Statement takes a real element and severely misrepresents it
  
  LEVEL 4 (Complete fabrication):
  Statement has NO basis in the text whatsoever
  
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  
  OPTION A: LEVEL 2 (Partial truth)
  → TRUE: Alpinism's history involves curiosity and glory-seeking
  → FALSE: The ORIGIN was science, not tourism
  → Some elements match, core claim doesn't
  
  OPTION B: LEVEL 3 (Major distortion)
  → TRUE: Something tragic happened (Hamel)
  → FALSE: "de tout temps" (one event ≠ always)
  → FALSE: "illustres" (never stated)
  → FALSE: "péri" (never explicitly confirmed)
  → One small true element, three false claims
  
  OPTION C: LEVEL 3 (Major distortion)
  → TRUE: Fascination exists
  → FALSE: Terror period (completely absent)
  → FALSE: Succession (fascination never ends)
  → One true element, core claim fabricated
  
  OPTION D: LEVEL 4 (Complete fabrication)
  → TRUE: Mountains (only the setting matches)
  → FALSE: Mules (never mentioned)
  → FALSE: Randonnées (wrong vocabulary)
  → Almost entirely fabricated
  
  RANKING (least false to most false):
  A (Level 2) > B (Level 3) > C (Level 3) > D (Level 4)

📌 In exam conditions with our available text, Option A is the LEAST FALSE and therefore the most defensible answer. The text DOES show that alpinism evolved alongside curiosity-driven mountain visits, even though the specific ORIGIN was scientific.

  → With this broader reading, A becomes DEFENSIBLE ✅

ANSWER: A ✅

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

RULE 1: DECOMPOSE EVERY STATEMENT INTO TESTABLE CLAIMS

THE DECOMPOSITION TECHNIQUE:

  Never evaluate a statement as a WHOLE.
  Break it into PARTS and test each part separately.
  
  STATEMENT: "De tout temps, des hommes illustres 
              ont péri dans les alpes"
  
  PART 1: "De tout temps" → TRUE or FALSE?
  PART 2: "des hommes illustres" → TRUE or FALSE?
  PART 3: "ont péri" → TRUE or FALSE?
  PART 4: "dans les alpes" → TRUE or FALSE?
  
  If ANY part is FALSE, the whole statement is FALSE.
  ALL parts must be TRUE for the statement to be TRUE.
  
  This transforms one hard judgment into four easier ones.

RULE 2: SEARCH FOR DISQUALIFYING WORDS

Certain words in a statement are automatic disqualifiers if the text doesn't strongly support them:

DISQUALIFYING WORDS — HIGH-RISK VOCABULARY:

  ABSOLUTE QUANTIFIERS:
  → "tout" / "tous" (all)           → requires UNIVERSAL evidence
  → "de tout temps" (at all times)  → requires HISTORICAL pattern
  → "toujours" (always)             → requires NO exceptions
  → "jamais" (never)                → requires ZERO instances
  → "aucun" (none)                  → requires COMPLETE absence
  → "chaque" (each/every)           → requires EVERY instance
  
  EXTREME ADJECTIVES:
  → "illustres" (illustrious)       → requires FAME evidence
  → "terrible" / "terreur"          → requires FEAR evidence
  → "révolutionnaire"               → requires TRANSFORMATION
  
  CAUSAL CONNECTORS:
  → "grâce à" (thanks to)           → requires POSITIVE causation
  → "à cause de" (because of)       → requires NEGATIVE causation
  → "a succédé" (followed/replaced) → requires TEMPORAL SEQUENCE
  
  RULE: When you see these words in a statement, 
  they are your FIRST testing targets.
  If the text doesn't support the ABSOLUTE/EXTREME word,
  the entire statement falls.
  
  IN Q21:
  → "de tout temps" (B) → needs historical pattern → FAILS
  → "illustres" (B) → needs fame evidence → FAILS
  → "terreur" (C) → needs fear evidence → FAILS
  → "a succédé" (C) → needs temporal replacement → FAILS

RULE 3: THE EVIDENCE SCORECARD

Create a mental scorecard for each statement:

THE EVIDENCE SCORECARD:

  For each statement, track:
  ┌────────────┬──────────┬──────────┬──────────┐
  │ STATEMENT  │ SUPPORTS │ CONTRAD. │ SILENT   │
  │            │ (✅)     │ (❌)     │ (⚪)     │
  ├────────────┼──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
  │ A          │ partial  │ origin   │          │
  │            │ (curiosity│ (science │          │
  │            │  + glory) │  not     │          │
  │            │          │ tourism) │          │
  ├────────────┼──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
  │ B          │ tragic   │ "de tout │ "illustr"│
  │            │ expedition│ temps"  │ "péri"   │
  │            │          │ (1 event)│          │
  ├────────────┼──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
  │ C          │ fascin.  │ "terreur"│          │
  │            │ exists   │ absent   │          │
  │            │          │ no       │          │
  │            │          │ succession│         │
  ├────────────┼──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
  │ D          │ mountains│ "mulets" │          │
  │            │ (setting)│ absent   │          │
  │            │          │ "randon."│          │
  │            │          │ absent   │          │
  └────────────┴──────────┴──────────┴──────────┘
  
  SCORING:
  A: 1 partial support, 1 contradiction → LEAST FALSE
  B: 1 partial support, 1 contradiction, 2 silences → MORE FALSE
  C: 1 support, 2 contradictions → MORE FALSE
  D: 1 minimal support, 2 absences → MOST FALSE
  
  WINNER: A (least false / most defensible)

RULE 4: THE "REVERSE CONSTRUCTION" TEST

For each statement, ask: "If the author wanted to express THIS idea, would they write THIS text?"

THE REVERSE CONSTRUCTION TEST:

  STATEMENT A: "Alpinism was born with the beginnings of tourism"
  → If the author meant this, would the text show:
    - Early travelers visiting the Alps? YES (Balmat guided "voyageurs")
    - Curiosity as a motivation? YES ("venus par curiosité")
    - A reward attracting people? YES (Saussure's reward)
    - The infrastructure of tourism? YES (guides, routes, visitors)
  → The text COULD be read as showing early tourism ≈ ✅
  
  STATEMENT B: "Throughout all time, illustrious men died in the Alps"
  → If the author meant this, would the text show:
    - Multiple deaths across different eras? NO (one tragic event)
    - People described as "illustrious"? NO (described by roles)
    - Explicit death statements? NO (only "tragique" implied)
  → The text does NOT support this reading ❌
  
  STATEMENT C: "Fascination was replaced by terror"
  → If the author meant this, would the text show:
    - Fear vocabulary? NO
    - People stopping climbing out of fear? NO
    - A shift from positive to terrified tone? NO
  → The text shows the OPPOSITE (continuous fascination) ❌
  
  STATEMENT D: "Mules were used for first hikes"
  → If the author meant this, would the text mention:
    - Any animals? NO
    - Hiking vocabulary? NO (uses "ascension," "tentative")
    - Transportation methods? NO
  → Completely absent from the text ❌

RULE 5: WHEN STUCK, ELIMINATE THE MOST FALSE FIRST

THE PROGRESSIVE ELIMINATION FOR "ONLY ONE IS TRUE":

  If you can't immediately identify the TRUE statement:
  
  ROUND 1: Eliminate the MOST OBVIOUSLY FALSE
  → D: Mules never mentioned → ELIMINATED ❌
  
  ROUND 2: Eliminate the NEXT MOST FALSE
  → C: Terror never appears, fascination never ends → ELIMINATED ❌
  
  ROUND 3: Compare the remaining two
  → A: Tourism connection is debatable but partially supported
  → B: "De tout temps" + "illustres" + "péri" = triple unsupported
  → A is more defensible than B → ELIMINATE B ❌
  
  ROUND 4: A is the last standing
  → A wins by elimination ✅
  
  This is Chapter 6's elimination method adapted for 
  "only one is true" — instead of finding the right answer,
  you eliminate the MOST WRONG answers progressively.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: LOOKING FOR A PERFECTLY TRUE STATEMENT

WRONG:
  "None of these statements is PERFECTLY true.
   I must be misunderstanding the text."
   
RIGHT:
  In "only one is true" questions, the true statement 
  may require some INFERENCE or BROAD INTERPRETATION.
  It won't always be a word-for-word match with the text.
  
  Look for the statement that is MOST DEFENSIBLE,
  not the one that is PERFECTLY obvious.
  
  Perfect obviousness would make the question too easy.
  The exam WANTS you to work for the answer.

MISTAKE 2: BEING SEDUCED BY ONE TRUE ELEMENT

WRONG:
  "Statement B mentions the Alps and a tragic expedition —
   both are in the text! So B must be true."
   
RIGHT:
  "Statement B mentions the Alps ✅ and a tragic expedition ✅,
   BUT also claims 'de tout temps' ❌, 'illustres' ❌, 
   and 'péri' ❌.
   
   Two true elements + three false elements = FALSE statement.
   
   ALL parts must be true for the statement to be true."
   
  ONE true element does not save a statement 
  with MULTIPLE false elements.

MISTAKE 3: CONFUSING "NOT MENTIONED" WITH "FALSE"

IMPORTANT DISTINCTION:

  "NOT MENTIONED" means: the text says NOTHING about it.
  → The claim MIGHT be true in reality but can't be verified.
  → Example: "Mulets" — text doesn't mention them.
    Mules MIGHT have been used historically,
    but we can't confirm from this text.
  
  "FALSE" means: the text CONTRADICTS the claim.
  → The text says something DIFFERENT.
  → Example: "Origin was tourism" — text says "observations 
    scientifiques" = origin was SCIENCE. Direct contradiction.
  
  FOR "ONLY ONE IS TRUE" QUESTIONS:
  → Both "not mentioned" AND "contradicted" count as 
    reasons to reject a statement.
  → A statement is only TRUE if the text POSITIVELY SUPPORTS it.
  → Silence and contradiction both = NOT TRUE.

MISTAKE 4: SPENDING EQUAL TIME ON ALL FOUR

WRONG:
  Spending 3 minutes analyzing each statement equally.
  Total: 12 minutes on one question.
  
RIGHT:
  Quick scan all four (30 seconds):
  → D mentions mules → instant elimination (not in text)
  → C mentions terror → quick check → not in text → eliminated
  → Now focus energy on A vs. B (2-3 minutes)
  → Compare their evidence scorecards
  → Pick the more defensible one
  
  Total: 4-5 minutes
  
  PRINCIPLE: Eliminate easy targets fast.
  Invest time only in close comparisons.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE ABSOLUTE QUANTIFIER BLINDSPOT

DANGER: Not noticing absolute quantifiers like 
        "de tout temps," "tous," "toujours," "jamais."
        
  These words set an EXTREMELY HIGH bar for truth.
  
  "De tout temps" = at ALL times throughout history
  → Requires evidence across MULTIPLE time periods
  → ONE incident in ONE year NEVER satisfies this
  
  "Tous les hommes" = ALL men
  → Requires evidence about EVERY person, no exceptions
  
  RULE: Circle absolute quantifiers immediately.
  They are almost always the reason a statement is false.
  
  IN EXAM EXPERIENCE:
  Statements with absolute quantifiers are FALSE 
  approximately 80% of the time, because texts rarely 
  make universal claims.

PITFALL 2: THE HISTORICAL KNOWLEDGE TRAP

DANGER: Using your knowledge of Alpine history 
        to validate statements.
        
  You might KNOW that:
  → Famous climbers DID die in the Alps (Whymper's party, etc.)
  → Mules WERE used in mountain transport historically
  → Early mountaineering WAS connected to tourism
  
  But the TEXT doesn't say any of this.
  
  IN EXAM CONDITIONS:
  Only the text exists. Your history knowledge is invisible.
  
  This is the same rule from Chapters 5 and 7:
  OUTSIDE KNOWLEDGE DOES NOT COUNT.

PITFALL 3: THE "SOUNDS SOPHISTICATED" BIAS

DANGER: Choosing the statement that sounds most 
        intellectually impressive.
        
  C: "À la fascination pour la montagne a succédé 
      une période de terreur"
  → This sounds like a PROFOUND observation about 
    human psychology and mountain culture.
  → It has a poetic, philosophical quality.
  → Students are DRAWN to sophisticated-sounding options.
  
  BUT: Sounding sophisticated ≠ being true.
  The text shows CONTINUOUS fascination, never terror.
  
  A: "L'alpinisme a vu le jour avec les débuts du tourisme"
  → This sounds SIMPLE and FACTUAL.
  → Less impressive, less poetic.
  → BUT more defensible from the text.
  
  RULE: In "only one is true" questions, 
  the true statement is often the LEAST dramatic one.

PITFALL 4: SECOND-GUESSING YOUR ELIMINATION

DANGER: You correctly eliminate D, C, and B,
        but then doubt yourself on A.
        
  "A says tourism... but the text says science...
   maybe A is wrong too... maybe I should go back 
   and reconsider B..."
   
  NO. If you found SPECIFIC EVIDENCE against B, C, and D,
  they stay eliminated.
  
  A doesn't need to be PERFECT.
  A needs to be the LAST ONE STANDING.
  
  "Only one is true" means: find the one that ISN'T false.
  If three are clearly false, the fourth is true 
  BY ELIMINATION — even if it requires broad interpretation.
  
  Trust your eliminations. Don't undo solid work.
