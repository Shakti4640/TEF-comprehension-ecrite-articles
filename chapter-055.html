Chapter 3: LES PARCS-RELAIS — UNDERSTANDING A CONCEPT EXPLANATION

🅿️ Texte 72 | Phase 1: Very Easy | Foundation Thinking

📄 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Un parc-relais est un parking situé à proximité immédiate d'une station de tramway, de métro, voire d'autobus, dans lequel l'automobiliste peut laisser sa voiture. Il continue alors son voyage en transport en commun, généralement vers le centre-ville, là où la place manque pour circuler ou stationner. Mais cela ne suffit pas. La réussite du parc-relais dépend des conditions d'accueil des voitures: il doit être gardé. Et une formule tarifaire attractive doit être proposée aux automobilistes. C'est Strasbourg qui a montré la voie, suivie par Nantes et Montpellier : le conducteur de la voiture paie pour la journée une somme correspondant à environ une heure de stationnement en centre-ville, et reçoit gratuitement autant de billets aller-retour en tramway qu'il y a de passagers dans son véhicule. Succès assuré à Strasbourg, où plusieurs milliers de places sont ainsi offertes aux citadins, comme à Nantes. Sur la foi de ce résultat, la pratique de « l'intermodalité », selon le terme en usage, est appelée à se développer dans d'autres villes, à commencer par Montpellier et Orléans.

THE FOUR QUESTIONS

Question 14 : Les parcs-relais se situent

  • A — en centre-ville
  • B — en périphérie ✅
  • C — dans une station de métro
  • D — dans un jardin

Question 15 : Le succès des parcs-relais est lié

  • A — à la gratuité du service
  • B — à la mentalité des automobilistes
  • C — à la grève des tramways
  • D — à la surveillance des voitures ✅

Question 16 : À Strasbourg, on offre

  • A — des places de parking gratuites
  • B — des tickets de tramway gratuits ✅
  • C — des locations de véhicules gratuites
  • D — des heures de stationnement en centre-ville

Question 17 : L'intermodalité

  • A — est un succès à Montpellier et Orléans
  • B — se développera à Strasbourg et Nantes
  • C — est une réussite à Strasbourg et Nantes ✅
  • D — a commencé à Montpellier et Nantes

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What's different from Projects 1 and 2?

In Project 1, each paragraph = one topic. In Project 2, each paragraph = one disorder. The structure was visually obvious — you could see the borders.

This text is ONE continuous block. No paragraph breaks. No subheadings. Five cities are mentioned (Strasbourg, Nantes, Montpellier, Orléans, and implicitly Paris/generic). Information about DIFFERENT cities is woven together in the same sentences.

This creates a new danger:

When multiple specific details coexist in a flowing text, the exam will MIX details from one entity with details from another.

Step 1: Identify the text type

Quick scan reveals:

  • Defines a concept ("Un parc-relais est...")
  • Explains how it works
  • Gives examples from specific cities
  • Projects future development
  • Neutral, factual tone

Verdict: CONCEPT EXPLANATION TEXT (texte explicatif)

Concept Explanation FeaturesWhat It Means for Questions
Opens with a definitionQ1 often tests if you understood the definition
Lists conditions for successA question will ask about success factors
Gives specific examplesQuestions will test if you assign the right detail to the right city
Ends with future projectionA question will test present vs. future

Step 2: Read questions first — spot the DETAIL TRACKING challenge

  • Q14 → WHERE are parcs-relais? → Tests the DEFINITION
  • Q15 → WHY are they successful? → Tests CONDITIONS
  • Q16 → WHAT is offered in Strasbourg? → Tests SPECIFIC CITY DETAIL
  • Q17 → WHERE does intermodalité work? → Tests CITY-STATUS MATCHING

Questions 16 and 17 are the dangerous ones. They require you to assign the right fact to the right city without mixing things up.

Step 3: The "Entity Tracking Table" technique (NEW)

Before answering, build a mental (or physical) table of WHO gets WHAT:

CityStatus in the TextWhat Happens There
StrasbourgPioneer ("a montré la voie")Pays daily fee, gets free tram tickets. Thousands of places. SUCCESS.
NantesFollower ("suivie par")Same system. SUCCESS ("comme à Nantes").
MontpellierFollower ("suivie par") + Future ("appelée à se développer")Mentioned twice — as follower AND as future development city
OrléansFuture only ("à commencer par")Not yet operational — WILL develop

This table is your cheat sheet. Every question about a specific city can be answered by checking this table.

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #8: When a text mentions multiple entities (cities, people, dates, companies), build a mental tracking table BEFORE answering. The exam WILL try to swap details between entities.

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT — Question by Question

QUESTION 14: Les parcs-relais se situent...

The key sentence:

"Un parc-relais est un parking situé à proximité immédiate d'une station de tramway, de métro, voire d'autobus"

And:

"Il continue alors son voyage en transport en commun, généralement vers le centre-ville"

The logical chain:

Parc-relais = near a tram/metro station
         ↓
Driver leaves car there
         ↓
Takes public transport TO center-ville
         ↓
Therefore: parc-relais is NOT in center-ville
         ↓
It's OUTSIDE center-ville = périphérie

Now let's test each option:

Option A: "en centre-ville"

The text says drivers go FROM the parc-relais TO the centre-ville. If the parc-relais were already in the centre-ville, there would be no need to take a tram. The entire POINT of the service is that the parking is NOT in the center.

But here's why this trap is clever: the text DOES mention "centre-ville" — twice. A speed-reader might associate "parc-relais" with "centre-ville" simply because both appear in the same paragraph.

This is Golden Rule #1 from Project 1 — vocabulary recycling. "Centre-ville" appears in the text but describes the DESTINATION, not the LOCATION.

❌ ELIMINATED — Centre-ville is the destination, not the location

Option B: "en périphérie"

The text says: "à proximité immédiate d'une station de tramway"

Tram/metro stations that serve as entry points to the public transport network (before you reach center-ville) are typically in the periphery (outskirts).

But notice: the text NEVER uses the word "périphérie." This answer is an inference — a logical deduction from the text's information.

What the text saysWhat you must deduceAnswer
Near tram stations + drivers take tram TO centre-villeThe parking must be OUTSIDE centre-ville"En périphérie"

This is different from Projects 1 and 2 where the answer was always a paraphrase of something explicitly stated. Here, the answer requires a logical step beyond the text.

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #9: Sometimes the correct answer is not a direct paraphrase but a LOGICAL INFERENCE — a conclusion you must draw from the text's information. The text gives you the pieces; you must assemble the puzzle.

✅ CORRECT — Logical inference from location + direction of travel

Option C: "dans une station de métro"

The text says: "à proximité immédiate d'une station de tramway, de métro"

"À proximité immédiate" = very close to, NEAR.
"Dans" = INSIDE.

NEAR a metro station ≠ INSIDE a metro station.

Text saysOption C saysThe difference
"à proximité immédiate de" (near)"dans" (inside)NEAR ≠ INSIDE

One preposition changes everything. This is Subtle Substitution (from Project 1), but applied to a preposition rather than a noun.

❌ ELIMINATED — Preposition substitution (near ≠ inside)

Option D: "dans un jardin"

The text never mentions a garden. The word "parc" in "parc-relais" means PARKING LOT, not park/garden. This exploits the double meaning of "parc":

WordMeaning 1Meaning 2
ParcPark / GardenParking lot / Fleet

"Parc-relais" uses meaning 2. Option D pretends it's meaning 1.

This is Golden Rule #6 from Project 2 — same word, different context. But here it's even more subtle because it exploits a compound word where one element has multiple meanings.

❌ ELIMINATED — False meaning of "parc" (parking lot ≠ garden)

🎯 Answer: B — en périphérie

QUESTION 15: Le succès des parcs-relais est lié...

The key sentence:

"La réussite du parc-relais dépend des conditions d'accueil des voitures: il doit être gardé. Et une formule tarifaire attractive doit être proposée aux automobilistes."

The text gives TWO success conditions:

Condition 1: "il doit être gardé" → must be supervised/guarded
Condition 2: "formule tarifaire attractive" → attractive pricing

Now let's test:

Option A: "à la gratuité du service"

The text says "formule tarifaire attractive" — attractive PRICING. Attractive pricing ≠ free. The driver PAYS ("paie pour la journée une somme"). The tram tickets are free, but the parking itself is not. "Gratuité" (free of charge) is an exaggeration of "attractive pricing."

This is the Exaggeration Trap from Q15 in Project 2 — taking a real element and pushing it too far.

❌ ELIMINATED — Exaggeration (attractive pricing ≠ free)

Option B: "à la mentalité des automobilistes"

The text discusses CONDITIONS of the service (guarding, pricing), not the MINDSET of drivers. Nothing about psychology or attitudes.

❌ ELIMINATED — Not in the text (fabrication)

Option C: "à la grève des tramways"

The text mentions tramways as the transport used AFTER parking. Strikes ("grève") are never mentioned. This is pure fabrication — an invented scenario.

❌ ELIMINATED — Pure fabrication

Option D: "à la surveillance des voitures"

The text says: "il doit être gardé"

Text saysOption D saysRelationship
"gardé" (guarded/watched)"surveillance des voitures" (car surveillance)PARAPHRASE ✅

"Gardé" = guarded → "surveillance" = monitoring/watching. Same concept, different words.

But wait — the text gives TWO conditions (guarding AND pricing). Why is only guarding in the answers?

Because the exam only needs ONE correct answer. If both conditions appeared as separate options, there would be two correct answers. The exam chose the condition that was easiest to paraphrase and built traps around the other (pricing → "gratuité" trap).

✅ CORRECT — Direct paraphrase of "gardé"

🎯 Answer: D — à la surveillance des voitures

THE LESSON FROM Q15:

When the text gives MULTIPLE factors (A and B cause C), the correct answer may only reference ONE of them. Don't reject an answer just because it's "incomplete" — it only needs to be accurate, not exhaustive.

QUESTION 16: À Strasbourg, on offre...

This is where Entity Tracking becomes critical.

The key sentence:

"le conducteur de la voiture paie pour la journée une somme correspondant à environ une heure de stationnement en centre-ville, et reçoit gratuitement autant de billets aller-retour en tramway qu'il y a de passagers dans son véhicule."

Let me decompose this sentence precisely:

WHAT THE DRIVER PAYS:
→ A daily fee ≈ one hour of city-center parking

WHAT THE DRIVER RECEIVES FREE:
→ Tram round-trip tickets
→ Number of tickets = number of passengers in the car

Two elements: one PAID, one FREE. The question asks what is OFFERED ("on offre"). "Offre" = gives for free.

Now let's test:

Option A: "des places de parking gratuites"

The driver PAYS for parking ("paie pour la journée une somme"). Parking is NOT free. The text explicitly states a fee is charged.

This trap inverts the paid/free relationship. The parking is paid; the tram tickets are free. Option A claims parking is free.

❌ ELIMINATED — Inversion of paid/free elements

Option B: "des tickets de tramway gratuits"

The text says: "reçoit gratuitement autant de billets aller-retour en tramway"

Text saysOption B saysRelationship
"reçoit gratuitement...billets aller-retour en tramway""tickets de tramway gratuits"PARAPHRASE ✅

"Billets" → "tickets" (synonym)
"Gratuitement" → "gratuits" (same root, different grammar)
"Aller-retour en tramway" → "de tramway" (simplified)

✅ CORRECT — Close paraphrase

Option C: "des locations de véhicules gratuites"

The text mentions "véhicule" — but it refers to the driver's OWN car ("passagers dans son véhicule"). There is no vehicle RENTAL mentioned anywhere.

This is vocabulary recycling (Rule #1) combined with fabrication — "véhicule" is real but "location" (rental) is invented.

❌ ELIMINATED — Vocabulary recycling + fabrication

Option D: "des heures de stationnement en centre-ville"

The text says the daily fee corresponds to "environ une heure de stationnement en centre-ville" — but this describes the PRICE COMPARISON, not what's offered. The text is saying "you pay roughly what one hour of city-center parking would cost." It's a pricing benchmark, not a gift.

This is a sophisticated trap: it takes a comparison element and presents it as the benefit.

TEXT STRUCTURE:
"paie une somme correspondant à une heure de stationnement"
       ↑                              ↑
   THE ACTION (pays)           THE COMPARISON (equivalent to)
                               NOT the gift

❌ ELIMINATED — Misreads a price comparison as a benefit

🎯 Answer: B — des tickets de tramway gratuits

THE NEW LESSON FROM Q16:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #10: When a text contains a COMPARISON or EQUIVALENCE ("correspondant à," "équivalent de," "comme"), the exam may present the comparison benchmark as if it were a separate fact. Always identify WHAT is being compared to WHAT.

This introduces a new trap type:

Trap TypeHow It WorksExample
🪤 Comparison-as-FactTakes a comparison element and presents it as a standalone benefit or fact"une heure de stationnement" (price comparison) → presented as "what is offered"

QUESTION 17: L'intermodalité...

This is the HARDEST question in this text. It tests whether you can assign the correct STATUS to the correct CITY.

Let me re-examine the Entity Tracking Table:

The key sentences:

"C'est Strasbourg qui a montré la voie, suivie par Nantes et Montpellier"
"Succès assuré à Strasbourg [...] comme à Nantes."
"la pratique [...] est appelée à se développer dans d'autres villes, à commencer par Montpellier et Orléans."

Now let me build the PRECISE status map:

CityVerb Tense/MoodStatusTemporal Category
Strasbourg"a montré" (passé composé) + "Succès assuré" (present)Pioneer. Current success.✅ PAST + PRESENT
Nantes"suivie par" (past participle) + "comme à Nantes" (present)Follower. Current success.✅ PAST + PRESENT
Montpellier"suivie par" (past) BUT ALSO "appelée à se développer" (future projection)Started but still developing⚠️ AMBIGUOUS
Orléans"appelée à se développer" (future projection) onlyNot yet successful — future🔮 FUTURE ONLY

This is where the exam gets clever. Montpellier appears in TWO different temporal zones. Orléans appears only in the future zone.

Now let's test:

Option A: "est un succès à Montpellier et Orléans"

The text says intermodalité "est appelée à se développer" in Montpellier and Orléans. "Est appelée à" = is expected to / will likely. This is a FUTURE PROJECTION, not a current success.

Critical grammar point:

French expressionMeaningTemporal value
"est appelée à se développer"is expected to developFUTURE
"est un succès"is a successPRESENT

Option A assigns PRESENT SUCCESS to cities that only have FUTURE PROJECTION status.

❌ ELIMINATED — Treats future projection as present reality

This introduces an absolutely critical skill:

🏆 GOLDEN RULE #11: Pay extreme attention to VERB TENSES and MODAL EXPRESSIONS. "Is a success" ≠ "is expected to become a success." The exam tests whether you distinguish between what IS and what WILL BE / MIGHT BE.

Option B: "se développera à Strasbourg et Nantes"

"Se développera" = will develop (future tense).

But the text says Strasbourg and Nantes already HAVE success ("Succès assuré à Strasbourg [...] comme à Nantes"). They don't need to DEVELOP — they've already succeeded.

This option assigns FUTURE status to cities with PRESENT status. The exact opposite of Option A's error, but the same TYPE of error — temporal misassignment.

❌ ELIMINATED — Assigns future tense to already-successful cities

Option C: "est une réussite à Strasbourg et Nantes"

The text says: "Succès assuré à Strasbourg [...] comme à Nantes"

Text saysOption C saysRelationship
"Succès assuré à Strasbourg...comme à Nantes""est une réussite à Strasbourg et Nantes"PARAPHRASE ✅

"Succès" → "réussite" (synonym)
"Assuré" → "est" (confirmed status)
Same cities, same temporal value (present).

✅ CORRECT — Synonym paraphrase with correct temporal assignment

Option D: "a commencé à Montpellier et Nantes"

The text says: "à commencer par Montpellier et Orléans"

Two problems:

Problem 1: "À commencer par" is an expression meaning "starting with" (listing the first examples). It does NOT mean "has begun at." This is an expression trap (like "rythme de croisière" in Project 1).

ExpressionMeaningNOT
"à commencer par"starting with / first of allhas started at

Problem 2: The text says "Montpellier et Orléans." Option D says "Montpellier et Nantes." Orléans has been swapped for Nantes — a city swap trap.

Two traps in one answer — the exam is testing whether you catch EITHER one.

❌ ELIMINATED — Expression misinterpretation + city swap

🎯 Answer: C — est une réussite à Strasbourg et Nantes

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

The "Entity-Status-Time" Technique (NEW — formalized here)

For ANY text that mentions multiple entities with different statuses, build a three-column mental table:

| ENTITY      | STATUS              | TIME          |
|-------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Strasbourg  | Success             | Present ✅    |
| Nantes      | Success             | Present ✅    |
| Montpellier | Developing          | Future 🔮     |
| Orléans     | Developing          | Future 🔮     |

Then when you read an answer, check ALL THREE columns:

  1. Is the ENTITY correct?
  2. Is the STATUS correct?
  3. Is the TIME correct?

If ANY column is wrong, the answer is wrong.

This technique will be essential in Projects 12, 14, 19, and 20 where multiple people/countries/dates are intertwined.

✅ WHAT YOU SHOULD DO

For Concept Explanation Texts Specifically:

1. Identify the DEFINITION sentence.
It usually starts with "Un X est..." or "On appelle X..." — Q1 almost always tests this.

2. Identify the CONDITIONS for success/failure.
Look for "dépend de," "il faut," "il doit" — a question will target these.

3. Build the Entity Tracking Table immediately.
Before answering ANY question, map who/what gets which attribute.

4. Flag TEMPORAL MARKERS obsessively.
Circle (mentally) every verb tense and every time expression:

Marker TypeExamplesWhat They Signal
Past accomplished"a montré," "a été"Done and confirmed
Present state"est un succès," "succès assuré"Current reality
Future projection"est appelée à," "sera," "va se développer"Not yet real
Expression"à commencer par"Listing, NOT temporal

5. When two answers look similar, find the ONE difference.
Options A and C in Q17 both mention success + two cities. The ONLY difference is WHICH cities. That one difference is everything.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 8: The Temporal Misassignment (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 12, 17, 20, 25.

What it is: An answer assigns the wrong temporal status to an entity — treating future as present, or present as past.

How to detect: Check the VERB TENSE in the text for that specific entity. Then check the verb tense in the answer. Do they match?

In this text: "Est appelée à se développer" (future) → Option A says "est un succès" (present)

Defense: Build the Entity-Status-Time table.

PITFALL 9: The Entity Swap (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 12, 14, 19, 20.

What it is: An answer takes a true fact but assigns it to the WRONG entity (wrong city, wrong person, wrong date).

How to detect: When an answer mentions a specific name/place, go BACK to the text and verify: "Does the text actually say THIS about THIS entity?"

In this text: Option D says "Montpellier et Nantes" — but the text says "Montpellier et Orléans."

PITFALL 10: The Paid/Free Inversion (NEW)

First introduced here. Will reappear in Projects 16, 25.

What it is: In a text that describes both paid and free elements, an answer swaps which is which.

How to detect: When money is involved, explicitly list: "What costs money? What is free?"

In this text: Parking = PAID. Tram tickets = FREE. Option A inverts this.

🌉 BRIDGE TO Chapter 4

Projects 1-3 all dealt with texts that present FACTS — a service, medical conditions, a transport concept. The questions tested whether you understood WHAT the text says.

Project 4 (Grande Enquête de Consommation) introduces a fundamentally different challenge: a FORMAL LETTER with conditions, obligations, and promises.

You will learn:

  • How to distinguish mandatory requirements from optional elements ("il est impératif" vs. "vous n'êtes pas obligé")
  • How to identify the TRUE PURPOSE of a text versus its stated content
  • How to handle conditional promises ("si vous... vous pourrez...")
  • The critical difference between what you MUST do and what you CAN do

The difficulty shifts from "understanding facts" to "understanding rules and conditions."
