Texte 69 : Anatomie d'un plat

Avez-vous remarqué que votre tranche de saumon en super promo est souvent plus colorée qu'une tranche de saumon sauvage qui, elle, tend plutôt vers le rose pâle du fait des crustacés chargés naturellement de caroténoïdes que notre poisson mangeait auparavant ? Car les saumons d'élevage ont changé de régime et sont aujourd'hui gavés de protéines végétales de soja. Résultat : les saumons domestiques sont tout gris. Pour attirer les consommateurs il fallait donc ajouter un cocktail d'additifs chimiques comme l'astaxanthine, également utilisée dans les autobronzants ou l'alimentation des poules pour redonner le jaune d'œuf. Pire, le saumon norvégien, qui constitue 80% de notre consommation, est également plein de pesticides. Agglomérés dans leurs fermes piscicoles, ils sont envahis par les poux de mer, d'où la nécessité de les épouiller avec ces produits phytosanitaires intégrés dans leur nourriture, mais susceptibles d'augmenter les risques de cancer s'ils sont ingérés par l'homme.

Step 2: Map the Text Architecture — The Revelation Chain

This text has no paragraph breaks (like Chapter 4), but it follows a clear escalation structure. Each segment reveals something worse than the last:

SEGMENT 1: THE VISIBLE CLUE (lines 1-2)
    Your promo salmon is MORE colorful than wild salmon
    Wild salmon is naturally pale pink (from crustacean carotenoids)
    → OBSERVATION: something is wrong with the color

SEGMENT 3: THE COSMETIC FIX (lines 3-5)

Step 3: Identify the NEW Challenge — Multi-Step Cause-Chain Reasoning

This text's defining feature is a chain of causes and effects where each step triggers the next. This is more complex than anything you've seen before:

THE CAUSE CHAIN:

STEP 1: Diet change
  Farmed salmon fed soy protein instead of crustaceans
           │
           ▼
STEP 2: Color loss
  Salmon flesh turns grey (no natural carotenoids)
           │
           ▼
STEP 3: Chemical coloring
  Astaxanthine added to restore pink color
  (to make the fish attractive to consumers)
           │
           ▼
STEP 4: Parasite problem (separate chain)
  Crowded fish farms → sea lice infestation
           │
           ▼
STEP 5: Pesticide treatment
  Pesticides added to food to kill parasites
           │
           ▼
STEP 6: Human health risk
  Pesticides may increase cancer risk if ingested by humans

There are actually two parallel chains that converge on one conclusion: farmed salmon is artificially colored AND potentially dangerous.

CHAIN A (cosmetic):  Diet change → grey flesh → chemical coloring → consumer deception
CHAIN B (health):    Crowded farms → parasites → pesticides → cancer risk

The exam will ask about specific links in these chains. You must know which step causes which effect, and not confuse steps from Chain A with steps from Chain B.

Step 4: Master the "Each Step Has ONE Function" Principle

This is the chapter's core analytical tool. In a cause chain, each step serves one specific purpose. The exam tests whether you can identify the correct function of each step:

🧩 HOW TO SOLVE EACH QUESTION

QUESTION 17: Cet article traite...

Step 1: Identify the question type

"Cet article traite" = this article deals with. This is a GLOBAL TOPIC question — what is the text about overall? Not one detail, but the entire text's subject.

Step 2: Determine the text's overall subject

Survey all four segments:

Segment 1: Observation about farmed salmon color
Segment 2: Diet change in farmed salmon
Segment 3: Chemical additives in farmed salmon
Segment 4: Pesticides in farmed salmon

COMMON THREAD: Everything describes what happens TO farmed salmon
               = PRACTICES in salmon farming/aquaculture

The text describes practices — what the industry does to farmed salmon (feeding, coloring, treating). It's not about what consumers want, not about the absence of regulation, and not about wild salmon.

Step 3: Test each option

Option A: "Des pratiques en vigueur dans l'élevage de saumons"
(Practices in use in salmon farming.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text describes: soy feeding (practice), chemical coloring (practice), pesticide treatment (practice). ALL are practices currently in use in salmon farming. "En vigueur" (in use/current) matches the present tense of the text. "Élevage" (farming/breeding) matches "saumons d'élevage" and "fermes piscicoles."Every segment describes an industry practice.✅ COMPLETE MATCH

Option B: "Des exigences alimentaires des consommateurs de saumons"
(The dietary demands of salmon consumers.)

TestReasoningVerdict
The text mentions consumers only once: "Pour attirer les consommateurs." Consumers are the TARGET of the practices, not the SUBJECT. The text never describes what consumers demand or require. It describes what the INDUSTRY does. The focus is on production, not on consumption demands.Consumers are mentioned but not the subject.❌ CONFUSES TARGET WITH SUBJECT

Option C: "De l'absence de contrôle sanitaire dans l'élevage des saumons"
(The absence of health/sanitary controls in salmon farming.)

TestReasoningVerdict
SOPHISTICATED TRAP. The text reveals alarming practices (chemicals, pesticides, cancer risk). A reader might CONCLUDE that controls are lacking. But the text never SAYS controls are absent. It describes WHAT happens, not WHETHER it's regulated. The absence of regulation is a reader's inference, not the text's stated topic. The text could describe these practices in a context where they ARE regulated but still concerning.The text describes practices, not regulatory gaps.❌ READER'S INFERENCE ≠ TEXT'S TOPIC

This is a critical distinction. Let me elaborate:

TEXT SAYS:        "Pesticides are added to salmon food"
                  "They may increase cancer risk"
                  
TEXT DOES NOT SAY: "There are no health controls"
                   "Regulations are absent"
                   "No one is monitoring this"

Option C claims the text is ABOUT absent controls.
But the text is ABOUT the practices themselves.
The reader might THINK controls are lacking,
but the text never addresses regulation at all.

Option D: "Des nouvelles contraintes alimentaires des saumons sauvages"
(The new dietary constraints of wild salmon.)

TestReasoningVerdict
Wild salmon is mentioned only as a COMPARISON point: "une tranche de saumon sauvage qui, elle, tend plutôt vers le rose pâle." The entire text is about FARMED salmon (saumons d'élevage), not wild salmon. Wild salmon's diet hasn't changed — it still eats crustaceans. The constraints are on FARMED salmon.Wild salmon is a reference point, not the subject.❌ COMPARISON ELEMENT MISTAKEN FOR SUBJECT

✅ ANSWER: A — Des pratiques en vigueur dans l'élevage de saumons

The mechanism that got you here:

  1. Surveyed all four segments for the common thread
  2. Identified "practices in salmon farming" as the unifying subject
  3. Defeated Trap B by distinguishing the TARGET (consumers) from the SUBJECT (industry)
  4. Defeated Trap C by separating the TEXT'S TOPIC (practices) from the READER'S INFERENCE (absent controls)
  5. Defeated Trap D by recognizing wild salmon as a comparison, not the subject

New concept — Text Topic vs. Reader's Inference:

This is one of the most important distinctions in reading comprehension:

TEXT'S TOPIC:        What the text EXPLICITLY discusses
READER'S INFERENCE:  What the reader CONCLUDES from the text

Example:
  TEXT'S TOPIC:      Pesticides are added to salmon food (FACT described)
  READER'S INFERENCE: Health controls must be lacking (CONCLUSION drawn)

The exam asks about the TEXT'S TOPIC, not the READER'S INFERENCE.
Option C describes a valid inference — but the text isn't ABOUT that inference.

This distinction builds on the fact-vs-attitude skill from Chapter 8 (→ Project 8). There, you learned that neutral descriptions ≠ expressed attitudes. Here, you learn that described practices ≠ diagnosed regulatory failures.

QUESTION 19: Les traitements administrés aux poissons servent à...

Step 1: Identify the question type

"Les traitements" = the treatments. "Servent à" = serve to / are used for. This asks about the PURPOSE of the treatments. Go to Segment 4 (the pesticide section).

Step 2: Identify what treatments are described and why

The text describes ONE treatment in Segment 4:

"ils sont envahis par les poux de mer, d'où la nécessité de les épouiller avec ces produits phytosanitaires intégrés dans leur nourriture"

ProblemSea lice (poux de mer) — parasites

"Poux de mer" = sea lice = parasites. "Épouiller" = to delouse = to remove parasites. The treatment's purpose is to eliminate parasites.

Step 3: Test each option

(Make them grow faster.)

The pesticides don't improve anything — they treat a parasite problem while creating a health risk.Treatments mask or create problems, not improve quality.❌ CONTRADICTS THE TEXT'S ENTIRE ARGUMENT

✅ WHAT SHOULD BE DONE

1. Map the Cause Chain Before Answering

This is the chapter's defining strategy. Whenever a text describes a process with multiple steps, draw the chain BEFORE touching the questions:

STEP 1: [CAUSE]     → produces → [EFFECT 1]
STEP 2: [EFFECT 1]  → requires → [ACTION 1]
STEP 3: [ACTION 1]  → creates  → [NEW PROBLEM]
STEP 4: [NEW PROBLEM] → requires → [ACTION 2]
STEP 5: [ACTION 2]  → risks    → [CONSEQUENCE]

For this text:

CHAIN A (Color):
  Natural diet (crustaceans) → pink flesh
  Changed diet (soy) → grey flesh
  Grey flesh → chemical coloring (astaxanthine) → fake pink

CHAIN B (Health):
  Crowded farms → sea lice infestation
  Sea lice → pesticide treatment
  Pesticides → cancer risk for humans

Each question targets ONE specific link. Your chain map tells you instantly which link to examine.

2. Identify Escalation Markers

Exposé texts use escalation language — each revelation is presented as worse than the last. Spotting these markers helps you understand the text's structure:

"Pire" is the most important marker. It tells you: "What I just told you was bad. What I'm about to tell you is WORSE." Everything after "Pire" is the text's most critical content.

3. Distinguish "Text Topic" from "Reader's Inference"

Formalized from Q17's analysis, this principle applies to ALL critical/exposé texts:

THE TEXT DESCRIBES:           THE READER MAY CONCLUDE:
├── Practices (what is done)  ├── Controls are lacking
├── Processes (how it works)  ├── Regulations should change  
├── Risks (what could happen) ├── The industry is irresponsible
└── Facts (what exists)       └── Consumers should boycott

EXAM ASKS ABOUT: What the text DESCRIBES
NOT ABOUT: What the reader CONCLUDES

If an option describes a reader's likely conclusion rather than the text's stated topic, it's wrong — even if the conclusion is perfectly reasonable.

4. Check Every Adjective in Multi-Word Phrases

The noun is the same (protéines).
The preposition phrase is close (de soja / no specification).
Only the ADJECTIVE is swapped.

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT BE DONE

1. Do NOT Confuse Two Parallel Chains

This text contains TWO chains: Color Chain (A) and Health Chain (B). The exam asks about specific links in specific chains. Do NOT mix them:

WRONG: "The pesticides serve to color the fish"
       (Pesticides are in Chain B / Color is in Chain A)

WRONG: "The astaxanthine serves to kill parasites"
       (Astaxanthine is in Chain A / Parasites are in Chain B)

RIGHT: "The astaxanthine serves to restore color" (Chain A)
RIGHT: "The pesticides serve to kill parasites" (Chain B)

Each treatment has ONE function. Don't assign Chain A's function to Chain B's treatment or vice versa.

2. Do NOT Assume the Text Discusses Everything Related to the Topic

The text discusses salmon farming practices. It does NOT discuss:

3. Do NOT Read "Pour Attirer les Consommateurs" as "Consumer Demands"

This is the core trap in Q17 (Option B). The text says:

"Pour attirer les consommateurs, il fallait ajouter des additifs chimiques."
= In order to attract consumers, chemicals had to be added.

This describes the INDUSTRY'S strategy to attract consumers. It does NOT describe what consumers demand or require. The difference:

INDUSTRY STRATEGY:  "We color the fish to attract buyers"
                    → The industry acts TO INFLUENCE consumers
                    → Subject: the industry's actions

CONSUMER DEMAND:    "Consumers require colorful fish"
                    → Consumers act TO INFLUENCE the industry  
                    → Subject: consumers' requirements

The text describes the industry acting on consumers, not consumers demanding from the industry. The direction of influence matters.

4. Do NOT Mistake the Overall Message for a Specific Link's Function

Trap B in Q19 says treatments "improve the quality of flesh." This sounds like it could be the overall GOAL of the industry. But the specific treatments (pesticides) don't improve quality — they address a specific problem (parasites).

OVERALL INDUSTRY GOAL (implied): Sell attractive, marketable fish
SPECIFIC TREATMENT FUNCTION:     Kill sea lice

The question asks about the SPECIFIC FUNCTION, not the OVERALL GOAL.

This is a scope mismatch — the option operates at the wrong level of specificity. The exam asks about what ONE treatment does, and the trap offers what the ENTIRE industry aims for.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The "Reasonable Inference" Trap

Option C in Q17 ("absence of health controls") is the most dangerous trap in this text because it's a perfectly reasonable inference. After reading about chemicals and pesticides in food, any reader would think: "Where are the health inspectors?"

But the exam doesn't ask what you think. It asks what the text is about. And the text is about PRACTICES, not about the ABSENCE OF REGULATION.

How to detect this pitfall: Ask yourself: "Does the text USE words like 'contrôle,' 'réglementation,' 'inspection,' 'surveillance,' 'normes'?" If not, the text isn't about regulation — even if the content makes you think about it.

WORDS PRESENT:    gavés, additifs, astaxanthine, pesticides, poux de mer
WORDS ABSENT:     contrôle, réglementation, normes, inspection, surveillance

TOPIC = What words ARE present describe = PRACTICES
TOPIC ≠ What words ARE ABSENT suggest = REGULATION

Pitfall 2: The "Opposite of the Text's Spirit" Trap

Option B in Q19 ("improve flesh quality") goes against the text's ENTIRE critical spirit. The text is an EXPOSÉ — it reveals bad practices. Everything in it is framed negatively:

TEXT'S SPIRIT:    CRITICAL (things are bad, getting worse)
  - "gavés" (force-fed) → negative connotation
  - "tout gris" (all grey) → unappealing
  - "cocktail d'additifs chimiques" → alarming
  - "Pire" (worse) → escalation
  - "risques de cancer" → health alarm

OPTION B'S SPIRIT: POSITIVE (treatments improve things)
  - "améliorer la qualité" → improvement

When the text is entirely critical and an option is entirely positive, there's a fundamental tonal mismatch. The option contradicts not just a specific fact but the text's overall stance.

Defense: Before selecting an option, ask: "Does this option's TONE match the text's TONE?" In an exposé, positive options about the industry are almost always wrong.

Pitfall 3: The "I Know About Fish Farming" Override

Candidates with knowledge of aquaculture might know that:

  • Fish farms do use growth hormones (→ tempting for Option A in Q19)
  • Consumer demand does drive industry practices (→ tempting for Option B in Q17)
  • Wild salmon populations are declining (→ tempting for Option D in Q17)

None of these facts appear in THIS text. The exam tests your reading of THIS text, not your knowledge of fish farming.

YOUR KNOWLEDGE:              THIS TEXT:
Growth hormones exist    →   Not mentioned
Consumer demands matter  →   Not the subject
Wild salmon decline      →   Not discussed

Rule (reinforced from → Project 1): The text is your only authority. External knowledge is irrelevant and potentially misleading.

Pitfall 4: The "Super Promo = Pricing Discussion" Confusion

The text opens with "votre tranche de saumon en super promo" (your super-discount salmon fillet). This mention of "promo" might make candidates think the text discusses pricing, leading them toward Option A in Q18 ("align prices with Norwegian market").

But "super promo" is merely the entry point — the observation that hooks the reader. It's not the text's SUBJECT. The subject is what happens BEHIND the discount: the hidden practices that make cheap salmon possible.

"SUPER PROMO" FUNCTION:  Context / hook / entry point
TEXT'S SUBJECT:          What's hidden behind the attractive price and color

Entry points are not subjects. The opening sentence draws you in; the rest of the text reveals the reality.

The Cause-Chain Analysis Framework

For any text describing a process, use this template:

□ Identify the STARTING CONDITION (what exists naturally)
□ Identify the CHANGE (what humans/industry altered)
□ Trace each CONSEQUENCE of the change
□ Note each RESPONSE to each consequence
□ Note each SIDE EFFECT of each response
□ Draw the chain: Condition → Change → Consequence → Response → Side Effect
□ For each exam question, identify WHICH LINK is being asked about
□ Match the option to THAT SPECIFIC LINK, not to the overall chain

🌉 BRIDGE TO PROJECT 10

You've now mastered cause-chain reasoning in a text with one subject (salmon farming) and two parallel chains (color and health). The text was short, dense, and single-voiced.

In Chapter 10 (Un Piège pour les Baleines — Following a Nature Narrative), you'll encounter a longer text with multiple actors (whales, polar bears, Inuit communities, scientists) each performing different actions. This introduces:

  • Multi-actor tracking — who did what? Who tried what? Who succeeded?
  • Distinguishing attempted vs. successful actions — the Inuit tried to save the whales (did they succeed?)
  • Separating alarm from reassurance — scientists say the phenomenon isn't alarming, even though it looks dramatic
  • A text where the emotional impact (dramatic nature scene) conflicts with the scientific assessment (this is normal)

The cause-chain skill from this chapter will help you trace how one event leads to another in the natural world. But you'll add the complexity of tracking who acts at each step and whether their action succeeds or fails.
