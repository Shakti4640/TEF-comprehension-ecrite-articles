📰 COMPLETE ARTICLE

Les potins ? C'est aussi de l'info (texte à trous)

Les sociologues britanniques ont effectué une étude sur les conversations qui se déroulent sur le lieu de travail. (...36...) Neuf personnes sur dix ont déjà révélé des confidences au travail. Il s'agit dans la moitié des cas d'informations personnelles. (...37...) Questions confidentielles, promotions et augmentations, mutations et licenciements — rien n'est épargné. Pourquoi les salariés sont-ils donc prêts à risquer leur carrière en révélant des informations sensibles ? (...38...) Ils utilisent l'information pour garder le contrôle.

(...40...) Dans certains cas, le responsable de service ne découvre un problème interne qu'en bavardant avec les gens au café, après le travail. De cette façon on peut discrètement faire passer l'information au supérieur hiérarchique.

THE QUESTIONS:

Question 36:

  • A) Selon cette étude, 80% des conversations sont consacrées aux commérages.
  • B) Selon cette étude, 80% des salariés communiquent difficilement entre eux.
  • C) Selon cette étude, 80% des salariés manquent de contact avec l'administration.
  • D) Selon cette étude, la communication améliore la rentabilité de l'entreprise de 80%.

Question 37:

  • A) Mais un tiers porte sur les problèmes d'argent et de santé.
  • B) Mais un tiers touche les problèmes familiaux et les enfants.
  • C) Mais un tiers de ces conversations concernent le travail.
  • D) Mais un tiers des cas recensés ont un rapport à la politique.

Question 38:

  • A) Certains agissent par peur de l'administration.
  • B) Certains cherchent à acquérir du pouvoir.
  • C) Certains essayent de soutenir leurs collègues.
  • D) Certains le font sans aucun objectif particulier.

Question 39:

  • A) Les employeurs voient donc les colporteurs de rumeurs d'un très mauvais œil.
  • B) Aussi les patrons examinent-ils chaque information avec beaucoup de réserve.
  • C) De ce fait, l'administration favorise la circulation des potins par tous les moyens.
  • D) Les employeurs se montrent donc assez condescendants envers ce genre de potins.

Question 40:

  • A) De surcroît, l'information officieuse induit régulièrement l'administration en erreur.
  • B) Par ailleurs, par le biais des commérages, l'employeur fait passer les mesures impopulaires.
  • C) Cependant, cette forme d'échanges représente l'unique moyen de pression sur l'employeur.
  • D) Pourtant, les canaux d'information informels sont parfois le seul moyen de repérer les problèmes.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

Why This Is Your Phase 4 Entry Point

In Chapters 9-10, you learned basic texte à trous: checking logical flow, matching connectors, verifying pronoun references. In Chapters 14-15, you applied these skills to medical and workplace contexts.

Now everything changes. This text is qualitatively harder for five specific reasons:

REASON 1: The text has an ARGUMENTATIVE STRUCTURE with a TWIST

This isn't a linear description. It's a three-act argument:

ACT 1 (Gaps 36-38): DESCRIPTION — What gossip looks like at work
                     Statistics → Content → Motivation

ACT 2 (Gap 39):     NEGATIVE JUDGMENT — Gossip costs companies money
                     Employers react negatively

ACT 3 (Gap 40):     REVERSAL — But gossip is ALSO useful
                     The title promised this: "C'est aussi de l'info"

The title "Les potins ? C'est aussi de l'info" is your MASTER KEY. It tells you: this article will FIRST describe gossip, THEN criticize it, THEN argue it has VALUE. Gap 40 MUST deliver on the "aussi de l'info" promise.

REASON 2: Statistical data CONSTRAINS gap choices

The text uses precise numbers (80%, 9/10, half, one-third, one in six). These numbers create a mathematical logic chain that narrows your options. If the text says "half are personal information" and the gap says "but a third concern work," you can CHECK: does half + one-third leave a reasonable remainder? This kind of arithmetic reasoning was never required in Phases 1-3.

REASON 3: The gaps are SEMANTICALLY interdependent

In basic texte à trous (Chapter 9), each gap was relatively independent — you could solve them in any order. Here, the gaps form a CHAIN: Gap 36's content determines what Gap 37 can say, which determines the context for Gap 38. Solving one incorrectly cascades into errors on the others.

REASON 4: Power dynamics vocabulary

The text uses workplace hierarchy language: salariés, carrière, informations sensibles, contrôle, employeurs, supérieur hiérarchique, administration. The correct answers use this same register. Wrong answers either mismatch the register or misrepresent the power relationships.

REASON 5: The final gap REVERSES the argument

This is the hardest aspect. After Gap 39 establishes that gossip is BAD for companies, Gap 40 must introduce the OPPOSITE view: gossip can be USEFUL. You must select a phrase that CONTRADICTS the preceding paragraph — a concession connector ("pourtant," "cependant") is your signal.

The Structural Map (with gaps)

PARAGRAPH 1: THE STUDY'S FINDINGS
├── Context: British sociologists studied workplace conversations
├── [GAP 36]: Key statistical finding of the study
├── Detail: 9/10 have revealed confidences at work
├── Detail: Half are personal information
├── [GAP 37]: What the OTHER portion concerns
├── Examples: Confidential matters, promotions, transfers, layoffs
├── Question: WHY do employees risk their careers?
├── [GAP 38]: The MOTIVATION behind gossip
└── Consequence: They use information to maintain control

PARAGRAPH 2: THE NEGATIVE IMPACT
├── Thesis: Gossip can cost companies dearly
├── [GAP 39]: HOW employers react to this
└── Evidence: 1 in 6 companies has formally warned employees

PARAGRAPH 3: THE POSITIVE REVERSAL
├── [GAP 40]: HOWEVER, gossip also has value
├── Example: Managers discover problems through informal chats
└── Mechanism: Information passes discreetly to hierarchy

The Master Strategy: Anchor-and-Chain

For advanced texte à trous, use the Anchor-and-Chain method:

STEP 1: Identify ANCHORS — fixed text that CANNOT change
         (sentences before and after each gap)

STEP 2: Build CHAINS — logical connections between anchors
         (what MUST the gap contain to connect anchor A to anchor B?)

STEP 3: Test CONNECTORS — does the gap's opening word match the
         logical relationship? (mais = contrast, donc = consequence,
         pourtant = reversal, de surcroît = addition)

STEP 4: Verify COHERENCE — read the entire paragraph with your
         chosen gap inserted. Does it flow naturally?

🧩 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

GAP 36: The Study's Key Finding

ANCHORS:

BEFORE: "Les sociologues britanniques ont effectué une étude sur les
         conversations qui se déroulent sur le lieu de travail."

[GAP 36]

AFTER:  "Neuf personnes sur dix ont déjà révélé des confidences au travail."

CHAIN ANALYSIS:

The BEFORE anchor tells us: a study was conducted about workplace conversations.
The AFTER anchor gives a specific finding: 9 out of 10 people have revealed confidences.

The gap MUST:

  1. Present a finding from this study (it's the study's first result)
  2. Be about conversations at work (the study's topic)
  3. Lead logically INTO the "9/10 confidences" statistic (the gap's finding should make the 9/10 stat a natural continuation)

Step 1: Check each option.

Option A — "Selon cette étude, 80% des conversations sont consacrées aux commérages."

  • "Selon cette étude" → introduces a study finding ✓ (matches BEFORE anchor)
  • "80% des conversations" → about workplace conversations ✓ (matches study topic)
  • "consacrées aux commérages" → dedicated to gossip ✓ (matches article topic: "potins")

Does it connect to the AFTER anchor? The gap says 80% of conversations are gossip. Then the text says 9/10 have revealed confidences. This flows: the study found gossip dominates conversations → and most people participate in it. Logical progression from GENERAL finding (80% of conversations) to SPECIFIC finding (9/10 people participate).

✅ STRONG CANDIDATE

Option B — "Selon cette étude, 80% des salariés communiquent difficilement entre eux."

  • "communiquent difficilement" → employees have DIFFICULTY communicating

This doesn't match the article's topic at all. The article is about GOSSIP (successful, active communication), not about communication DIFFICULTIES. If employees communicate poorly, why would 9/10 of them reveal confidences? The AFTER anchor contradicts this gap: people who communicate with difficulty don't share secrets with 90% frequency.

❌ ELIMINATED — Contradicts the AFTER anchor and the article's theme

Option C — "Selon cette étude, 80% des salariés manquent de contact avec l'administration."

  • "manquent de contact avec l'administration" → lack of contact with management

This introduces a management-employee disconnect that is NOT the article's topic. The article is about GOSSIP between colleagues, not about hierarchical communication gaps. Nothing before or after this gap discusses administrative contact.

❌ ELIMINATED — Introduces an irrelevant theme

Option D — "Selon cette étude, la communication améliore la rentabilité de l'entreprise de 80%."

  • "améliore la rentabilité de 80%" → communication improves profitability by 80%

This is about ECONOMIC PERFORMANCE, which is irrelevant to the study described. The study is about the CONTENT of conversations, not their effect on profits. Also, 80% improvement in profitability is an extraordinary claim that the text never substantiates.

❌ ELIMINATED — Wrong topic + implausible statistic

✅ ANSWER: A

The Logic Chain:

Study conducted → [80% of conversations = gossip] → 9/10 people reveal confidences
     ↓                      ↓                                ↓
  CONTEXT              GAP 36 (A)                     CONTINUATION
 (general)          (key finding)                   (specific detail)

The gap provides the study's HEADLINE finding, and the AFTER anchor provides supporting detail. This is a general-to-specific progression — one of the most common patterns in academic texts.

GAP 37: What the Other Portion Concerns

ANCHORS:

BEFORE: "Il s'agit dans la moitié des cas d'informations personnelles."

[GAP 37]

AFTER:  "Questions confidentielles, promotions et augmentations,
         mutations et licenciements — rien n'est épargné."

CHAIN ANALYSIS:

This is where the mathematical logic becomes critical.

The BEFORE anchor says: HALF of the confidences involve personal information.
The gap must tell us what the OTHER PORTION involves.
The AFTER anchor gives EXAMPLES of this other portion.

Let's look at the AFTER anchor's examples:

  • "Questions confidentielles" → confidential matters (workplace)
  • "promotions et augmentations" → promotions and raises (workplace)
  • "mutations et licenciements" → transfers and layoffs (workplace)

ALL of these examples are about WORK. They are professional/career matters, not personal ones.

So the gap MUST say: the other portion concerns WORK-related topics.

Now, every option starts with "Mais un tiers" (but a third). This gives us the MATHEMATICAL CHECK:

HALF (50%) = personal information (BEFORE anchor)
A THIRD (33%) = [gap content]
REMAINING (17%) = unspecified
TOTAL: 50% + 33% + 17% = 100% ✓

This arithmetic works. Now let's check WHAT the "third" is about:

Option A — "Mais un tiers porte sur les problèmes d'argent et de santé."

"Problèmes d'argent et de santé" = money and health problems. These sound PERSONAL, not professional. But wait — the BEFORE anchor already said "half = personal information." If the gap ALSO describes personal topics, there's a REDUNDANCY problem: half is personal, and then a third is ALSO personal (money/health)? That would mean 83% is personal. But the AFTER anchor lists exclusively PROFESSIONAL examples. The gap needs to TRANSITION from personal to professional.

❌ ELIMINATED — Redundant with "personal information" + contradicts AFTER anchor examples

Option B — "Mais un tiers touche les problèmes familiaux et les enfants."

"Problèmes familiaux et les enfants" = family problems and children. Again, this is PERSONAL information. Same redundancy problem as Option A: it doesn't transition to the professional examples in the AFTER anchor.

❌ ELIMINATED — Same problem as A: personal when AFTER anchor is professional

Option C — "Mais un tiers de ces conversations concernent le travail."

"Concernent le travail" = concern WORK.

Let's verify:

  • BEFORE: half = personal ✓
  • GAP: a third = work-related ✓ (CONTRAST with personal — hence "mais")
  • AFTER: examples of work topics (promotions, transfers, layoffs) ✓✓✓

The "mais" (but) creates the CONTRAST: half is personal, BUT a third is about work. Then the AFTER anchor provides EXAMPLES of these work-related topics. Perfect chain.

✅ CORRECT

Option D — "Mais un tiers des cas recensés ont un rapport à la politique."

"Rapport à la politique" = related to politics. None of the AFTER anchor examples involve politics. Promotions, raises, transfers, layoffs — these are workplace HR matters, not political topics.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Politics" doesn't match the AFTER anchor examples

✅ ANSWER: C

The Critical Lesson: The Before-Gap-After Triangle

BEFORE:  "moitié... informations personnelles"  [PERSONAL]
           ↓ (contrast: "mais")
GAP 37:  "un tiers... concernent le travail"    [PROFESSIONAL]
           ↓ (examples follow)
AFTER:   "promotions, augmentations, mutations,  [PROFESSIONAL EXAMPLES]
          licenciements"

The gap serves as a PIVOT — it transitions from the personal domain (BEFORE) to the professional domain (AFTER). The connector "mais" signals this pivot.

RULE: In texte à trous, when the BEFORE anchor describes category X and the AFTER anchor gives examples of category Y, the gap MUST transition from X to Y. The connector word ("mais," "en revanche," "cependant") signals this transition.

GAP 38: The Motivation Behind Gossip

ANCHORS:

BEFORE: "Pourquoi les salariés sont-ils donc prêts à risquer leur carrière
         en révélant des informations sensibles ?"

[GAP 38]

AFTER:  "Ils utilisent l'information pour garder le contrôle."

CHAIN ANALYSIS:

The BEFORE anchor asks a WHY question: why do employees risk their careers?
The gap MUST provide the ANSWER to this question.
The AFTER anchor ELABORATES on this answer: they use information to maintain control.

The word "contrôle" (control) in the AFTER anchor is the KEY. It tells us the motivation is about POWER — using information strategically to gain or maintain influence.

Step 1: Check each option.

Option A — "Certains agissent par peur de l'administration."

"Par peur" = out of fear. Fear is a DEFENSIVE motivation. But the AFTER anchor says they use information "pour garder le contrôle" — that's an OFFENSIVE, strategic motivation. Fear and control are psychologically opposite:

FEAR = I act because I'm threatened (passive, defensive)
CONTROL = I act to gain power (active, strategic)

The AFTER anchor describes active power-seeking. Option A describes passive fear. They don't connect.

❌ ELIMINATED — Defensive motivation contradicts offensive AFTER anchor

Option B — "Certains cherchent à acquérir du pouvoir."

"Acquérir du pouvoir" = acquire power.

Let's verify the chain:

  • BEFORE (question): Why risk your career?
  • GAP: To acquire power ✓
  • AFTER: They use information to maintain control ✓

"Acquérir du pouvoir" → "garder le contrôle" = POWER acquisition → POWER maintenance. These are two aspects of the same motivation: first you GET power, then you KEEP it. The gap introduces the motivation, the AFTER anchor describes the mechanism.

✅ CORRECT

Option C — "Certains essayent de soutenir leurs collègues."

"Soutenir leurs collègues" = support their colleagues. This is an ALTRUISTIC motivation. But the AFTER anchor says they use information for CONTROL — that's SELF-INTERESTED, not altruistic.

❌ ELIMINATED — Altruistic motivation contradicts self-interested AFTER anchor

Option D — "Certains le font sans aucun objectif particulier."

"Sans aucun objectif" = without any particular goal. But the AFTER anchor says they DO have a goal: "pour garder le contrôle." You can't say someone has NO goal in one sentence and then describe their SPECIFIC goal in the next.

❌ ELIMINATED — "No goal" directly contradicts "pour garder le contrôle"

✅ ANSWER: B

The Logic Chain:

WHY risk career? → [To acquire power] → They use info to maintain control
    QUESTION          GAP 38 (B)              ELABORATION
    (BEFORE)          (ANSWER)                (AFTER)

This is a Question → Answer → Elaboration chain. The BEFORE asks, the gap answers, the AFTER explains HOW.

RULE: When the BEFORE anchor is a question, the gap MUST be the answer. Then check: does the AFTER anchor logically elaborate on your chosen answer? If the AFTER anchor contradicts or is irrelevant to your gap choice, you've chosen wrong.

GAP 39: Employer Reaction

ANCHORS:

BEFORE: "Les commérages peuvent coûter cher à l'entreprise."

[GAP 39]

AFTER:  "Près d'une société sur six a déjà mis formellement en garde
         ses salariés à ce sujet."

CHAIN ANALYSIS:

We've now entered ACT 2: THE NEGATIVE JUDGMENT. The text shifts from describing gossip (Act 1) to evaluating its COST.

The BEFORE anchor establishes: gossip is EXPENSIVE for companies.
The AFTER anchor provides evidence: 1 in 6 companies has formally warned employees.

The gap MUST connect these two: if gossip costs money → [employer reaction] → companies formally warn employees.

The AFTER anchor's action ("mis formellement en garde" = formally warned) is a NEGATIVE reaction — employers are AGAINST gossip. The gap must reflect this negativity.

Step 1: Check each option.

Option A — "Les employeurs voient donc les colporteurs de rumeurs d'un très mauvais œil."

  • "donc" = therefore (consequence of gossip costing money) ✓
  • "voient... d'un très mauvais œil" = view very negatively ✓
  • "colporteurs de rumeurs" = rumor-spreaders (= gossipers) ✓

Chain test:

Gossip costs money → [Employers view gossipers very negatively] → 1/6 formally warn employees
    CAUSE                      GAP 39 (A)                              EVIDENCE

The logical flow is: gossip is expensive (CAUSE) → employers disapprove (REACTION) → some formalize this disapproval as warnings (ACTION). This is a perfect cause → reaction → evidence chain.

✅ STRONG CANDIDATE

Option B — "Aussi les patrons examinent-ils chaque information avec beaucoup de réserve."

"Examinent avec réserve" = examine with reservation/caution. This suggests employers are CAUTIOUS about information — they verify before acting. But the AFTER anchor says employers WARN employees, not that they verify information. The emphasis is on DISAPPROVAL (warning against gossip), not CAUTION (checking facts).

Also, "examinent chaque information" suggests employers ENGAGE with the gossip content, which contradicts the warning approach (which aims to STOP gossip, not analyze it).

❌ ELIMINATED — Describes caution/engagement instead of disapproval/prohibition

Option C — "De ce fait, l'administration favorise la circulation des potins par tous les moyens."

"Favorise la circulation des potins" = ENCOURAGES gossip circulation. This is the complete OPPOSITE of the AFTER anchor, where companies WARN AGAINST gossip. You can't encourage gossip in one sentence and warn against it in the next.

❌ ELIMINATED — Direct logical contradiction with AFTER anchor

Option D — "Les employeurs se montrent donc assez condescendants envers ce genre de potins."

"Condescendants" = condescending, but in this context it implies employers look DOWN on gossip while TOLERATING it. This suggests a passive, dismissive attitude. But the AFTER anchor describes ACTIVE, FORMAL warnings — that's much stronger than mere condescension. If employers were merely condescending, they wouldn't bother with formal warnings.

More critically, "condescendant" carries a nuance of tolerant superiority — "I'm above this but I'll allow it." The AFTER anchor shows employers taking CONCRETE PUNITIVE ACTION, which is the opposite of tolerant allowance.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Tolerant condescension" contradicts "formal warning"

✅ ANSWER: A

The Logic Chain:

CAUSE:      "coûter cher à l'entreprise"
    ↓ (donc = therefore)
REACTION:   [employeurs voient d'un très mauvais œil]    GAP 39 (A)
    ↓ (evidence follows)
EVIDENCE:   "une société sur six a mis en garde ses salariés"

This is a Cause → Reaction → Evidence chain. The BEFORE gives the cause, the gap gives the reaction, the AFTER provides supporting evidence.

RULE: When the BEFORE anchor states a PROBLEM and the AFTER anchor shows a CONCRETE ACTION, the gap must provide the ATTITUDE or JUDGMENT that motivates that action. The attitude must be STRONG enough to justify the action described.

GAP 40: The Reversal — Gossip Has Value

ANCHORS:

BEFORE: (End of paragraph 2) "Près d'une société sur six a déjà mis
         formellement en garde ses salariés à ce sujet."

[GAP 40]

AFTER:  "Dans certains cas, le responsable de service ne découvre un
         problème interne qu'en bavardant avec les gens au café, après
         le travail. De cette façon on peut discrètement faire passer
         l'information au supérieur hiérarchique."

CHAIN ANALYSIS:

This is the most critical gap — and the most difficult. It's where the article delivers on its title's promise: "C'est aussi de l'info."

The BEFORE anchor concludes ACT 2 (gossip is bad → companies warn against it).
The AFTER anchor begins ACT 3 (gossip helps managers discover problems → information flows upward).

The gap MUST:

  1. REVERSE the negative judgment from Act 2
  2. INTRODUCE the positive utility of gossip
  3. Use a CONCESSION CONNECTOR (pourtant, cependant, néanmoins, toutefois) to signal the reversal

The AFTER anchor is KEY: a manager discovers internal problems ONLY through informal chats ("ne découvre... qu'en bavardant"). This means gossip is sometimes the ONLY WAY to identify problems. The gap must set up this revelation.

Step 1: Check each option's CONNECTOR first.

OptionConnectorTypeCompatible with reversal?
A"De surcroît"Addition (furthermore)NO — adds to the same argument ❌
B"Par ailleurs"Addition (moreover)NO — adds a parallel point ❌
C"Cependant"Concession (however)YES — signals a reversal ✓
D"Pourtant"Concession (however/yet)YES — signals a reversal ✓

Options A and B use ADDITIVE connectors — they would CONTINUE the negative argument from Act 2. But the AFTER anchor is clearly POSITIVE (gossip helps managers). We need a REVERSAL connector.

This narrows us to C and D. Now we check the CONTENT.

Option C — "Cependant, cette forme d'échanges représente l'unique moyen de pression sur l'employeur."

  • "Cependant" → reversal connector ✓
  • "l'unique moyen de pression sur l'employeur" → the ONLY means of pressure ON the employer

Wait — the AFTER anchor describes gossip as useful FOR the employer (managers discover problems through it). Option C says gossip is a pressure tool AGAINST the employer. These are OPPOSITE directions:

OPTION C:    Gossip = weapon AGAINST employer (pressure)
AFTER ANCHOR: Gossip = tool FOR employer (discovering problems)
                         ↑ CONTRADICTION

The AFTER anchor describes a COLLABORATIVE benefit: managers use informal channels to learn about problems. Option C describes an ADVERSARIAL use: employees pressure management. These don't connect.

❌ ELIMINATED — Adversarial framing contradicts collaborative AFTER anchor

Option D — "Pourtant, les canaux d'information informels sont parfois le seul moyen de repérer les problèmes."

  • "Pourtant" → reversal connector ✓ (despite the negative view, there's value)
  • "canaux d'information informels" → informal information channels = gossip ✓ (paraphrase)
  • "parfois le seul moyen" → sometimes the only way ✓
  • "repérer les problèmes" → identify problems ✓

Chain test with AFTER anchor:

GAP:   Informal channels are sometimes the only way to spot problems
AFTER: In some cases, the manager only discovers a problem by chatting
       informally at the café

"Parfois le seul moyen de repérer les problèmes" → "ne découvre un problème interne qu'en bavardant"

The gap makes the GENERAL claim; the AFTER anchor provides the SPECIFIC example. Perfect general-to-specific chain.

✅ CORRECT

Now let's also verify why A and B fail on CONTENT (not just connector):

Option A — "De surcroît, l'information officieuse induit régulièrement l'administration en erreur."

"Induit en erreur" = misleads. This says gossip MISLEADS management. But the AFTER anchor says gossip HELPS management discover problems. Misleading and helping are opposites.

❌ ELIMINATED — "Misleads" contradicts "helps discover"

Option B — "Par ailleurs, par le biais des commérages, l'employeur fait passer les mesures impopulaires."

"L'employeur fait passer les mesures impopulaires" = the employer pushes through unpopular measures via gossip. This makes the EMPLOYER the active gossiper — but the entire article discusses EMPLOYEES gossiping. It reverses who the gossiper is.

❌ ELIMINATED — Reverses the actor (employer instead of employee)

✅ ANSWER: D

The Complete Article (Reconstructed):

Now let's read the ENTIRE text with all gaps filled to verify total coherence:

Les sociologues britanniques ont effectué une étude sur les conversations qui se déroulent sur le lieu de travail. Selon cette étude, 80% des conversations sont consacrées aux commérages. Neuf personnes sur dix ont déjà révélé des confidences au travail. Il s'agit dans la moitié des cas d'informations personnelles. Mais un tiers de ces conversations concernent le travail. Questions confidentielles, promotions et augmentations, mutations et licenciements — rien n'est épargné. Pourquoi les salariés sont-ils donc prêts à risquer leur carrière en révélant des informations sensibles ? Certains cherchent à acquérir du pouvoir. Ils utilisent l'information pour garder le contrôle.

Les commérages peuvent coûter cher à l'entreprise. Les employeurs voient donc les colporteurs de rumeurs d'un très mauvais œil. Près d'une société sur six a déjà mis formellement en garde ses salariés à ce sujet.

Pourtant, les canaux d'information informels sont parfois le seul moyen de repérer les problèmes. Dans certains cas, le responsable de service ne découvre un problème interne qu'en bavardant avec les gens au café, après le travail. De cette façon on peut discrètement faire passer l'information au supérieur hiérarchique.

Read it through. The argumentative flow is seamless:

ACT 1: Study finds 80% of conversations are gossip → most people participate →
       half personal, a third professional → motivation: power
ACT 2: Gossip costs money → employers disapprove → companies warn employees
ACT 3: YET gossip is sometimes the only way to spot problems → managers
       learn through informal chats → information flows up the hierarchy

The three-act structure delivers exactly what the title promised: gossip is problematic, BUT it's also information.

✅ WHAT COULD BE DONE

Strategy 1: The Title-as-Compass Method

In texte à trous with argumentative structure, the TITLE tells you the article's destination:

TITLE: "Les potins ? C'est AUSSI de l'info"
                          ↑
                     KEY WORD: "aussi" = also

IMPLICATION: The article will argue that gossip is NOT ONLY bad
             but ALSO useful. The final gap MUST deliver this "also."

Before solving any gap, read the title and ask: "What promise does this title make?" The last gap almost always FULFILLS that promise.

Strategy 2: The Connector-First Filter

For advanced texte à trous, check the CONNECTOR before reading the content:

ADDITIVE connectors (de surcroît, par ailleurs, en outre, de plus):
→ CONTINUE the same argument
→ Used when BEFORE and AFTER are on the SAME side

CONTRAST connectors (pourtant, cependant, néanmoins, toutefois, mais):
→ REVERSE the argument
→ Used when BEFORE and AFTER are on OPPOSITE sides

CONSEQUENCE connectors (donc, ainsi, de ce fait, par conséquent):
→ DRAW A CONCLUSION from the BEFORE anchor
→ Used when the gap is a logical result of what precedes it

In Gap 40: BEFORE is negative (companies warn against gossip), AFTER is positive (gossip helps managers). You NEED a contrast connector. This immediately eliminates A ("de surcroît") and B ("par ailleurs").

Strategy 3: The Mathematical Coherence Check

When a texte à trous contains percentages or fractions, verify that they ADD UP:

THIS TEXT:
- 80% of conversations = gossip (Gap 36)
- Half of confidences = personal information
- A third = work-related (Gap 37)
- Remaining ~17% = unspecified
- 9/10 people have revealed confidences
- 1/6 companies have issued warnings (Gap 39 context)

CHECK: Do these numbers create contradictions?
- 50% + 33% = 83%, leaving 17% unaccounted for ✓ (plausible)
- 80% gossip + 9/10 participating → high consistency ✓

If your gap choice creates a mathematical impossibility (e.g., 50% personal + 50% personal again = 100% with no room for the AFTER anchor's professional examples), it's wrong.

Strategy 4: The Three-Act Structure Detection

Many advanced argumentative texts follow a three-act structure:

ACT 1: DESCRIPTION    — What the phenomenon looks like
ACT 2: PROBLEM        — Why it's negative/costly
ACT 3: REVERSAL       — Why it's also valuable/necessary

Or alternatively:

ACT 1: THESIS         — The initial claim
ACT 2: ANTITHESIS     — The counter-argument
ACT 3: SYNTHESIS      — The balanced conclusion

Detecting which ACT each gap belongs to tells you:

  • ACT 1 gaps: descriptive, factual, statistical
  • ACT 2 gaps: negative, critical, evaluative
  • ACT 3 gaps: concessive, reversing, nuancing

Strategy 5: The Direction-of-Action Check

Always verify WHO does WHAT to WHOM:

GAP 40 OPTIONS:
Option B: EMPLOYER → uses gossip → to push unpopular measures
Option D: EMPLOYEES → use gossip → managers discover problems

THE TEXT'S REALITY: EMPLOYEES gossip → MANAGERS benefit

Option B reverses the ACTOR (employer gossips instead of employee)
Option D matches the TEXT's direction of action

✅ WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

DO #1: Solve gaps in ORDER (left to right)

Unlike basic texte à trous where gaps could be solved independently, advanced gaps form a CHAIN. Gap 37 depends on Gap 36's content. Gap 38's context depends on the paragraph built by 36 and 37. Solve sequentially.

DO #2: Read the title BEFORE solving any gap

The title is your roadmap. It tells you where the argument is going. "C'est aussi de l'info" guarantees that the article will eventually DEFEND gossip — this knowledge directly helps with Gap 40.

DO #3: Check connectors BEFORE checking content

A wrong connector eliminates an option in 2 seconds. If the text needs a reversal ("pourtant") and the option offers addition ("de surcroît"), it's wrong — regardless of content. This is the fastest elimination technique for advanced texte à trous.

DO #4: Verify the direction of the argument on both sides of each gap

For every gap, ask:

  • What is the ARGUMENT before the gap? (positive/negative/descriptive)
  • What is the ARGUMENT after the gap? (positive/negative/descriptive)
  • Does my gap choice CONNECT these two correctly?
If BEFORE = negative and AFTER = negative → gap = CONTINUATION (donc, de plus)
If BEFORE = negative and AFTER = positive → gap = REVERSAL (pourtant, cependant)
If BEFORE = question and AFTER = explanation → gap = ANSWER

DO #5: Re-read the ENTIRE reconstructed text after solving all gaps

This is your final verification. Read the whole text with your chosen phrases inserted. If ANY sentence feels jarring, illogical, or redundant, go back and reconsider that gap.

❌ WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

DON'T #1: Solve gaps in isolation

In advanced texte à trous, each gap's context is PARTIALLY CREATED by previous gaps. If you solve Gap 37 before Gap 36, you might choose a phrase that contradicts what Gap 36 established. Always solve left to right.

DON'T #2: Ignore the title

The title is NOT decorative. In argumentative texte à trous, it's your most powerful tool. "C'est aussi de l'info" told you EXACTLY what Gap 40 needed to accomplish — before you even read the text.

DON'T #3: Match connector words to the wrong logical relationship

"De surcroît" sounds sophisticated and impressive. But if the text needs a REVERSAL and you choose an ADDITIVE connector, you've broken the argumentative logic. Sophistication of vocabulary doesn't matter — logical function does.

DON'T #4: Confuse WHO acts on WHOM

Option B of Gap 40 made the EMPLOYER the gossiper. The entire text discusses EMPLOYEES gossiping. Always check: does your gap choice maintain the same ACTORS as the rest of the text?

DON'T #5: Accept "close enough" in content matching

Option C of Gap 40 used "cependant" (correct connector!) and mentioned gossip's utility (correct theme!). But it said gossip is a PRESSURE tool against employers, while the AFTER anchor says it HELPS employers. "Close enough" in content is NOT enough — the DIRECTION of benefit must match.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: The "Correct Connector, Wrong Content" Trap

YOUR BRAIN: "Gap 40 needs a reversal → Option C has 'cependant' → must be right!"
REALITY:    Option C: gossip = pressure AGAINST employer
            AFTER anchor: gossip = help FOR employer
            Right connector, wrong direction of benefit
LESSON:     The connector is a NECESSARY condition, not a SUFFICIENT one.
            After passing the connector test, you MUST still verify content.

PITFALL 2: The "Actor Reversal" Trap

YOUR BRAIN: "Option B mentions commérages and employer → sounds relevant!"
REALITY:    Option B: EMPLOYER uses gossip to push measures
            Text reality: EMPLOYEES gossip, MANAGERS benefit
            The actor is reversed.
LESSON:     Always check: WHO is the subject of the gap's sentence?
            Does this match who the TEXT says is doing the action?

PITFALL 3: The "Additive Instead of Contrastive" Error

YOUR BRAIN: "De surcroît sounds like a good academic connector!"
REALITY:    "De surcroît" = furthermore = ADDS to the same argument
            But BEFORE is negative and AFTER is positive
            You need a CONTRAST connector, not an ADDITIVE one
LESSON:     Map the argument direction BEFORE and AFTER the gap.
            SAME direction → additive connector
            OPPOSITE direction → contrastive connector

PITFALL 4: The "Redundancy Blindness" Error

YOUR BRAIN: "Gap 37: Option A says 'problems of money and health' → sounds reasonable!"
REALITY:    BEFORE anchor already said "half = personal information"
            Money and health problems are ALSO personal
            This would mean 50% + 33% = 83% personal → no room for
            the AFTER anchor's PROFESSIONAL examples
LESSON:     The gap must TRANSITION, not REPEAT.
            If BEFORE says X, the gap should say Y (the OTHER category),
            not more of X.

PITFALL 5: The "Motivation Mismatch" Error

YOUR BRAIN: "Gap 38: Option A says 'fear of administration' → employees fear bosses!"
REALITY:    AFTER anchor: "pour garder le contrôle" = to maintain control
            Fear = DEFENSIVE (I'm scared)
            Control = OFFENSIVE (I want power)
            These are opposite psychological postures
LESSON:     The gap's MOTIVATION must match the AFTER anchor's described BEHAVIOR.
            If the behavior is strategic and power-seeking,
            the motivation must be strategic and power-seeking.

PITFALL 6: The "Statistical Impossibility" Error

YOUR BRAIN: "Any fraction works in Gap 37 — a third is just a number!"
REALITY:    Half = personal (BEFORE) + Third = work (GAP) = 83%
            This leaves 17% unaccounted for → PLAUSIBLE ✓
            
            But if GAP said "half" again:
            Half = personal + Half = [gap] = 100%
            No room for other categories → IMPOSSIBLE ❌
            
LESSON:     In texts with multiple fractions/percentages,
            verify they don't exceed 100% or create logical impossibilities.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY + BRIDGE TO NEXT CHAPTER

What Chapter 18 Taught You:

The Five Laws of Advanced Texte à Trous:

LAW 1: THE TITLE-AS-COMPASS LAW — In argumentative texte à trous, the title reveals the article's DESTINATION. The last gap almost always fulfills the title's promise. Read the title first, solve the last gap with it in mind.

LAW 2: THE CONNECTOR-FIRST LAW — Before checking content, check the connector word. Additive connectors (de surcroît, par ailleurs) continue the argument. Contrastive connectors (pourtant, cependant) reverse it. If the connector doesn't match the needed logical relationship, the option is wrong — regardless of content.

LAW 3: THE CHAIN DEPENDENCY LAW — Advanced gaps are interdependent. Solve them left-to-right. Each gap creates context for the next. A wrong choice in Gap N cascades into confusion in Gap N+1.

LAW 4: THE DIRECTION-OF-ACTION LAW — Always verify WHO does WHAT to WHOM. If the text says employees gossip and managers benefit, a gap that reverses the actor (employer gossips) or the direction (gossip harms managers) is wrong.

LAW 5: THE THREE-ACT LAW — Argumentative texts often follow Description → Problem → Reversal. Identify which ACT each gap belongs to. ACT 1 gaps are factual. ACT 2 gaps are critical. ACT 3 gaps are concessive/nuancing.
