🔍 STEP 2: HOW TO APPROACH IT

Why this text comes FIRST in our journey

This is the shortest text in the entire collection. Only two paragraphs. Only three questions. Every answer exists word-for-word in the text. There is no opinion, no debate, no hidden meaning. This is pure factual extraction.

And yet — students get these wrong. Not because the text is hard, but because they read carelessly and fall into traps designed to look almost right.

The Golden Rule you will use for ALL 25 projects

READ THE QUESTIONS FIRST. THEN READ THE TEXT. THEN READ THE TEXT AGAIN.

Here's why this matters even with a simple text:

When you read the questions first, your brain becomes a scanner. You know WHAT you're looking for. Without questions, you read passively. With questions loaded in your mind, you read actively — hunting for specific information.

The anatomy of THIS specific text

TYPE:        Fait divers (news brief / human interest story)
LENGTH:      ~80 words (very short)
STRUCTURE:   Paragraph 1 = WHAT happened + WHO did it + WHY
             Paragraph 2 = WHAT happened AFTER
DIFFICULTY:  ★☆☆☆☆ (1/5)
TRAP LEVEL:  ★★☆☆☆ (2/5) — surprisingly tricky choices

Your mental checklist before reading ANY CE text

STEP A → Read ALL questions and ALL answer choices (2 minutes)
STEP B → Underline KEY WORDS in each question
STEP C → Read the text slowly, ONE sentence at a time
STEP D → After EACH sentence, ask: "Does this answer any question?"
STEP E → For each question, ELIMINATE wrong answers before choosing
STEP F → Verify your chosen answer against the EXACT text

Let me now show you this process live on each question.

🧩 STEP 3: HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

QUESTION 14: Le toast au caviar...

First — Load the question into your brain:

The question stem is simply: "Le toast au caviar..." — meaning "What is TRUE about the toast?"

Second — Read each choice and mark what to HUNT for:

ChoiceWhat I need to verify in the text
A — est entré dans le livre des recordsDid it ACTUALLY enter the record book?
B — a été fabriqué au bord d'une rivièreWas it made NEAR a river?
C — a été réalisé par les employés d'un hôtelWHO made it?
D — a été préparé avec du caviar rouge ET noirWhat TYPE of caviar was used?

Third — Go to the text and hunt:

Let me dissect the relevant sentence:

"...a été préparé par les cuisiniers d'un hôtel de luxe..."

🔑 "les cuisiniers d'un hôtel" = the cooks of a hotel = employees of a hotel

Now let's ELIMINATE the wrong ones:

Choice A: "est entré dans le livre des records"

Text says: "ses cuisiniers ESPÈRENT entrer dans le livre de records"
                         ^^^^^^^^
Key word: ESPÈRENT = they HOPE to enter

❌ ELIMINATED. They HOPE to enter. They haven't entered. The text uses the verb "espérer" (to hope). Choice A states it as a FACT. This is the "hope vs. reality" trap — the most common trap in CE exams at every level.

Choice B: "a été fabriqué au bord d'une rivière"

Text says: "surmontée d'une RIVIÈRE de 130 mètres de caviar rouge"

❌ ELIMINATED. The word "rivière" is used as a metaphor — a "river" of caviar means a long strip. The toast was NOT made near an actual river. This is the "word reuse" trap — the exam takes a word from the text and uses it with a DIFFERENT meaning in the answer choice.

Choice D: "a été préparé avec du caviar rouge ET noir"

Text says: "caviar rouge. Moins cher que le caviar noir"

❌ ELIMINATED. The text MENTIONS black caviar only to explain that red caviar is cheaper. Only RED caviar was used. This is the "both mentioned ≠ both used" trap — just because two things appear in the text doesn't mean both apply.

Choice C: "a été réalisé par les employés d'un hôtel"

Text says: "préparé par les cuisiniers d'un hôtel de luxe"

✅ CORRECT. "Cuisiniers d'un hôtel" = employees of a hotel. The answer paraphrases the text. "Cuisiniers" becomes "employés" and "réalisé" replaces "préparé." This is standard CE technique — the correct answer almost NEVER uses the exact same words as the text. It rephrases.

✅ Answer 14: C

QUESTION 15: À la fin de la cérémonie, la tartine a été...

Load the question: What happened to the toast AT THE END?

Hunt in the text — specifically paragraph 2:

"Le toast a ensuite été exposé dans la rue, le long de la 
façade de l'hôtel AVANT d'être ENGLOUTI par les invités 
de la cérémonie d'anniversaire."

🔑 The KEY word is: ENGLOUTI

What does "englouti" mean?

  • Literal meaning: swallowed, devoured, gobbled up
  • In this context: eaten by the guests

Now ELIMINATE:

Choice A: "Exposée devant l'hôtel"

Text says: "exposé dans la rue...AVANT d'être englouti"
                                  ^^^^^

❌ ELIMINATED. The toast WAS exposed in the street, but this happened BEFORE the end. The question asks about "à la fin." This is the "sequence trap" — an answer describes something TRUE but at the WRONG moment in the timeline.

Choice C: "Gardée par les cuisiniers de l'hôtel"

❌ ELIMINATED. Nothing in the text says this. Pure invention.

Choice D: "Conservée au sein de l'établissement"

❌ ELIMINATED. "Conservée" means preserved/kept. The toast was eaten, not preserved.

Choice B: "Mangée par les participants"

"englouti par les invités" = eaten by the guests = mangée par les participants

✅ CORRECT. "Englouti" is paraphrased as "mangée." "Les invités" is paraphrased as "les participants."

✅ Answer 15: B

QUESTION 16: Cet évènement a eu lieu pour...

Load the question: WHY did this event take place?

Hunt in the text:

"à l'occasion du 135° anniversaire de la fondation de l'établissement"

🔑 Breaking this down:

  • à l'occasion du = for the occasion of
  • 135° anniversaire = 135th anniversary
  • de la fondation = of the founding/creation
  • de l'établissement = of the establishment (the hotel)

Now ELIMINATE:

Choice B: "L'ouverture d'un hôtel de luxe"

❌ ELIMINATED. "Ouverture" means opening (a new hotel). But this is the 135th ANNIVERSARY — the hotel already exists. It's not opening; it's celebrating its birthday.

Choice C: "L'anniversaire du cuisinier d'un grand hôtel"

❌ ELIMINATED. It's the anniversary of THE HOTEL, not of a cook. This is the "subject swap" trap — the exam swaps WHO or WHAT the event is about.

Choice D: "La fête nationale dans la deuxième ville de Russie"

Text says: "Saint-Pétersbourg. La deuxième ville de Russie"

❌ ELIMINATED. The text DOES call Saint-Petersburg "la deuxième ville de Russie" — but the event is NOT a national holiday. This is another "word reuse" trap — borrowing real text details to build a false answer.

Choice A: "La commémoration de la création d'un hôtel en Russie"

"anniversaire de la fondation de l'établissement"
= commémoration de la création d'un hôtel

✅ CORRECT. "Anniversaire" → "commémoration." "Fondation" → "création." "Établissement" → "hôtel." Three paraphrases in one answer.

✅ Answer 16: A

🎯 STEP 4: WHAT COULD BE DONE

Here are strategies you could apply to this type of text:

Strategy 1: The "Traffic Light" Marking System

When you read each answer choice, mentally mark it:

🟢 GREEN  = This matches the text perfectly (paraphrased)
🟡 YELLOW = This is partially true or I'm unsure
🔴 RED    = This clearly contradicts the text or is invented

For Question 14:

A — espèrent ≠ sont entrés     → 🔴 RED
B — rivière is metaphorical     → 🔴 RED
C — cuisiniers = employés       → 🟢 GREEN
D — rouge only, not noir        → 🔴 RED

Strategy 2: The "Paraphrase Detector"

Train yourself to recognize that correct answers REPHRASE the text. They almost NEVER copy it word-for-word. Build this mental reflex:

TEXT WORD          →    ANSWER WORD (paraphrase)
─────────────────────────────────────────────
cuisiniers         →    employés
préparé            →    réalisé
englouti           →    mangé
invités            →    participants
anniversaire       →    commémoration
fondation          →    création
établissement      →    hôtel

💡 RULE: If an answer choice uses the EXACT same rare word from the text, be SUSPICIOUS. It might be a trap. Correct answers rephrase. Traps quote.

Strategy 3: The "Time Stamp" Check

For questions that ask about WHEN something happened ("à la fin," "d'abord," "ensuite"), map the timeline:

TIMELINE OF THE TEXT:
1. Toast was PREPARED by hotel cooks
2. Toast was DISPLAYED in the street
3. Toast was EATEN by guests

Question 15 asks about "à la fin" → Step 3 → eaten → Choice B ✅

✅ STEP 5: WHAT SHOULD YOU DO

The 6 Commandments for Factual Texts

1. Read questions FIRST — always, without exception

Even with a 2-paragraph text. Load your brain with targets before reading.

2. Underline KEY verbs in the text

In this text: "espèrent," "exposé," "englouti" — these three verbs hold ALL three answers.

3. Check EVERY word in the answer choice against the text

Don't just match the general idea. Check: Is it "entered" or "hopes to enter"? Is it "red AND black" or "red ONLY"? Precision wins.

4. Eliminate before selecting

Never pick an answer because it "feels right." First, prove that the other three are WRONG. The correct answer is the one you CANNOT eliminate.

5. Beware of answer choices that QUOTE the text directly

As we saw with "rivière" in Choice B and "deuxième ville de Russie" in Choice D — direct quotes from the text are often used to BUILD traps, not to signal correct answers.

6. Distinguish between FACTS and INTENTIONS in the text

"Espèrent entrer" (hope to enter) is an INTENTION.
"Est entré" (entered) is a FACT.
The exam LOVES this distinction.

❌ STEP 6: WHAT SHOULD YOU NOT DO

The 5 Deadly Sins of CE Reading

❌ SIN 1: Reading the text first without knowing the questions

You'll read passively, miss critical details, and waste time re-reading.

❌ SIN 2: Choosing an answer because ONE word matches

Choice B has "rivière" — which IS in the text. But the meaning is completely different. One matching word ≠ correct answer.

❌ SIN 3: Assuming something the text doesn't say

Choice C of Question 16 says "l'anniversaire du cuisinier." Your brain might think: "Well, cooks are mentioned, and anniversary is mentioned, so maybe..." NO. The text says anniversary of THE HOTEL, not of a cook. Never connect dots the text doesn't connect.

❌ SIN 4: Ignoring small grammatical words

The difference between "espèrent entrer" and "sont entrés" is the difference between right and wrong. Small words like "espèrent," "avant," "moins cher que" carry ENORMOUS weight.

❌ SIN 5: Spending equal time on every question

With a factual text like this, if you've found the answer and verified it, MOVE ON. Don't second-guess. Save your time and energy for harder texts later.

Failure Mode Analysis

FAILURE MODE 1: "I read too fast"
→ SYMPTOM: You pick A for Q14 because "livre des records" jumps out
→ CAUSE: You didn't read "espèrent" carefully
→ FIX: Slow down. Read the COMPLETE sentence. Every word matters.

FAILURE MODE 2: "I trusted my memory instead of re-checking"
→ SYMPTOM: You remember "rivière" and pick B for Q14
→ CAUSE: You recalled a word but not its context
→ FIX: ALWAYS go back to the text. Never answer from memory alone.

FAILURE MODE 3: "I picked the first plausible answer"
→ SYMPTOM: You pick A for Q15 because the toast WAS displayed
→ CAUSE: You stopped checking after finding something partially true
→ FIX: Check ALL FOUR choices. The best answer wins, not the first okay one.
