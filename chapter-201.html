📄 THE COMPLETE ARTICLE (REFERENCE)

L'alpinisme — Un peu d'histoire !

En 1760, Horace-Bénédict de Saussure savant Genevois désireux de faire
des observations scientifiques sur la cime la plus haute des Alpes, promet
une forte récompense au montagnard qui trouvera une route praticable pour
y parvenir. Sur le moment la chose parut sans doute extravagante, car la
première tentative n'eut lieu qu'au bout de 15 ans. Elle fut arrêtée,
comme les suivantes, plutôt par la longueur de la course, l'étonnement,
le dépaysement des hautes neiges que par de véritables difficultés. Le
glacier des Bossons, la Mer de Glace, l'Arête du Goûter sont
successivement explorés, sans succès. Il faut, pour que le pas décisif
soit franchi, l'entrée en scène de Jacques Balmat. Originaire du village
des Pèlerin, déjà père de famille, Jacques Balmat avait 24 ans en 1786.
C'était un grand coureur de glaciers ; il conduisait des voyageurs, mais
n'était pas guide en titre. Le 30 Juin de cette année-là, descendant
d'une course, il rencontre une caravane qui part conquérir la récompense
promise par Saussure. Il se joint à eux, fait une tentative par l'Arête
des Bosses, est abandonné par ses compagnons et redescend sur le Grand
Plateau. Il raconte ensuite avoir ensuite gravi la pente à droite des
Rochers Rouges, atteint l'épaule du Mont Blanc et vu à ses pieds le
versant italien. Mais pris par le brouillard, il ne peut continuer ce
soir-là. Après un bivouac improvisé, il redescend dans la vallée, sûr
d'avoir trouvé l'accès à la cime. Un mois après, le 7 Août, Balmat
repart avec Michel Gabriel Paccard. C'est le docteur de Chamonix, neveu
du doyen des guides de la vallée, homme instruit, qui a déjà fait trois
tentatives au Mont Blanc. Après une nuit passée au sommet de la montagne
de la Côte, reprenant l'itinéraire des Rochers rouges, ils arrivent tous
deux au sommet à 18h30. Le Mont Blanc était vaincu, la plus grande
«première» des Alpes était accomplie. Saussure lui-même se fit conduire
au Mont Blanc en 1787. Il fit un long séjour en 1788 au col du Géant,
multipliant les observations scientifiques. Si la science lui avait fait
faire la conquête du Mont Blanc, on peut dire qu'en retour les Alpes
l'avaient conquis, comme beaucoup d'entre nous. C'est seulement en 1808,
qu'une servante de Chamonix, Maria Paradis, fit la première ascension
féminine. Citons aussi la tragique expédition du docteur Hamel en 1820,
l'ascension de Mlle d'Angeville (1838) qui, exténuée, disait à ses
guides: «Si je meurs avant d'avoir atteint la cime, promettez-moi d'y
porter mon corps et de l'ensevelir là-haut.» Mais aucun des amateurs qui
suivent les traces de Saussure, ne laisse une trace durable dans
l'histoire alpine. Ils sont venus là par curiosité, peut-être pour la
gloire. La preuve en est que malgré le nombre de ces ascensions,
l'exploration du Mont Blanc n'avance pas. La première voie, avec
toutefois le détour par le mur de la Côte, est la seule connue jusqu'en
1855. Cette année-là seulement est faite l'ascension par l'Arête du
Goûter, bien que la voie ait été reconnue dès les premières tentatives.
L'Arête des Bosses ne sera vaincue qu'en 1859. Quand à la connaissance
du massif, il semble qu'elle se borne à la voie du col du Géant. Dans
le reste des Alpes, c'est le silence.

📌 Speed reading under pressure is NOT about reading faster. It is about reading LESS of the text MORE STRATEGICALLY. You cannot read 450 words of archaic French in 2 minutes. But you CAN read the 100 words that contain the answers.

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT: THE TRIAGE READING SYSTEM

THE CORE PRINCIPLE: TRIAGE

In emergency medicine, triage means sorting patients by urgency — treating the most critical first, not the first one who arrives. Applied to reading comprehension:

READING TRIAGE PRINCIPLE:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  NOT: Read everything → then answer questions
  
  YES: Scan everything quickly → identify what matters →
       read ONLY what matters deeply → answer questions
  
  THREE TRIAGE LEVELS:
  
  🔴 CRITICAL: Sections that DIRECTLY answer questions
     → Read carefully, word by word
     → Spend 60-70% of your time here
  
  🟡 IMPORTANT: Sections that provide CONTEXT for answers
     → Read for general meaning
     → Spend 20-25% of your time here
  
  🟢 BACKGROUND: Sections that are interesting but 
     NOT tested by any question
     → Skim or skip entirely
     → Spend 5-10% of your time here

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Together = the text's skeleton.

  In a historical text, proper nouns = PEOPLE and PLACES.
  Scanning for capital letters gives you the WHO and WHERE
  in seconds.

  WHAT YOUR EYES CATCH:
  
  Horace-Bénédict de Saussure → PERSON (first mentioned)
  Alpes → PLACE (setting)
  Bossons → PLACE (glacier)
  Mer de Glace → PLACE (glacier)
  Arête du Goûter → PLACE (route)
  Jacques Balmat → PERSON (hero)
  Pèlerin → PLACE (his village)
  Arête des Bosses → PLACE (route)
  Grand Plateau → PLACE (landmark)
  Rochers Rouges → PLACE (route)
  Mont Blanc → PLACE (the summit)
  Michel Gabriel Paccard → PERSON (partner)
  Chamonix → PLACE (town)
  Côte → PLACE (mountain/camp)
  Géant → PLACE (col/pass)
  Maria Paradis → PERSON (first woman)
  Hamel → PERSON (tragic figure)
  Mlle d'Angeville → PERSON (passionate climber)
  
  IN 15 SECONDS: You know all the actors and locations.
  You don't know their ROLES yet, but you know they EXIST
  and roughly WHERE they appear in the text.

DO 3: MARK THE TEXT PHYSICALLY (IF ALLOWED)

If your exam allows writing on the text:

PHYSICAL MARKING SYSTEM:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  During your 90-second first pass, use these marks:

  📌 CIRCLE all dates
     → Creates instant visual timeline
  
  📌 UNDERLINE all proper nouns (people)
     → Creates instant character register
  
  📌 BRACKET [ ] key events

     → "tragique expédition" → [TRAGEDY]

     → "aucun des amateurs... ne laisse une trace durable"
     → "c'est le silence"
  
  RESULT: When you return to answer questions, your

  Looking for "who reached the summit?" 
  → Find the [SUMMIT] bracket → read around it
  
  Looking for "was there a tragedy?"
  → Find the [TRAGEDY] bracket → read around it
  
  THIS SAVES 30-60 SECONDS per question in re-reading.

  │  ████████████████░░░░░                 │
  │  READING (70%)    ANSWERING (30%)      │
  │                                        │

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐

DO 5: THE RE-READING WINDOW TECHNIQUE

When a question requires you to go back to the text, do NOT re-read the whole text. Use the window technique:

THE RE-READING WINDOW:
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Instead of re-reading the whole text, open a 
  READING WINDOW of 3-5 sentences around your target.

  EXAMPLE: Q21 Option B — "hommes illustres ont péri"
  
  TARGET: Evidence of death in the Alps
  LOCATION: Section E (from your section map)
  
  WINDOW: Open 3 sentences around "tragique"
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                              │
  │  "C'est seulement en 1808, qu'une servante   │
  │   de Chamonix, Maria Paradis, fit la         │
  │   première ascension féminine. Citons aussi  │
  │   la TRAGIQUE expédition du docteur Hamel    │
  │   en 1820, l'ascension de Mlle d'Angeville   │
  │   (1838) qui, exténuée, disait à ses guides: │
  │   «Si je meurs avant d'avoir atteint la      │
  │   cime...»"                                  │
  │                                              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  
  WINDOW SIZE: 3 sentences
  READING TIME: 15-20 seconds
  INFORMATION EXTRACTED:
  → "Tragique expédition" = death confirmed ✓
  → "Si je meurs" = death risk acknowledged ✓
  → Evidence for Option B: people DID perish ✓
  
  COMPARE TO: Re-reading the whole text (2-3 minutes)
  WINDOW SAVED: ~2 minutes

📌 The window technique is the MOST powerful time-saving tool in this chapter. Instead of re-reading 450 words, you re-read 50-80 words. Five windows for five checks = 250-400 words, still less than the full text, but TARGETED to exactly what you need.

❌ WHAT YOU SHOULD NOT DO

MISTAKE 1: READING THE WHOLE TEXT BEFORE LOOKING AT QUESTIONS

THE FULL-READ TRAP:
                                                          
  "I need to understand everything before I can answer."
  
  REALITY: You will FORGET details from the beginning 
  by the time you reach the end of a 450-word text.
  
  Human short-term memory holds 5-9 items.
  This text has 45+ data points.
  You CANNOT hold them all from a single read.
  
  INSTEAD: 
  → Quick scan (90 seconds) → mental map
  → Read questions → know what to look for
  → Targeted re-reading → find specific answers
  
  You will ultimately read LESS total text 
  but understand MORE relevant information.

MISTAKE 2: RE-READING THE SAME SECTION FOR DIFFERENT QUESTIONS

THE REPETITIVE RE-READING TRAP:
                                                          
  Q20 → you re-read Section A to understand the beginning
  Q21 → you re-read Section A AGAIN for Option A
  Q21b → you re-read Section A AGAIN looking for "Cervin"
  
  You read Section A THREE TIMES.
  
  FIX: BATCH your re-reading by SECTION, not by QUESTION.
  
  Before answering ANY question:
  1. Map which sections each question needs (Step 2 above)
  2. Re-read each section ONCE, noting information 
     relevant to ALL questions that need it
  3. THEN answer all questions using your notes
  
  BATCHED APPROACH:
  → Re-read Section A once: note "science motivation" 
    (for Q21-A) + "reward offered" (context)
  → Re-read Section E once: note "tragique" (for Q21-B) 
    + "féminine" (for Q21 context)
  → Re-read Section F once: note "amateurs" + "silence" 
    (for Q20 theme + Q21-C check)
  
  THREE sections read ONCE each = 3 reads
  vs. THREE sections read for EACH of 3 questions = 9 reads
  
  EFFICIENCY GAIN: 67% reduction in re-reading time

MISTAKE 3: SPENDING EQUAL TIME ON EACH QUESTION

THE EQUAL-TIME FALLACY:
                                                          
  "I have 8 minutes for 3 questions = ~2.5 minutes each"
  
  REALITY: Questions have DIFFERENT difficulty levels.
  
  Q20 (Best title):     EASY  → 30 seconds is enough
  Q21b (Mont Cervin):   MEDIUM → 60 seconds is enough
  Q21 (True statement):  HARD  → 2-3 minutes needed
  
  OPTIMAL TIME ALLOCATION:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                                                │
  │  First pass (scanning):     1.5 minutes        │
  │  Q20 (easy, answer first):  0.5 minutes        │
  │  Q21b (medium, second):     1.0 minutes        │
  │  Q21 (hard, last):          3.0 minutes        │
  │  Review all 3:              1.0 minutes        │
  │  Buffer:                    1.0 minutes        │
  │                                                │
  │  TOTAL:                     8.0 minutes        │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  
  The hard question gets 6x more time than the easy one.
  This is CORRECT time allocation.

MISTAKE 4: PANIC-READING WHEN TIME IS SHORT

THE PANIC-READING SPIRAL:
                                                          
  You check the clock → 3 minutes left → 2 questions remain
  
  PANIC RESPONSE:
  → Eyes dart across the text
  → Reading speed doubles but comprehension halves
  → You grab at any answer that "sounds right"
  → You select based on gut feeling, not analysis
  → You get both wrong
  
  CALM RESPONSE (trained):
  → 3 minutes left, 2 questions remain
  → Question A is easy → spend 30 seconds → answer ✓
  → Question B is hard → spend 2 minutes → systematic 
    elimination → answer ✓
  → 30 seconds to verify both
  → Both correct
  
  THE DIFFERENCE:
  Panic = emotional reaction → irrational choices
  System = trained response → efficient choices
  
  FIX: When time pressure hits, SLOW DOWN for 5 seconds.
  Take one breath. Ask: "Which question can I answer 
  fastest?" Answer THAT one first. Then tackle the harder 
  one with remaining time.
  
  COUNTERINTUITIVE TRUTH:
  Slowing down for 5 seconds when panicked SAVES time
  because it prevents the 30-60 seconds you'd waste 
  on frantic, unproductive scanning.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

PITFALL 1: THE "I DIDN'T FINISH READING" GUILT

SYMPTOM: You feel guilty that you only scanned the text 
         instead of reading every word.

INTERNAL VOICE: "What if I missed something important 
                 because I didn't read carefully enough?"

REALITY CHECK:
→ Your 90-second scan captured the chronological skeleton
→ Your section map located all key events
→ Your targeted re-reading covered the specific lines 
  needed for each question
→ You answered all 3 questions correctly

→ You did NOT "miss" anything. You PRIORITIZED.

THE UNREAD PARTS:
→ Detailed descriptions of Balmat's climbing route
→ Exact placement of place names relative to each other
→ Nuances of archaic literary expressions
→ Precise wording of transitional sentences

WERE ANY OF THESE TESTED? No.
Did you need them for any question? No.
Were they "missed"? Technically yes.
Did it matter? NOT AT ALL.

PITFALL 2: THE ANCHOR POINT MISFIRE

SYMPTOM: Your 90-second scan MISIDENTIFIES a section's 
         content, and you later search for information 
         in the wrong part of the text.

EXAMPLE:
  You scan and note: "1787-1788 = Balmat does more climbing"
  
  ACTUAL CONTENT: "1787-1788 = SAUSSURE (not Balmat) 
  goes up and does science at col du Géant"
  
  CONSEQUENCE: When Q21 asks about scientific motivation, 
  you search in the wrong section.
  
  FIX: During your 90-second scan, connect DATES to NAMES:
  
  WRONG: "1787 → something happened"
  RIGHT: "1787 → SAUSSURE went up"
  
  The DATE + NAME combination prevents misidentification.
  
  ADDITIONAL SAFEGUARD:
  If your targeted re-reading finds DIFFERENT content 
  than expected, don't panic. Adjust your section map 
  and continue. The map is a GUIDE, not gospel.

PITFALL 3: THE LONG-TEXT FATIGUE EFFECT

SYMPTOM: By the time you reach L'Alpinisme (often the 
         LAST text on the exam), your brain is tired 
         from previous texts. Concentration drops.

THE FATIGUE PROGRESSION:
  Text 1: 100% concentration → good performance
  Text 2:  85% concentration → decent performance
  Text 3:  60% concentration → declining performance
  
  L'Alpinisme is usually the HARDEST text AND you read 
  it when you're MOST tired.

FIX: THE ENERGY MANAGEMENT STRATEGY
                                                          
  1. BEFORE starting the last text:
     → Take 10 seconds to close your eyes
     → Take 3 deep breaths
     → Reset your focus
  
  2. DURING the last text:
     → Use the 90-second scan (it's LESS tiring than 
       a full read because it's active scanning, not 
       passive reading)
     → Answer the EASIEST question first (quick win 
       builds momentum and confidence)
     → Save the hardest question for your final push
  
  3. TRUST YOUR SYSTEM:
     → You don't need MORE concentration for the hard text
     → You need BETTER STRATEGY for the hard text
     → The triage system COMPENSATES for reduced concentration
     → You read less but more strategically

PITFALL 4: THE ARCHAIC FRENCH SLOWDOWN

SYMPTOM: Passé simple and literary constructions slow 
         your reading speed to a crawl.

"la chose parut sans doute extravagante, car la 
 première tentative n'eut lieu qu'au bout de 15 ans. 
 Elle fut arrêtée, comme les suivantes, plutôt par 
 la longueur de la course..."

NORMAL READING: ~30 seconds for these 3 lines
ARCHAIC FRENCH READING: ~60-90 seconds (double or triple)

FIX: THE PASSÉ SIMPLE SPEED-DECODE TABLE

  During your scan, when you hit passé simple:
  
  → "parut" → SEEMED (don't stop, keep going)
  → "eut lieu" → TOOK PLACE (don't stop)
  → "fut arrêtée" → WAS STOPPED (don't stop)
  → "fit" → DID/MADE (don't stop)
  
  YOUR BRAIN SHOULD AUTO-TRANSLATE THESE FORMS
  WITHOUT CONSCIOUS EFFORT.
  
  TRAINING: Before exam day, memorize the 10 most 
  common passé simple forms (Chapter 17 list):
  fut, eut, fit, put, vit, vint, dit, prit, mit, parut
  
  If you can recognize these 10 forms INSTANTLY,
  archaic French loses 80% of its slowdown power.
