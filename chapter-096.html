🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

What makes this text the HARDEST of the three texte à trous?

You have now completed Chapter 16 (connectors: mais, car, malgré, dès lors que) and Chapter 17 (structural devices: rhetorical questions, colons, "donc," pronoun tracking). Those tools remain your foundation — we will not re-explain them here.

This text introduces three new challenges that make it the most demanding gap-fill exercise:

New ChallengeWhere it appearsWhy it's harder
The "Inverted Logic" Decoy PatternGaps 37 and 383 out of 4 options say the EXACT OPPOSITE of what the text needs. The exam is testing whether you can detect logical inversion under time pressure.
The "Temporal Shift" OpeningGap 36The first sentence establishes a BEFORE/NOW contrast. The gap must complete the NOW side of the shift.
"Ainsi" as Backward ConfirmationGap 40The word "ainsi" (thus) AFTER the gap means the companies that follow are EXAMPLES proving what the gap states. You must work backward from examples to general claim.

The text's argumentative architecture

PARAGRAPH 1: SHIFT from personal to corporate concern → CONSEQUENCE on health/absenteeism
PARAGRAPH 2: ADDITIONAL consequence (productivity) → COST to employers
PARAGRAPH 3: SOLUTION (employers must act) → EVIDENCE (government report + corporate programs)
FINAL LINE:  PROOF (companies got results)

This is a problem → consequence → solution → proof structure. Each gap sits at a critical node in this chain:

Gap 36 = The SHIFT (personal → corporate)
Gap 37 = The CONSEQUENCE (obesity → sick leave)
Gap 38 = The ADDITIONAL consequence (obesity → less productive)
Gap 39 = The SOLUTION (employers must care about health)
Gap 40 = The BRIDGE to proof (programs work)

Knowing this architecture means you already know the ROLE of each gap before testing any options.

QUESTION 36 — The Temporal Shift Gap

The landscape:

BEFORE the gapTHE GAPAFTER the gap
"Considérée il y a très peu comme un problème personnel,"??? (36)"l'obésité est liée à un très grand nombre de problèmes de santé."

Step 1: Decode the temporal structure.

The opening phrase establishes a BEFORE:

"Considérée il y a très peu comme un problème personnel"
= "Considered until very recently as a personal problem"

The word "il y a très peu" (very recently) signals that something has CHANGED. The sentence structure demands:

BEFORE (past):  personal problem
GAP (present):  [NEW STATUS]
AFTER:          linked to many health problems

The gap must state what obesity has BECOME — its new status.

Step 2: Test each option for the temporal shift.

OptionCompletes the SHIFT from "personal" to...?Logical flow to "linked to health problems"?
A) assimilée aujourd'hui à une faute professionnellePersonal → professional fault? Extreme. Obesity isn't a "fault" — that's a moral judgment the text doesn't makeWeak — being a "fault" doesn't explain health links
B) en passe de devenir une préoccupation des entreprisesPersonal → corporate concern ✅ Natural, measured shift. "En passe de" (in the process of becoming) matches "il y a très peu" — the change is recent and ongoing✅ Companies are concerned BECAUSE obesity is linked to health problems
C) délaissée aujourd'hui au profit d'autres préoccupationsPersonal → neglected? This says obesity is being IGNORED — the opposite of the article's message❌ If obesity is neglected, why write an entire article about corporate interest in it?
D) se transforme en une préoccupation personnelle de chaque salariéPersonal → personal? No shift at all. This REPEATS the "before" state❌ "Considered as personal... becomes personal" — no contrast, no change

Step 3: Verify the full sentence.

"Considérée il y a très peu comme un problème personnel, l'obésité est en passe de devenir une préoccupation des entreprises. L'obésité est liée à un très grand nombre de problèmes de santé."

Translation: "Considered until very recently as a personal problem, obesity is becoming a corporate concern. Obesity is linked to a very large number of health problems."

The SHIFT is clear: personal → corporate. And the REASON follows: because it's linked to health problems that affect work.

✅ Answer: B

🔑 New technique: TEMPORAL SHIFT DETECTION

When a sentence begins with a past-time marker ("il y a peu," "autrefois," "jadis," "considéré longtemps comme"), the gap must complete the present contrast:

PAST:    "Considered as X..."
GAP:     "[it is now becoming Y]"
PRESENT: "because of Z..."

The gap always states the NEW STATUS. Eliminate any option that:

  • Repeats the past status (option D)
  • Contradicts the article's direction (option C)
  • States something too extreme for the tone (option A)

QUESTION 37 — The "Résultat:" Consequence Gap (Inverted Logic Pattern)

The landscape:

BEFORE the gapTHE GAPAFTER the gap
"l'obésité est liée à un très grand nombre de problèmes de santé. Résultat :"??? (37)"Le mal au dos, par exemple, explique Amanda Wynne... est la première cause d'absentéisme."

Step 1: Decode "Résultat :"

You learned the colon as a definition device in Chapter 17 (Gap 38). Here, the colon works with "Résultat" to create a consequence structure:

CAUSE: obesity is linked to health problems
"Résultat:" = CONSEQUENCE:
GAP: [what happens as a result]
EXAMPLE: back pain → absenteeism

The gap must state the GENERAL CONSEQUENCE that "back pain causing absenteeism" is an EXAMPLE of.

What general consequence does "back pain → absenteeism" illustrate? Obese employees take more sick leave.

Step 2: The Inverted Logic Trap — CRITICAL WARNING

Look at the four options:

OptionWhat it saysDirection
A) les plus obèses sont les plus résistants aux maladiesObese = MOST resistant to disease🔴 POSITIVE — opposite of text
B) plus ils sont obèses, plus ils prennent de congés maladiesObese = MORE sick leave🟢 NEGATIVE — matches text
C) les plus obèses sont les moins absents au lieu de travailObese = LEAST absent🔴 POSITIVE — opposite of text
D) plus ils sont obèses, mieux ils supportent la fatigue et le stressObese = BETTER at handling fatigue🔴 POSITIVE — opposite of text

Three out of four options say the EXACT OPPOSITE of what the text needs.

This is the Inverted Logic Decoy Pattern — the most dangerous trap in texte à trous. The exam deliberately creates options that:

  • Use the same sentence structure as the correct answer
  • Discuss the same topic (obesity and work)
  • Sound grammatically perfect
  • But reverse the LOGICAL DIRECTION

Option A: "most resistant" instead of "most vulnerable"
Option C: "least absent" instead of "most absent"
Option D: "better support" instead of "worse support"

Only option B maintains the NEGATIVE direction that matches "Résultat" (consequence of a problem):

Obesity = health problems → Result: more sick leave → Example: back pain = #1 cause of absenteeism

Step 3: The "par exemple" confirmation.

The phrase "par exemple" (for example) after the gap confirms that the gap states a GENERAL claim and the text provides a SPECIFIC illustration:

GAP (general):  Obese employees take more sick leave
"par exemple":  Back pain is the #1 cause of absenteeism

If the gap said "obese employees are the most resistant" (option A), then "back pain causing absenteeism" would be a COUNTER-example, not an example. The "par exemple" would make no sense.

✅ Answer: B

🔑 New technique: THE INVERTED LOGIC DECOY PATTERN

This is the single most important trap pattern to recognize in Phase 4. Here's how it works:

THE EXAM'S STRATEGY:
1. The text clearly argues in ONE direction (obesity = problem)
2. Three options argue in the OPPOSITE direction (obesity = advantage)
3. Only ONE option maintains the correct direction
4. All four options are grammatically perfect and topically relevant

How to defeat it:

Before reading any option, determine the LOGICAL DIRECTION the gap must take:

Ask yourselfFor this gap
Is the text discussing a PROBLEM or a BENEFIT?Problem (obesity)
Does the gap need a NEGATIVE or POSITIVE consequence?Negative (it follows "Résultat" of a problem)
Does the example AFTER the gap illustrate something good or bad?Bad (absenteeism)

Then scan the options for DIRECTION before reading details:

  • A: "résistants" → positive → ELIMINATE
  • B: "congés maladies" → negative → KEEP
  • C: "moins absents" → positive → ELIMINATE
  • D: "mieux supportent" → positive → ELIMINATE

This takes 10 seconds and eliminates 3 options instantly.

QUESTION 38 — The "En outre" Addition Gap (Inverted Logic Pattern, Round 2)

The landscape:

BEFORE the gapTHE GAPAFTER the gap
"Or, l'obésité est l'une des principales causes de ce type de douleur. En outre,"??? (38)"Et ça, aucun employeur n'en veut. Car l'obésité a un coût."

Step 1: Decode "En outre" — the addition marker.

"En outre" means "Moreover" / "In addition." It signals that the gap adds a SECOND negative consequence to the first one (absenteeism).

FIRST consequence (before):  Obesity causes back pain → absenteeism
"En outre" = IN ADDITION:
GAP:                         [SECOND negative consequence]
AFTER:                       "And THAT, no employer wants."

Step 2: Decode "Et ça, aucun employeur n'en veut."

This sentence is the KEY. "And that, no employer wants." The word "ça" (that) refers to whatever the gap states. And the employer's reaction is rejection — they don't want it.

So the gap must state something that employers find UNDESIRABLE. Something NEGATIVE about obese employees' work performance.

Step 3: Apply the Inverted Logic Detection — scan for DIRECTION.

OptionKey wordDirectionEmployer would want this?
A) plus efficacesefficaces = efficient🔴 POSITIVE✅ Employers WANT efficiency → "no employer wants this" makes NO sense
B) particulièrement assidusassidus = dedicated/present🔴 POSITIVE✅ Employers WANT dedication → "no employer wants this" makes NO sense
C) risques d'être moins productifsmoins productifs = less productive🟢 NEGATIVE❌ Employers DON'T want reduced productivity → "no employer wants this" is PERFECT
D) grande concentrationconcentration = focus🔴 POSITIVE✅ Employers WANT concentration → "no employer wants this" makes NO sense

The pattern is identical to Question 37: three positives, one negative.

Only option C creates a coherent sentence:

"En outre, les salariés qui souffrent vraiment de surpoids ont des risques d'être moins productifs. Et ça, aucun employeur n'en veut."

Translation: "Moreover, employees who truly suffer from excess weight risk being less productive. And that, no employer wants."

Step 4: Verify the "ça" reference.

"Et ça" (and that) = reduced productivity. Does it work?

  • "No employer wants reduced productivity" ✅ Perfect
  • "No employer wants greater efficiency" ❌ Absurd
  • "No employer wants great concentration" ❌ Absurd
  • "No employer wants dedicated employees" ❌ Absurd

✅ Answer: C

🔑 Reinforced technique: TESTING "ÇA/CELA" REFERENCES

This builds on Chapter 17's pronoun tracking. When "ça," "cela," or "ceci" appears after a gap, it refers to the CONTENT of the gap. Insert each option and ask: "Does 'that' make sense when referring to this option?"

This is a two-step verification:

Step 1: Determine DIRECTION (positive/negative) → eliminate 3 options
Step 2: Verify "ça" reference → confirm the remaining option

MarkerFunctionDirection

"Ainsi" tells you: "The companies mentioned next are PROOF of what the gap states."

Standard Life Healthcare and Unilever observed a clear improvement → this PROVES what the gap claims.

So the gap must make a GENERAL CLAIM that "clear improvement in employee diet" serves as evidence for.

When an option contains a connector, check whether that connector is COMPATIBLE with the connector in the text after the gap. Two conflicting connectors create logical chaos.

🔑 New technique: THE "AINSI" BACKWARD CONFIRMATION

"Ainsi" connects a general claim to specific proof. The structure is:

GAP:              [General positive/negative claim about a phenomenon]
"ainsi":          [Therefore/Thus]
Named examples:   [Specific companies/people/cases that PROVE the claim]

To solve: read the EXAMPLES first, determine whether they are SUCCESS stories or FAILURES, then select the gap option that makes a matching general claim.

Additionally, always perform the CONNECTOR CLASH TEST: if an option contains its own connector (Mais, Pourtant, Cependant, En revanche), check whether it clashes with the connector in the surrounding text.

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS AND FAILURE MODES

Pitfall 1: The Triple Inversion Ambush

What happens: You read Gap 37, see four options about obesity and work, and feel overwhelmed because they all seem plausible.

Why it's dangerous: Under time pressure, your brain gravitates toward the option that "sounds most reasonable." But three options sound reasonable precisely because they're well-constructed — they just say the OPPOSITE of what's needed.

The fix: NEVER evaluate options by how "reasonable" they sound in isolation. Always evaluate them against the text's DIRECTION. The question isn't "Is this statement true about obesity?" but "Does this statement FIT the argument being made HERE?"

🔑 Rule: Plausibility is not the test. Coherence with the text's direction is the test.

Pitfall 2: The "Semantic Neighbor" Trap

What happens: You pick an option because its vocabulary is CLOSE to the right topic but belongs to a different sub-field.

Example: Gap 39 — "insertion des obèses dans l'activité professionnelle" (option B) uses workplace vocabulary. But "insertion" is a social inclusion term (anti-discrimination), while the text discusses health and wellness programs. These are neighboring but distinct policy domains.

The fix: After selecting an option, list the KEY NOUNS in both the option and the surrounding text:

Option B: insertion, obèses, activité professionnelle
Text after: activité physique, alimentation, régimes, sommeil, stress

MISMATCH: "activité professionnelle" ≠ "activité physique"
          "insertion" ≠ "régimes, sommeil, stress"

🔑 Rule: Close vocabulary is not matching vocabulary. Check the EXACT nouns, not the general topic.

Pitfall 3: Ignoring Connector Clashes

What happens: You evaluate an option's CONTENT without noticing that it begins with a connector (Mais, Pourtant, Cependant) that contradicts the text's own connector.

Example: Gap 40 — Option D starts with "Pourtant" (However), but the text after contains "ainsi" (thus). "However... thus" creates a logical impossibility: you can't concede AND confirm simultaneously.

The fix: Always check the FIRST WORD of each option. If it's a connector, test whether it's compatible with the connector in the surrounding text.

Compatible pairsIncompatible pairs
"En effet" + "ainsi" (both confirm)"Mais" + "ainsi" (contradiction + confirmation)
"De plus" + "en outre" (both add)"Pourtant" + "ainsi" (concession + confirmation)
"Or" + "donc" (premise + conclusion)"Cependant" + "d'ailleurs" (opposition + support)

🔑 Rule: Connectors are not decoration. They are logical operators. Two incompatible operators in sequence = broken logic.

Pitfall 4: Misreading "Pour [Name]" as Purpose Instead of Attribution

What happens: You read "Pour Amanda Wynne" and interpret "Pour" as "For" (purpose) instead of "According to" (attribution).

  • "For Amanda Wynne" = something done for her benefit
  • "According to Amanda Wynne" = her professional opinion

The fix: In French, "Pour + [person's name]" at the beginning of a sentence almost always means "In [person's] view" / "According to [person]." This is an attribution marker, not a purpose marker.

"Pour Amanda Wynne, les employeurs doivent..."
= "In Amanda Wynne's view, employers must..."
≠ "For Amanda Wynne's sake, employers must..."

🔑 Rule: "Pour + proper noun + comma" = attribution of opinion. Always.
